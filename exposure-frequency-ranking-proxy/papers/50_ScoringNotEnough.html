Scoring Is Not Enough: Addressing Gaps in Utility-fairness Trade-offs for Ranking

Singh et al. · arXiv · 2026

arXiv:2606.26369 · PDF · SP: SP5 · SP8

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

임의의 per-item scoring 후 정렬(score-then-sort)은 합성 의존적(non-decomposable) exposure 목표에 대해 Pareto 열등임을 증명한다(2026). 리스트 구성/빈도 수준 제어를 re-scoring 계열 전체 위로 구조적으로 끌어올리는, 사용자 설정에 가장 중요한 부정적 결과.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Fair learning-to-rank overwhelmingly follows one paradigm: learn a per-document scoring function (possibly fairness-aware), then rank at inference time by sorting scores. Prior work (Fair-PGRank, group-fair PL models, etc.) assumed this in-processing route can reach any desired utility-fairness trade-off given enough data. No prior work asked whether the score-then-sort architecture is itself structurally incapable of reaching the Pareto frontier when the fairness objective (group exposure disparity) is not decomposable into per-document contributions.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

A limitation theorem plus a tractable workaround:

  1. Theorem 1 (deterministic): for any position-weight vector w with w_i + w_{m−i+1} non-constant (includes DCG weights) and any α < 1, there is a problem instance and query distribution where no deterministic scoring function — even one that knows true relevance, group, and the query distribution (the infinite-data abstraction) — achieves the optimal expected trade-off. Core mechanism: the score sees only individual documents, but exposure disparity depends on the group composition of the whole query; an adversarial mixture of two query compositions defeats any fixed score ordering.
  2. Theorem 2 (randomized): randomized scoring (incl. Plackett-Luce) fails too — a randomized ranker that mimics the score-induced ranker except on the adversarial event strictly improves the trade-off.
  3. Appendix C: same conclusion for across-query (amortized) exposure fairness, via a linear-programming construction of the optimal ranking.
  4. Positive side: ex-post re-ranking on learned relevances — greedy, and a novel beam-search variant (Algorithm 1) — empirically approaches the exhaustive Pareto frontier at all α, dominating trained PL scorers.
Key Results주요 결과
  • Corollary 1 (DCG weights, m = 9): every scoring function has trade-off gap ≥ 0.05·(1−α); Monte-Carlo shows the true gap ≈ 0.30 at α = 0 (~6× the theoretical lower bound), vanishing as α → 1 or as relevance asymmetry γ grows.
  • Synthetic data (k = 5, m = 8): trained PL-RELAX overlaps the best possible deterministic scorer (SimScoring) — i.e., scoring is at its ceiling — yet both are dominated by greedy and beam search; beam (B = 4) nearly matches the brute-force ex-post Pareto frontier, especially at low α.
  • COMPAS (m = 10, race groups, GBR relevance model R² = 0.79): beam search weakly dominates greedy and clearly dominates PL-RELAX at all α.
  • German Credit: both PL-RELAX and Fair-PGRank (retrofitted with the paper's objective) are dominated by greedy/beam at all α.
  • MovieLens 100K (popularity groups, weak relevance models R² = 0.09–0.11): ex-post methods still match or beat PL; decoupling relevance learning from trade-off selection means no retraining when α changes, unlike scoring.
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: theoretical (impossibility/limitation result) + system (beam-search post-processing) — mixed. Context: challenges Singh & Joachims (Fair-PGRank, 2019; Fairness of Exposure, 2018), Gorantla et al. 2023 (group-fair PL), Ovaisi et al. 2024 (greedy re-ranking); uses Gadetsky et al. 2019 PL-RELAX estimator; positions against the Zehlike et al. 2022 fair-ranking survey taxonomy. Correctness: counterexample constructions are simple and checkable (two document types, equal relevance); Corollary 1 quantifies the gap; experiments report percentile bars (Fig. 3). arXiv preprint, not yet peer-reviewed. [SKIM — proofs in Appendix B read plausible but not line-checked.] Contributions: (1) formal scorability definition + proof that deterministic/randomized/across-query scoring is Pareto-suboptimal for exposure-disparity fairness; (2) the root cause identified as non-decomposability of fairness vs. decomposable utility; (3) beam-search ex-post ranking as a tractable near-Pareto alternative. Clarity: reproducible — problem instances, algorithm pseudocode, dataset preparation, and hyperparameters (B = 4, softmax temperature schedule) are all stated.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: score-then-sort cannot cover the achievable utility-fairness trade-off region; the issue persists for deterministic, randomized, and across-query settings; beam search approaches exhaustive post-processing tractably.
  • Demonstrated: theorems cover one specific disparity family (Eq. 3, binary groups, per-position weights); empirical dominance of ex-post shown on 1 synthetic + 3 real datasets with small queries (m = 8–20); exhaustive-ex-post comparison only on the synthetic set (m = 8).
  • Gap: authors themselves scope this out — fairness notions defined as properties of the score itself (e.g., Zhu et al. 2020) are not covered, and utilities like MAP fall outside the framework (they conjecture but do not prove the gap persists). Generality beyond binary groups and the specific disparity normalization is asserted as "likely" but not proven. Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Scorability (Definition 1)

  • What it computes: takes a (utility, unfairness) objective pair (U, V) and a trade-off level α → asks whether, for every problem instance (documents, relevances, groups) and query distribution P, there exists a scoring function score(rel(d), g(d)) whose sort-induced ranking attains the max expected trade-off E_Q[αU − (1−α)V] over all (query-dependent) rankings → outputs yes/no per (U, V, α).
  • Why it was created: the fair-LTR literature implicitly assumes "given enough data, a learned scorer can reach the best utility-fairness trade-off"; no prior work had formalized or tested that assumption.
  • Concrete example: with DCG weights w_i = 1/(log2(i)+1) and m = 9, the paper's Corollary 1 shows every scoring function leaves a trade-off gap of at least 0.05·(1−α) vs. the optimal ranker; simulation puts the actual gap ≈ 6× larger (≈ 0.30 at α = 0).
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the central definition; Theorems 1–2 are counterexamples proving non-scorability for deterministic and randomized scoring, and Appendix C extends to across-query fairness.

Interaction-disparity unfairness (per-query, Eq. 3)

  • What it computes: given a ranking σ of query Q → |mean over group-a documents of w_i·rel − mean over group-b documents of w_i·rel| → a scalar exposure-disparity between two document groups, where w_i·rel is the position-weighted interaction (exposure × relevance).
  • Why it was created: instantiates a generic family of group-exposure fairness metrics that is not sum-decomposable over documents — the structural property driving all the impossibility results.
  • Concrete example: with one document type per group at equal relevance 1, ranking p group-a docs first gives unfairness φ(p) = (1/p)Σ_{i≤p} w_i − (1/(m−p))Σ_{i>p} w_i.
  • How it is used in this paper: the V term of the trade-off objective T_α = αU − (1−α)V in all theorems and experiments.

Beam Trade-off Search (Algorithm 1)

  • What it computes: input = (documents, relevances, groups), beam width B, trade-off level α → maintains B partial rankings; at each position expands each beam with the top remaining document from each group, evaluates the truncated trade-off, prunes back to top-B → output = full ranking with best trade-off. Cost O(m log m + m·B·log B).
  • Why it was created: exhaustive ex-post re-ranking is O(m!) and greedy re-ranking is myopic at low α (fairness-heavy regimes); beam search relaxes greed tractably.
  • Concrete example: B = 4 gave the best results on the synthetic dataset (k = 5 document types, m = 8).
  • How it is used in this paper: the proposed positive alternative — Section 5.2 and all experiments (Fig. 2, 5, 6).

Plackett-Luce ranking and REINFORCE-style gradient estimation are used as-is (PL-RELAX from Gadetsky et al. 2019), representing scoring-based methods; standard, not re-defined here.

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: only relevance estimates + group labels at inference; greedy is O(m log m + m), beam O(m log m + m·B·log B) per query — trivially cheap. Datasets used (COMPAS, German Credit, MovieLens 100K) are public.

  • Modifications: for our setting, replace the binary-group disparity V with the target exposure-frequency deviation; beam search applies unchanged as long as the objective is computable on partial rankings (truncated trade-off).
  • Unknowns: missing link — behavior of beam search when exposure targets are amortized across queries at scale (App. C solves the small case by LP, not by beam); missing link — sensitivity to relevance-estimation noise beyond the softmax-temperature heuristic used for German Credit.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Fairness of Exposure in Rankings (Singh & Joachims, 2018) — the canonical exposure-fairness formulation this paper's disparity metric descends from; foundational for SP5/SP8.
  • Policy Learning for Fairness in Ranking / Fair-PGRank (Singh & Joachims, 2019) — the in-processing scoring approach this paper proves structurally limited; used as a baseline here.
  • Fairness of Interaction in Ranking under Position, Selection, and Trust Bias (Ovaisi et al., 2024) — the greedy ex-post method the paper's greedy baseline represents; fits the same fairness framework but missed the scoring-suboptimality point.
  • Optimizing Group-Fair Plackett-Luce Ranking Models (Gorantla et al., 2023) — post-processing-aware score learning; a candidate middle ground between the two paradigms contrasted here.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = queries of m = 8–20 documents with known/learned relevances and binary group labels; outputs = full permutations per query; conditions = infinite-data abstraction for theory, exposure measured as position-weight × relevance disparity between two groups, per-query (mainly) or amortized across queries. Our setting (SP5/SP8 — exposure frequency as a ranking proxy): systems that keep per-instance rankings score-driven while imposing exposure-frequency targets on items/groups, typically at production scale with many items and amortized exposure accounting. Differences:

  • Score-driven per-instance ranking + exposure targets is exactly the architecture the paper proves Pareto-suboptimal (Thm 1–2 per-query, App. C amortized) — this is the paper's direct relevance as a limitation bound on our SP's core mechanism: resolved — the theorems transfer whenever the exposure objective is non-decomposable over items.
  • Binary groups vs. many items/groups or item-level exposure targets: missing link — counterexamples use two groups; extension to per-item exposure constraints is plausible but not shown.
  • Small query sizes (m ≤ 20) and known relevances vs. large-scale retrieval with noisy scores: missing link — beam search's near-Pareto behavior is only validated at toy scale; exhaustive frontier unavailable beyond m = 8.
  • Fairness-as-score-property formulations (score decorrelated from group): reference only — explicitly excluded from the impossibility result, and a possible escape route for score-driven designs.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper isolates and proves the structural root cause (per-document scores cannot express an objective that depends on whole-query group composition, i.e., non-decomposability), rather than patching an observed metric gap; its beam-search remedy then follows from the diagnosis by moving the fairness accounting to where the query composition is visible (ex-post).
