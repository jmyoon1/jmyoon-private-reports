Optimizing Long-term Social Welfare in Recommender Systems: A Constrained Matching Approach

Mladenov et al. · ICML 2020 · 2020 · 71 citations

arXiv:2008.00104 · PDF · SP: SP9

P0Track A

한 줄 요약

장기 사회 후생을 제약 매칭으로 최적화하는 추천(ICML 2020). relevance-argmax 배분이 provider 이탈을 통해 장기 후생을 붕괴시킴을 보인다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

RS research assumes a user's utility can be maximized independently of other agents' utility. In reality, ecosystem dynamics couple all agents' long-term utility: content providers require a minimum level of user engagement to remain viable, and the standard myopic policy (match each query to the reward-argmax provider) starves niche providers below their viability threshold. Their exit permanently removes content that is ideal for some user segments, driving the system to a low-welfare, low-diversity equilibrium — a failure invisible to any per-query relevance objective.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Formulate recommendation as equilibrium selection in the induced dynamical system, and show it reduces to a single-epoch constrained matching problem:

$$\max_\pi \sum_{u \in U} \mathbb{E}[f(r^\pi(u)) \mid \pi] \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \pi_{q,c} > 0 \text{ only if } \sum_{u, q_u} Q(q_u)\, \pi_{q_u,c} \geq \nu_c \;\; \forall q, c$$

where $\pi_{q_u,c}$ is the proportion of queries $q_u$ matched to provider $c$. Applying this single-epoch policy every epoch is proven to maximize long-run average social welfare (the anticipated equilibrium is reached in the first epoch; non-stationary deviations give only transient gains). For additive/discounted utility this becomes a MILP with provider-viability binaries $y_c$ (Eq. 4), NP-hard but $\frac{1}{e}$-approximable via submodular greedy (Theorem 1) or, in practice, LP-relaxation rounding (LP-RS). For non-linear (e.g. sigmoidal) utility, a column-generation model is developed. [SKIM - verify column-generation details and robustness extensions in deep read]

Key Results주요 결과
  • Worked example (Fig. 1a): myopic policy long-run per-period welfare $8+2\varepsilon$ vs. optimal constrained matching $10-2\varepsilon$, with max user regret only $2\varepsilon$ — provider diversity and fairness emerge as by-products of maximizing user welfare alone.
  • Theorem 1: the welfare-optimal constrained matching MILP is approximable to factor $\frac{1}{e}$ in polynomial time (via submodularity of $cSW$ in the provider set).
  • Welfare vs. max-regret tradeoff is real but often mild (Fig. 1b: +$\varepsilon$ welfare costs $8\varepsilon$ max regret); diminishing-returns (discounted) utility makes viability easier to maintain with lower regret.
  • Empirical: LP-RS produces "much higher social welfare than myopic policies" and greater provider diversity; LP rounding indistinguishable from greedy in welfare with better runtime. [SKIM - exact numbers in Sec. 4 not read]

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Provider viability threshold ($\nu_c$)

  • What it computes: provider $c$'s cumulative user engagement during an epoch $E^\pi_k(c)$ → comparison against a fixed threshold $\nu_c$ → binary viability outcome: if $E^\pi_k(c) \geq \nu_c$ the provider stays on the platform for epoch $k+1$; otherwise it abandons the platform and can no longer be matched.
  • Why it was created: prior RS models treat the provider catalog as static; this makes provider attrition — the mechanism by which myopic ranking degrades long-run welfare — invisible to the optimization.
  • Concrete example: in Fig. 1(a), each of 3 providers requires 2 user impressions per period; the myopic policy gives $c_3$ only 1 impression, so $c_3$ exits and its ideal user $u_6$ gets reward 0 forever after.
  • How it is used in this paper: appears as constraint (2)/(4) in the matching optimization — a provider may be matched only if the policy routes enough expected query mass to keep it above $\nu_c$.

Equilibrium selection via constrained matching

  • What it computes: user/query distribution $Q$, reward function $r(q_u,c)$, thresholds $\nu_c$ → single-epoch welfare-maximizing stochastic matching $\pi_{q_u,c}$ subject to viability constraints → a stationary policy whose induced viable-provider set $V \subseteq C$ is a maximal-welfare equilibrium of the ecosystem dynamical system.
  • Why it was created: myopic per-query argmax policies drive the dynamical system to a poor equilibrium (few viable providers, low welfare); the authors show the long-run problem collapses to a single-epoch constrained matching, avoiding a full MDP/POMDP treatment.
  • Concrete example: Fig. 1(a): myopic long-run per-period welfare $8+2\varepsilon$ vs. constrained-matching welfare $10-2\varepsilon$ by "subsidizing" $c_3$ with user $u_5$.
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the core problem formulation (Eq. 1–2), solved as a MILP (Eq. 4).

Constrained social welfare submodularity ($cSW$)

  • What it computes: a fixed candidate viable-provider set $C' \subseteq C$ → LP solve of the matching with $y_c$ fixed → maximum welfare $cSW(C')$; the paper proves $cSW$ is submodular in $C'$.
  • Why it was created: the MILP (4) is NP-hard (contains facility location as the $\nu_c = 0$ special case); submodularity licenses a greedy provider-addition algorithm with a $\frac{1}{e}$ approximation guarantee (Theorem 1).
  • Concrete example: greedy adds providers one at a time, each time re-solving a polynomially sized LP, until no further provider can be kept viable.
  • How it is used in this paper: the approximation-guarantee backbone; in practice replaced by the LP-rounding heuristic LP-RS, which performs indistinguishably and faster.

Maximum user regret $MR(\pi)$

  • What it computes: per user, the gap between average utility under the user's personally optimal policy and under $\pi$; $MR(\pi)$ is the max over users.
  • Why it was created: welfare maximization can sacrifice individual users (a subsidizing user gets a slightly worse match); regret quantifies that individual cost and exposes the welfare-vs-max-regret tradeoff (Fig. 1(b): welfare +$\varepsilon$ at max-regret cost $8\varepsilon$).
  • Concrete example: in Fig. 1(a), the welfare-optimal policy has max regret only $2\varepsilon$; a naive alternative subsidy assignment ($u_3 \to c_3$) yields max regret $1+\varepsilon$ and lower welfare $9+\varepsilon$.
  • How it is used in this paper: analyzed as a fairness measure; the paper argues welfare-optimal matchings tend to have low max regret, especially under diminishing-returns utility ($\alpha_t = \gamma^{t-1}$ discounting). [SKIM - verify tradeoff mechanism in Sec. 3.3–4]

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = known user/query embeddings in $X \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$, known reward $r(q_u,c) = q_u^\top c$, known provider viability thresholds $\nu_c$, stationary user distribution; outputs = stochastic matching policy over providers; conditions = stylized simulation, epoch-based viability, expectations realized exactly (LLN), fixed embeddings, complete information. Our setting: exposure frequency used as a proxy signal in ranking — item/provider exposure counts feed back into ranking decisions; SP9 concerns ecosystem-level welfare effects of exposure allocation under such proxies. Differences:

  • Known viability thresholds $\nu_c$: our setting has no observable provider thresholds — exposure-frequency proxies would have to estimate viability indirectly. missing link
  • Complete-information matching (known embeddings, known $Q$): real ranking systems learn under uncertainty and exploration. resolved: paper acknowledges this and argues incomplete information exacerbates (not invalidates) the myopic failure; robustness extensions sketched in Sec. 3 [SKIM - verify]
  • Provider-level matching vs. item-level ranking with position effects: paper matches queries to providers 1-to-1 per query, no ranked slates or position bias. missing link
  • Long-run equilibrium objective vs. per-query proxy metrics: directly relevant — the paper is the formal argument for why exposure allocation must anticipate provider dynamics. reference only
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper identifies the structural root cause of ecosystem collapse (myopic per-query optimization ignoring the coupling between exposure allocation and provider viability) and reformulates the objective itself as equilibrium selection, rather than patching symptoms with diversity or exposure-quota heuristics.
