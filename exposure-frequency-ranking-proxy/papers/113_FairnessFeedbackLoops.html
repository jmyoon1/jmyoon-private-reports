Fairness Feedback Loops: Training on Synthetic Data Amplifies Bias

Wyllie et al. · arXiv · 2024 · 78 citations

arXiv:2403.07857 · PDF · SP: SP10

P2Track C

한 줄 요약

합성 데이터 학습이 편향을 증폭하는 공정성 feedback loop를 분석한다. 루프 무시 offline 지표가 붕괴 중인 시스템을 정상으로 인증함(상대 정확도 ~100% vs 실제 붕괴)을 보인다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Existing work treats model-driven distribution shifts as separate silos: performative prediction (Perdomo et al. 2020) analyzes one deployment generation of a supervised model; model collapse (Shumailov et al. 2023) covers generative-model chains; disparity amplification (Hashimoto et al. 2018) covers user disengagement. Practitioners lack (a) a common framework to analyze these shifts when several models participate in the same data ecosystem over many generations, and (b) any account of the fairness consequences — prior model-collapse work measured only utility loss, not minoritized-group erasure.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Empirical-framework paper. (1) Unify the three phenomena as model-induced distribution shifts (MIDS), classified by the model action that pollutes the ecosystem (predictions become labels; generated outputs become inputs; group disengagement). (2) Build sequential-training simulations (SeqClass, SeqGen±SeqClass) with oracle models $G_0, A_L, A_S$ approximating the original distribution, and track accuracy, demographic-parity difference, equalized-odds difference, group representation, and KL divergence of intersectional "strata" over up to 40 generations. (3) Show harms arise even in initially balanced datasets and even when the shift flows through labels alone. (4) Propose STAR quota-sampled training batches as a simulated algorithmic-reparation intervention that partially reverses the harms.

Key Results주요 결과
  • CelebA under model collapse + performative prediction: ~15% accuracy drop and complete erasure of the minoritized group (0% representation).
  • ColoredMNIST/ColoredSVHN SeqClass: 10–15% accuracy drop over 40 generations; DP and EOdds unfairness each up ~0.2 (ColoredSVHN).
  • FairFace SeqClass: accuracy 57% → random within 10 generations; EOdds unfairness +0.2.
  • Measuring generation $i$ against generation $i-1$'s labels (instead of original data) reports near-100% accuracy and near-perfect fairness — unawareness of MIDS systematically overstates both.
  • Sequential classifiers adapt to model collapse (10–20 pp higher accuracy than non-sequential ones) but become substantially more unfair (EOdds +0.6 on ColoredMNIST) — the feedback loop trades accuracy preservation for amplified bias.
  • cla-STAR significantly reduces DP/EOdds unfairness in SeqClass; gen-STAR preserves group/label balance better under 100%-synthetic training; non-synthetic data (50-50 mix) slows all MIDS.
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: empirical framework + simulation study (with a mitigation algorithm, STAR) Context: builds directly on performative prediction (Perdomo et al. 2020), model collapse (Shumailov et al. 2023; Alemohammad et al. 2023), data feedback loops (Taori & Hashimoto 2023), disparity amplification (Hashimoto et al. 2018), and algorithmic reparation (Davis et al. 2021). For this survey it is the Track-C bridge between model-collapse literature and the recommender-feedback-loop literature. Correctness: [SKIM] — figures report 95% CIs over multiple seeds (5–25 depending on dataset); CelebA/FairFace runs terminated early (5–10 generations) for compute cost, so long-run claims there rest on extrapolation. Contributions: (1) MIDS unification enabling analysis of co-occurring shifts; (2) evidence that generational feedback degrades fairness and erases minoritized groups, and that unawareness of MIDS overstates both accuracy and fairness (relative metrics show ~100% accuracy while true accuracy collapses); (3) STAR as an intentional counter-MIDS. Clarity: [SKIM] — architectures, hyperparameters, and Algorithm 1 are given in appendices; anonymized code linked.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장

[SKIM — verify in deep read] Notable from the skim: the authors themselves flag that they provide no real AR use case and "cannot make claims of equity or justice" — the uniform fairness ideal is a stand-in. The synthetic-data setting is worst-case (all data from the immediately preceding model), which they acknowledge may overstate collapse speed. Whether the MIDS framework yields predictions beyond the four vision datasets is claimed but not demonstrated.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Model-Induced Distribution Shift (MIDS)

  • What it computes: an umbrella category, defined operationally — given a lineage of models where generation $i$ is trained (partly) on outputs of generation $i-1$, MIDS is the measurable drift of the data-ecosystem distribution relative to the original reference distribution $\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{L} \times \mathcal{S}$ (inputs × labels × sensitive attributes).
  • Why it was created: prior literature studied performative prediction, model collapse, and disparity amplification as separate phenomena; no framework could track them when they co-occur in one data ecosystem (e.g., a pseudo-labeler annotating synthetic images).
  • Concrete example: in the paper's CelebA experiments, model collapse plus performative prediction produces a 15% accuracy drop and complete erasure (0% representation) of the minoritized group within ~8 generations.
  • How it is used in this paper: organizing framework (Table 1) mapping each MIDS to the model action that induces it and the resulting data-ecosystem change; drives the SeqClass / SeqGenSeqClass simulation settings.

SeqClass / SeqGenSeqClass settings

  • What it computes: simulation pipelines. SeqClass: classifier $C_i$ is trained on inputs from a fixed generator $G_0$ but labels from $C_{i-1}$ (predictions become next-generation ground truth). SeqGenSeqClass: generators $G_i$ are also chained ($G_i$ trained on $G_{i-1}$ samples), and classifiers train on $G_i$ outputs labeled by $C_{i-1}$.
  • Why it was created: to observe multiple MIDS individually and co-occurring over many (up to 40) generations, which single-generation performative-prediction analyses do not cover.
  • Concrete example: FairFace SeqClass accuracy drops from 57% to random within 10 generations; equalized-odds unfairness jumps by 0.2.
  • How it is used in this paper: the two main experimental suites (Section 4).

STAR (STratified sampling Algorithmic Reparation)

  • What it computes: a batch-curation algorithm — sample a pool of $b + r$ items from the generator/dataset, annotate with label model $C$ and sensitive-attribute oracle $A_S$, fill a per-category quota (Cartesian product of label × group) toward a target "fairness ideal" distribution (uniform in the paper), backfill the remainder randomly. $r$ is a reparation budget (25–33% of batch size).
  • Why it was created: to test whether an intentional, equity-directed MIDS (algorithmic reparation, from Davis et al. 2021) can counteract harmful MIDS in the same ecosystem.
  • Concrete example: with binary label × binary group, ideal = [b/4, b/4, b/4, b/4] per batch (Algorithm 1).
  • How it is used in this paper: two variants — cla-STAR (applied when training classifiers) and gen-STAR (applied when training generators) — evaluated against no-reparation baselines.

MIDS "enablers" — sampling, data annotation, pseudo-labelling, generative feedback: mechanisms that are not themselves MIDS but transmit or permit them (e.g., a pseudo-labeler letting model collapse and fairness feedback loops co-occur). [SKIM — Appendix B has the full taxonomy]

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: standard vision stack (PyTorch, Fairlearn), VAEs + CNN/ResNet classifiers, GPU-weeks for the face datasets (CelebA/FairFace chains took ~1 week per run); the ColoredMNIST setting runs on a single T4.
  • Code: anonymized repository linked in the paper (anonymous.4open.science/r/FairFeedbackLoops-1053); GitHub release promised on acceptance — [SKIM: check whether released].
  • Modifications: for SP10, replace the classifier/generator chain with a ranker/logging-policy chain and demographic strata with exposure strata; the metric suite (actual vs. relative performance; KL to a target distribution) transfers directly.
  • Unknowns: missing link — no guidance on choosing the "fairness ideal" distribution in non-demographic settings; missing link — behavior when synthetic/model-influenced data fraction is unknown (their ablation assumes it is controlled).
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • The Curse of Recursion: Training on Generated Data Makes Models Forget (Shumailov et al., 2023) — the model-collapse setting this paper extends with fairness metrics; likely already in this survey's Track C.
  • Data Feedback Loops: Model-Driven Amplification of Dataset Biases (Taori & Hashimoto, 2023) — bias-amplification upper bound for prediction-contaminated datasets; the closest formal predecessor.
  • Performative Prediction (Perdomo et al., 2020) — single-generation formalism generalized here to multi-generation chains.
  • Fairness Without Demographics in Repeated Loss Minimization (Hashimoto et al., 2018) — source of disparity amplification; its DRO mitigation is explicitly rejected here as inappropriate for reparative goals.
  • Runaway Feedback Loops in Predictive Policing (Ensign et al., 2017) — the FML precedent for training-set re-weighting that inspired cla-STAR; directly relevant to SP10's exposure-loop framing.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = image datasets (ColoredMNIST, ColoredSVHN, CelebA, FairFace); outputs = binary classification labels + VAE-generated images across model generations; conditions = explicit sensitive-attribute annotations, oracle annotators $A_L/A_S$, fully controlled retraining chains, 5–40 generations, fairness measured as DP/EOdds differences between groups. Our setting (SP10): exposure/frequency-driven feedback in ranking systems — a ranker's output distribution (what gets exposed) shapes the interaction data that retrains the ranker, making exposure frequency a self-reinforcing proxy for relevance/quality. Differences:

  • Feedback channel: here predictions become labels or synthetic inputs; in ranking, the model shapes which items receive feedback at all (exposure → observation bias) — structurally analogous (both are MIDS in the paper's taxonomy, closest to performative prediction) but not identical: missing link — the paper does not model position/exposure effects.
  • Modality: vision classification/generation vs. ranked item lists: reference only.
  • Sensitive-attribute framing: paper's fairness is group-based (race/gender); SP10's "minoritized" analogue is low-exposure items/providers: resolved — the strata/quota mechanism transfers by substituting item-popularity strata for demographic strata.
  • Intervention: STAR's quota-sampled training batches map onto exposure-aware resampling of ranking training data: resolved conceptually, missing link empirically (never tested on ranking data).
  • The "relative vs. actual performance" finding maps directly onto ranking evaluation on logged (model-influenced) data vs. unbiased holdout: resolved — same mechanism, strong conceptual import for SP10.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the MIDS framework correctly names the root cause (model outputs re-entering training data as ground truth) and the diagnostic finding about relative-metric fairwashing is root-cause-level; but the proposed mitigation, STAR, rebalances batch strata by quota sampling, which patches the observable representation imbalance without removing the feedback channel itself (the loop keeps running; STAR just biases it toward a chosen ideal, and the authors show it fails when the generators can no longer produce minority samples at all).
