Fast solution to the fair ranking problem using the Sinkhorn algorithm

Yuki Uehara et al. · arXiv · 2024 · 3 citations

arXiv:2406.10262 · PDF · SP: SP3

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

Sinkhorn 알고리즘으로 공정 ranking 문제를 고속으로 근사 해결한다. exposure 단위 선택(count/position/impact)에 따라 수혜자가 뒤집힘을 보여주는 사례(43.2% 항목이 impact 기준으로는 악화)이기도 하다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

The impact-based fair ranking policy of Saito & Joachims (2022) — a stochastic ranking maximizing Nash Social Welfare over per-user doubly stochastic matrices, guaranteeing envy-freeness, dominance over uniform rankings, and Pareto optimality — requires solving a large-scale constrained nonlinear optimization problem. Solving it with commercial optimization software (Mosek) does not scale to practical recommender systems.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여
  1. Formulate, per user $u$, the entropic-regularized optimal transport problem (7): minimize $\sum_{i,k} c_{uik} x_{uik} + \varepsilon \sum_{i,k} x_{uik}(\log x_{uik} - 1)$ subject to the doubly-stochastic constraints (1)–(3); its solution $X_u^\star(C_u)$ is computed by the Sinkhorn algorithm.
  2. Transform the constrained NSW problem (6) into the unconstrained problem (8): optimize $F(X^\star(C))$ over cost matrices $C \in \mathbb{R}^{|U|\times|I|\times m}$. Theorem 1 shows this loses no feasible points ($c_{uik} = -\varepsilon \log x_{uik}$ recovers any feasible $X_u$).
  3. Algorithm 1: repeat — for each user in parallel, compute $X_u = X_u^\star(C_u)$ via Sinkhorn; update $C$ by gradient ascent on $F$ (chain rule through the Sinkhorn solution, implemented with PyTorch Adam) — until the gradient norm falls below threshold. GPU-parallelizable across users.
Key Results주요 결과
  • NSW(Algo1) matches NSW(Mosek) quality on synthetic data ($|U|=1000, |I|=500, m=11$): user utility 2.16 vs 2.16, mean max envy 0.00 both, items-better-off 97.28% both, items-worse-off 0.04% both.
  • MaxRele baseline: higher user utility (4.29) but mean max envy 0.17, items-worse-off 92.96% — relevance-only ranking is severely unfair by impact metrics.
  • ExpFair(Mosek) (exposure-based fairness): utility 2.97 but items-worse-off 43.20% — exposure fairness does not deliver impact fairness.
  • NSW(Algo1+GPU) is ~10x faster than the CPU version and ~1000x faster than NSW(Mosek); its runtime is nearly independent of the number of consumers.
  • Delicious dataset ($|U|=1014, |I|=100$): NSW(Algo1) 99.80% items better off, 0.00% worse off, envy 0.00.
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: new method (fast algorithm for an existing objective) Context: directly accelerates Saito & Joachims (2022) impact-based NSW fair ranking; contrasts with exposure-based fairness (Singh & Joachims 2018; Biega et al. 2018) and scalable exposure control (Togashi et al. 2024). Builds on Cuturi (2013) Sinkhorn distances. Correctness: Theorem 1 (feasible-region preservation) is proven and simple; no convergence/optimality guarantee for the non-convex outer problem (8) — quality is validated only empirically. Results averaged over 5 trials; no error bars visible in bar charts. Contributions: (1) unconstrained Sinkhorn-based reformulation of the NSW fair ranking problem; (2) GPU-parallel gradient ascent algorithm ~1000x faster than Mosek with comparable ranking quality; (3) empirical validation on synthetic + Delicious datasets. Clarity: reproducible — the formulation, Theorem 1 proof, and Algorithm 1 pseudocode fit in 6 pages; anonymous code link provided.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: fast solution to the impact-based fair ranking problem; ~1000x speedup over commercial software; high-quality fair rankings.
  • Demonstrated results: speedup and quality parity are shown on one synthetic setup and one small public dataset (Delicious, $|I|=100$); Theorem 1 proves representability, not convergence to the NSW optimum.
  • Gap: "practical-scale recommender systems" motivates the work, but the largest experiment is ~1600 items/consumers — no truly industrial-scale demonstration; no guarantee the non-convex outer problem reaches the global NSW optimum (empirical parity with Mosek is the only evidence).
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Note: the paper is 6 pages total, so this skim covered the full text; residual [SKIM] markers are omitted where the full content was visible.

Impact-based fair ranking (NSW objective)

  • What it computes: given relevance scores $r(u,i)$ and position exposure probabilities $e(k)$, computes per-item impact $\mathrm{Imp}_i(X_i) = \sum_{u}\sum_{k} r(u,i)\, e(k)\, x_{uik}$ and maximizes the Nash Social Welfare $F(X) = \sum_{i} \log \mathrm{Imp}_i(X_i)$ over doubly stochastic ranking matrices $X_u$.
  • Why it was created: introduced by Saito & Joachims (2022) — exposure-based fairness leaves the relationship between exposure and actual marketing impact (clicks, conversions) on items unclear; impact fairness targets the downstream quantity directly.
  • Concrete example: on the synthetic dataset ($|U|=1000$, $|I|=500$, $m=11$), NSW methods get 0.00 mean max envy vs. 0.17 for relevance-only ranking.
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the objective (problem 6) whose scalable solution the paper provides.

Sinkhorn reparametrization of the doubly-stochastic constraint set (Theorem 1)

  • What it computes: replaces the constrained problem over doubly stochastic matrices $X_u$ with an unconstrained problem over transport cost matrices $C_u$; each $X_u^\star(C_u)$ is the entropic-regularized optimal transport solution computed by the Sinkhorn algorithm. Theorem 1: for any feasible $X_u$, setting $c_{uik} = -\varepsilon \log x_{uik}$ recovers it exactly — so the reparametrized feasible region is unchanged.
  • Why it was created: solving the large-scale constrained nonlinear NSW program with commercial solvers (Mosek) is intractable at practical recommender-system scale.
  • Concrete example: gradient ascent over $C$ with Sinkhorn in the inner loop, GPU-parallel over users, is ~1000x faster than Mosek on the synthetic benchmark.
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the core algorithmic contribution (Algorithm 1: while gradient norm > threshold, run Sinkhorn per user in parallel, then Adam-update $C$ via chain-rule gradients).

Sinkhorn algorithm / entropic optimal transport — standard prior tool (Cuturi 2013); relied on, not introduced. Solves the entropy-regularized transport problem (7) by alternating row/column scaling; GPU-friendly.

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: relevance matrix $r(u,i)$, exposure model $e(k)$, PyTorch (Adam) + a GPU for the fast variant; memory $O(|U||I|m)$ for the cost tensor.
  • Code: available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/nsw-with-optimal-transport-1C04/ (anonymous repository).
  • Modifications: to use for pure exposure-frequency control (SP3), swap the NSW-of-impact objective for an exposure-target objective over the same doubly stochastic matrices — the Sinkhorn inner loop is objective-agnostic.
  • Unknowns: missing link: sensitivity to the entropic regularization $\varepsilon$ (bias toward smoothed rankings) is not reported; missing link: behavior at industrial catalog sizes ($10^5$+ items) untested.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Saito & Joachims, "Fair Ranking as Fair Division: Impact-Based Individual Fairness in Ranking" (KDD 2022) — the objective this paper accelerates; defines the impact/NSW framework.
  • Singh & Joachims, "Fairness of Exposure in Rankings" (KDD 2018) — the exposure-based family this paper uses as the ExpFair baseline; core to SP3's exposure-as-proxy framing.
  • Togashi, Abe & Saito, "Scalable and provably fair exposure control for large-scale recommender systems" (arXiv 2402.14369, 2024) — the exposure-side scalable alternative; direct comparison point for SP3.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = given relevance scores $r(u,i)$ and position exposure model $e(k)$; outputs = per-user doubly stochastic ranking matrices (stochastic ranking policy); conditions = two-sided marketplace, impact-based (NSW) fairness objective, offline optimization, up to ~1600 items. Our setting (SP3): exposure frequency as a controllable ranking-proxy quantity — post-processing methods that compute exposure-allocating stochastic rankings from fixed scores. Differences:

  • Objective is impact fairness (NSW over $r \cdot e \cdot x$), whereas SP3's proxy interest is exposure itself: reference only — but the same Sinkhorn machinery applies to any objective over doubly stochastic $X_u$ (the paper's own future-work note), so the technique transfers. resolved: the doubly-stochastic + position-exposure formalism is identical to the exposure-based family (Singh & Joachims 2018), which appears here as the ExpFair baseline.
  • Requires known relevance $r(u,i)$ (true scores assumed given, no estimation noise): missing link — sensitivity of the fair policy to score estimation error is not studied.
  • Scale evidence stops at ~10^3 items: missing link for web-scale exposure control (cf. Togashi et al. 2024, cited as the scalable exposure-control alternative).
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the targeted problem is computational intractability of the constrained NSW program, and the paper removes that root cause by an exact reparametrization (Theorem 1) rather than by heuristic pruning or problem shrinkage; the one caveat is that global optimality of the resulting non-convex outer problem is only empirically supported.
