Fairness in Online Ad Delivery

Baumann et al. · FAccT 2024 · 2024 · 9 citations

link · SP: SP7

P4Track A

온라인 광고 전달에서의 공정성 문제를 정리한 FAccT 2024 연구.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Prior work can measure and describe unfair distribution of high-stakes ads (employment, housing, credit) as it occurs on real platforms, but provides no tools to model and predict what would happen if popularly proposed fairness constraints were actually enforced — i.e., what utility costs platforms, users, and advertisers would bear, and whether the interventions would even help the disadvantaged group.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Simulation study [SKIM - verify simulator design]: the authors build an ad distribution system simulator and enforce each of several proposed fairness criteria (statistical parity, predictive parity, equality of opportunity), measuring the resulting utility of the platform and of user groups under varying scenarios (including asymmetric advertising costs across groups). Empirical findings from the abstract:

  1. Statistical parity costs platforms much more utility than predictive parity or equality of opportunity in many realistic scenarios.
  2. The fairness notions trade off against each other — enforcing one worsens others.
  3. When one group is costlier to advertise to, fairness enforcement tends to "level down" (no group benefits) unless the platform itself carries the fairness cost.
Key Results주요 결과
  • Statistical parity imposes a much higher platform-utility cost than predictive parity or equality of opportunity in many realistic scenarios. [SKIM - exact numbers in paper]
  • Fairness criteria conflict: enforcing one criterion degrades outcomes on the others. [SKIM - magnitude]
  • With group-asymmetric advertising costs, fairness enforcement produces leveling-down effects; these are prevented only if the platform carries the fairness cost rather than passing it to users or advertisers.
  • Limiting targeting options alone is insufficient to address delivery-layer discrimination (delivery optimization reintroduces skew).

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Note: skim from abstract only — PDF unavailable (ACM DL blocks direct download; no arXiv or repository copy found). Concept definitions below are standard fairness- criteria one-liners; how this paper operationalizes them in the ad-delivery simulation is [SKIM - verify in deep read].

Statistical parity (demographic parity) in ad delivery

  • What it computes: group membership + ad-exposure outcome → constraint that the rate of receiving the (high-stakes) ad is equal across demographic groups → equal exposure rates. [SKIM - verify how exposure rate is defined per auction vs. per user]
  • Why it was created: exposure to employment/housing/credit ads was observed to skew by group even without explicit targeting.
  • Concrete example: if 30% of group A sees a job ad, 30% of group B must too, regardless of predicted click/conversion probability.
  • How it is used in this paper: one of the enforced fairness constraints whose platform-utility cost is measured in simulation; shown to be the most expensive to enforce.

Predictive parity / equality of opportunity (as delivery constraints)

  • What it computes: predicted relevance scores + group labels → constraints on error-rate or precision parity across groups (equal precision for predictive parity; equal true-positive rate for equality of opportunity) → group-conditional calibration/recall equality. [SKIM - verify exact operationalization on ad outcomes]
  • Why it was created: statistical parity ignores genuine group differences in ad relevance; these criteria condition on the outcome or prediction.
  • Concrete example: equality of opportunity — among users who would actually benefit from (e.g., click/convert on) a job ad, delivery probability is equal across groups.
  • How it is used in this paper: compared against statistical parity; shown to cost the platform substantially less utility in many realistic scenarios.

Leveling down (in fairness enforcement)

  • What it computes: pre- vs. post-intervention group utilities → check whether equality was achieved by lowering the better-off group's outcome without raising the worse-off group's → a flag that no group benefited.
  • Why it was created: names a known failure mode of equality-only constraints from the political-philosophy/fairness literature.
  • Concrete example: if group B is more expensive to advertise to, enforcing parity may simply cut group A's ad exposure to group B's level, benefiting no one.
  • How it is used in this paper: identified as the likely outcome when one group is costlier to reach; the proposed remedy is that platforms absorb the fairness cost rather than passing it to users/advertisers.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = simulated ad auction/delivery system with user groups, advertiser budgets, group-dependent costs; outputs = platform/user/advertiser utility under enforced fairness constraints; conditions = simulation (no real platform data at delivery time), group labels known, high-stakes ad domains. Our setting (SP7 — delivery-layer fairness landscape for exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy): exposure/impression frequency used as an observable proxy for ranking or delivery decisions; interest in how delivery-layer optimization skews exposure and how fairness interventions alter exposure distributions. Differences:

  • Simulation vs. observational real-platform exposure data: missing link — the paper predicts intervention effects but does not validate against a live platform's exposure logs.
  • Ad-delivery utility framing vs. ranking-proxy framing: reference only — the paper treats exposure as the fairness outcome itself, not as a proxy signal for ranking quality; useful as landscape anchor, not as method source.
  • Group fairness criteria vs. exposure-frequency measurement: resolved: partially — its exposure-rate-per-group quantities are directly the kind of exposure statistics SP7 studies. [SKIM - verify exact exposure metrics used]
