Modeling impression discounting in large-scale recommender systems

Lee et al. · KDD 2014 · 2014 · 36 citations

· SP: SP7

P0Track C

한 줄 요약

대규모 recommender에서 이미 본 item의 노출을 감쇠하는 impression discounting(LinkedIn, KDD 2014). production 빈도 개입의 고전.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Recommender systems at LinkedIn (PYMK, Skill Endorsements) repeatedly re-show items the user has already seen and ignored. Prior work models explicit feedback (ratings, accepts) or estimates CTR for new/first-exposure items (Agarwal 2009; Richardson 2007); the link between an item's impression history and its future conversion (not click) probability was unmodeled — so high-scored but repeatedly ignored items dominate the list and depress overall conversion rate.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

A plugin re-ranking layer, deliberately external to the (black-box) recommendation engine:

  1. Formalize each impression as a tuple $\mathcal{T} = (user, item, conversion, [behaviors], t, R)$; group by (user, item) into impression sequences.
  2. Verify Hypothesis 1 (no-action impression = negative feedback) empirically: conversion rate decreases monotonically with ImpCount on all three datasets (PYMK, Endorsement, Tencent SearchAds).
  3. Per behavior, pick the decay function (linear/inverse/exponential/quadratic) with the lowest fit RMSE via GAM-guided correlation analysis; aggregate multiple behaviors linearly or multiplicatively, linearize to one linear regression.
  4. Fit coefficients with anti-noise regression (outlier removal + density-weighted least squares, ridge fallback).
  5. At serving time compute $d \in (0,1]$ and re-rank by $R \cdot d$.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Offline PYMK: P@10 improvement +13.7% with (LastSeen, ImpCount); +31.3% adding Position + UserFreq (vs. no-discounting ranking).
  • Online A/B on LinkedIn PYMK: invitation-rate P@10 improvement +11.97% to +13.26% (±0.2%) across three decay-function combinations; best = inverse decay on LastSeen × exponential decay on ImpCount.
  • Endorsement (short sequences): only +1.3% to +3.4% — discounting matters most where impression sequences are long.
  • Exponential decay best fits conversion-vs-behavior curves (RMSE 0.0012 on LastSeen vs. 0.0041 linear).
  • Anti-noise regression: support-weighted RMSE 0.0188 vs. 0.1121 for ordinary linear regression on PYMK.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: no-action impressions are negative feedback (Hypothesis 1); the plugin approach improves conversion; anti-noise regression beats ordinary regression on skewed-support data.
  • Demonstrated: monotone conversion decay on 3 datasets; offline P@k and online A/B gains on PYMK; RMSE gains for the regression method.
  • Gap: Hypothesis 1 is verified as aggregate correlation only — the paper does not disentangle exposure fatigue (causal) from item-quality selection (items surviving many impressions without conversion are simply worse matches), yet the discount is interpreted causally. Online A/B is reported only for PYMK, not Endorsement/SearchAds. [SKIM - verify in deep read]
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Impression discounting factor $d$

  • What it computes: input = a candidate item's historical impression sequence for a given (user, item) pair (behaviors such as ImpCount, LastSeen, Position, UserFreq) → operation = evaluate a learned regression model $\tilde{y} = \sum_{i=0}^{m} u_i \tilde{X}_i$ predicting conversion rate, then normalize $d = \tilde{y} / \max \tilde{y} \in (0, 1]$ → output = a multiplier applied to the base recommendation score: $\mathcal{T}^*.R = \mathcal{T}.R \cdot d$.
  • Why it was created: prior recommenders modeled explicit feedback (ratings, accepts) but ignored "no-action" implicit negative feedback — an item repeatedly shown without conversion kept its high score and kept being re-shown.
  • Concrete example: in PYMK, normalized conversion rate falls steeply as ImpCount grows from 1 to ~6 (Figure 3); an item impressed many times without an invite gets $d \ll 1$ and drops in the re-ranked list.
  • How it is used in this paper: the core mechanism — a plugin multiplied onto the black-box engine's score $R$ to re-rank the top-100 candidate list before display.

Behavior signals (ImpCount, LastSeen, Position, UserFreq)

  • What it computes: per (user, item) impression tuple, four observable features: ImpCount = number of historical impressions before the current one; LastSeen = days since the last impression of the same pair; Position = rank offset in the displayed list; UserFreq = the user's visit frequency to the recommender.
  • Why it was created: to formalize the facets of no-action impression history that correlate with future conversion.
  • Concrete example: GAM correlation analysis on PYMK shows LastSeen has a stronger (narrower-CI) negative correlation with conversion rate than ImpCount; UserFreq correlates positively (active users convert more).
  • How it is used in this paper: as the $X_i$ inputs to the aggregated discounting model; adding Position + UserFreq on top of (LastSeen, ImpCount) more than doubles offline precision gains.

Discounting functions (linear / inverse / exponential / quadratic decay)

  • What it computes: parametric forms $f_L(x) = \alpha_1 x + \alpha_2$, $f_I(x) = \frac{\alpha_1}{x} + \alpha_2$, $f_E(x) = e^{\alpha_1 x + \alpha_2}$, $f_Q(x) = \alpha_1 (x-\alpha_2)^2 + \alpha_3$ mapping one behavior value to a predicted conversion-rate component; combined by linear ($\hat{y} = \sum_i w_i f(X_i)$) or multiplicative ($\hat{y} = w \prod_i f(X_i)$) aggregation, then linearized to a single linear regression.
  • Why it was created: to give the plugin an interpretable functional form for the empirically observed decay of conversion with each behavior, instead of an opaque classifier.
  • Concrete example: exponential discounting achieves the minimal RMSE for both ConveRate~LastSeen (0.0012) and ConveRate~ImpCount (0.0028) on PYMK (Table 1).
  • How it is used in this paper: the "atoms" of the aggregated model; the deployed A/B model fixes exponential decay on ImpCount and varies the LastSeen atom.

Anti-noise (density weighted) regression

  • What it computes: input = observations $(X, y)$ with support counts → operation = (1) migrate density-based-clustering concepts to regression: classify observations as core/border/outlier via support-sum density in an $\varepsilon$-neighborhood with tolerance $\gamma$, discard outliers (~5%); (2) weight each squared error by $v_i$ = neighborhood support share, solving $\hat{u} = (X^T V^2 X + \lambda I)^{-1} X^T V^2 y$ → output = regression coefficients robust to sparse-tail noise.
  • Why it was created: at high ImpCount/LastSeen values observation support is sparse, so the empirical conversion-rate curve tail fluctuates wildly and ordinary least squares overfits to those noisy tail points; naive support-threshold truncation is hard to calibrate at billion-record scale.
  • Concrete example: on PYMK with $\varepsilon = 2, \gamma = 0.01, \delta = 2$, support-weighted RMSE drops from 0.1121 (plain linear regression) to 0.0188.
  • How it is used in this paper: the fitting procedure for all discounting-model coefficients; claimed as a standalone methodological contribution.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: impression-level logging with (user, item, timestamp, conversion, position); any linear-regression package; trivially cheap at serving time (evaluate a small parametric function).
  • Code: not available (industrial system; no public repo).
  • Modifications: for a research setting, decay-function fitting is directly reproducible on any public impression log (e.g., the KDD Cup 2012 Tencent SearchAds data they used, which is public); the anti-noise regression needs {ε, γ, δ} retuning per dataset.
  • Unknowns: missing link: observation-window length, fitted coefficient values, and how discount interacts with position bias at serving time are not disclosed.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Gupta et al., "Factoring past exposure in display advertising targeting" (KDD 2012) — the ad-side predecessor this paper positions itself against; the frequency-capping lineage for SP7's taxonomy.
  • Hu, Koren & Volinsky, "Collaborative filtering for implicit feedback datasets" (ICDM 2008) — the in-model implicit-feedback alternative to the plugin approach.
  • Post-2016 exposure-bias / propensity-weighting work (e.g., Joachims et al. unbiased LTR) — addresses the causal gap this paper leaves open; check which survey refs cover it.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = logged impression tuples with per-(user,item) impression counts and recency from LinkedIn feed-style recommenders + Tencent search ads; outputs = multiplicative discount on an existing engine's score; conditions = billions of impressions, binary conversion label, item deduplication after conversion, 2014-era linear-regression tooling. Our setting: exposure frequency as a ranking proxy — understanding when impression/exposure counts are a valid signal (or confounded proxy) inside ranking objectives for modern recommenders/LLM-era systems. Differences:

  • Uses ImpCount as a demotion signal learned from aggregate conversion decay, exactly the O1 taxonomy entry (feed-native sibling of frequency capping): reference only — this is the taxonomic anchor SP7 needs.
  • Correlational treatment of impression→conversion decay; no causal/selection-bias correction (no IPS, no exposure model): missing link — modern exposure-bias literature (post-2016) addresses precisely this hole.
  • Plugin (score × d) architecture vs. in-model exposure features: resolved — the paper explicitly discusses the plugin-vs-in-model tradeoff (modularity, model confidentiality) and picks plugin.
  • 2014 linear/parametric decay functions vs. learned sequence models of exposure: reference only.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Symptom — the paper demotes items along the observed aggregate conversion-decay curve without identifying why conversion decays with impressions (user fatigue vs. self-selection of low-affinity items into long impression sequences), so the underlying exposure/relevance confound remains unmodeled; the fix improves the metric while leaving the causal structure of "no action" unresolved.
