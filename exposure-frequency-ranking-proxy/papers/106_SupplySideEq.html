Supply-Side Equilibria in Recommender Systems

Jagadeesan et al. · NeurIPS 2023 · 2023 · 49 citations

arXiv:2206.13489 · PDF · SP: SP9

P0Track C

한 줄 요약

recommender의 공급 측 균형(supply-side equilibria)을 분석한 NeurIPS 2023 논문. 전문화 상전이(β*)를 특성화한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Recommender systems shape not just what users consume but what producers create, yet existing theory cannot answer "what content will be produced at equilibrium?" under personalized recommendation. Two gaps in prior work: (1) existing ML-side models (Ben-Porat et al.) restrict producers to finite topic menus, missing the infinite multi-dimensional embedding space where genres emerge; (2) classical economic models of competition (Bertrand price competition, Hotelling/Salop facility location) are low-dimensional and lack the nonlinear production-cost structure of digital goods, so they cannot express the quality-vs-genre tradeoff producers face under embedding-based matching.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

A game-theoretic model: users and goods are vectors in $\mathbb{R}^D_{\geq 0}$; the platform shows each user the good with max dot product; producer $j$'s profit is $\mathbb{E}[\sum_i \mathbb{1}[j^*(u_i) = j]] - \|p_j\|^\beta$. Main results (from intro overview):

  1. Existence & structure (Props. 1–3): pure equilibria never exist; symmetric mixed equilibria always exist (via Reny's discontinuous-games machinery) and are atomless (infinite support).
  2. When specialization occurs (Theorem 1): a single-genre equilibrium exists iff $\max_{y \in S^\beta} \prod_{i=1}^N y_i = \max_{y \in \bar{S}^\beta} \prod_{i=1}^N y_i$ (equality of the Nash-welfare max over $S^\beta$ and its convex hull). Depends on user geometry and cost function, not on the number of producers P. Single-genre equilibria exist exactly for $\beta \leq \beta^*$.
  3. Form of specialization (Theorem 2, two equal populations, $\ell_2$ cost): equilibria have either one or infinitely many genres for finite P — producers randomize over a continuum of genres balancing the two populations. Two-genre equilibria re-emerge only in the $P \to \infty$ limit (Theorem 3). [SKIM - verify Theorem 2/3 statements and continuity conditions in deep read]
  4. Single-genre location (Corollary 6): the single genre maximizes users' Nash social welfare $\arg\max_{\|p\|=1} \sum_i \log \langle p, u_i \rangle$ — competition implicitly maximizes a fairness-efficiency-compromise welfare.
  5. Market competitiveness (Props. 7–8): multi-genre (specialized) equilibria can yield strictly positive producer profit (e.g., $1 - 2/\beta$ for the $e_1,e_2$, $P=2$ case); single-genre equilibria always have zero profit. Specialization ⇒ local monopolies, paralleling homogeneous vs differentiated goods markets.
Key Results주요 결과
  • Theorem 1: single-genre equilibrium exists iff the Nash-welfare maximum over $S^\beta$ equals that over its convex hull $\bar{S}^\beta$; independent of the number of producers P.
  • Corollary 4: for two user populations at angle $\theta^*$ with $\ell_2$ cost, $\beta^* = 2/(1 - \cos\theta^*)$ — interpolates from 2 (orthogonal users) to $\infty$ (identical users).
  • Theorem 2: for finite P, equilibria have either 1 or $\infty$ genres — never finitely many $> 1$; two-genre equilibria exist only at $P = \infty$ (Theorem 3).
  • Corollary 7 / Props. 7–8: equilibrium profit $= 1 - 2/\beta > 0$ at multi-genre equilibria (2 users, $P=2$, $\beta \geq 2$); always 0 at single-genre equilibria. Sufficient condition for positive profit: $\max_{\|p\| \leq 1} \min_i \langle p, u_i/\|u_i\| \rangle < N^{-P/\beta}$.
  • MovieLens-100K + NMF empirics (§3.4): higher embedding dimension D lowers the estimated $\beta^*$ ⇒ specialization more likely; the single-genre direction is not the mean user and tilts toward cheaper dimensions. Platform can tune specialization via the number of matrix-factorization factors. [SKIM - exact figures in Fig. 5 not extracted]

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Supply-side equilibrium (producer game over content embeddings)

  • What it computes: Given N user vectors $u_i \in \mathbb{R}^D_{\geq 0}$ and P producers each choosing a content vector $p_j \in \mathbb{R}^D_{\geq 0}$, the platform assigns each user to $j^*(u) = \arg\max_j \langle u, p_j \rangle$ (hardmax dot-product recommendation); producer profit = (expected # users won) − $\|p_j\|^\beta$. The equilibrium object is a symmetric mixed Nash equilibrium: a single distribution $\mu$ over $\mathbb{R}^D_{\geq 0}$ from which all producers sample.
  • Why it was created: Prior supply-side models (Ben-Porat & Tennenholtz 2018; Ben-Porat, Rosenberg & Tennenholtz 2020) restrict producers to a finite topic set, which cannot express the emergence of genres in a continuous multi-dimensional content space.
  • Concrete example: 2 users at $e_1, e_2$, $P=2$, cost $\|p\|_2^\beta$: for $\beta \geq 2$ the equilibrium is supported on the quarter-circle of radius $(2\beta^{-1})^{1/\beta}$ with angle density $2\cos\theta\sin\theta$ (Prop. 5).
  • How it is used in this paper: The central object; all results characterize its support and profit level.

Specialization / Genre($\mu$)

  • What it computes: Genre($\mu$) := $\{p/\|p\| \mid p \in \mathrm{supp}(\mu)\}$ — the set of content directions at equilibrium, separating direction (genre) from magnitude (quality). Specialization occurs iff $|\mathrm{Genre}(\mu)| > 1$.
  • Why it was created: Specialization is inherently an asymmetric phenomenon, but asymmetric equilibria are intractable; defining it via the support of a symmetric mixed equilibrium makes it analyzable.
  • Concrete example: In Figure 1, at $\beta = 2$ the equilibrium support is a single ray (one genre); at $\beta = 2.5$ it becomes a quarter-circle arc (infinitely many genres).
  • How it is used in this paper: The main dependent variable — Theorems 1–2 characterize when $|\mathrm{Genre}(\mu)| = 1$ vs $> 1$.

Specialization threshold $\beta^*$

  • What it computes: $\beta^* := \sup\{\beta \geq 1 \mid \max_{y \in S^\beta} \prod_i y_i = \max_{y \in \bar{S}^\beta} \prod_i y_i\}$, where $S$ = image of the unit cost ball under the user matrix $U$ and $S^\beta$ its coordinate-wise $\beta$-power. Single-genre equilibria exist iff $\beta \leq \beta^*$; the condition is a convexity-type property of $S^\beta$ (Theorem 1), proven via strong duality of a minimax program (Lemma 1, Sion's theorem). [SKIM - proof details in Appendix C not verified]
  • Why it was created: No closed form exists for the equilibrium in general, so the existence of a single-genre equilibrium had to be characterized implicitly.
  • Concrete example: For 2 user populations at angle $\theta^*$ with cost $\|p\|_2^\beta$: $\beta^* = \frac{2}{1-\cos(\theta^*)}$ (Corollary 4) — orthogonal users give $\beta^* = 2$, aligned users give $\beta^* = \infty$.
  • How it is used in this paper: Phase-transition boundary between homogenization and specialization; estimated empirically on MovieLens-100K embeddings.

Producer cost exponent $\beta$ (cost $c(p) = \|p\|^\beta$, arbitrary norm)

  • What it computes: One-time production cost as a superlinear function of content "quality" $\|p\|$; the norm + exponent jointly encode how expensive it is to be good on multiple dimensions at once (e.g., a movie that is both drama and comedy).
  • Why it was created: Classical spatial-competition models (Hotelling, Salop) have no analog of nonlinear multi-dimensional production cost; this is the lever that drives specialization.
  • Concrete example: Under the $\ell_\infty$ norm (pay only for the max coordinate) specialization never occurs regardless of $\beta$; under $\ell_q$ norms, $\beta^* \geq q$ with equality at standard-basis users (Corollary 3).
  • How it is used in this paper: Together with user-vector geometry, fully determines the equilibrium regime.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: For the empirical pipeline: a ratings dataset (MovieLens-100K), NMF via scikit-surprise, cvxpy for the Nash-welfare program and the $\beta_e$ binary-search estimate; trivially CPU-scale. The theory needs no compute.
  • Code: available at https://github.com/mjagadeesan/supply-side-equilibria
  • Modifications: To use for SP9's exposure-frequency questions, the win-count objective $\sum_i \mathbb{1}[j^*(u_i)=j]$ must be replaced with an exposure-frequency payoff (e.g., softmax or top-k slot shares) — the duality argument for Theorem 1 relies on the hardmax structure, so it is unclear how much survives. Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = fixed user embeddings $u_i \in \mathbb{R}^D_{\geq 0}$ known perfectly by the platform; producers choose one static content vector each; outputs = symmetric mixed equilibrium distribution over content; conditions = hardmax single-item recommendation (top-1 only), one-shot simultaneous game, per-user fixed revenue, homogeneous cost function across producers. Our setting (SP9 — how exposure/ranking rules shape content supply; exposure frequency as ranking proxy): ranked lists with multiple exposure slots and position-dependent exposure frequency; producers respond to realized exposure over time (repeated, learning dynamics); platform learns embeddings from noisy feedback; producer payoff proportional to exposure frequency, not just top-1 wins. Differences:

  • Top-1 hardmax matching vs. multi-slot ranked exposure with position effects: missing link — the paper's win-or-lose structure has no notion of graded exposure frequency; Hron et al.'s softmax variant is the closest bridge.
  • One-shot equilibrium vs. repeated best-response / learning dynamics: missing link — explicitly deferred by the authors (§6); convergence-to-equilibrium is studied in Ben-Porat et al. 2020 under finite topics only.
  • Perfect platform knowledge of embeddings vs. learned/noisy embeddings: partially resolved — §3.4 grounds the model in NMF-learned MovieLens embeddings, showing the model's quantities are computable from real matrix-factorization output, but strategic response to estimation error is not modeled.
  • Fixed per-user revenue vs. exposure-weighted monetization: reference only — authors note the pricing extension as future work.
  • Static content quality vs. content evolving with feedback: reference only.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct. The paper attacks the root question — what content supply an embedding-based exposure rule causes at equilibrium — by deriving necessary-and-sufficient conditions (Theorem 1) from the primitives (user geometry, cost structure), rather than measuring downstream diversity symptoms; the identified mechanism (convexity of $S^\beta$ / phase transition at $\beta^*$) explains why homogenization or specialization occurs, not merely that it does.
