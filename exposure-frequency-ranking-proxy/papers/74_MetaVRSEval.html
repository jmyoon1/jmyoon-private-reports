External Evaluation of Discrimination Mitigation Efforts in Meta's Ad Delivery

Imana et al. · FAccT 2025 · 2025 · 2 citations

arXiv:2506.16560 · PDF · SP: SP7

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

Meta 광고 전달의 차별 완화(VRS)를 외부에서 평가한 FAccT 2025 감사. impression 분산 지표는 18/18 준수하면서 고유 도달(reach)은 −9.82%, CPP +12% — 그리고 순진한 집단별 빈도 예산 분할(순수 빈도 개입)이 score 측 개입을 모든 집단에서 지배함을 보인다. denomination이 부하를 받는 이유의 핵심 증거.

Problem해결하려는 문제

The Meta/DoJ 2022 settlement and Meta's VRS are the first legally mandated fairness intervention on a production ad-delivery system, but no one outside Meta had tested whether it actually improves access to opportunities. Three specific gaps in the settlement terms make compliance possible without benefit: (1) variance is defined over impressions, not unique individuals reached, so repeat exposure to the same users satisfies it; (2) coverage targets count ads, not impressions, allowing selective application of VRS to small ads while exempting large ones; (3) nothing prevents leveling down (reducing everyone's exposure to equalize).

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Empirical audit, not a new method. Three parts:

  1. Settlement-terms analysis (§3): shows via Meta Ad Library data (32,867 political ads, 1.3B impressions, power-law spend distribution) that exempting the largest 19% of ads (permitted under the 81% race-coverage target) would exclude 78.9% of impressions from the fairness intervention vs. 19% under random exclusion.
  2. Black-box paired-campaign evaluation (§4): N=36 experiments — 6 ad creatives × race/gender × 3 audience replications, $20/24h per ad, ~1,500 impressions each — identical ads run with and without VRS (toggled via housing self-declaration) on disjoint demographically balanced audiences.
  3. Budget-splitting alternative (§5): split the $20 budget into four $5 campaigns targeting White-male/White-female/Black-male/Black-female subgroups separately; compare variance, reach, and cost per 1,000 reached (CPP) against a single VRS-enabled campaign.
Key Results주요 결과
  • VRS reduces race variance from >10% (all 18 no-VRS cases) to <10% in 18/18 and <5% in 15/18 cases — first external confirmation VRS meets its compliance metric.
  • Enabling VRS reduces mean reach by 9.82% and increases cost per 1,000 unique users reached (CPP) by 12.02% across 36 paired experiments — fewer unique people see opportunity ads for the same budget.
  • Leveling down observed in 4/6 hair-product experiments: both groups lose reach (e.g. Black −268, White −36 in HA2-r).
  • VRS's voluntary extension to employment/credit ads is much weaker: credit/job-declared ads deliver to 55–60% Black users (vs. 43–44% for housing-declared, ~60–65% no-VRS) and variance stays above the 10% housing threshold.
  • Budget-splitting with the same total budget beats VRS on reach and CPP for all four groups (Black, White, female, male) across N=36 comparisons.
  • Selective-application analysis: exempting the largest 19% of ads excludes ~1.03B of 1.3B impressions (78.9%) from the intervention.
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: system evaluation / external audit (empirical finding). Context: builds on paired-ads audit methodology (Ali & Sapiezynski et al. 2019; Imana et al. 2021, 2024), leveling-down theory (Mittelstadt et al. 2023; Baumann et al. 2024 simulations), and Meta's own VRS description (Timmaraju et al., KDD 2023). Within this survey it is the limitation-type entry for SP7: evidence that a delivery-layer impression-reallocation patch over an untouched auction does not fix exposure inequity. Correctness: [SKIM] — 36 paired experiments with 3 replications each; no confidence intervals visible in skimmed figures; eligible-ratio baseline is an estimate (mean of observed delivery ratios) that the authors themselves flag as noisy (BISG + DP noise unaccounted). Errata note: original FAccT version miscalculated male-group reach/CPP figures; revised without changing conclusions. Contributions: (1) first independent in-the-wild verification that VRS reduces impression variance per compliance metrics; (2) demonstration that VRS reduces reach (−9.82% mean) and raises advertiser cost (+12.02% CPP), passing the fairness cost to advertisers and producing leveling down; (3) demonstration that naive budget-splitting dominates VRS on both reach and cost for every group. Clarity: methodology reproducible — black-box, uses only regular-advertiser capabilities; all experiment data public.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Variance Reduction System (VRS)

  • What it computes: for each ad self-declared as housing/employment/credit, input = latest privacy-noised measurement of per-group impression variance → an ML module outputs an adjust-up/adjust-down action on a "VRS multiplier" that scales the advertiser-bid term of Meta's auction Total Value (Advertiser Bid × Estimated Action Rate + Ad Quality Score) → output = shifted delivery ratios toward the eligible ratios. The auction mechanism itself is untouched; only the bid component is reweighted per potential viewer.
  • Why it was created: Meta's 2022 DoJ settlement required reducing demographic skew in housing-ad delivery caused by the delivery-stage ML, even under neutral advertiser targeting.
  • Concrete example: if delivery ratio is (0.4, 0.6) male/female and eligible ratio is (0.5, 0.5), variance = ½(|0.5−0.4|+|0.5−0.6|) = 0.1, so VRS must move a 0.1 fraction of impressions toward males.
  • How it is used in this paper: VRS is the audited system — the paper measures its effect via paired live ad campaigns with VRS on vs. off.

Eligible ratio / Delivery ratio (impression-based variance metric)

  • What it computes: eligible ratio = group g's share of impressions received by the ad's eligible audience (all targeted users active in last 30 days) across all Meta ads; delivery ratio = group g's share of this ad's impressions. Variance = ½·Σ_g |eligible − delivery|.
  • Why it was created: the settlement's operationalization of "fair delivery" — align actual audience demographics with eligible audience demographics.
  • Concrete example: paper's estimated eligible ratios: 0.42/0.58 Black/White, 0.45/0.55 male/female (from mean delivery ratios over 36 VRS-enabled ads).
  • How it is used in this paper: the paper's central critique — the metric counts impressions, so showing an ad 100× to one woman and 1× each to 100 men achieves zero gender variance while exposing far fewer women.

Impressions vs. reach

  • What it computes: impressions = total display count of an ad; reach = number of unique accounts shown the ad.
  • Why it was created: standard ad-platform metrics; the paper elevates the distinction to a fairness-metric design flaw.
  • Concrete example: an algorithm showing an ad once to 100 unique men and 100 times to 1 woman scores perfect impression-parity but 100:1 reach disparity.
  • How it is used in this paper: the recommended fix is to measure variance over reach, not impressions — the exact frequency-vs-unique-exposure gap this SP studies.

Leveling down

  • What it computes: achieving parity by lowering the better-served group's outcome to the worse-served group's level, with no group gaining.
  • Why it was created: named in Mittelstadt et al. 2023; here shown to be permitted by the settlement (no non-decreasing-impressions constraint) and observed empirically for VRS.
  • Concrete example: in 4/6 hair-product-ad experiments, both demographic groups saw reduced reach after enabling VRS (e.g. HA2-r: Black −268, White −36).
  • How it is used in this paper: §3.3 predicts it from settlement terms; §4.2.3 confirms it with live ads.

Paired-ads black-box audit (VRS on/off) — builds on the paired-ads methodology of Ali & Sapiezynski et al. 2019; the novelty is toggling VRS by declaring/not declaring a non-housing ad as "housing," on disjoint balanced Custom Audiences (NC voter data, 30k people, race inferred via DMA-location proxy). Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: a Meta advertiser account, ~$20 × 72+ campaigns budget, a public voter file with race/location (NC), Meta Custom Audience + DMA-breakdown reporting APIs.
  • Code: experiment data public at https://ant.isi.edu/datasets/addelivery-vrs/ ; [SKIM] — code availability beyond data unclear.
  • Modifications: for SP7 use, the transferable artifact is the metric critique (measure reach/unique exposure, not impressions) and the leveling-down test design, not the ad-buying pipeline.
  • Unknowns: missing link: VRS's internal RL module, DP noise parameters (k, ε), and BISG threshold effects — opaque even to the settlement's own third-party reviewer.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Timmaraju et al., "Towards Fairness in Personalized Ads Using Impression Variance Aware Reinforcement Learning" (KDD 2023) — Meta's own description of the VRS mechanism this paper audits.
  • Imana, Korolova & Heidemann, "Auditing for Bias in Ad Delivery Using Inferred Demographic Attributes" (FAccT 2025) — companion paper on inference-error effects (BISG) in exactly this audit setting.
  • Baumann et al., "Fairness in Online Ad Delivery" (FAccT 2024) — the simulation work predicting leveling down that this paper confirms in the wild.
  • Gelauff et al., "Advertising for demographically fair outcomes" (2020) — the pre-settlement empirical precursor of the budget-splitting alternative.
  • Mittelstadt, Wachter & Russell, "The Unfairness of Fair Machine Learning" (2023) — source of the leveling-down concept.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = live Meta ad campaigns (6 creatives, $20/24h, balanced 30k-person Custom Audiences from NC voter data); outputs = per-group impressions, reach, CPP; conditions = black-box external audit of a production ad-auction + delivery-layer fairness patch (VRS bid multiplier), race inferred via location proxy, N=36 paired experiments. Our setting (SP7 — exposure frequency as ranking proxy): ranking/recommendation systems where per-item or per-group exposure counts (impressions/frequency) are used as the measured or optimized fairness quantity in place of unique-user access or true relevance-conditioned exposure. Differences:

  • Domain is ad auctions with paid bids, not organic ranking — the auction's Total Value plays the role of a ranking score, and VRS is a post-hoc score reweighting: resolved: structurally analogous to delivery-layer re-ranking over an untouched relevance model, which is why it serves as SP7's limitation exhibit.
  • The paper's fairness unit is demographic group share of impressions; SP7's concern is impression-count vs. unique-exposure as a proxy generally: resolved: the impressions-vs-reach gap (§3.1) is exactly the frequency-vs-unique-exposure proxy failure, with a live-system demonstration.
  • Advertiser-cost (CPP) side of the tradeoff has no direct analogue in organic ranking: reference only.
  • Black-box audit methodology (paired campaigns, location-as-race proxy): reference only for our setting — an audit design, not a ranking method.
  • No access to the platform's internal ranking model or eligible-ratio data; eligible ratio is estimated externally: missing link — the exact eligible-ratio computation and VRS's RL module internals remain opaque even after this audit.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — as an audit, the paper traces the observed failure (parity achieved without improved access) to its structural causes: the impression-based metric definition, the ad-count coverage target, and the bid-multiplier-over-untouched-auction implementation, and validates the diagnosis by showing a mechanism-level alternative (budget-splitting) avoids the failure. (The audited system, VRS, is itself a Symptom-type fix by the paper's own analysis — it lowers a variance statistic while reducing actual access.)
