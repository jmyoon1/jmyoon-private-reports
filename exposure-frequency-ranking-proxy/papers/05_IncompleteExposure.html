Fairness of Exposure in Light of Incomplete Exposure Estimation

Heuss et al. · SIGIR 2022 · 2022 · 35 citations

arXiv:2205.12901 · PDF · SP: SP1 · SP4

P0Track A

한 줄 요약

top-k 표시 상황으로 BvN 분해를 일반화하고(O(k^3 n^2)), 확률적 ranking 정책이 곧 impression-frequency 스케줄임을 명시한다. 항목 간 의존성으로 position 기반 exposure 추정이 깨질 때 분해를 재표집해 문제 있는 ranking을 피하는 FELIX를 제안한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

All existing fairness-of-exposure methods compute the expected exposure of every ranking in a stochastic policy's support from a position-only user model (PBM or ERR-based). When inter-item dependencies (e.g., one item is a visible outlier in its displayed context) make user attention deviate from the position model, the exposure distribution of those rankings is unknown — so fairness of the whole policy can neither be guaranteed nor even measured, and click propensities become miscalibrated. Additionally, the convex-optimization approach to fair stochastic ranking existed only for full-length rankings, not the practically dominant top-$k$ setting.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Two-part solution:

  1. Top-$k$ extension: generalize the Birkhoff-von Neumann theorem to $n \times k$ column-stochastic / row-substochastic matrices (any such matrix is a convex sum of top-$k$ permutation matrices), with an $O(k^3 n^2)$ decomposition algorithm via an extra aggregate column plus adjusted Hopcroft–Karp matching. The fairness LP becomes $\mathbf{P} = \arg\max \mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{P} \mathbf{v}$ s.t. $\mathbb{1}_n^T\mathbf{P} = \mathbb{1}_k$, $\mathbf{P}\mathbb{1}_k \le \mathbb{1}_n$, $\mathbf{P}$ fair.
  1. FELIX re-sampling (Algorithm 2): decompose the MRP matrix; move rankings with known exposure distribution directly into the policy; re-aggregate the unknown-exposure rankings ($\widetilde{\mathbf{P}} = \sum_{unknown} \alpha_i P_i$) and re-decompose, shuffling items into new contexts where they may no longer be outliers; after iter rounds, add any remaining unknown rankings back (preserving the exact fairness/utility of the optimized MRP matrix). Key insight: the BvN decomposition is non-unique, so one can search decompositions for one avoiding bad rankings, entirely post-hoc to the constrained optimization.
Key Results주요 결과
  • TREC20 top-10: FELIX$_{iter=20}$ gets $P(u|\pi)$ = 0.20 vs. PL@10 0.47, OMIT@10 0.33, FELIX$_{iter=1}$ 0.39 (all significantly worse), with identical NDCG@10 (0.279) and best EE-L (6.22).
  • TREC20 full-length: $P(u|\pi)$ = 0.24 vs. Vanilla 0.40 and OMIT 0.34, again at identical NDCG@10 (0.302) and EE-L (24.5).
  • FELIX$_{iter=1}$ vs. PL@10 (RQ1): no significant utility/fairness differences — the top-$k$ convex-optimization extension is a viable fair top-$k$ ranker.
  • Sensitivity (simulated): relative $P(u|\pi)$ reduction improves with more candidate items (approaching −80 to −100% at 200 items) and saturates after ~5–20 re-sampling iterations depending on the outlier-feature distribution (power-law hardest: 60.5% base outlier rate per 10-item list).

[SKIM — proof details not verified line-by-line] Contributions: (1) the new task of fair ranking under incomplete exposure estimation; (2) FELIX, a decomposition-level re-sampling method that reduces unknown-exposure rankings at no fairness/utility cost; (3) top-$k$ generalization of the convex-optimization approach with an efficient $O(k^3n^2)$ BvN algorithm. Clarity: algorithms given as explicit pseudocode (Algorithms 1, 2); code released. Reproducible.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: FELIX significantly reduces unknown-exposure rankings without compromising utility or fairness; first approach to fairness of exposure in top-$k$ for the convex-optimization line; FELIX generalizes beyond outliers to any inter-item-dependency use case.
  • Demonstrated: the reduction claim is demonstrated with numbers on TREC19/20 + simulation, for one instantiation of "unknown exposure" (Z-score citation-count outliers).
  • Gap: generality across other inter-item-dependency use cases (clickbait, other cognitive biases, cascade-model users) is claimed but untested — the authors explicitly acknowledge this ("to prove the generalizability of this approach, experiments with other use cases are needed"). Also, whether avoided rankings' exposure was actually mis-estimated is never validated with real user behavior; the outlier predicate is a proxy.
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Rankings with unknown exposure distribution

  • What it computes: given a ranking $\sigma$ and a user model (here the position-based model, PBM), a binary predicate: does the actual exposure users give items in $\sigma$ follow the model's position-only prediction, or do inter-item dependencies (e.g., a visual outlier drawing extra attention) make the true exposure distribution unestimable? In the experiments the predicate is instantiated as "top-$k$ of $\sigma$ contains a Z-score outlier ($|z| > 2.5$) on a visible feature (citation count)".
  • Why it was created: all prior fairness-of-exposure work assumes exposure of every ranking in the policy's support is reliably estimable from position alone; Sarvi et al. (WSDM 2022) showed outliers break this, which means fairness of the policy can no longer be verified.
  • Concrete example: a top-10 list of papers where one paper has 10,000 citations and the rest have ~50 — the outlier attracts attention disproportionate to its rank, so $\epsilon(d_i|\sigma) = 1/\log(1+\text{rank})$ is wrong for that ranking.
  • How it is used in this paper: defines the new task (fair ranking under incomplete exposure estimation) and the quantity to minimize, $P(u\,|\,\pi)$ = probability that a ranking sampled from the policy has unknown exposure distribution.

FELIX (Fairness of Exposure in Light of Incomplete eXposure estimation)

  • What it computes: input = candidate items $\mathcal{D}_q$, merit vector $\mathbf{u}$, position-bias vector $\mathbf{v}$, iteration count; operation = (1) solve the Singh & Joachims linear program for the marginal rank probability (MRP) matrix $\mathbf{P}$, (2) BvN-decompose $\mathbf{P}$ into rankings, (3) keep rankings with known exposure, re-aggregate the unknown-exposure rankings' weighted matrices into a new sub-doubly-stochastic matrix, and re-decompose — repeated iter times; output = a stochastic ranking policy $\pi$ with (much) less probability mass on unknown-exposure rankings, with the MRP matrix (hence fairness and utility) unchanged.
  • Why it was created: exploits non-uniqueness of the BvN decomposition to pick a decomposition avoiding problematic rankings, instead of adding a regularizer to the optimization (OMIT's approach), which fights against the fairness constraints.
  • Concrete example: on TREC20 top-10, 20 iterations cut $P(u|\pi)$ from 0.39 (1 iteration) to 0.20 while NDCG@10 (0.279) and EE-L (6.22 vs 6.23) are unchanged.
  • How it is used in this paper: the core contributed method; evaluated on TREC Fair Ranking 2019/2020 against PL, Vanilla, deterministic, and OMIT baselines.

Generalized Birkhoff-von Neumann decomposition (top-$k$, $n \times k$)

  • What it computes: input = an $n \times k$ matrix with columns summing to 1 and row sums $\le 1$ (a top-$k$ MRP matrix); operation = extend with one aggregate column and run bipartite perfect matching (adjusted Hopcroft–Karp) iteratively; output = a convex combination of top-$k$ permutation matrices, i.e., an executable stochastic top-$k$ policy. Complexity $O(k^3 n^2)$ vs. $O(n^4\sqrt{n})$ for the naive square BvN.
  • Why it was created: the convex-optimization approach to fairness of exposure (Singh & Joachims 2018) was defined only for full-length $n \times n$ rankings; real systems show top-$k$ lists, and square BvN is infeasible for large $n$.
  • Concrete example: for $n=200$ candidates and $k=10$ display slots, decomposition cost drops from order $200^{4.5}$ to order $10^3 \cdot 200^2$.
  • How it is used in this paper: Theorem 4.1 / Lemma 4.2 / Algorithm 1; enables all top-$k$ experiments (FELIX@10, OMIT@10).

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: per-query item scores (any fixed scorer), a position-bias vector, an LP solver for the MRP matrix, bipartite-matching code (networkx + Birkhoff package extended per Algorithm 1), and a computable predicate for "unknown exposure distribution" (here: Z-score outlier detection on a visible feature). Modest compute — $O(k^3n^2)$ per query decomposition.
  • Code: available at https://github.com/MariaHeuss/2022-SIGIR-FOE-Incomplete-Exposure
  • Modifications: for the survey's black-box setting, replace ListNet output with black-box scores directly (no retraining needed since fairness is pure post-processing); replace the outlier predicate with whatever ranking-admissibility constraint the application needs — the FELIX re-sampling loop is agnostic to the predicate.
  • Unknowns: missing link — LP solving per query for individual fairness at large $n$ (constraint count grows quadratically in items); missing link — how policy probabilities map onto discrete impression-frequency budgets in a production serving stack.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Sarvi et al., "Understanding and Mitigating the Effect of Outliers in Fair Ranking" (WSDM 2022) — the predecessor that established outliers break position-based exposure estimation; OMIT is the main baseline FELIX beats.
  • Singh & Joachims, "Fairness of Exposure in Rankings" (KDD 2018) — the LP + BvN pipeline this paper extends to top-$k$; the survey's SP1 seed.
  • Wang & Joachims, "User Fairness, Item Fairness, and Diversity for Rankings in Two-Sided Markets" (ICTIR 2021) — the other exploitation of BvN non-uniqueness (for diversity); useful contrast for the survey's taxonomy of decomposition-level interventions.
  • Diaz et al., "Evaluating Stochastic Rankings with Expected Exposure" (CIKM 2020) — source of the EE-L metric and the expected-exposure evaluation framework used here; relevant to SP4.
  • Singh, Kempe & Joachims, "Fairness in Ranking under Uncertainty" (NeurIPS 2021) — the complementary uncertainty axis (uncertain merit vs. this paper's uncertain exposure); candidate for the survey's open-questions section.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = per-query candidate items with predicted relevance scores from a trainable LTR model (ListNet) that the authors control and normalize; outputs = a stochastic ranking policy (distribution over full or top-$k$ rankings) executed over repeated impressions; conditions = position-based user model, individual merit-based fairness constraint, academic-search TREC Fair Ranking data, post-processing regime (scores fixed at ranking time). Our setting (survey): a black-box recommender whose scores cannot be transformed or re-weighted; the only control is exposure frequency (impression allocation) over repeated servings; goal = control the induced ranking via exposure adjustment alone. Differences:

  • Score immutability: FELIX is genuinely post-processing — it consumes fixed scores as merit $\mathbf{u}$ and only manipulates the distribution over rankings. The score normalization to $[\epsilon,1]$ is a monotone rescaling used as relevance probability. resolved: treat black-box scores as the merit vector; FELIX never modifies them.
  • Exposure-frequency knob: a stochastic policy $\pi$ over rankings IS an impression-frequency schedule (each item's expected exposure = weighted frequency of appearing at each position across servings) — this paper is a direct formalization of the survey's control mechanism. resolved conceptually; the mapping from $\pi$ to per-item impression caps/boosts is the survey's synthesis job.
  • Fairness only in expectation over many servings: applies only to head queries / frequently-served slates. reference only — same limitation any frequency-based proxy inherits; the paper states it explicitly.
  • Unknown-exposure focus: the specific inter-item-dependency problem is orthogonal to the survey's core question, but the "we cannot verify the induced exposure" concern transfers directly to black-box settings where the user model is unvalidated. missing link: how to certify realized exposure when only impression frequency is controlled and user behavior is unmodeled.
  • Domain: academic search with relevance judgments vs. general recommender feeds. reference only.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed. The top-$k$ BvN generalization directly solves its root problem (no executable-policy construction existed for $n \times k$ MRP matrices — the theorem plus algorithm closes it). The incomplete-exposure half, however, is avoidance rather than resolution: the root cause is that no user model captures inter-item dependencies, and FELIX sidesteps rankings where the model fails instead of estimating their true exposure — the paper itself frames this as "an important first step," and its future-work section names the direct fix (extending user models to include inter-item dependencies) as unsolved.
