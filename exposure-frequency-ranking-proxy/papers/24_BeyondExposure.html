Beyond Exposure: Optimizing Ranking Fairness with Non-linear Time-Income Functions

Xuancheng Li et al. · arXiv · 2026

arXiv:2602.03345 · PDF · SP: SP2 · SP4 · SP5

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

exposure가 provider 효용의 손실 있는 proxy라는 관점에서, 비선형 시간-소득 함수로 ranking 공정성 목표를 재정의한다. exposure→효용 변환의 이질성이라는 세 번째 부하 가정을 드러낸 2026년 논문.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Exposure fairness (Biega et al. 2018; Singh & Joachims 2018) allocates position-induced attention proportional to merit, under the proxy assumption that provider utility is directly represented by (or proportional to) exposure. When the exposure-to-income conversion is context-dependent — e.g., time-varying value of a news impression or peak-vs-off-peak restaurant exposure — a ranking that satisfies exposure fairness can still produce unequal realized provider income. Existing exposure-fair methods, even when their fairness signals are re-derived on income, do not model the dynamic coupling between the current ranking decision, the exposure it assigns, and the context-dependent unit income at that timestep.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Formalize Amortized Income Fairness: cumulative income $I(d,n) = \sum_i \Delta E(d,i)\,\mu(d,q,\xi_i)$ must be proportional to relevance, measured by the pairwise quadratic violation metric (Eq. 13, computable with three scalar sums, Eq. 14). Optimize the combined objective $\text{Obj} = \text{Eff} + \gamma\,\text{fair}_{\text{inc}}$ via one-step marginal gains: the effectiveness term is linear in exposure (first-order exact), and the income-fairness term is quadratic in income (second-order Taylor exact). The DIDRF greedy policy scores each candidate per rank with the prefix-aware fairness gain

$$G^{\text{fair}}_j(d) = \delta_j(d)\big[g(d,n) + \sigma R(d) B_{j-1}\big] - \tfrac{\sigma}{2}\big(A - R(d)^2\big)\delta_j(d)^2,$$

scaled by the bounded, hyperparameter-free state calibration $\omega_j$, and selects the argmax per position. [SKIM — appendix derivations (Eqs. 43–49) present but not line-verified.]

Key Results주요 결과
  • Offline MQ2008/Criteo-Ads: DIDRF income unfairness 1.21 vs best baseline MCFair 1.34 (-10%), with best fairness-aware cNDCG@5 176.57 (FARA 173.22).
  • Offline MSLR-WEB30K/YOOCHOOSE-Ecom: unfairness 1.01×10⁻³ vs FairK 1.07 (-6%), cNDCG@5 177.74 vs FARA 150.78 (+18%).
  • Online settings: DIDRF best on both fairness and fairness-aware effectiveness (e.g., MQ2008 unfairness 1.50 vs MCFair 1.69; cNDCG@5 176.73), all †significant at p<0.05.
  • Degenerate case (homogeneous unit income = exposure fairness): DIDRF still best (MQ2008 offline unfairness 11.55 vs MCFair 11.86) — income formulation subsumes the exposure case.
  • Runtime near FairK/MCFair ($O(k_c m)$), ~3–7× faster than MMF and ~2–4× faster than FARA.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: exposure is only an intermediate allocation signal; income fairness is the more realistic provider-side objective; DIDRF improves income fairness while keeping competitive effectiveness.
  • Demonstrated: on 2 LTR datasets × 2 log-calibrated semi-synthetic income environments × offline/online, DIDRF dominates income-adapted baselines with significance tests; ablations support each component (second-order term, $\omega_j$, optimism bonus).
  • Gap: the core premise (real provider income diverges materially from exposure) is motivated by examples and citations, not measured on real systems; $\mu$ is assumed available and error-free — no experiment on estimated/noisy income signals; document-to-income-trajectory assignment is a random hash, so income heterogeneity is decoupled from item semantics. Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Income Fairness (and Amortized Income Fairness)

  • What it computes: for each item pair $(d_x, d_y)$ in a query's candidate set, the squared violation of income–relevance proportionality $\left(I(d_x,n)R(d_y) - I(d_y,n)R(d_x)\right)^2$, averaged over pairs (Eq. 13) — a direct analog of merit-proportional exposure fairness but with cumulative income $I(d,n)$ in place of cumulative exposure $E(d,n)$.
  • Why it was created: exposure fairness assumes provider utility is proportional to exposure; the paper argues this proxy assumption fails when the exposure-to-income conversion varies by context (time, location, market), so equal exposure can yield unequal realized provider utility.
  • Concrete example: a breaking-news article shown at position 1 early earns much more income per unit exposure than the same exposure delivered a day later; a restaurant exposed off-peak converts less than exposed at peak hours.
  • How it is used in this paper: as the fairness objective and evaluation metric replacing exposure unfairness; all baselines are re-derived under it for comparison.

Unit-income function $\mu(d, q, \xi_i)$

  • What it computes: expected income per unit exposure for item $d$ under query $q$ and context $\xi_i$ (instantiated as time); item income at timestep $i$ is $v^i_d = \Delta E(d,i)\,\mu(d,q,\xi_i)$ — position-driven exposure times context-dependent conversion.
  • Why it was created: to cleanly separate what the ranker controls (exposure via position) from what it does not (context-dependent conversion), making the exposure-proxy failure explicit and optimizable.
  • Concrete example: in the Criteo-Ads environment, $\mu$ is a log-calibrated trajectory over 62 twelve-hour bins built from attributed advertising-conversion value; each LTR query-document pair is hash-assigned to one of 675 campaign trajectories.
  • How it is used in this paper: assumed given (external signal) in all derivations; simulated semi-synthetically from Criteo and YOOCHOOSE logs in experiments.

DIDRF (Dynamic-Income-Derivative-aware Ranking Fairness)

  • What it computes: a greedy per-position ranking score $\text{score}_j(d) = p_j R^+(d) + \gamma\,\omega_j\,G^{\text{fair}}_j(d)$, where $G^{\text{fair}}_j(d)$ is an exact second-order Taylor expansion of the quadratic income-unfairness metric (first-order income-deficit signal $g(d,n)$, prefix-interaction term $\sigma R(d)B_{j-1}$, and a self-curvature correction), and $\omega_j \in [1/2, 3/2)$ is a bounded state-calibration factor comparing residual unfairness to local curvature.
  • Why it was created: naively swapping income for exposure in existing fair-ranking methods ignores the dynamic coupling between ranking decisions, exposure, and time-varying unit income; the quadratic structure of the unfairness metric makes the second-order expansion exact and cheap ($O(k_c m)$ time via scalar sums instead of $O(m^2)$ pairs).
  • Concrete example: at rank $j$, placing item $d$ contributes income increment $\delta_j(d) = \mu(d,q,\xi_n)p_j$; the calibration $\omega_j = \tfrac12 + \sqrt{\mathcal U_{j-1}}/(\sqrt{\mathcal U_{j-1}}+\sqrt{\Lambda_j}+\epsilon)$ strengthens the correction when residual unfairness is large.
  • How it is used in this paper: the proposed method, instantiated offline (oracle relevance) and online (shrinkage-estimated relevance plus a UCB-style variance-normalized optimism bonus $R^+$).
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: standard LTR benchmarks (MSLR-WEB30K, MQ2008); Criteo Attribution and YOOCHOOSE logs for income calibration; light compute (simulator runtimes are seconds per run; 10k iterations × 5 seeds).
  • Code: available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/DIDRF (anonymous review repo — persistence uncertain).
  • Modifications: for a real exposure-proxy audit, replace the hash-assigned $\mu$ trajectories with item-linked income estimates; extend context $\xi$ beyond time.
  • Unknowns: missing link: sensitivity of DIDRF to noise/bias in estimated $\mu$; missing link: whether the claimed full 8-setting result grid (footnote 7) holds uniformly.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • FARA: Future-aware Ranking Algorithm for Fairness Optimization (Yang et al., 2023) — strongest planning-based baseline; same Taylor-objective lineage this paper extends.
  • Marginal-Certainty-aware Fair Ranking (MCFair, Yang et al., WSDM 2023) — closest marginal-optimization predecessor; DIDRF's gap claim (coarser granularity, no income coupling) is stated against it.
  • A Taxation Perspective for Fair Re-ranking (TaxRank, Xu et al., SIGIR 2024) — the welfare-economics alternative adapted here as TaxRank-Income; relevant to SP objectives on reformulating the allocation target.
  • Fair ranking: a critical review (Patro et al., FAccT 2022) — the cited source for exposure being an unreliable provider-utility proxy; foundational for this survey's framing.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = LTR candidate sets (MSLR-WEB30K, MQ2008) with 5-level relevance + an externally supplied time-varying unit-income signal $\mu$ calibrated from ad/e-commerce logs; outputs = top-$k_c{=}5$ ranked lists over 10,000 iterations; conditions = known position-bias curve, fixed-query amortized fairness, semi-synthetic income, offline (oracle relevance) and online (click-estimated relevance). Our setting: interrogating exposure frequency as a proxy in ranking systems (SP2/SP4/SP5) — whether cumulative exposure is a valid stand-in for the outcome that actually matters (provider utility / downstream income), and what breaks when the proxy is optimized directly. Differences:

  • Paper treats exposure→income conversion as the proxy failure and replaces the objective; our question is about exposure frequency as a measurement/optimization proxy more broadly: resolved — the paper is direct evidence that exposure-proportional allocation does not guarantee utility-proportional outcomes when conversion is non-linear/context-dependent.
  • $\mu$ assumed known/externally estimated; any real audit of the exposure proxy needs income estimation from logs: missing link.
  • Income heterogeneity is hash-assigned to documents (no semantic coupling); real proxy-gap magnitude on production data is not established: missing link.
  • Fixed-query, single-slate amortized dynamic ranking with known examination probabilities; broader recommendation/retrieval settings: reference only.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the paper attacks the root cause of the exposure-proxy problem at the objective level (fairness redefined on realized income, with the exposure/conversion decomposition made explicit and the optimization derived exactly from the metric's quadratic structure), but it assumes the unit-income signal is externally given and validates only on semi-synthetically simulated income, leaving the hard part of the proxy failure — knowing the conversion function — outside the method.
