Evaluating Stochastic Rankings with Expected Exposure

Diaz et al. · CIKM 2020 · 2020 · 203 citations

arXiv:2004.13157 · PDF · SP: SP1 · SP2 · SP4 · SP5

P2Track C

한 줄 요약

확률적 ranking을 expected exposure로 평가하는 EE-D/EE-R 지표와 동등 기대 노출 원칙을 제안한다. score를 활용하는 Plackett-Luce 무작위화가 score-agnostic 무작위화를 모든 disparity 수준에서 지배함을 보인다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Classic IR/recommendation evaluation assumes one static ranking per query, so (i) items of equal relevance necessarily receive unequal exposure (exposure decays roughly exponentially with rank), and (ii) existing relevance metrics cannot even detect this inequity — a system that equalizes exposure among relevance ties is indistinguishable, under RBP/ERR/MAP, from one that systematically starves some equally-relevant items. Prior fairness-of-exposure work (Singh & Joachims 2018; Biega et al. 2018) proposed exposure constraints but outside the standard IR-evaluation formalism and without user browsing models grounded in existing metrics.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

An evaluation methodology, not a single algorithm:

  1. Define target exposure $\epsilon^*$ as the expected exposure under an oracle sampling uniformly over all optimal rankings (per relevance grade).
  2. Compute system exposure $\epsilon$ for any policy $\pi$ using the user model embedded in an existing metric (RBP: $\epsilon_d = \sum_\sigma \pi(\sigma|q)\gamma^{\sigma_d}$; ERR analog with stopping probabilities $\phi$).
  3. Score the policy by $\ell(\epsilon, \epsilon^*) = \|\epsilon - \epsilon^*\|_2^2$, decomposed into EE-D (disparity) and EE-R (relevance); sweep the randomization parameter to trace a disparity–relevance curve, summarized by EE-AUC.
  4. Optimize directly: loss $\ell_\lambda(\epsilon, \epsilon^*) = \lambda\|\epsilon\|_2^2 - (1-\lambda)\epsilon^\top\epsilon^*$, trained by Gumbel-noise Plackett-Luce sampling + smooth-rank approximation with a straight-through estimator (following Bruch et al. 2020). Group-fairness variant replaces $\|\epsilon\|_2^2$ with group exposure $\|\xi\|_2^2$, $\xi_A = A^\top \epsilon$.

The framework also subsumes existing constructs: demographic parity, disparate treatment, and disparate impact (Singh & Joachims) fall out as choices of the target group-exposure vector, and intent-aware diversity metrics (IA-RBP) reduce to a scaled relevance term of the disparate-impact metric.

Key Results주요 결과
  • On Robust2004 (440 TREC runs, 249 queries), static RBP orders runs within a fixed randomization policy well ($\tau = 0.89$, $p<0.05$) but cannot distinguish PL from RT randomization, while EE-AUC separates them cleanly — the headline evidence that exposure metrics measure something static metrics miss.
  • PL randomization dominates RT across all disparity points on both Robust2004 and MovieLens25M (IMF and BPR recommenders), confirming score-aware randomization beats score-agnostic shuffling.
  • Directly optimizing the expected-exposure loss on MSLR-WEB10k gives EE-AUC 0.238 vs. 0.229 for both pointwise and pairwise LTR (+3.9%, paired t-test $p<0.01$).
  • Demographic-parity task: direct parity optimization reaches AUC 0.178 vs. 0.141 (EE loss), 0.112 (pointwise), 0.108 (pairwise); all pairwise gaps significant ($p<0.01$).
  • EE-R correlates near-perfectly with the expectation of the matching static metric ($r=0.99$ with expected RBP; exact with expected ERR); EE-D correlates with generalized entropy over relevant documents ($r=0.47$–$0.65$).

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Expected exposure ($\epsilon$)

  • What it computes: a ranking policy $\pi$ (distribution over permutations $\sigma \in S_n$) + a user browsing model → for each document $d$, the attention it receives averaged over sampled rankings → an $n \times 1$ vector $\epsilon$. Under an RBP browsing model: $\epsilon_d = \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \pi(\sigma|q)\, \gamma^{\sigma_d}$, where $\gamma$ is the browsing patience parameter and $\sigma_d$ is $d$'s (base-0) rank.
  • Why it was created: classic IR metrics evaluate one static ranking per query and ignore differences in exposure among items of equal relevance — a static ranking always concentrates exposure at the top even among relevance ties.
  • Concrete example: with $\gamma = 0.5$ and $m$ relevant documents, the oracle target exposure of each relevant document is $\epsilon^*_d = \frac{1-\gamma^m}{m(1-\gamma)}$; for $m=2$ that is $0.75$ each, versus $1.0$ / $0.5$ under any static ranking.
  • How it is used in this paper: the core measured quantity throughout; compared against target exposure $\epsilon^*$ via squared error (Eq. 1).

Principle of equal expected exposure / target exposure ($\epsilon^*$)

  • What it computes: given a fixed information need, the exposure vector produced by an oracle that samples uniformly among all $m!(n-m)!$ optimal rankings — every item of the same relevance grade gets identical expected exposure.
  • Why it was created: gives a normative reference point that no deterministic ranker can satisfy, making exposure inequity among equally relevant items measurable.
  • Concrete example: for graded judgments with RBP, a grade-$g$ document's target is $\epsilon^*_d = \frac{\gamma^{m_{>g}} - \gamma^{m_{>g-1}}}{m_g(1-\gamma)}$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the comparison target in the metric and the training target in the optimization loss.

EE-D / EE-R / EE-AUC decomposition

  • What it computes: expanding $\ell(\epsilon, \epsilon^*) = \|\epsilon - \epsilon^*\|_2^2 = \underbrace{\|\epsilon\|_2^2}_{\text{EE-D}} - \underbrace{2\epsilon^\top \epsilon^*}_{\text{EE-R}} + \|\epsilon^*\|_2^2$. EE-D (disparity) measures how unequally exposure is distributed; EE-R (relevance) measures how much exposure lands on relevant items; EE-AUC is the area under the disparity–relevance tradeoff curve swept by a randomization parameter.
  • Why it was created: a single relevance metric cannot distinguish a system that equalizes exposure from one that does not; the decomposition makes the disparity–relevance tradeoff explicit and summarizable.
  • Concrete example: a static optimal ranking has optimal EE-R but EE-D as bad as a static ranking with all relevant items at the bottom (disparity depends only on exposure concentration, not relevance).
  • How it is used in this paper: the evaluation metric in §5 (metric analysis) and the training objective in §6 ($\ell_\lambda = \lambda\|\epsilon\|_2^2 - (1-\lambda)\epsilon^\top\epsilon^*$).

Stochastic ranking policy

  • What it computes: query → distribution over permutations, $\sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \pi(\sigma|q) = 1$; deterministic rankers are the special case with all mass on one permutation. Two post-processing instantiations: Plackett-Luce sampling with temperature $\alpha$ over retrieval scores, and rank-transposition random walks with restart probability $\theta$.
  • Why it was created: only a distribution over rankings can equalize expected exposure among relevance ties.
  • Concrete example: PL with $\alpha = 0$ is a uniform shuffle (minimal EE-D, minimal EE-R); $\alpha \to \infty$ recovers the static ranking.
  • How it is used in this paper: the object being evaluated; PL and RT randomizations of TREC Robust2004 runs and MovieLens25M recommenders.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = query + corpus with (binary or graded) editorial relevance judgments; outputs = distribution over rankings; conditions = offline batch evaluation (TREC Robust2004, MovieLens25M implicit feedback, MSLR-WEB10k), exposure computed from a known parametric browsing model ($\gamma = 0.5$, depth 20, 50 sampled rankings), per-query (micro) rather than amortized-across-needs measurement. Our setting: exposure frequency observed in logged interactions used as a proxy signal for ranking quality/relevance — exposure is an empirical log quantity, and relevance labels are typically absent or replaced by engagement proxies. Differences:

  • Exposure here is a model-derived expectation requiring known $\pi$ and a browsing model; our setting observes realized exposure frequencies from logs without access to the policy's full distribution: missing link
  • Requires relevance judgments to define target exposure $\epsilon^*$; our proxy setting usually lacks graded labels: missing link
  • Paper measures exposure per information need (explicitly rejecting amortization across queries, footnote 1); log-based frequency proxies typically aggregate across queries/sessions — the paper itself warns this macro/micro distinction changes what the metric means: reference only [SKIM - verify which SPs need per-query vs. amortized]
  • Stochastic-policy assumption (system can randomize) vs. evaluation of a fixed production ranker: resolved — deterministic policies are a special case of the formalism, so the metric still applies (EE-D is just maximal)
  • The relevance-exposure decoupling (EE-R vs. EE-D) directly formalizes why raw exposure frequency is a biased proxy for relevance — exposure concentrates by position regardless of relevance ties: resolved — this is the conceptual import for the survey
