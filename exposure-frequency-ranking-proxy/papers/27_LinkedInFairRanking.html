Fairness-Aware Ranking in Search & Recommendation Systems with Application to LinkedIn Talent Search

Sahin Cem Geyik et al. · KDD 2019 · 2019 · 447 citations

arXiv:1905.01989 · PDF · SP: SP2

P0Track C

한 줄 요약

LinkedIn Talent Search에 실배포된 공정성 인지 re-ranking(DetConstSort). 접두사 비율 제약이라는 merit-blind 목표를 사용해 score 오염과 무관하게 제약 만족이 유지되는 production 사례다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Ranked lists produced by ML models trained on biased data systematically under-expose already-disadvantaged groups in the top results (hiring, lending, admissions). Existing fair-ranking methods were impractical for web-scale deployment: FA*IR (Zehlike et al. 2017) handles only a single binary protected attribute; Celis et al. (2018) is theoretical; Singh & Joachims (2018) requires solving a linear program with N² variables, infeasible under production latency; Asudeh et al. assumes a fixed candidate set and linear models. No fairness-aware ranking framework had been deployed at scale.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

A post-processing framework over a fixed, unmodified ML scorer:

  1. Measure: Skew@k / MinSkew / MaxSkew (per-prefix log-ratio vs. a desired categorical distribution over protected attribute values) and NDKL (position-discounted KL over all prefixes).
  2. Re-rank: four deterministic algorithms that take per-attribute-value score-sorted lists plus a desired distribution p, and enforce per-prefix representation bounds ⌊p_{a_i}·k⌋ ≤ count_k(a_i) ≤ ⌈p_{a_i}·k⌉ for all k — DetGreedy, DetCons, DetRelaxed (greedy, Alg. 1–2) and DetConstSort (interval-constrained sorting, Alg. 3, provably feasible; Theorem 3.3). Theorem 3.2: the greedy three are feasible iff |A| ≤ 3; DetGreedy provably not for |A| ≥ 4.
  3. Configure: choosing p = qualified-set distribution ⇒ equal opportunity; p = full-population distribution ⇒ demographic parity.
  4. Deploy: two-tier production architecture (Fig. 5) — desired gender distribution computed over the qualified set inside the Galene search stack, representative re-ranking applied after both first- and second-level scorers; DetGreedy in production.
Key Results주요 결과
  • Online A/B (LinkedIn Recruiter, 3 weeks, 2018): queries with representative results 33% → 95% (~3x); ranking feasible per Def. 3.1 in >95% of searches.
  • Average MinSkew@100: −0.259 (vanilla) → −0.011 (fairness-aware), p < 1e-16.
  • Business metrics unchanged: InMails sent and InMails accepted within ±1%, p > 0.5 — fairness gain at no measured utility cost.
  • Simulation (1M tasks per |A|): all four algorithms feasible for |A| ≤ 3; DetGreedy's InfeasibleIndex grows with |A| ≥ 4; DetCons/DetRelaxed/DetConstSort empirically feasible for all |A| ≤ 10 (only DetConstSort proven).
  • Utility: DetGreedy has highest NDCG@100 among fair algorithms (~0.94–0.985 vs. vanilla 1.0); fairness metrics (MinSkew, NDKL) nearly indistinguishable among DetCons/DetRelaxed/DetConstSort.
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: new method + system (production deployment with A/B evidence) Context: builds on ranking-fairness measures of Yang & Stoyanovich 2017 (NDKL is their measure adapted) and fairness notions of Dwork et al. 2012 / Hardt et al. 2016; positions against FA*IR (Zehlike et al. 2017), Celis et al. 2018, Singh & Joachims 2018 (fairness of exposure), Biega et al. 2018 (equity of attention). The canonical production post-hoc re-ranking-over-a-fixed-scorer reference that later exposure-fairness lines extend or argue against. Correctness: feasibility theorems proven (appendix A.2–A.3, incl. an explicit counterexample for DetGreedy at |A|=4); simulations use 1M synthetic ranking tasks per |A| ∈ {2..10}; A/B test over three weeks, hundreds of thousands of users, p-values reported (MinSkew improvement p < 1e-16; business metrics p > 0.5). Simulation scores are uniform-random, so utility results may not transfer to real score distributions (acknowledged). Contributions: (1) representation-based bias measures for ranked lists (Skew@k family, NDKL); (2) four deterministic re-ranking algorithms with feasibility analysis; (3) first web-scale deployed fair-ranking system, with A/B evidence that fairness improved ~3x with no business-metric change. Clarity: pseudocode for all algorithms, proofs in appendix, architecture diagram — reproducible from the paper alone.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: framework quantifies and mitigates bias; achieves equal opportunity or demographic parity via choice of p; first large-scale deployed fairness framework in hiring; no business-metric impact.
  • Demonstrated: representation (proportion) constraints per prefix are enforced, measured, and improved online for a 3-value gender attribute with DetGreedy; simulations cover |A| up to 10 with synthetic uniform scores.
  • Gap: the equal-opportunity mapping is itself flagged as "rough" — it equates "qualified" with "matches the search criteria," which is a proxy, not ground-truth qualification. Feasibility of DetCons/DetRelaxed for |A| ≥ 4 is conjectured from simulation, not proven. No real-dataset offline evaluation (synthetic scores only, position bias cited as the reason). Protected-attribute values are assumed known (inferred gender in production) — reliability of that inference is deferred to future work. Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Skew@k

  • What it computes: ranked list τ_r + attribute value a_i + desired proportion p_{q,r,a_i} → log_e(proportion of a_i in top-k / desired proportion of a_i) → a signed scalar; negative = under-representation of a_i in top k, positive = over-representation.
  • Why it was created: prior fairness measures did not give an interpretable, per-attribute-value, per-cutoff measure of deviation from a target distribution; log makes over/under-representation symmetric around 0.
  • Concrete example: desired male proportion 0.4 (32K of 80K qualified candidates); top 100 has 20 males → Skew_male@100 = log_e(0.20/0.40) ≈ −0.3, i.e. males shown at 50% of desired rate.
  • How it is used in this paper: primary bias metric in simulations and in the A/B test (MinSkew@100 went from −0.259 to −0.011 after deployment).

MinSkew@k / MaxSkew@k

  • What it computes: min (resp. max) of Skew@k over all attribute values → the worst representation disadvantage (resp. largest unfair advantage) in one number. MinSkew ≤ 0 and MaxSkew ≥ 0 always, since both distributions sum to 1.
  • Why it was created: Skew@k is per-attribute-value; these give a combined single-number view.
  • Concrete example: with gender values {male, female}, if Skew_male@100 = −0.3 and Skew_female@100 = +0.2, MinSkew@100 = −0.3.
  • How it is used in this paper: headline online metric (average MinSkew@100 over all search requests).

NDKL (normalized discounted cumulative KL-divergence)

  • What it computes: ranked list → for each prefix length i, KL-divergence between the top-i attribute distribution and the desired distribution → position-discounted (1/log2(i+1)) weighted sum, normalized → a scalar ≥ 0; 0 iff every prefix matches the desired distribution.
  • Why it was created: Skew@k depends on a single k and single attribute value; NDKL gives one whole-list, all-attribute-values measure (adapted from Yang & Stoyanovich 2017).
  • Concrete example: for p_male = p_female = 0.5, a list that under-represents females in early prefixes has NDKL > 0; the paper notes NDKL cannot distinguish which group is disadvantaged (same value if roles flip).
  • How it is used in this paper: simulation metric (Fig. 3); vanilla ranking has NDKL 0.21–0.34 vs. < 0.06 for all fairness-aware algorithms.

InfeasibleIndex / InfeasibleCount

  • What it computes: for a ranked list, the number of prefix lengths k (resp. (attribute value, k) pairs) at which the minimum-representation condition count_k(a_i) ≥ ⌊p_{a_i}·k⌋ is violated → integer counts of feasibility violations.
  • Why it was created: to quantify how often an algorithm fails the per-prefix minimum representation guarantee (Definition 3.1's infeasibility).
  • Concrete example: with 4 attribute values and proportions (0.4, 0.4, 0.1, 0.1), DetGreedy picks a4, a3 first; at k=3 both a1 and a2 hit floor requirements simultaneously → 1 infeasible index.
  • How it is used in this paper: simulation metric showing DetGreedy becomes infeasible for |A| ≥ 4 while DetCons/DetRelaxed/DetConstSort stay at 0.

DetGreedy / DetCons / DetRelaxed (greedy re-ranking algorithms)

  • What it computes: per-attribute-value score-sorted candidate lists + desired categorical distribution p + k_max → an ordered top-k_max list. At each position: if any attribute value is about to violate its floor ⌊p_{a_i}·k⌋, pick the highest-scoring such value; otherwise DetGreedy picks the highest-scoring value below its ceiling ⌈p_{a_i}·k⌉, while DetCons/DetRelaxed pick the value whose floor constraint will bind soonest (minimizing ⌈k·p_{a_i}⌉/p_{a_i}, DetRelaxed breaking ties by score).
  • Why it was created: prior fair-ranking algorithms (FA*IR: binary attribute only; Celis et al.: theoretical; Singh & Joachims: N² linear program) were impractical at web scale or limited to binary attributes.
  • Concrete example: proportions (0.55, 0.3, 0.15), top-9 has counts (5, 3, 1); at k=10 no floor binds, but a1's floor binds at k=11 (⌊11·0.55⌋=6) vs. a3's at k=14, so DetCons picks a1.
  • How it is used in this paper: DetGreedy is the deployed production algorithm (chosen for simplicity + highest NDCG; feasible for |A| ≤ 3, gender has 3 values).

DetConstSort (interval-constrained sorting re-ranker)

  • What it computes: same inputs → advances a counter k; whenever some attribute value's floor count increases, inserts that value's next candidate at the next empty slot, then swaps it toward earlier positions while the left neighbor has lower score and no maximum-index (feasibility) constraint is violated → a provably feasible ranked list (Theorem 3.3).
  • Why it was created: the three greedy variants lack a general feasibility proof (DetGreedy provably infeasible for |A| ≥ 4); DetConstSort is the provably feasible alternative for arbitrary |A|.
  • Concrete example: solves interval-constrained sorting: maximize score order subject to "candidate cannot appear later than its max index."
  • How it is used in this paper: the theoretical-guarantee option; slightly better NDCG than DetCons/DetRelaxed in simulation but not deployed.

Desired-distribution mapping to fairness notions

  • What it computes: a choice of target distribution p → a fairness criterion: p = attribute distribution over qualified candidates ⇒ equal opportunity (Hardt et al. 2016); p = distribution over all candidates ⇒ demographic parity.
  • Why it was created: decouples the re-ranking machinery from the normative choice of fairness notion; the algorithm is agnostic to how p is chosen.
  • Concrete example: LinkedIn deployment sets p = gender distribution of candidates matching the search criteria (qualified set) → equality of opportunity.
  • How it is used in this paper: §3.3; determines the production configuration.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: per-group score-sorted candidate lists, a target categorical distribution per query, and the protected-attribute value of each candidate; algorithms are O(k) greedy loops or a constrained sort — trivial compute, no training.
  • Code: not released (production system; simulation code not published). Algorithms 1–3 are fully specified in pseudocode; reimplementation is straightforward.
  • Modifications: for exposure-based (position-weighted) targets rather than count-based prefixes, the floor/ceiling constraints would need reformulation — the greedy structure does not directly carry over. For |A| ≥ 4 with guarantees, only DetConstSort applies.
  • Unknowns: missing link: how inferred gender is obtained and its error rate (production detail not disclosed); missing link: behavior when some group has too few candidates (fallback mechanism mentioned but not specified).
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Singh & Joachims, "Fairness of Exposure in Rankings" (KDD 2018) — the contemporaneous exposure-based (position-weighted, probabilistic) alternative this paper rejects on latency grounds; the conceptual pivot for SP2's exposure framing.
  • Biega, Gummadi & Weikum, "Equity of Attention" (SIGIR 2018) — amortized individual fairness across rankings, the cross-query counterpart to this paper's per-query group constraints.
  • Zehlike et al., "FA*IR" (CIKM 2017) — the binary-attribute predecessor; this paper generalizes to arbitrary categorical distributions.
  • Celis, Straszak & Vishnoi, "Ranking with Fairness Constraints" (ICALP 2018) — the theoretical treatment of the same floor/ceiling constraint family.
  • Geyik et al., SIGIR 2018 (LinkedIn talent search ML models) — describes the underlying scorer this framework re-ranks over.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = per-attribute-value score-sorted candidate lists from a fixed two-level ML scorer + a desired categorical distribution over disjoint protected-attribute values; outputs = re-ranked top-k list satisfying per-prefix floor/ceiling representation counts; conditions = web-scale talent search, low-cardinality attribute (gender, 3 values), latency-constrained production, attribute values and target distribution given as input. Our setting (SP2 — exposure/frequency in rankings as a proxy): analysis of how appearance frequency / exposure in ranked lists functions as the operative quantity that ranking interventions control, across the 2024–2026 lines that extend post-hoc re-ranking over a fixed scorer. Differences:

  • Fairness quantity: this paper controls proportional representation in top-k prefixes (counts), whereas the exposure line (Singh & Joachims 2018, Biega et al. 2018) controls position-weighted exposure; the paper's NDKL discounting is a partial bridge but its constraints are count-based: reference only — the paper explicitly contrasts itself with the exposure formulation (§5).
  • Scorer treatment: fixed scorer + post-hoc re-rank (our SP2's canonical baseline pattern): resolved — this is exactly the production pattern SP2's 2024–2026 papers extend.
  • Attribute cardinality: paper is strongest for |A| ≤ 3 (production = gender); higher-cardinality or continuous/intersectional attributes only simulated: missing link for settings needing many groups with guarantees (only DetConstSort proven).
  • Per-query group fairness vs. amortized/individual fairness across queries: paper enforces per-query constraints only: reference only (amortized exposure is Biega et al.'s line).
  • Ground-truth outcome data: paper never uses true relevance labels; "qualified" = query-match: missing link if SP2 needs qualification-calibrated exposure targets.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Symptom — the biased ML scorer is left untouched and the ranked output distribution is corrected post hoc; the paper itself frames post-processing as a "fail-safe" that is complementary to (not a substitute for) removing bias from data or training, so the root cause (biased scores driving exposure) persists behind the corrected surface distribution.
