A Taxation Perspective for Fair Re-ranking

Chen Xu et al. · SIGIR 2024 · 2024 · 23 citations

arXiv:2404.17826 · PDF · SP: SP3

P0Track A

한 줄 요약

기존 공정 re-ranking들을 item 수준 '세금'으로 통일하고(비Lipschitz 증명), 효용 차이에 대한 누진 과세 + item 수준 exposure 벡터 e* + optimal transport 배정의 2단계 구조(TaxRank)를 제안한다. Lipschitz 연속 knob과 Price-of-Taxation 상한이라는, 포함(top-K) 수준 제어의 첫 예측 가능 비용 보장을 제공한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Existing fair re-ranking methods (regularization-based like Do & Usunier's generalized Gini, constraint-based like FairRec/TFROM, and max-min like P-MMF) redistribute ranking exposure but, when unified as an item-level additive "tax" on scores, fail two properties a controllable fairness knob needs: (1) continuity — small changes in the fairness trade-off parameter cause discontinuous jumps in accuracy and fairness (from binary selection + non-differentiable fairness functions), and (2) controllability over accuracy loss — no bound predicts how much accuracy a given fairness level costs (the induced knapsack is non-linear and non-continuous). This makes the fairness/accuracy trade-off unstable and unpredictable to operate.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Reframe fair re-ranking as a taxation/redistribution process, then propose Tax-rank, which taxes based on the utility difference between item pairs rather than per-item, yielding the isoelastic ($\alpha$-fair) welfare objective:

$$\bm{x}^*(t) = \arg\max_{\bm{x}\in\mathcal{X}_s} \begin{cases} \sum_i \gamma_i \bm{v}_i^{1-t}/(1-t) & t\ge 0, t\neq 1 \\ \sum_i \gamma_i \log(\bm{v}_i) & t=1 \end{cases}, \quad \bm{v}_i=\sum_{u}w_{u,i}\bm{x}_{u,i}$$

Tax rate $t$ spans accuracy-first ($t=0$) → Nash/proportional ($t=1$) → max-min ($t\to\infty$). The continuous relaxation gives Lipschitz continuity, and Theorem 3 bounds the price of taxation $\text{POT}\le 1-O(|\mathcal{U}|^{-t/(1+t)})$. Optimization: construct an easy lower-bound problem (Eq 7), solve for optimal exposure $\bm{e}^*$, then use entropic-regularized OT + Sinkhorn to project onto feasible top-$K$ rankings (Algorithm 1).

Key Results주요 결과
  • Tax-rank Pareto-dominates all baselines (FairRec, FairRec+, P-MMF, Welf) on both Yelp (recommendation) and iPinYou (advertising), under both CTR-based and exposure-based settings, at $K=5$ and $K=10$ (Figs 3–4): higher accuracy for any given Gini and vice versa.
  • POT of Tax-rank is smooth and notably lower than P-MMF's across tax rates 0–1 (Fig 7a), matching the Thm 3 bound $1-O(|\mathcal{U}|^{-t/(1+t)})$.
  • Continuity: Lorenz curves (Fig 5) show near-uniform fairness increase as $t$ grows 10-fold, vs erratic/minimal shifts for P-MMF, Welf, FairRec.
  • Efficiency: inference stays under ~10s across user sizes (variable reduction $|\mathcal{U}||\mathcal{I}|\to|\mathcal{I}|$), while baselines grow linearly/exponentially (Fig 7b).

Context: builds on P-MMF Xu et al. 2023, FairRec [Patro 2020], FairRec+ [Biswas 2021], Welf/Lorenz-dominance [Do 2021], generalized-Gini [Do & Usunier 2022]; uses price-of-fairness [Bertsimas 2011], Nash bargaining, $\alpha$-fairness, and Sinkhorn OT [Cuturi/Peyré]. Directly relevant to SP3 (score-read-only re-ranking over fixed utilities). Correctness: three theorems with appendix proofs (Thm 1 unification via Lagrangian; Thm 2 non-Lipschitz; Thm 3 POT bound via Hölder inequality). Empirics on two public datasets (Yelp, iPinYou), Pareto frontiers + Lorenz curves + inference-time curves; no CIs/error bars reported (typical for this venue). Assumptions appear valid; POT bound derivation is clean. Contributions: (1) unifying taxation view showing prior methods = item-level tax; (2) Tax-rank utility-difference objective with proven continuity + POT bound; (3) Sinkhorn/OT algorithm scaling to $|\mathcal{I}|$ variables, Pareto-dominant on both datasets. Clarity: method reproducible — code released at github.com/XuChen0427/Tax-rank, algorithm boxed, cvxpy used for Eq 7. Some notation slips (FairTax vs Tax-rank naming in Sec 6.2).

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Item-level tax policy (unifying view of prior fair re-ranking)

  • What it computes: input = original ranking scores $\gamma_i w_{u,i}$ + a fairness function $r(\bm{v})$ and tax rate $\lambda$ → operation = reformulate regularization-based ($W_1$) and constraint-based ($W_2$) fair re-ranking as adding a per-item additive taxation term $\bm{\mu}_i$ to each item's score, then run top-$K$ selection (a knapsack) → output = the same re-ranking those prior methods produce. Theorem 1: $s_{u,i} = \gamma_i w_{u,i} + \bm{\mu}_i$.
  • Why it was created: to give a single economic lens (taxation/redistribution) under which most prior fair re-ranking methods [FairRec, P-MMF, CPFair, etc.] can be compared, exposing shared weaknesses.
  • Concrete example: a "rich" high-exposure item gets a negative tax added to its score, dropping it below a "poor" item so ranking slots redistribute — like federal tax redirecting subsidy to the poor (Fig 1a uses 2016 US federal tax quintiles).
  • How it is used in this paper: Section 4.1 establishes that prior methods = item-level tax; Section 4.2 then proves this family fails continuity (Thm 2) and controllability (POT unbounded), motivating Tax-rank.

Continuity (Lipschitz w.r.t. tax rate)

  • What it computes: input = tax rate $\lambda$ (or $t$) → property = whether a small change in tax rate causes only a small change in accuracy/fairness output. Item-level policies are proven non-Lipschitz (Thm 2) because the binary $\bm{x}_{u,i}$ and non-differentiable $r(\bm{v})$ create breakpoints.
  • Why it was created: to formalize why prior methods show erratic "jumps" in the Gini-vs-accuracy trade-off as the fairness knob is tuned (Fig 1b left).
  • Concrete example: a tiny $\delta\lambda$ swaps item $i_r$ out for $i_k$ in a user's list, producing a discrete accuracy jump.
  • How it is used in this paper: a controllability criterion Tax-rank is designed to satisfy; verified empirically via Lorenz curves (Fig 5).

Price of Taxation (POT)

  • What it computes: input = accuracy at tax rate 0 vs at rate $t$ → operation = relative accuracy loss $\text{POT}(\lambda) = (\text{Acc}(0)-\text{Acc}(\lambda))/\text{Acc}(0)$ → output = fraction of accuracy sacrificed for fairness. Adapted from "price of fairness" [Bertsimas].
  • Why it was created: to make the accuracy cost of a given fairness level predictable; prior item-level policies give no bound (non-linear non-continuous knapsack), so accuracy loss is uncertain.
  • Concrete example: Tax-rank bounds it as $\text{POT} \le 1 - O(|\mathcal{U}|^{-t/(1+t)})$ (Thm 3).
  • How it is used in this paper: the "controllability over accuracy loss" guarantee that distinguishes Tax-rank; validated in Fig 7a (Tax-rank POT smooth and lower than P-MMF).

Tax-rank objective (utility-difference taxation / $\alpha$-fair welfare)

  • What it computes: input = item utilities $\bm{v}_i$, weights $\gamma_i$, tax rate $t$ → operation = maximize the isoelastic welfare $\sum_i \gamma_i \bm{v}_i^{1-t}/(1-t)$ (or $\sum_i \gamma_i \log \bm{v}_i$ at $t=1$), taxing the utility gap between two items with value $\frac{\gamma_j}{\gamma_i}(\frac{v_i}{v_j})^t$ → output = a continuous re-ranking probability. $t=0$ → accuracy-first; $t=1$ → Nash/proportional; $t\to\infty$ → max-min equal utility.
  • Why it was created: to be a "progressive tax" (vs prior "flat tax") that satisfies both continuity and a POT bound.
  • Concrete example: two items, $K=1$: at $t=1$ utilities become proportional to weights $v_1{:}v_2 = 2{:}5$; at $t=\infty$ they equalize $v_1=v_2$ (Fig 2).
  • How it is used in this paper: the core proposed method, optimized via the Sinkhorn algorithm (see below).

Sinkhorn / optimal-transport projection for ranking constraint

  • What it computes: input = optimal averaged exposure $\bm{e}^*$ from the lower-bound problem (Eq 7), cost matrix $C_{u,i}=\gamma_i w_{u,i}$ → operation = entropic-regularized OT (Eq 9) solved by Sinkhorn iterations $\bm{m}\leftarrow K\bm{1}\oslash B\bm{n}$, $\bm{n}\leftarrow \bm{e}^*\oslash B\bm{m}$ → output = a feasible top-$K$ recommendation probability matrix $\tilde{\bm{x}}$ satisfying $\sum_i x_{u,i}=K$.
  • Why it was created: the raw Tax-rank objective is NP-hard (non-linear integral program); the lower-bound reduces variables from $|\mathcal{U}||\mathcal{I}|$ to $|\mathcal{I}|$ and OT maps the exposure allocation back to per-user rankings efficiently.
  • Concrete example: reducing variable size to $|\mathcal{I}|$ gives near-constant inference time under 10s across user sizes (Fig 7b).
  • How it is used in this paper: Algorithm 1 — the practical optimizer enabling scalability.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: user-item score matrix (BPR/CTR or exposure), item weights/bids; cvxpy for the lower-bound program (Eq 7); a Sinkhorn/OT routine; Yelp and iPinYou public datasets to reproduce.
  • Code: available at https://github.com/XuChen0427/Tax-rank
  • Modifications: for a general exposure-frequency proxy study, swap the fairness metric (Gini) for the survey's chosen exposure-inequality measure; the tax-rate $t$ maps cleanly onto "how strongly to equalize exposure frequency."
  • Unknowns: missing link — behavior under repeated rounds / feedback loops (deferred to LTP-MMF); tightness of the POT constant $\lambda$ in Thm 3; sampling variance of realized exposure vs target $\bm{e}^*$.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = fixed user-item utility scores $w_{u,i}$ (CTR from BPR, or exposure=1) and item weights $\gamma_i$; outputs = top-$K$ re-ranking that trades accuracy (eCN/eCPM) against item fairness (Gini); conditions = static single-shot re-ranking, item-side exposure fairness, offline optimization over the full user×item matrix. Our setting (SP3, exposure-frequency-ranking-proxy survey): score-read-only re-ranking that redistributes ranking slots given fixed scores, examining exposure/frequency as the proxy quantity being equalized. Differences:

  • Utility = exposure or CTR, treated as given and fixed: resolved / this is exactly SP3's "score-read-only" premise — Tax-rank never re-scores, only redistributes slots.
  • Fairness target is item/provider-side exposure equity (Gini over $\gamma_i v_i$): resolved / matches the "exposure-frequency as proxy" framing; the tax rate $t$ directly parameterizes how aggressively exposure is equalized.
  • Single-shot static re-ranking, no feedback loop: reference only / our survey may care about repeated-exposure/frequency dynamics over time (author's own LTP-MMF [50] handles loops; Tax-rank does not).
  • Continuous relaxation + multinomial sampling to build lists: missing link / how the sampled realized exposure distribution relates to the intended $\bm{e}^*$ under finite users is not bounded here.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — Tax-rank identifies the structural root cause of prior methods' instability (binary per-item additive taxation + non-differentiable fairness function → non-Lipschitz, unbounded accuracy loss) and replaces it with a continuous pairwise-difference welfare objective that provably restores continuity and a POT bound, rather than merely tuning a better trade-off coefficient on the old formulation.
