Know-Evolve: Deep Temporal Reasoning for Dynamic Knowledge Graphs

Trivedi et al. · ICML 2017 · 2017

arXiv:1705.05742 · PDF · SP: SP8

P0Track A

Problem
해결하려는 문제

Traditional knowledge-graph reasoning treats KGs as static snapshots and learns static entity/relation embeddings. Such methods (RESCAL, NTN, TransE/TransR) cannot exploit the rich temporal dynamics in event-based data where facts occur, recur, and evolve over time, and they cannot predict when a fact will occur. No prior multi-relational method models the continuous-time dynamics of dynamically evolving KGs.

정적 개체·관계 임베딩은 시간에 따라 변하는 사실의 유효성을 표현하지 못한다.

Solution / Contribution
해결책 / 기여

Know-Evolve models the occurrence of each fact (edge) as a relationship-modulated multivariate temporal point process. The conditional intensity (Eq. 3) is

$$\lambda_r^{e_s,e_o}(t\,|\,\bar{t}) = f\big(g_r^{e_s,e_o}(\bar t)\big)\,(t-\bar t)$$

where the bilinear score $g_r^{e_s,e_o}(t)=v_{e_s}(t^-)^T R_r v_{e_o}(t^-)$ (Eq. 4) depends on dynamically evolving entity embeddings updated by deep recurrent functions (Eq. 5–6) after every event. Training maximizes the point-process likelihood over the globally ordered event stream.

연속 시간 point process의 강도를 recurrent 임베딩 위 bilinear 점수로 변조하는 Know-Evolve를 제안하여 링크 순위화와 사건 발생 시각 예측을 동시에 수행하며 unseen 개체(OWA)를 지원한다.

Key Results
주요 결과
  • Link prediction (MAR, HITS@10) on ICEWS and GDELT (raw + filtered): Know-Evolve "consistently and significantly" outperforms all static baselines (RESCAL, NTN, ER-MLP, TransE, TransR) across all 12 sliding test windows, without degradation over time; NTN is second best.
  • Lowest STD in MAR across samples → most stable predictor over time.
  • Time prediction (MAE in hours): outperforms point-process baselines MHP and RTPP (simplified RMTPP); no prior multi-relational method can do this task at all.
  • Datasets: GDELT (Apr 2015–Mar 2016, 15-min granularity, 20 relations, ~32M events); ICEWS (2014, 24-hr granularity, 260 relations).
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Temporal Knowledge Graph (TKG)

  • What it computes: represents multi-relational data as a set of quadruplets $(e_s, r, e_o, t)$ — subject entity, relation, object entity, timestamp — instead of static triplets. An edge may recur at different time points.
  • Why it was created: static KGs treat data as a fixed snapshot and cannot exploit the temporal dynamics of event-based interaction data (GDELT, ICEWS).
  • Concrete example: quadruplet "(US, provide-material-support, country-X, 2015-04-01 12:15)".
  • How it is used in this paper: the input data model; the complete TKG is an $n_e \times n_e \times n_r \times T$ tensor over which reasoning is performed.

Relationship-modulated multivariate temporal point process

  • What it computes: models the occurrence of each fact as a point process with conditional intensity $\lambda_r^{e_s,e_o}(t\,|\,\bar{t}) = f(g_r^{e_s,e_o}(\bar{t}))\,(t-\bar{t})$, where $f=\exp(\cdot)$ ensures positivity, $\bar{t}$ is the most recent time either entity was involved in an event, and $(t-\bar t)$ is a Rayleigh-type term.
  • Why it was created: discrete epoch-based temporal models fail to capture intricate, non-periodic temporal dependencies between facts; a point process treats time as a continuous random variable.
  • Concrete example: intensity for "(Bob, visit, Burger King)" rises then decays according to a Rayleigh process, enabling prediction of when the event recurs.
  • How it is used in this paper: core generative model; also enables the novel time-prediction task (MAE in hours).

Bilinear relational score

  • What it computes: $g_r^{e_s,e_o}(t) = v_{e_s}(t^-)^T R_r\, v_{e_o}(t^-)$ — a per-relation weight matrix $R_r \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$ composing the most recent subject/object embeddings into a compatibility score that modulates the point-process intensity.
  • Why it was created: to inject multi-relational structural compatibility into the temporal intensity function.
  • Concrete example: $R_r$ for relation "conflict" scores two tense-country embeddings highly.
  • How it is used in this paper: first term of the intensity function (Eq. 3–4).

Dynamically evolving (deep recurrent) entity embeddings

  • What it computes: RNN-style update functions (Eq. 5–6) that update an entity's embedding $v_e(t)$ after each event it participates in, combining a time-gap term $W_t(t_p - t_{p-1})$ with a hidden state built from the entity's previous embedding, the partner entity's embedding, and the previous relation embedding.
  • Why it was created: static entity embeddings cannot capture that entities and their relationships change over time (self-evolution + mutual evolution).
  • Concrete example: after the US mentions country-X, both embeddings update; a later prediction uses the updated vectors.
  • How it is used in this paper: feeds the bilinear score, giving the model memory of the full event history; also enables prediction over previously unseen entities.

5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: new method (deep temporal-KG representation learning) Context: builds on static KG embedding methods (RESCAL, NTN, ER-MLP, TransE/TransR) and temporal point-process / co-evolutionary embedding work (Du et al. 2016 RMTPP; Wang/Dai et al. 2016 co-evolution). Foundational for the dynamic-KG evolution problem (SP8). Correctness: two large real-world datasets (GDELT, ICEWS); reports MAR, STD of MAR, HITS@10, and MAE. Sliding-window evaluation over 12 test windows adds temporal rigor. Contributions: (1) first deep architecture modeling knowledge evolution as a temporal point process; (2) novel time-prediction capability for facts; (3) Open-World Assumption support and prediction over unseen entities via dynamic embeddings. Clarity: method equations (3)–(6) given; full reproducibility (loss, sampling, hyperparameters) needs the appendices.

Contribution vs. Claim기여 대 주장

Abstract claims "significantly improved performance" and novel time prediction. Results section (Fig. 4–7) shows outperformance on MAR/HITS@10/MAE, but exact margins and whether gains hold uniformly across both raw/filtered variants need the figures read at deep depth. Open-World Assumption and unseen-entity prediction are claimed as capabilities; the empirical validation of the unseen-entity claim specifically was not confirmed from pages 1–5.

Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • DyRep (Trivedi et al., 2019) — extends Know-Evolve's temporal point-process representation learning to general dynamic graphs.
  • RE-Net / RE-GCN and later TKG reasoning models — cite Know-Evolve as the foundational dynamic-KG baseline (relevant to SP8's temporal-KG evolution line).
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting
조건 차이 — 우리 환경으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = globally-ordered stream of timestamped event quadruplets $(e_s, r, e_o, t)$ from geopolitical event repositories (GDELT, ICEWS); outputs = link prediction (entity ranking) + continuous event-time prediction; conditions = dyadic events only, no self-loops, no duplicate quadruples; large scale (up to ~32M events, 20–260 relations). Our setting: dependency extraction for knowledge-base construction (SP8 — dynamic/temporal KG evolution). Inputs = extracted entity–relation facts, potentially with temporal/version metadata; outputs = an evolving KB that must track how dependencies change over time. Differences:

  • Domain (geopolitical events vs. dependency/software-or-document KB): reference only — the temporal-point-process framing transfers, the entity/relation vocabulary does not.
  • Continuous timestamps at fine granularity vs. possibly coarse/versioned dependency changes: missing link — need to check whether dependency events carry usable continuous timestamps.
  • Time-prediction objective ("when will a fact occur"): reference only — SP8 likely cares about evolution/validity of dependencies, not exact event-time forecasting, though recurrence prediction may map.
  • Scale/OWA handling: resolved conceptually — dynamic embeddings + OWA are directly desirable for an evolving KB.
