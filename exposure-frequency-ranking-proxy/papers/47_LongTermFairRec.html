Towards Long-term Fairness in Recommendation

Ge et al. · arXiv · 2021

arXiv:2101.03584 · PDF · SP: SP5

P2Track A

한 줄 요약

누적 공정성을 강화학습(CMDP)으로 정식화한 장기 공정성 추천 연구.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Fairness-aware recommendation and ranking methods (fairness-constrained optimization, exposure-based reranking like FOE) are one-shot: they fix the protected group labels and produce a single fairness solution. In a live recommender, item popularity — and hence popularity-based group membership — changes because of the recommendation policy and user feedback, so a statically fair policy can drift unfair, and can even manufacture a new Matthew Effect inside the long-tail group. Prior dynamic work (Saito et al. 2020; Morik et al. 2020) adapts to changing item utility but keeps group labels fixed.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Model recommendation as a Constrained MDP and solve it with Constrained Policy Optimization (CPO), yielding FCPO:

  • Reward $R = \sum_{l=1}^{K}\mathbb{1}(a_t^l \text{ gets positive feedback})$; cost $C = \sum_{l=1}^{K}\mathbb{1}(a_t^l \in \text{sensitive group } G_0)$.
  • The Exact-K exposure-ratio constraint $\text{Exp}_t(G_0)/\text{Exp}_t(G_1) \le \alpha$ is rewritten as a per-step cost bound $C \le \alpha' K$ with $\alpha' = \alpha/(1+\alpha)$, giving discounted-cost limit $d = \sum_t \gamma_c^t \alpha' K$.
  • Actor-critic architecture with one actor (GRU state encoder + MLP → Gaussian over K ideal item embeddings → softmax over item catalog) and two critics: one for value, one for cost. Policy update is CPO's trust-region step; with one linear constraint the update has an analytical solution.
  • Because the cost constraint is evaluated against current (dynamically relabeled) groups, the policy continuously re-adapts as labels shift. [SKIM - verify]: exact line-search/backtracking details in Algorithm 1.
Key Results주요 결과
  • ML100K: FCPO-1 reaches Recall@20 14.48% / NDCG@20 9.32% vs. best baseline NCF (6.98 / 6.51) — ~33% average accuracy improvement over NCF across list lengths; on ML1M, ~18.7% over LIRD.
  • Fairness knob works monotonically: on ML100K@20, popularity rate falls 85.9% (FCPO-1) → 65.6% (FCPO-2) → 36.9% (FCPO-3), and Gini Index@20 falls to 73.2% (FCPO-3) vs. 97-99% for unconstrained baselines.
  • Pareto dominance over FOE reranking: at matched Gini, FCPO gets higher NDCG, and vice versa (Fig. 3), on both individual (Gini) and group (long-tail rate) fairness.
  • Efficiency: FOE reranking scales badly (90min → 60h30min for 200 → 800 rerank candidates on ML100K, single-core CPU); FCPO selects from the full 1682-item catalog in ~3h.
  • Long-term (400 steps, online updates, dynamic labels): FCPO continuously suppresses Gini and popularity rate while MF-FOE plateaus per round and only improves when labels are manually refreshed (Fig. 4).

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

FCPO (Fairness-Constrained Policy Optimization for recommendation)

  • What it computes: user state (GRU encoding of recent positive history + user embedding) → actor samples K "ideal item embedding" rows from a Gaussian, dot-products against the item embedding matrix, softmax + argmax per rank → a K-item recommendation list; two critics estimate discounted reward (clicks) and discounted cost (number of recommended items from the sensitive/popular group); policy updated by CPO's trust-region step subject to the cost constraint.
  • Why it was created: prior fairness-aware recommendation is static/one-shot — group labels (popular vs. long-tail) are fixed, so a one-time fairness-constrained solution goes stale as recommendation itself shifts item popularity over time.
  • Concrete example: cost $C(s_t,a_t,s_{t+1}) = \sum_{l=1}^{K} \mathbb{1}(a_t^l \in G_0)$ (Eq. 9), constrained so $C \le \frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha}K = \alpha' K$ per step; with $\alpha'=0.4$ (FCPO-3), popularity rate on ML100K drops to ~36.9% vs. ~100% for unconstrained baselines.
  • How it is used in this paper: the core proposed method; three variants FCPO-1/2/3 ($\alpha' = 1, 0.8, 0.4$) trace the accuracy-fairness trade-off curve.

CMDP formulation of exposure-fair recommendation

  • What it computes: an MDP $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R, \mu, \gamma)$ augmented with an auxiliary cost function and limit $d$; feasible policies are those whose discounted cumulative cost $J_C(\pi) = \mathbb{E}[\sum_t \gamma_c^t C_t] \le d = \sum_t \gamma_c^t \alpha' K$ (Eq. 10-11); the RL objective is $\max_\pi J(\pi)$ over that feasible set.
  • Why it was created: to make exposure fairness a constraint on the sequential policy rather than a post-hoc rerank of a static score list, so the policy adapts when group labels change.
  • Concrete example: separate discount factors $\gamma_r$ for reward and $\gamma_c$ for cost; one linear constraint admits the analytical CPO update (Eq. 4).
  • How it is used in this paper: §4 problem formulation; solved via CPO (Achiam et al. 2017, prior work — reused, not introduced here).

Exact-K fairness constraint

  • What it computes: requires $\text{Exposure}_t(G_0)/\text{Exposure}_t(G_1) \le \alpha$ within every length-K recommendation list at each iteration $t$ (Eq. 7); reduces to demographic parity when $\alpha = |G_0|/|G_1|$ with equality.
  • Why it was created: demographic parity over the whole catalog is impractical per-list; a tunable list-level exposure-ratio cap is operationally adjustable by the platform.
  • Concrete example: with $K=5$ and $\alpha' = 0.4$, at most 2 of 5 recommended items may come from the popular group per step.
  • How it is used in this paper: converted algebraically into the per-step cost bound $C \le \alpha' K$ that feeds the CMDP constraint.

Dynamic group labels

  • What it computes: item group membership ($G_0$ = top-20% by impression count, $G_1$ = remaining 80%) is recomputed from accumulated exposure records during the recommendation process, so the fairness constraint itself changes over time.
  • Why it was created: static fairness with fixed labels can create a new Matthew Effect inside the protected group (their four-item A/B/C/D example, §1) — a long-tail item that gains utility keeps being over-recommended because its label never updates.
  • Concrete example: long-term evaluation runs 400 recommendation steps with labels updated from current impression results; the baseline MF-FOE only refreshes labels every round of K=100 recommendations and must retrain.
  • How it is used in this paper: the defining feature of the "long-term fairness" evaluation protocol (§5.4.2, §6.4).
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: MovieLens-style interaction logs; PyTorch; PMF pretraining for embeddings; GRU + MLP actor, two MLP critics (LBFGS for critics); CPO trust-region update. Single-machine scale (ML1M runs ~11.5h on single-core CPU for evaluation).
  • Code: available at https://github.com/TobyGE/FCPO
  • Modifications: for SP5's cumulative per-item exposure control, the cost function must move from group-indicator counts to per-item exposure counters, which breaks the single-linear-constraint analytical CPO solution (would need multi-constraint or Lagrangian CPO). [SKIM]
  • Unknowns: missing link — sensitivity to how often group labels are recomputed; missing link — behavior when the click simulator's bias interacts with the constraint (offline replay bias).
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Fairness of Exposure in Rankings (Singh & Joachims, 2018) — the static exposure-fairness framework FCPO uses as its FOE baseline; likely already in this survey's exposure thread.
  • Controlling Fairness and Bias in Dynamic Learning-to-Rank (Morik et al., 2020) — closest dynamic-fairness predecessor; adapts to changing utility but not changing group labels.
  • Constrained Policy Optimization (Achiam et al., 2017) — the RL machinery FCPO instantiates; relevant for any SP5 extension to multiple/cumulative exposure constraints.
  • Delayed Impact of Fair Machine Learning (Liu et al., 2019) — the general "static fairness harms long-term outcomes" argument this paper imports into recommendation.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = offline MovieLens100K/1M interaction logs replayed as simulated user feedback; outputs = K-item recommendation lists from an RL policy; conditions = item-side group fairness (top-20% popularity split), per-step list-level exposure-ratio constraint, log-based click simulation, PMF-pretrained frozen embeddings. Our setting (SP5 — exposure frequency as ranking proxy): controlling cumulative item-level exposure frequency when exposure itself serves as a proxy signal in ranking; interest is in the RL formulation of cumulative exposure control. Differences:

  • Group-level (binary popular/long-tail) vs. item-level exposure control: missing link — FCPO's cost counts group membership, not per-item exposure counts; per-item generalization is left to future work ("individual fairness constraints", §7).
  • Per-step list constraint vs. genuinely cumulative exposure budget: partially resolved — the CMDP discounted-cost $J_C(\pi) \le d$ is cumulative in form, but the paper enforces/derives it from a per-iteration bound $C \le \alpha' K$, so cumulative slack cannot be traded across steps. [SKIM - verify]
  • Simulated log-replay feedback vs. real exposure→engagement feedback loops: reference only.
  • Popularity groups relabeled from exposure records: directly relevant to SP5's exposure-frequency-as-signal framing — resolved: shows one concrete mechanism where the exposure statistic dynamically redefines the constraint itself.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper identifies the root cause (static fairness solutions go stale because group labels are endogenous to the recommendation policy) and reformulates the problem at that level (constraint defined over dynamically relabeled groups inside a sequential decision process), rather than patching a static metric; the residual weakness is evaluative (simulated feedback), not structural.
