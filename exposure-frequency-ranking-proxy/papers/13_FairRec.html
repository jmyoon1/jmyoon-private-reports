FairRec: Two-Sided Fairness for Personalized Recommendations in Two-Sided Platforms

Patro et al. · WWW 2020 · 2020 · 272 citations

arXiv:2002.10764 · PDF · SP: SP1

P0Track C

한 줄 요약

추천을 공정 분할(fair division) 문제로 사상하여 item 포함 횟수에 대한 EF1과 MMS 보장을 O(mnk)에 달성한다. SP1에서 유일한 순수 show/don't-show(포함 횟수) 방식이나, 보장이 position 가중 exposure로는 이전되지 않음을 스스로 인정한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Customer-centric top-$k$ recommendation creates extreme inequality in producer exposure on two-sided platforms — on real datasets, the 50% least-exposed producers receive only 32%, 5%, and 11% of total exposure (GL-CUSTOM, GL-FACT, Last.fm respectively at $k=20$). The naive producer-centric fix (poorest-$k$: always recommend the least-exposed products) equalizes exposure but collapses mean customer utility (e.g. $\mu_\phi = 0.12$ on GL-CUSTOM vs. 1.0 for top-$k$) and distributes that loss unevenly across customers. Prior fairness work treated producer fairness or customer fairness separately; none gave simultaneous individual-level guarantees on both sides.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Map fair recommendation to constrained fair allocation of indivisible goods: products = items (with copies), customers = agents, relevance scores = valuations. The extension beyond standard fair allocation: exactly $k$ items per agent, at most one copy of each product per agent, and a free choice of how many copies of each product exist. The FairRec algorithm (two-phase Greedy-Round-Robin, see Concepts) guarantees:

  • EF1 for every customer (customer-side fairness),
  • at least $\alpha \cdot$MMS exposure for at least $n - k$ producers and non-zero exposure for all $n$ producers (producer-side fairness), when $k < n \le mk$,
  • $O(mnk)$ worst-case time, agnostic to the underlying relevance-scoring model.
Key Results주요 결과
  • Under top-$k$ ($k=20$), 50% least-exposed producers get 32% / 5% / 11% of total exposure on GL-CUSTOM / GL-FACT / Last.fm — the motivating inequality measurement.
  • FairRec satisfies the minimum-exposure guarantee for (near-)all producers ($H \approx 1$, matching poorest-$k$) while top-$k$ and mixed-$k$ leave large fractions unsatisfied.
  • FairRec's exposure loss vs. top-$k$ is at most ~20% ($L \le 0.2$), far below random-$k$ and poorest-$k$.
  • Mean customer utility $\mu_\phi$ under FairRec is nearly at par with top-$k$; poorest-$k$ collapses it (e.g. 0.12 on GL-CUSTOM); FairRec's mean average envy $Y$ is near zero, comparable to top-$k$.
  • Trade-off knob: raising $\alpha$ (exposure guarantee toward full MMS) monotonically lowers exposure inequality $Z$ but increases popular-producer exposure loss $L$ and lowers $\mu_\phi$ (Fig. 4).

  • Demonstrated: guarantees are proven (§7); quality loss measured on 3 dataset/scorer settings.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Producer exposure (uniform attention model)

  • What it computes: for each producer/product $p$, counts the number of customers whose top-$k$ recommendation set contains $p$: $E_p = \sum_{u \in U} \mathbb{1}_{R_u}(p)$; total exposure across all producers is fixed at $m \times k$.
  • Why it was created: exposure determines producers' economic opportunity on two-sided platforms, but customer-centric top-$k$ recommendation ignores how exposure is distributed across producers.
  • Concrete example: with $m = 11172$ customers and $k = 20$ (Google Local dataset), total exposure is $223440$ recommendation slots; under top-$k$, the 50% least-exposed producers receive only 5% of it (GL-FACT).
  • How it is used in this paper: the quantity being fairly allocated — the producer-side fairness guarantee is a floor on $E_p$. Note: assumes every slot in the top-$k$ list carries equal attention (no position bias).

Maximin Share (MMS) exposure guarantee (concept from Budish 2011, applied here to exposure)

  • What it computes: the exposure floor $\lfloor mk/n \rfloor$ — the value a producer is guaranteed if $mk$ total slots were split as evenly as possible among $n$ producers. FairRec targets $\bar{E} = \alpha \cdot \text{MMS}$, $0 \le \alpha \le 1$.
  • Why it was created: exact envy-freeness/proportionality is unachievable with indivisible goods; MMS is the standard achievable relaxation.
  • Concrete example: Last.fm has MMS $= 0$ for $k < 10$ and MMS $= 1$ for $k \in [10, 18]$ (because $n = 17632 \gg m = 1892$), which visibly changes the fairness curves at $k = 10$ and $k = 19$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the producer-side guarantee — FairRec ensures MMS exposure for at least $n - k$ of the $n$ producers, and non-zero exposure for all.

Envy-Free up to One item (EF1) (concept from Budish 2011)

  • What it computes: allocation property — for every pair of customers $u, w$, there exists one item $p \in A_w$ such that $V_u(A_u) \ge V_u(A_w \setminus \{p\})$: no customer envies another's recommendation list after hypothetically removing one item from it.
  • Why it was created: exact envy-freeness is unattainable for indivisible items; EF1 is the achievable relaxation that bounds how unevenly the customer-side utility loss is distributed.
  • Concrete example: two agents, one item — the empty-handed agent violates EF but satisfies EF1 (removing the single item eliminates the envy).
  • How it is used in this paper: the customer-side guarantee — FairRec's output is EF1 for every customer, ensuring the utility loss caused by producer fairness is spread fairly.

FairRec / constrained Greedy-Round-Robin

  • What it computes: input = relevance scores $V_u(p)$, $m$ customers, $n$ producers, list size $k$; output = an allocation giving each customer exactly $k$ distinct products. Phase 1 creates $\ell = \lfloor mk/n \rfloor$ copies of each product and round-robins customers picking their most-relevant feasible product; Phase 2 lifts the copy limit and fills remaining slots to exactly $k$, preserving EF1. Runs in $O(mnk)$.
  • Why it was created: prior fair-allocation algorithms do not handle the recommendation constraints (exactly $k$ items per agent, at most one copy of a product per agent, unbounded copies available).
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - verify in deep read: worked allocation trace in §6].
  • How it is used in this paper: the main algorithmic contribution; theoretical guarantees proved in §7.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: any relevance-score matrix (model-agnostic); datasets used are public (Google Local, Last.fm HetRec); algorithm is $O(mnk)$ — cheap, no training.
  • Code: available at https://github.com/gourabkumarpatro/FairRec_www_2020 (with dataset details).
  • Modifications: for our setting, exposure would need re-weighting by rank position (attention model), and the one-shot allocation would need a repeated/online variant; both are named as future work by the authors.
  • Unknowns: missing link: how the EF1/MMS guarantees degrade under position-weighted exposure or stochastic serving; missing link: behavior when relevance scores are themselves exposure-biased.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = precomputed relevance scores $V_u(p)$ for all customer–product pairs; outputs = one static size-$k$ recommendation set per customer, computed offline for all customers at once; conditions = uniform attention within the list (no position bias), exposure defined as recommendation-count, single allocation round. Our setting (SP1, exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy): exposure frequency is used as a proxy signal for item quality/relevance in ranking systems; concerns how recommendation-count-based exposure relates to true relevance and what distortions arise when exposure is constrained or reallocated. [SKIM - verify against SP1 statement] Differences:

  • Exposure = binary list membership summed over users vs. rank/attention-weighted exposure: missing link — the paper explicitly excludes position bias, so its guarantees do not transfer directly to rank-weighted exposure notions.
  • Offline one-shot batch allocation vs. online/repeated ranking: missing link — authors state online extension is future work.
  • Relevance scores taken as ground-truth valuations vs. our concern that exposure itself feeds back into estimated relevance (proxy circularity): reference only — FairRec assumes $V$ is fixed and exogenous; it does not model exposure→relevance feedback.
  • Individual-producer exposure floors as the fairness object: resolved — the paper gives a precise, computable definition (MMS on $E_p$) usable as the exposure-frequency quantity in our analysis.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the paper directly restructures the allocation problem (fair-division mapping with provable floors) rather than post-hoc re-ranking, which is root-cause treatment for the static exposure-inequality problem; but it patches the exposure metric itself at the symptom level, since uniform attention ignores position bias and the exposure→relevance feedback loop that generates the inequality in the first place.
