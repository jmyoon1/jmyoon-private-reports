Percentile Risk-Constrained Budget Pacing for Guaranteed Display Advertising in Online Optimization

Dai et al. · arXiv · 2023 · 5 citations

arXiv:2312.06174 · PDF · SP: SP7

P4Track A

한 줄 요약

보장형 디스플레이 광고의 percentile 위험 제약 budget pacing(RCPacing). 명시적으로 Dual Mirror Descent이며 O(√T) regret — 조사에서 pacing=dual-targeting 등가성의 결정적 증거이자, percentile 공간 dual이라는 드리프트 강건화 장치를 제공한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Smooth pacing control for guaranteed-display (GD) ad campaigns: publishers must deliver each campaign's contracted impression count evenly over the period, but existing techniques fail under (a) huge budget disparities across campaigns (thousands to millions of daily impressions), (b) drift in the user-arrival distribution, and (c) dynamic supply/demand shifts (campaigns going on/offline). Existing smooth-pacing work targets RTB ads and is incompatible with guaranteed allocation; GD-allocation work treats smoothness as a fixed side term, never dynamically adjusted or evaluated as a key metric. Dual-based methods (DMD) are additionally risky because a campaign whose dual variable sits at a high percentile of the impression-quality distribution suffers disproportionate participation-rate swings from small dual shifts or distribution drift (Theorems 2–3).

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

RCPacing = DMD-style dual allocation where dual variables live in the percentile space of each campaign's impression-performance (CTR) distribution, jointly with probabilistic throttling that keeps duals inside a low-risk percentile region. Components (Algorithm 2):

  1. Parametric percentile transformation: Box-Cox + normal CDF maps CTRs to uniform percentile space (forward, eq. 11); duals are updated in percentile space and mapped back for serving (backward, eq. 12).
  2. PTR factor decoupling: $PTR_{ij} = PTR^{base}_j \cdot fp(\bar\alpha_j) \cdot fv(\bar\alpha_j,\bar v_{ij})$ — base rate from budget/audience-size, exponential decay above the safety bound $P_{ub}$, linear performance-based non-uniform pacing (eqs. 13–18).
  3. Emergency PTR (ePTR) proportional controller capping spend speed at 2x expected (eqs. 20–21), plus cold-start initial trial rate.
  4. Adaptive gradient clipping using an importance-sampling bound $\psi_j^{-1}$ on the percentile update (eqs. 22–26).
  5. Modified Itakura-Saito Bregman divergence so dual steps shrink near percentile 1.0 (eq. 28).

Theory: $O(\sqrt T)$ dynamic regret when horizon and budgets scale proportionally (Theorem 1); Theorems 2–3 prove higher percentile ⇒ greater PR fluctuation under dual shift and under Beta-distribution drift.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Offline (600K impressions, 300 GD ads, 50 periods): unsmoothness 6.37 ± 0.72 vs PDOA 15.70 (−59.4%) and AUAF 12.95 (−50.8%), at 99.8% delivery rate.
  • Offline CTR 7.46% vs PDOA 6.06% (+23.1%) and AUAF 5.14% (+45.1%); Smart Pacing has higher CTR (7.88%) but only 95.9% delivery — unacceptable for guaranteed contracts.
  • Online 2-week A/B on Taobao brand advertising: +35.3% UI and +23.4% CTR vs PDOA; all delivery rates > 99.5%.
  • Ablations: Itakura-Saito divergence improves smoothness 3.74% over Euclidean at $P_{ub}=90\%$; gradient clipping cuts unsmoothness 11.54 → 8.26 at large step size $\eta=0.8$.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Probabilistic throttling / pass-through rate (PTR)

  • What it computes: for each (request i, campaign j) pair, a probability $PTR_{ij} \in [0,1]$ that the campaign is allowed to participate in the allocation auction at all; the campaign's bid is randomly zeroed with probability $1 - PTR_{ij}$.
  • Why it was created: bid modification alone controls spend poorly under dramatic changes in the bid win-rate curve; throttling decouples spend control from bid calculation (prior work: Agarwal et al. 2014, Xu et al. 2015).
  • Concrete example: eq. 13 decouples $PTR_{ij} = PTR^{base}_j \cdot fp(\bar\alpha_j) \cdot fv(\bar\alpha_j, \bar v_{ij})$, where $fv = 10(\bar v_{ij} - \bar\alpha_j) + 1$ gives higher pass-through to higher-CTR traffic.
  • How it is used in this paper: the pacing-control knob that constrains dual variables inside the "low-risk" percentile region and implements smooth delivery.

Percentile-space transformation of dual variables (RCPacing core)

  • What it computes: forward: predicted CTR $v_{ij}$ → Box-Cox transform (MLE-fitted $\lambda^*_j$) → standardize → normal CDF $\Phi$ → percentile value $\bar v_{ij} \in [0,1]$ (eq. 11, with a skew factor $\epsilon$ toward the middle percentiles for drift robustness). Backward: percentile dual $\bar\alpha_j$ → $\Phi^{-1}$ → inverse Box-Cox → original dual $\alpha_j$ (eq. 12).
  • Why it was created: dual variables in raw CTR space have wildly different scales across campaigns and are risk-inhomogeneous — the same dual shift causes much larger participation-rate swings at high percentiles (proved as Theorems 2–3).
  • Concrete example: figure 2 — under Beta(2,8), a dual at percentile giving 1.0% PR vs one giving 10.0% PR: shifting both left by 0.1 changes PR to 4.2% vs 25.8% respectively.
  • How it is used in this paper: all dual updates and PTR fine-tuning happen in the uniform percentile space; a safe upper bound $P_{ub}$ (e.g. 90%) caps how deep into the high-risk percentile region a campaign's dual may sit.

Dual Mirror Descent (DMD)

  • What it computes: online allocation via Lagrangian duals — assign each arriving impression to $\arg\max_j \{v_j^{(t)} - \alpha_j^{(t)}\}$ among non-exhausted campaigns, then mirror-descent-update $\alpha$ with stochastic subgradient $-\tilde x^{(t)} + \rho$ (Balseiro, Lu & Mirrokni 2020).
  • Why it was created: general online allocation under budget constraints with $O(\sqrt T)$ regret; prior paper's method, reused here as the backbone.
  • Concrete example: Algorithm 1 in the paper.
  • How it is used in this paper: RCPacing = DMD run in percentile space + throttling + clipping; also the primary baseline.

Modified Itakura-Saito divergence for dual updates

  • What it computes: reference function $h(\alpha) = -\ln(1.5 - \alpha)$ in the mirror-descent update, which makes the effective step size shrink as $\bar\alpha_j \to 1$ (eq. 28). [SKIM - verify derivation details in deep read]
  • Why it was created: PR fluctuates more per unit dual shift at high percentiles, so the update magnitude should decrease there; Euclidean $h(\alpha)=\alpha^2$ gives uniform steps.
  • Concrete example: ablation — 3.74% smoothness improvement over Euclidean at $P_{ub}=90\%$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the Bregman-divergence choice inside RCPacing's dual update.

Unsmoothness index (UI)

  • What it computes: $\frac1M \sum_j \sqrt{\frac1T \sum_t (\tilde x^{(t)}_j - \rho_j)^2}$ — per-campaign RMS deviation of actual delivery from the ideal even-paced rate, averaged over campaigns (eq. 31).
  • Why it was created: prior GD-allocation work treats smoothness as a fixed regularizer, not an evaluated metric.
  • Concrete example: RCPacing 6.37 ± 0.72 vs DMD 15.71 ± 1.46 offline.
  • How it is used in this paper: primary evaluation metric alongside delivery rate and average CTR.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: streaming request log with per-campaign predicted scores; historical logs to fit Box-Cox $\lambda^*_j$, $\mu_j$, $\sigma_j$; a feedback-control loop with periodic dual updates. Compute is light (closed-form transforms + bisection).
  • Code: available at https://github.com/danifree/RCPacing (dataset + all baselines included).
  • Modifications: replace campaign budgets with exposure-frequency targets; replace single-slot win-out with slot-position allocation for ranked lists; refit percentile transforms per item/provider group.
  • Unknowns: missing link: behavior when the impression-quality distribution is far from Beta/Box-Cox-normalizable (e.g. heavy multimodality); missing link: regret guarantee under adversarial (non-IID) arrivals.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Balseiro, Lu & Mirrokni, "Dual Mirror Descent for Online Allocation Problems" (ICML 2020) — the backbone algorithm and regret framework RCPacing transforms into percentile space.
  • Xu et al., "Smart Pacing for Effective Online Ad Campaign Optimization" (KDD 2015) — the probabilistic-throttling predecessor whose performance-layered pacing RCPacing continuizes.
  • Cheng et al., "An Adaptive Unified Allocation Framework for Guaranteed Display Advertising" (WSDM 2022) — the fixed-smoothness-term dual baseline (AUAF) this paper beats on both UI and CTR.
  • Zhou et al., "A Primal-Dual Online Algorithm for Online Matching Problem in Dynamic Environments" (AAAI 2021) — bid-modification-based smoothness baseline (PDOA), the strongest dynamic-environment comparator.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = streaming ad requests with DNN-predicted CTR per (impression, campaign); outputs = one-hot allocation of each impression to a GD campaign; conditions = known-IID arrivals, Beta-distributed impression quality, hard budget (impression-count) constraints, publisher-side control, billions of daily requests (Taobao). Our setting (SP7 — exposure/frequency control in ranking systems): exposure frequency as a controlled or proxy quantity in a general recommender/ranking pipeline; no guaranteed-contract structure; exposure targets may be soft (fairness/calibration) rather than hard contractual budgets. Differences:

  • Guaranteed hard budgets vs soft exposure targets: the throttling + dual machinery transfers, but the over-spend irreversibility framing weakens — missing link
  • Known-IID arrival assumption vs non-stationary user traffic in general ranking: paper partially addresses via skew factor and ePTR — resolved: percentile-space standardization is itself the drift-robustness device
  • CTR as the single quality score vs multi-objective ranking scores: reference only
  • Publisher-side impression allocation (one slot, one winner) vs top-k ranked lists: missing link — the one-hot single-slot assumption is load-bearing in the regret proof
  • [SKIM - verify] whether percentile standardization of score distributions transfers as an exposure-frequency normalization trick for heterogeneous item popularity
