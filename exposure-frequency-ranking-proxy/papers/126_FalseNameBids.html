The effect of false-name bids in combinatorial auctions: new fraud in Internet auctions

Yokoo et al. · Games and Economic Behavior 2004 · 2004 · 295 citations

link · SP: SP11

P2Track C

한 줄 요약

조합 경매에서 false-name 입찰이 일으키는 새로운 사기를 분석한 고전(GEB 2004). 콘텐츠 분할 공격이 구조적으로 false-name 입찰과 같음을 잇는 다리.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Combinatorial auction theory (VCG in particular) guarantees strategy-proofness and Pareto-efficient allocation under the assumption that the auctioneer can verify a one-to-one mapping between bidders and real economic agents. In Internet auctions identities are cheap and unverifiable, so a single agent can participate under multiple fictitious names. Prior mechanism-design results say nothing about whether efficiency and truthfulness survive this manipulation.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Theoretical analysis (impossibility result). Main claims from the abstract:

  1. VCG is vulnerable to false-name bids: a single bidder can profit by splitting its bid across multiple fictitious identities. [SKIM - the exact valuation conditions under which VCG is/isn't false-name-proof require the full paper]
  2. Impossibility theorem: no false-name-proof combinatorial auction protocol can always achieve a Pareto-efficient allocation. Robustness to identity splitting and full efficiency are jointly unattainable — the tension is fundamental, not an artifact of a particular protocol.

[SKIM - verify proof structure and any positive results (e.g., false-name-proof but inefficient protocols proposed) in deep read]

Key Results주요 결과
  • No false-name-proof combinatorial auction protocol always achieves a Pareto-efficient allocation (impossibility theorem)
  • VCG is manipulable via identity splitting (false-name bids) despite being strategy-proof under single identities
  • [SKIM] — quantitative characterizations (e.g., worst-case efficiency loss of false-name-proof protocols, valuation-class conditions) require the full paper

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

False-name bid

  • What it computes: a single strategic bidder → submits bids under multiple fictitious identifiers (e.g., multiple email addresses in an Internet auction) → the mechanism perceives several independent bidders where only one economic agent exists
  • Why it was created: classical mechanism design (VCG) assumes each participant controls exactly one identity; anonymous Internet auctions void that assumption, and no prior framework modeled this manipulation
  • Concrete example: an agent wanting bundle {A, B} splits into two identities, one bidding on A and one on B, changing the VCG payments each "bidder" owes relative to a single combined bid [SKIM - verify exact worked example in deep read]
  • How it is used in this paper: defines the manipulation class against which protocol robustness (false-name-proofness) is evaluated

False-name-proofness

  • What it computes: an incentive-compatibility condition — for every agent and every possible split of that agent into multiple identities, truthful bidding under a single identity → (weakly) highest utility; i.e., no identity-splitting strategy is profitable
  • Why it was created: strategy-proofness (truthfulness under one identity) is insufficient when identities are unverifiable; VCG is strategy-proof yet manipulable by identity splitting
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - verify in deep read] — the paper characterizes when VCG fails this condition (known to hold under submodular/gross-substitutes-like valuations; exact condition needs the paper text)
  • How it is used in this paper: the axiom whose joint satisfiability with Pareto efficiency is proven impossible

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = sealed bids over bundles of discrete items from strategic agents with unverifiable identities; outputs = allocation + payments; conditions = one-shot combinatorial auction, quasi-linear utility, monetary transfers available Our setting (SP11): a ranking/recommendation platform granting per-identity or per-item exposure guarantees (e.g., exposure floors), where a content provider can split one catalog/account into multiple identities or items to multiply its guaranteed exposure — no monetary transfers, repeated allocation of a divisible resource (impressions) rather than one-shot discrete items Differences:

  • Monetary payments available in auctions vs. no payments in exposure allocation: missing link — the impossibility proof leverages payment structure; whether an analogous "no splitting-proof + exposure-efficient allocation" theorem holds without money is exactly the open question SP11 cares about
  • Discrete one-shot bundle allocation vs. continuous repeated exposure allocation: missing link — divisibility may change what splitting gains an agent
  • Identity splitting of bidders vs. content splitting of items/accounts: reference only — structurally analogous (one agent → many mechanism-visible units), and this paper is the canonical formalization of that move, but the mapping must be argued, not assumed
  • Pareto efficiency objective vs. exposure-fairness/relevance objectives: reference only — the impossibility's flavor (robustness to splitting trades off against optimality of allocation) is the transferable lesson
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper attacks the root cause head-on: it formalizes identity unverifiability as a manipulation class and proves the resulting robustness–efficiency trade-off is fundamental (an impossibility), rather than patching a particular protocol against a particular exploit.
