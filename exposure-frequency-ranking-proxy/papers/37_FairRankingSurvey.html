Fairness in Ranking: A Survey

Zehlike, Yang & Stoyanovich · ACM Computing Surveys 2022 · 2022 · 104 citations

arXiv:2103.14000 · PDF · SP: SP3 · SP5

P1Track A

한 줄 요약

ranking 공정성 연구 전반의 taxonomy를 정리한 ACM Computing Surveys survey. WYSIWYG/WAE 세계관 축이 실질적으로 score 신뢰 축임을 읽어낼 수 있는 기준 문헌.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Fairness-in-ranking work is scattered across data management, algorithms, IR, and RecSys communities, with mutually incompatible fairness definitions (probability-based vs. exposure-based; merit-aware vs. merit-blind), no shared vocabulary, and — critically — no way to tell which methods are even comparable: papers compare methods whose disparate-exposure measures and targeted bias types differ entirely (e.g., Fair-PG-Rank vs. DELTR), producing meaningless comparisons. The normative value frameworks (worldview, equality-of-opportunity doctrine) that each technical choice silently encodes are almost never made explicit.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

A systematic survey (72 pp., published as CSUR Part I score-based + Part II LtR/RecSys) organized around four classification frameworks applied uniformly to every surveyed method:

  1. Group structure — sensitive-attribute cardinality (binary/multinary), number (one/multiple), handling (independent/combination).
  2. Bias type — pre-existing (societal), technical (e.g., position bias), emergent (feedback loops).
  3. Mitigation objective — worldview (WYSIWYG ↔ WAE, incl. continuous interpolation) × equality-of-opportunity doctrine (formal, formal-plus, substantive: luck-egalitarian / Rawlsian) × intersectionality.
  4. Mitigation method — score-based: intervene on scores / scoring function / ranked outcome; LtR: pre- / in- / post-processing.

Section 5 covers score-based methods (rND-rRD-rKL, constrained ranking maximization, balanced diverse ranking, diverse k-choice secretary, implicit-bias selection, CIF-Rank, fair ranking-function design); Section 6 covers fair LtR (iFair, DELTR, Fair-PG-Rank, pairwise fairness, FA*IR, LinkedIn, CFAθ, Fairness of Exposure, Equity of Attention); Section 7 recommendation/matching; Sections 8–9 frameworks/benchmarks (FairSearch, TREC Fair Ranking, Ranking Facts) and evaluation recommendations.

Key Results주요 결과

(Survey — no new experiments; representative synthesized findings:)

  • Two measurement families identified: probability-based (per-prefix statistical significance tests; strict, no compensation) vs. exposure-based (expected position-bias-weighted attention; serves individual or group fairness) — no consensus exposure definition exists, and methods disagree on whether merit belongs in the measure.
  • Methods that embed utility in the fairness objective (Fair-PG-Rank, Equity of Attention, Singh & Joachims' "disparate treatment") map to WYSIWYG + formal EO; merit-excluding methods (DELTR, FA*IR, demographic-parity exposure) map to WAE + substantive EO — a small design detail can flip the framework (LinkedIn's tie-breaking by highest score makes it inconsistent with substantive EO for >1 sensitive attribute, unlike the seemingly identical FA*IR).
  • Research gaps surfaced: only one method (Equity of Attention) explicitly addresses emergent bias, and none doing so is WAE-consistent; no LtR method handles intersectional discrimination.
  • Dataset practice is criticized: surveyed papers rarely justify dataset choice beyond availability (e.g., COMPAS reused for distributive-justice tasks though collected for legal decision-making).
  • Multiple surveyed results (DELTR, Sühr et al., Mehrotra et al.) show fairness does not always cost utility — sometimes optimizing fairness increases system utility.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Exposure (of a candidate / group in a ranking)

  • What it computes: ranking distribution $\pi$ over permutations + position-bias vector $v$ → expectation → $\mathrm{Exposure}(\tau(i)) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi}[v(\tau(i))]$, the expected attention a candidate receives given where rankings place it (Eq. 8, p. 9).
  • Why it was created: raw rank position alone does not quantify how much user attention an item actually gets; attention decays geometrically/logarithmically with rank, so two adjacent ranks can differ enormously in visibility.
  • Concrete example: in the survey's running example (Fig. 28), position biases for ranks 1–8 are v = 3.32, 2.10, 1.66, 1.43, 1.28, 1.18, 1.11, 1.04 — the top slot gets >3x the attention of rank 8.
  • How it is used in this paper: the organizing quantity for one of the two main families of fairness measures ("exposure-based fairness"); individual unfairness is $D(a,b) = |\mathrm{Exposure}(a)-\mathrm{Exposure}(b)|$ and group unfairness averages exposure within groups (Eqs. 9–10).

Position bias

  • What it computes: rank position $i$ → empirical attention model → weight $v(i)$, decaying geometrically or logarithmically (e.g., $1/\log_2(i+1)$).
  • Why it was created: models the observation that users of ranked interfaces attend far more to top positions (a technical bias inherent to any 1-D ranked list).
  • Concrete example: DCG-style discount: utility of rank 3 is weighted $1/\log_2 4 = 0.5$ vs. 1.0 at rank 1.
  • How it is used in this paper: the shared building block linking discounted utility (Eq. 3), NDCG (Eq. 7), and exposure (Eq. 8); classified as "technical bias" in the survey's bias-type framework.

Probability-based fairness (vs. exposure-based)

  • What it computes: a ranking + minimum protected proportion $p$ → binomial/multinomial significance test at every prefix → pass/fail fairness verdict (e.g., FA*IR asks whether the ranking could have been generated by a fair Bernoulli process).
  • Why it was created: gives a strict, per-prefix group-fairness guarantee — a ranking failing the test at any position is unfair, with no later compensation (unlike exposure averaging).
  • Concrete example: FA*IR table (p. 46): with $p=0.7, \alpha=0.1$, at least 1 protected candidate required in top-2, 2 in top-4.
  • How it is used in this paper: one of the two main measurement families; frames post-processing methods FA*IR [114, 117] and LinkedIn Recruiter re-ranking [46].

Rank-aware proportional representation (rND / rRD / rKL)

  • What it computes: ranking → at cut-offs $k=10,20,\dots$ compute set-wise statistical-parity difference/ratio/KL-divergence between top-$k$ group proportion and overall proportion → sum with $1/\log_2 k$ discount, normalized (Eqs. 11–15).
  • Why it was created: set-wise parity ignores where in the ranking the protected group appears; discounting makes parity at high ranks count more.
  • Concrete example: rKL compares $P_k = (|\tau_{1..k}\cap G_1|/k, |\tau_{1..k}\cap G_2|/k)$ against overall $Q$ at each prefix.
  • How it is used in this paper: presented as the first formalization of rank-aware fairness (Yang & Stoyanovich [112]); rKL is the survey's recommended variant (smooth, multinary-capable).

WYSIWYG vs. WAE worldviews (construct space / observed space / decision space)

  • What it computes: a classification of fairness methods by their implicit assumption about the mapping $g$: construct space (true qualification) → observed space (measured scores). WYSIWYG assumes distortion ≤ ε (scores trustworthy); WAE assumes group differences in scores are entirely observation bias.
  • Why it was created: (Friedler et al.) makes explicit that no fairness definition is belief-free; the survey uses it to map every surveyed method to the normative stance it silently encodes.
  • Concrete example: methods whose fairness objective includes utility/merit (Fair-PG-Rank, Equity of Attention) map to WYSIWYG + formal EO; methods that exclude merit (DELTR, FA*IR, demographic-parity exposure) map to WAE + substantive EO.
  • How it is used in this paper: one of the four classification frameworks (with group structure, bias type, equality-of-opportunity doctrine) applied to every method via a recurring mind-map figure.

Not re-defined here (survey covers them as prior work): NDCG, statistical parity, individual vs. group fairness (Dwork et al.), Rooney Rule, structural causal models / counterfactual fairness.

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: nothing to run — a taxonomy. To reuse: the exposure definitions (Eqs. 8–10), rND/rRD/rKL (Eqs. 11–15), and the dataset table (Table 2: COMPAS, German Credit, XING, Yahoo! LTR, IIT-JEE, LSAC, ...) are directly reusable specifications.
  • Code: survey itself has none; it points to FairSearch (https://github.com/fair-search — DELTR + FA*IR as Elasticsearch plugins) and Ranking Facts (https://github.com/DataResponsibly/RankingFacts).
  • Modifications: to use exposure as a proxy metric (our setting) strip the fairness constraints and keep the measurement layer: position-bias model choice (geometric vs. logarithmic) + individual/group aggregation + optional amortization over ranking sequences.
  • Unknowns: missing link — which position-bias model the survey considers empirically best-grounded for measurement (it notes "consensus on a definition of exposure has not yet been found"); missing link — Part II details on merit-weighted exposure interpretations (Sections 6–7 skimmed only at structure level).

  • Ekstrand, Burke & Diaz, "Fairness and Discrimination in Retrieval and Recommendation" (SIGIR 2019 tutorial) — companion systematization for the IR/RecSys side cited by this survey.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = candidates (people) with sensitive attributes + scores or preference-labeled training data; outputs = permutations/top-k; conditions = fairness toward ranked items' producers (group visibility shares), score-based (Part I) and supervised LtR (Part II); position bias treated as the attention model to be equalized over. Our setting: exposure frequency used as a measurable proxy for ranking quality/impact (SP3: fixed-scorer score-based setting; SP5): exposure is the survey's core measured quantity, but our interest is exposure as an evaluation/proxy signal rather than a fairness constraint to enforce. Differences:

  • Fairness-constraint framing (equalize/bound exposure across protected groups) vs. our proxy-measurement framing (use exposure as an observable stand-in): reference only — the exposure formalism (Eq. 8, position-bias models, amortization over rankings) transfers directly; the normative machinery does not need to.
  • Part I fixed-scorer score-based setting matches SP3's fixed-scorer setting exactly (per source note): resolved: adopt Section 5 taxonomy (intervene on scores / function / outcome) as the SP3 design-space map.
  • Survey assumes candidates are people with legally sensitive attributes; our items may be arbitrary: resolved: group structure generalizes to any categorical partition (survey itself notes techniques apply to non-person entities).
  • Amortized/temporal exposure (Equity of Attention, Eq. 26–27) vs. single-shot ranking: missing link — whether SP5 needs amortized exposure accounting is not determinable from this skim.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — for its target problem (unsystematized, incomparable fairness-in-ranking literature) the survey attacks the root cause: it traces each technical choice back to the normative assumption (worldview, EO doctrine, bias type) that generates the incompatibility, rather than merely cataloguing methods by surface features; the "don't compare apples to oranges" recommendation follows from that root-cause analysis.
