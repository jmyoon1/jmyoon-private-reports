Scalable and Provably Fair Exposure Control for Large-Scale Recommender Systems

Togashi et al. · WWW 2024 · 2024

arXiv:2402.14369 · PDF · SP: SP4

P0Track B

한 줄 요약

대규모 recommender를 위한 확장 가능하고 증명 가능한 공정 exposure 제어(WWW 2024)를 제안한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Existing exposure-fairness methods for recommendation — post-processing on doubly stochastic matrices, Frank–Wolfe rankers, FairRec's round-robin, and PL-model in-processing with SGD — all carry an $O(|U||V|)$ time or space factor (or a non-parallelizable loop), so none can control the accuracy–fairness tradeoff on industry-scale systems with millions of users and items. The scalable baseline (iALS) offers no fairness control at all.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Minimize $L(V,U) + \lambda_{ex} R_{ex}(V,U)$ where $L$ is the iALS objective (Eq. 1) and $R_{ex}(V,U) = \frac{1}{|V|}\|\frac{1}{|U|}VU^\top\mathbb{1}\|_2^2$ is the second moment of per-item average predicted scores. Since $R_{ex}$ destroys the row-separability iALS depends on, reformulate with auxiliary $s = \frac{1}{|U|}U^\top\mathbb{1}$ and solve the augmented Lagrangian by 4-block alternation: (1) V-step — closed-form row-wise ridge solves with an extra rank-one term $\lambda_{ex}ss^\top$; (2) U-step — proximal gradient: parallel gradient descent on the iALS loss, then a closed-form proximal map made $O(|U|d)$ by Sherman–Morrison; (3) s-step — $d\times d$ linear solve reusing the item Gramian; (4) dual ascent on $w$. Theorem 3.1 proves that under bounded iterates and $\rho, \gamma$ conditions, the augmented Lagrangian converges and all residuals and gradients converge to 0 (KKT points), despite non-convex multi-block ADMM having no such guarantee in general.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Training time on MSD (571K users, 41K items): exADMM 1,533 s vs. iALS 858 s (≈1.8× overhead); Mult-VAE 22,580 s (~15× slower than exADMM); Multi-FR and FairRec infeasible (NA) on MSD.
  • Multi-FR is ~2,350× slower than exADMM on Epinions (4,011 s vs. 1.7 s) and infeasible on ML-20M and MSD.
  • On Epinions, exADMM matches Multi-FR's accuracy–fairness Pareto frontier (nDCG@K vs. Gini@K) despite the scalability gap; vs. iALS+FairRec, exADMM wins the tradeoff in the high-accuracy regime, FairRec wins under strong fairness requirements.
  • $\lambda_{ex}$ acts as a monotonic controller: Lorenz curves of item exposure shift smoothly toward equality as $\lambda_{ex}$ grows (e.g., ML-20M: 1e−10 → 3e−6), with $\lambda_{ex}=0$ recovering vanilla iALS exactly.
  • Time complexity strictly better than iALS in the $|U|$ term ($|U|d^2$ vs. $|U|d^3$) because the U-step avoids the linear solve.
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: new method (scalable in-processing fairness algorithm) + theoretical convergence analysis. Context: builds directly on iALS (Hu et al. 2008; Rendle et al. 2021) and ADMM (Boyd et al. 2011); positions against Singh & Joachims' fairness-of-exposure line, FairRec (Patro et al. 2020), Multi-FR (Wu et al. 2022), Do & Usunier's Gini/Lorenz work. Correctness: convergence theorem with full appendix proofs (assumes bounded iterates — a stated assumption, not proven); experiments report Pareto frontiers over grid search rather than single cherry-picked points; no error bars / repeated-run CIs reported [SKIM - verify whether runs are averaged]. Contributions: (1) first fairness-controllable method with iALS-level scalability ($O(\text{nz}(R)d^2 + |U|d^2 + |V|d^3)$); (2) ADMM optimization tools (auxiliary-variable splitting + linearized U-step + Sherman–Morrison prox) with a convergence guarantee for the non-convex 3-block problem; (3) empirical demonstration on ML-20M, MSD, Epinions that the tradeoff is controllable where competitors are infeasible. Clarity: high — Algorithm 1 gives line-by-line pseudocode with per-line complexity annotations, and code is public.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: first scalable method for flexible accuracy–fairness control at million-user/item scale; provable convergence; tradeoff effectiveness similar to inefficient fairness baselines.
  • Demonstrated: scalability and controllability are shown convincingly (Tables 2–3, Figures 1–2); convergence theorem proven in the appendix under a bounded-iterates assumption.
  • Gap: [SKIM - verify in deep read] the fairness notion actually optimized is a score-second-moment proxy, while evaluation uses position-based Gini@K — the paper asserts a "monotonic relationship" between scores and exposure but does not formally bound the proxy-to-metric gap; "provably fair" in the title means provable convergence of the fairness-regularized objective, not a provable fairness guarantee on the ranked output. Largest datasets tested are ~571K users, below the "millions" the claims target.
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

exADMM (exposure-aware ADMM)

  • What it computes: implicit feedback matrix $R \in \{0,1\}^{|U|\times|V|}$ → alternating updates of user embeddings $U$, item embeddings $V$, auxiliary variable $s$, and dual variable $w$ under an augmented Lagrangian → embeddings whose predicted scores $UV^\top$ trade off nDCG against exposure equality, controlled by a single weight $\lambda_{ex}$.
  • Why it was created: prior fairness-aware rankers (doubly-stochastic-matrix optimization, Frank–Wolfe, FairRec, Plackett–Luce + SGD) all carry an $O(|U||V|)$ time or space factor and are infeasible at millions of users/items; vanilla iALS scales but has no fairness knob.
  • Concrete example: on MSD (571K users, 41K items, 33.6M interactions), exADMM trains in 1,533 s vs. iALS's 858 s, while Multi-FR and FairRec are infeasible (NA) on that dataset.
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the paper's core contribution — Algorithm 1, with per-step complexity $O(\text{nz}(R)d^2 + |U|d^2 + |V|d^3)$, lower than iALS's $O(\text{nz}(R)d^2 + (|U|+|V|)d^3)$.

Second-moment exposure regularizer $R_{ex}$

  • What it computes: predicted score matrix $UV^\top$ → mean predicted score per item averaged over users, then L2 norm over items → $R_{ex}(V,U) = \frac{1}{|V|}\left\|\frac{1}{|U|}VU^\top\mathbb{1}\right\|_2^2$, a scalar penalty that is large when some items' average scores are extreme.
  • Why it was created: exact exposure metrics (Gini over ranked positions) are non-smooth and non-separable; this proxy exploits the monotonic link between an item's predicted score and the exposure it receives in ranked lists, while staying quadratic.
  • Concrete example: setting $\lambda_{ex}$ from 0 → 3e−6 on ML-20M moves the Lorenz curve of cumulative item exposure monotonically toward equality (Figure 2).
  • How it is used in this paper: added to the iALS objective as $L(V,U) + \lambda_{ex} R_{ex}(V,U)$; it introduces the structural dependency across all users that the ADMM machinery then decouples.

Separability-restoring ADMM splitting (auxiliary $s$ + proximal-gradient U-step + Sherman–Morrison proximal map)

  • What it computes: the non-separable term $\frac{1}{|U|}U^\top\mathbb{1}$ is replaced by an auxiliary $s \in \mathbb{R}^d$ with equality constraint; the U-update linearizes the loss (proximal gradient), and the resulting $|U|\times|U|$ inverse in the proximal map collapses via Sherman–Morrison to a rank-one correction computable in $O(|U|d)$.
  • Why it was created: the fairness regularizer couples all rows of $U$, destroying the row-wise independence that gives iALS its parallelism; naive proximal mapping costs $O(|U|^3)$.
  • Concrete example: $\text{prox}_\gamma^k(U) = \left(\frac{-1}{|U|^2(1/|U|+1/\rho\gamma)}\mathbb{1}\mathbb{1}^\top + I\right)\left(U + \frac{\rho\gamma}{|U|}\mathbb{1}(s^k - w^k)^\top\right)$ — three parallelizable passes over user rows.
  • How it is used in this paper: Sections 3.2.1–3.2.3; this is what makes the fairness-regularized objective run at iALS-like cost.

iALS (implicit alternating least squares) — background method, defined here as used: alternating row-wise ridge solves on the implicit-feedback MF objective with Gramian trick, $O(\text{nz}(R)d^2 + (|U|+|V|)d^3)$ per epoch. Standard prior work (Hu et al. 2008; Rendle et al. 2021), summarized in §2 of the paper.

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: implicit-feedback dataset; C++ (built on Rendle et al.'s public iALS implementation); CPU-parallel; $d$ = 32–512, $T$ = 50 epochs; hyperparameters $\{\lambda_{L2}, \alpha_0, \lambda_{ex}, \rho, \gamma\}$ with the scale-reparametrization $\lambda_{ex} = \lambda_{ex}^* |U|^2$.
  • Code: available at https://github.com/riktor/exADMM-recommender
  • Modifications: to serve SP4, exposure would need to be measured on realized rankings over time, not on score averages; extending beyond MF (e.g., to two-tower or LTR scorers) breaks the closed-form ridge steps.
  • Unknowns: missing link: sensitivity of convergence to the bounded-iterates assumption in practice; missing link: how the score-level regularizer behaves when score→exposure monotonicity is broken by downstream re-ranking.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Saito & Joachims, "Fair Ranking as Fair Division: Impact-Based Individual Fairness in Ranking" (KDD 2022) — the paper's own named future direction (scalable impact-based fairness); relevant to SP4's exposure-vs-impact distinction.
  • Patro et al., "FairRec" (WWW 2020) — main post-processing baseline; the in- vs. post-processing tradeoff regimes found here (high-accuracy vs. strong-fairness) are directly relevant to SP4.
  • Do & Usunier, "Optimizing generalized Gini indices for fairness in rankings" (SIGIR 2022) — the exact-Gini optimization line that exADMM's second-moment proxy approximates; candidate for quantifying the proxy gap.
  • Rendle et al., "Revisiting the Performance of iALS on Item Recommendation Benchmarks" (2021) — the scalable backbone; needed to reproduce exADMM.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = binary implicit-feedback matrix (users × items), outputs = MF embeddings scoring all items per user for top-K ranking; conditions = static offline training, exposure measured post-hoc by position-discounted Gini@K, fairness handled in-processing as a differentiable score-level regularizer. Our setting (SP4 — exposure frequency as ranking proxy): we care about how item exposure frequency functions as, or distorts, a proxy signal in ranking pipelines — i.e., the relationship between an item's exposure and its inferred relevance/rank quality. Differences:

  • Paper controls exposure inequality as an optimization target; our SP treats exposure frequency as a measurement/proxy problem — resolved: the paper's score↔exposure monotonicity argument (§3.1) is directly usable as the formal bridge from model scores to expected exposure.
  • Fairness proxy operates on predicted scores, never on realized ranked exposure — missing link: no bound relating score second-moment to Gini@K of actual rankings.
  • Static one-shot training, no feedback loop — missing link: exposure→interaction→retraining dynamics (popularity-bias amplification) are out of scope here; the paper cites but does not model them.
  • Implicit-feedback CF only (MF embeddings) — reference only: LTR / LLM-ranker settings in other SP4 papers need re-derivation of the separability machinery.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the scalability problem is solved at its root (the regularizer's destruction of subproblem separability is analyzed exactly and repaired with ADMM splitting + Sherman–Morrison, with a convergence proof), but the fairness side is a symptom-level proxy: it flattens the second moment of predicted scores rather than guaranteeing anything about realized position-based exposure, leaving the proxy-to-metric gap unaddressed.
