CRAB: Codebook Rebalancing for Bias Mitigation in Generative Recommendation

Zezhong Fan et al. · arXiv · 2026

arXiv:2604.05113 · PDF · SP: SP2

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

생성형 추천의 codebook 수준에서 인기 편향이 각인됨을 보이고 codebook 재균형과 fine-tuning으로 완화하는 CRAB을 제안한다. white-box 접근이 필요하다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Generative recommenders (TIGER, MiniOneRec) not only inherit popularity bias from interaction data — they amplify it beyond traditional sequential models: vs SASRec, popular-item recommendation frequency rises by 6.7% (MOR) and 7.2% (TIGER) while unpopular-item frequency drops 3.8–4.1%. Existing LLM-recommendation debiasing methods operate only at the model level and miss the tokenization-level cause: semantically similar popular items collapse onto the same codebook token, whose accumulated frequency biases autoregressive generation toward everything mapped to that token. Balanced-codebook baselines cap items-per-token but ignore interaction-frequency-driven token popularity.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Two-stage post-hoc pipeline on a trained GeneRec model:

  1. Codebook Rebalancing: identify over-popular tokens (top of the $P(c_k^l)$ ranking, Eq. 3); split each into $M$ new tokens by regularized K-means over child-token mean residuals with a popularity-balance penalty (Eq. 5–6), keeping child tokens and the tree structure intact. An Eq. 8 variant handles RQ-VAE codebooks where children can have multiple parents.
  2. Hierarchical Semantic Alignment: fine-tune token embeddings (plus LoRA on attention layers) with $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{Rec} + \gamma \mathcal{L}_T$, where $\mathcal{L}_T$ pulls sibling-token embeddings toward their parent-group mean (Eq. 9).
Key Results주요 결과
  • Industrial dataset: CRAB reduces MGU@10 by 16.5% vs MOR (0.091 vs 0.109) and 14.2% vs D2LR; DGU@10 down 14.8% vs MOR (0.356 vs 0.418) — while HR@10 (0.152) and NDCG@10 (0.117) match or slightly beat MOR (0.152 / 0.116)
  • Motivating analysis: items under top-5% popular tokens get 1.8× the exposure of SASRec; GU gap between popular-token and unpopular-token item groups reaches 0.42 on MOR
  • Efficiency: 0.28h training vs 3.11h (Reweighting) and 2.75h (D2LR) on Industrial
  • Splitting-position ablation: splitting the middle codebook level (B) helps most — intermediate tokens concentrate excessive semantic information ("Hourglass" phenomenon); splitting the last level hurts
  • Splitting ratio sweet spot below 10%; over-splitting degrades semantic integrity and performance

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Semantic ID (SID) / residual quantization tokenization

  • What it computes: item text description → frozen encoder embedding $z_i$ → $L$ rounds of nearest-codeword lookup with residual update ($s_i^l = \arg\min_k \|r_i^l - c_k^l\|^2$, $r_i^{l+1} = r_i^l - c_{s_i^l}^l$) → a tuple of $L$ discrete tokens identifying the item
  • Why it was created: lets a sequence model recommend over a compact token vocabulary instead of a huge item-ID vocabulary (efficiency, cold-start adaptation)
  • Concrete example: a 3-level codebook maps an office product to a token triple like ⟨A_3, B_5, C_1⟩
  • How it is used in this paper: the substrate whose imbalance CRAB diagnoses and rebalances (RQ-KMeans and RQ-VAE variants both handled)

Token popularity score $P(c_k^l)$

  • What it computes: sum of training-set interaction frequencies $f_i$ over all items whose $l$-th token is $c_k^l$ (Eq. 3)
  • Why it was created: prior debiasing work measured bias at item/model level; this score localizes bias to individual codebook tokens
  • Concrete example: top 5% of tokens by this score ($T_{pop}$) vs bottom 5% ($T_{unp}$); on MOR, the Group Unfairness gap between their item groups reaches 0.42
  • How it is used in this paper: the diagnostic quantity for identifying which tokens to split, and the quantity the balanced loss $\mathcal{L}_{bal}$ equalizes across new tokens

Codebook rebalancing via regularized K-means split

  • What it computes: an over-popular parent token → redistribution of its child tokens into $M$ new parent tokens, minimizing weighted residual variance plus $\lambda \mathcal{L}_{bal} = \lambda \sum_m (P(c_{k(m)}^l) - \bar{P})^2$ (Eq. 6), with the hard constraint that each child token stays intact
  • Why it was created: prior balanced-codebook methods only cap the item count per token; they ignore popularity accumulated from historical interaction frequency
  • Concrete example: 10% splitting ratio per level, $M \le 3$ new tokens, $M$ set by the target token's frequency over the level average
  • How it is used in this paper: stage 1 of the two-stage post-hoc pipeline

Hierarchical semantic alignment regularizer $\mathcal{L}_T$

  • What it computes: for every parent token, the mean squared distance of its children's LLM embeddings from their mean (Eq. 9); added to the recommendation loss as $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{Rec} + \gamma \mathcal{L}_T$
  • Why it was created: newly split and unpopular tokens have weak representations; sibling supervision transfers signal to them
  • Concrete example: $\gamma = 0.2$ is the operating point; NDCG drops sharply for $\gamma > 0.2$
  • How it is used in this paper: stage 2, fine-tuning with LoRA (r=8) on the rebalanced codebook

Group Unfairness (GU) — defined in prior work (Jiang et al., WWW 2024): discrepancy between recommendation exposure and historical interaction frequency; used here as the bias-amplification metric (MGU@K, DGU@K). [SKIM - exact formula in the cited prior paper, not restated here]

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = user interaction histories tokenized into hierarchical semantic IDs; outputs = next-item token sequences from an autoregressive LLM; conditions = e-commerce sequential recommendation, post-hoc access to a trained model and its codebook, item frequency statistics available. Our setting (SP2): analyzing exposure frequency as a distorted proxy for relevance/ranking quality — how the ranking/generation mechanism itself reshapes the exposure distribution relative to underlying preference. Differences:

  • Paper localizes exposure distortion to a discrete tokenization artifact (token popularity), giving a concrete mechanism for why generated exposure diverges from interaction frequency: resolved — directly usable as the mechanism evidence SP2 needs for the generative-retrieval case
  • Paper's bias metric (GU: exposure minus historical frequency) treats historical interaction frequency as the fairness reference, whereas SP2 questions whether frequency itself is a valid proxy: missing link — CRAB debiases toward historical frequency, not toward true relevance
  • Post-hoc intervention on a specific architecture (RQ codebooks + LLM); does not apply to dense-retrieval or classical ranking pipelines: reference only
  • Private industrial dataset for the headline numbers: missing link — magnitudes not independently checkable [SKIM - verify Office-only numbers suffice]
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — it directly identifies and structurally fixes the codebook-level amplification mechanism (over-popular tokens are split, not merely down-weighted at inference), but the deeper source it debiases toward is still historical interaction frequency, so the item-level popularity skew in the data — and the question of whether frequency reflects true relevance — remains untouched.
