Frequency capping in online advertising

Buchbinder et al. · Journal of Scheduling · 2011 · 31 citations

· SP: SP7

P4Track C

한 줄 요약

frequency capping 하 온라인 광고 배분의 경쟁비 이론(Journal of Scheduling 2011). 구조화된 경우 3/4(tight), 일반 1−1/e — 결정론적 exposure 상한(cap)의 예측 가능 비용 보장.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Online ad-impression allocation with per-user frequency caps: $n$ advertisers, each with total demand $d_i$, per-unit value $v_i$, and a cap $f_i$ on units associated with any single user. Supply units arrive online (each tagged with a user) and must be irrevocably allocated. Prior online allocation models (online matching, AdWords, Goel–Mehta) had no mechanism for the per-user repetition constraint that real ad systems enforce via frequency capping — the goal is value-maximizing allocation under this constraint with worst-case guarantees.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Competitive analysis of the frequency-capped online allocation problem (from abstract; proofs not read at skim depth):

  1. Hardness: no deterministic online algorithm beats 3/4-competitive, even when all $f_i = 1$ and all advertisers have identical values and demands.
  2. General case: a reduction to the Goel–Mehta (WINE 2007) model gives a ratio approaching $1 - 1/e$.
  3. Main contribution: two 3/4-competitive greedy algorithms — one for equal values, one for arbitrary values with equal integral demand-to-frequency-cap ratios ($d_i / f_i$) — matching the deterministic upper bound in those cases.
  4. A primal-dual algorithm proposed as a route to improving on $1 - 1/e$ in the general case. [SKIM - verify construction and its proven ratio in deep read]

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Frequency cap ($f_i$)

  • What it computes: per-advertiser constraint — advertiser $a_i$ accepts at most $f_i$ supply units (impressions) associated with any single user; input = (unit, user id, advertiser), operation = count check against $f_i$, output = allocation feasibility.
  • Why it was created: models the industry practice of limiting how often one user sees the same ad; prior online allocation/AdWords models constrained only total demand $d_i$, not per-user repetition.
  • Concrete example: $f_i = 1$ means advertiser $i$ takes at most one impression per user — the paper shows the hardness (3/4 deterministic upper bound) holds even in this unit-cap case with identical values and demands.
  • How it is used in this paper: the defining constraint of the online allocation problem; all competitive-ratio results are parameterized by the relationship between $d_i$, $v_i$, and $f_i$.

Competitive ratio (online allocation)

  • What it computes: worst-case ratio of an online algorithm's total allocated value to the offline-optimal value over all arrival sequences.
  • Why it was created: standard yardstick for online algorithms where the future (supply-unit arrivals) is unknown.
  • Concrete example: the paper's greedy algorithms achieve 3/4; the general-case reduction achieves $1 - 1/e \approx 0.632$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the sole evaluation criterion — this is a pure theory paper with proofs, not experiments.

Primal-dual online algorithm [SKIM - verify in deep read]

  • What it computes: maintains a feasible dual (LP) solution alongside the online primal allocation; dual variables guide each unit's assignment and certify the competitive ratio.
  • Why it was created: a general framework (Buchbinder–Naor line of work) for deriving and analyzing online algorithms beyond ad-hoc greedy arguments.
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - the specific dual construction requires the full paper]
  • How it is used in this paper: given as a proposed starting point for improving beyond $1 - 1/e$ in the general case.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = online stream of supply units (impressions) each tagged with a user; outputs = irrevocable unit→advertiser assignments; conditions = known per-advertiser $(d_i, v_i, f_i)$, adversarial arrival order, value maximization, worst-case (competitive) analysis, no learning/estimation. Our setting (SP7, exposure frequency as a ranking proxy): recommendation/ranking systems where item exposure frequency is measured, capped, or used as a proxy signal for relevance/fairness; stochastic user behavior, learned value estimates, and repeated-exposure effects on user response. Differences:

  • Frequency cap as a hard feasibility constraint on allocation vs. exposure frequency as a measured proxy/regularization signal in ranking: reference only — the paper gives the allocation-theoretic root of the frequency-cap concept, not a proxy methodology.
  • Advertiser-side values $v_i$ known and fixed vs. our setting's learned/uncertain relevance estimates: missing link — no uncertainty or learning in this model.
  • Adversarial worst-case arrivals vs. stochastic user traffic in real recommenders: missing link — competitive ratios are pessimistic relative to stochastic performance.
  • Value independent of exposure count (a unit is worth $v_i$ regardless of how many the user already saw, up to the cap) vs. diminishing/changing user response with repeated exposure: missing link — the cap is the only repetition mechanism; no response model.
