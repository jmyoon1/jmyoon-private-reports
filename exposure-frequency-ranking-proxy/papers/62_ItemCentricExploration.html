Item-centric Exploration for Cold Start Problem

Wang et al. · RecSys 2025 Industry Track · 2025 · 2 citations

arXiv:2507.09423 · PDF · SP: SP6

P3Track B

한 줄 요약

Google production의 item 중심 cold-start 탐색(RecSys 2025 Industry). 의도적 과다 노출의 산업 사례이며, 더 적지만 더 잘 맞는 impression이 이긴다는 역전(관련성 표적 탐색이 무작위 슬롯 채움을 지배)을 보고한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Even with existing cold-start mechanisms (auxiliary data, meta-/transfer learning), a purely user-centric ranking objective yields suboptimal initial audience targeting for new items: the "best item for a user" argmax can permanently starve a long-tail item of its ideal audience, and the resulting weak early interaction statistics prevent the item from being picked up by the main exploitation system — worsening popularity bias in very large item spaces.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

A lightweight item-centric filtering control after the ranking stage of an exploration system. Filter condition:

$$p(S_+|u,i) < \mu_i - 2\sigma_i$$

where $p(S_+|u,i)$ comes from a multi-task ranking model's satisfaction head, and $(\mu_i, \sigma_i)$ are the posterior mean/std of the item's intrinsic satisfaction rate under a Beta-Bernoulli conjugate model updated online: posterior $B(\alpha_0+N_+,\ \beta_0+N-N_+)$ with $N$ impressions and $N_+$ satisfied interactions. Intuition: if this user's predicted satisfaction is significantly below what the item typically achieves, this user is the wrong audience — save the impression for a better-matched user. Requires low-latency aggregation of interaction counts because the posterior moves fast in the first few hundred impressions.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Live experiment on a large-scale short-video platform (YouTube Shorts, per author affiliation/bio): Satisfaction Metric 1 +50%, Satisfaction Metric 2 +40% on explored content vs. production control.
  • Exploration impressions −20% (system is more selective in matching audiences), i.e., quality gains came with less exploration traffic.
  • Recommendable item corpus +10% — more cold items graduate to being distributable despite fewer exploration impressions.
  • Satisfaction predictions are well-calibrated across bins except at very low rates, where miscalibration was empirically harmless to the control.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: item-centric control markedly improves cold-start targeting, satisfaction with explored content, and exploration efficiency; conceptual claim that item-centricity is the right paradigm for cold-start in large inventories.
  • Demonstrated results: one live A/B with large relative gains (+50%/+40%/−20%/+10%) but anonymized metrics, no error bars, no offline reproducibility.
  • Gap: the paradigm claim (item-centric > user-centric) is supported only by a filter that removes mismatches; no evidence is given that actively selecting optimal users (the full paradigm) works, and popularity-bias reduction is asserted via corpus growth rather than measured on an exposure-concentration metric (e.g., Gini). Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Item-centric recommendation

  • What it computes: for a given new item → search over candidate users → the optimal audience for that item (transpose of the usual "best item for this user" argmax over items).
  • Why it was created: in user-centric systems with huge, fast-growing inventories, per-user ranking can permanently hide a new item's ideal audience — a long-tail item may never win any user's top-k, so it never accumulates the interactions needed for broader distribution.
  • Concrete example: paper's 2x2 toy table (Fig. 1) — user-centric argmax per row assigns Item 1 to both users (scores 8 and 4), so Item 2 (scores 1, 3) never gets shown, even though user 2 is its relatively best audience.
  • How it is used in this paper: framing concept only; the realized system is a post-ranking filter, not a full item-centric retrieval.

Beta-posterior satisfaction-rate filter (μ_i − 2σ_i rule)

  • What it computes: input = predicted user-item satisfaction probability $p(S_+|u,i)$ from a multi-task ranking model's prediction head, plus the item's impression/satisfaction counts $(N, N_+)$ → operation = compute Beta posterior $B(\alpha_0+N_+, \beta_0+N-N_+)$ mean $\mu_i$ and std $\sigma_i$; filter the item from the candidate list iff $p(S_+|u,i) < \mu_i - 2\sigma_i$ → output = pass/filter decision per (user, item) exposure.
  • Why it was created: exposing a new item to a "wrong" audience wastes its scarce exploration impressions and depresses its early engagement statistics, blocking graduation to the main exploitation system.
  • Concrete example: $\mu_i = \frac{\alpha_0+N_+}{\alpha_0+\beta_0+N}$; σ_i shrinks with impressions (Fig. 3: converges within ~first few hundred impressions), so the filter is loose for brand-new items and tightens as evidence accumulates.
  • How it is used in this paper: the entire deployed method — an item-centric control inserted after the ranking stage of the exploration system.

User-corpus co-diverted experiment

  • What it computes: an A/B framework that diverts both users AND disjoint item sets into treatment/control, so corpus-level outcomes (growth of the recommendable item corpus) can be measured alongside user satisfaction metrics.
  • Why it was created: standard user-diverted A/B tests cannot apply distinct treatments to disjoint item sets, so they cannot measure item-side effects like corpus expansion.
  • Concrete example: used to attribute the +10% recommendable-corpus growth to the treatment.
  • How it is used in this paper: the online evaluation methodology in Section 3.1. [SKIM - the paper gives no design details beyond this description; likely elaborated in their citation [2]/[5] line of work]
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: impression + satisfaction event logs per item; a calibrated per-(user,item) satisfaction predictor; two Beta prior parameters; low-latency count aggregation if run online.
  • Code: not available (proprietary Google system).
  • Modifications: for offline SP6 analysis, replay a logged dataset (e.g., KuaiRand/MIND) computing Beta posteriors per item and simulating the μ−2σ filter; satisfaction label must be redefined from available feedback (clicks/watch-ratio).

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = live impression/satisfaction logs + a production multi-task ranker's satisfaction head; outputs = per-(user,item) exposure filter decisions; conditions = industrial short-video system, millions of new items, online Bayesian count aggregation, live A/B evaluation. Our setting (SP6, exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy survey): analyzing how exposure/impression frequency functions as a proxy signal in ranking and how exposure allocation feeds back into item popularity — offline/analytical rather than a live production loop. Differences:

  • Live production system with real-time count aggregation vs. our offline/analytical setting: reference only — we cannot rerun their experiment, but the μ−2σ filter is directly simulable from any impression log.
  • Their item quality signal is the satisfaction-per-impression rate — an exposure-normalized quantity, deliberately decoupled from raw exposure count: resolved — this is exactly the kind of exposure-frequency-robust proxy SP6 is looking for (quality estimate whose posterior tightens with exposure but whose mean is not exposure-count-driven).
  • Ground-truth "satisfaction" label definition is proprietary/unspecified: missing link.
  • Their gains are on anonymized internal metrics; no public benchmark comparability: missing link.

Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the paper correctly names a root cause (the user-centric argmax structurally hides the ideal audience of nascent items) but the deployed remedy is a post-ranking veto filter on bad matches, which prunes wrong exposures rather than actively performing the item-centric search for optimal users that the framing calls for; the authors themselves label it an "initial realization."
