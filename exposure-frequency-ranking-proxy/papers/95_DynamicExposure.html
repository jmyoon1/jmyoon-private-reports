Fairness of Exposure in Dynamic Recommendation

Mansoury et al. · arXiv · 2023

arXiv:2309.02322 · PDF · SP: SP8

P2Track C

한 줄 요약

동적(재학습 포함) 추천에서 exposure 공정성을 600 라운드 폐루프(re-rank→클릭→재학습)로 평가한 조사 내 첫 사례. 라운드별 memoryless 제약은 누적으로 실패하고, position 가중 누적 ledger로 예산을 정규화하는 DM^dynamic이 경험적으로 교정함을 보인다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Exposure-fairness mitigation methods are designed and evaluated in a static setting: one round of recommendations is re-ranked to satisfy a fairness constraint. In a deployed system operating over time — model retrained each round on feedback from its own (re-ranked) recommendations — a method that is fair per-round can still be unfair in the long run, because its constraint carries no memory of which items already accumulated exposure. Whether static mitigation survives the feedback loop was untested for the DM family.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Empirical finding + one-line method adaptation:

  1. Finding: repeatedly applying $DM^{static}$ (Eq. 3 constraint) in a 600-round simulated feedback loop fails to equalize cumulative exposure, even though it is fair within each round.
  2. Fix: make the target-exposure constraint history-aware: $C^{\le t}(i) = \lfloor C^t(i) / E^{\le t}(i) \rfloor$ (Eq. 4) — divide the static per-round target by the item's cumulative exposure to date, then run the same min-cost-flow re-ranking. Over-exposed items get throttled; under-exposed items get boosted.
Key Results주요 결과
  • nDCG per round: MF slightly above both DM variants — fairness re-ranking costs little ranking quality (Fig. 2; values ~0.02–0.05 range, no aggregate number given).
  • Cumulative aggregate diversity at $T=600$: $DM^{dynamic}$ ≈ 0.63 > $DM^{static}$ ≈ 0.53 > MF ≈ 0.36 (read from Fig. 3b).
  • Cumulative equality of exposure ($1 - $ Gini over $E^{\le t}$): $DM^{dynamic}$ ≈ 0.125 > $DM^{static}$ ≈ 0.11 > MF ≈ 0.04, with MF declining over rounds — direct evidence of bias amplification by the feedback loop (Fig. 4b).
  • Per-round metrics show only slight differences between the two DM variants; the gap appears in cumulative metrics — supporting the paper's core point that per-round evaluation hides long-run unfairness.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: static bias-mitigation methods "are unable to satisfy fairness of exposure for items in long run"; the adapted method achieves long-run exposure fairness while maintaining accuracy.
  • Demonstrated results: the claim is shown for exactly one method (DM), one recommender (MF), one dataset (ML-1M), one simulated click model.
  • Gap: the general claim about "existing bias mitigation methods" rests on a single-method case study; note also that even $DM^{dynamic}$'s cumulative equality-of-exposure tops out around 0.125 (Gini ≈ 0.875) — far from uniform, so "achieves exposure fairness" means "improves relative to baselines," not fairness in an absolute sense. Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Dynamic recommendation setting (as formalized here)

  • What it computes: at each round $t \in \{1,\dots,T\}$: generate top-$K$ lists $R^t_u$ for all users → simulate clicks with acceptance probability $prob(i|R^t_u) = e^{\alpha \cdot rank_i}$ ($\alpha = -0.5$) → append clicks to user profiles → retrain and recommend at round $t+1$.
  • Why it was created: static exposure-fairness studies analyze a single round; the feedback loop (biased recs → biased clicks → more biased recs) only appears when the model/data co-evolve over rounds.
  • Concrete example: MovieLens 1M, MF base recommender, long lists of 40 re-ranked to final lists of 10, simulated for $T = 600$ rounds via librec-auto.
  • How it is used in this paper: the simulation testbed for comparing static vs. dynamic bias mitigation (follows the protocol of Mansoury et al. 2020, "Feedback loop and bias amplification in recommender systems").

Cumulative exposure $E^{\le T}(i)$

  • What it computes: per-round exposure $E^t(i) = \sum_{u}\sum_{k=1}^{K} \mathbb{1}(i = R^t_u(k)) \cdot \frac{1}{\log_2(1+k)}$ (position-discounted, nDCG-style drop-off), summed over rounds: $E^{\le T}(i) = \sum_{t=1}^{T} E^t(i)$.
  • Why it was created: per-round exposure hides long-run concentration — a system can be fair every round yet give a few items nearly all cumulative exposure (their Fig. 1 toy example).
  • Concrete example: in the motivating example (8 items, 5 rounds, lists of 4), Alg1 is 50/50 group-fair each round yet piles all cumulative exposure on items $i_1, i_2, i_5, i_6$; items $i_3, i_4, i_7, i_8$ get zero.
  • How it is used in this paper: the quantity the dynamic constraint normalizes by, and the basis of the cumulative Gini-index evaluation.

$DM^{dynamic}$ (history-aware Discrepancy Minimization constraint)

  • What it computes: replaces the static per-item target exposure $C^t(i) = \lfloor nK / \sum_{i} \mathbb{1}(i \in \mathcal{L}) \rfloor$ with $C^{\le t}(i) = \lfloor C^t(i) / E^{\le t}(i) \rfloor$ — the round-$t$ exposure budget for item $i$ is inversely proportional to its cumulative exposure so far. Fed into the same min-cost-network-flow re-ranking of DM.
  • Why it was created: DM's constraint is memoryless — it redistributes each round's slots independently, so repeated application still concentrates cumulative exposure.
  • Concrete example: an item over-exposed up to round $t$ gets a smaller integer target at round $t$; an under-exposed item gets a larger one.
  • How it is used in this paper: the paper's sole methodological contribution; compared against $DM^{static}$ and plain MF over 600 simulated rounds.

Background concept reused: Discrepancy Minimization (DM) — post-processing re-ranker of Antikacioglu & Ravi (KDD 2017): builds a user-item bipartite graph over long recommendation lists, converts it to a flow network, and solves min-cost flow to pick a recommendation subgraph minimizing discrepancy from per-item target-exposure constraints while preserving accuracy.

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: MovieLens 1M (or any implicit/explicit feedback dataset), an MF implementation, a min-cost-flow solver for the DM re-ranker, librec-auto for the simulation loop; compute is modest (600 rounds × MF on ML-1M).
  • Code: not mentioned in the pages read [SKIM - check for repo in deep read]; librec-auto itself is public.
  • Modifications: replace the equality-of-exposure target with a merit- or relevance-proportional target; replace the rank-only click model with a relevance-dependent one; adapt the cumulative-normalization trick to whatever exposure-frequency proxy the SP studies.
  • Unknowns: missing link: sensitivity to $\alpha$ (click-model steepness) and to the floor/integer rounding in Eq. 4 when $E^{\le t}(i) < 1$; missing link: behavior when item catalog changes over rounds (cold-start items have $E^{\le t}=0$).

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = explicit-feedback user-item ratings (ML-1M), MF-generated candidate lists of 40 per user; outputs = re-ranked top-10 lists per user per round; conditions = offline simulation, 600 rounds, position-based exposure $1/\log_2(1+k)$, simulated clicks $e^{-0.5 \cdot rank}$, equality-of-exposure fairness target across all items. Our setting (SP8, exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy): exposure/impression frequency used as a signal or proxy inside ranking, where the feedback loop makes observed frequency endogenous to past ranking decisions; concern is reallocating impressions over time and de-confounding frequency from merit. Differences:

  • Fairness objective (equalize exposure) vs. our proxy-validity objective (frequency as merit signal): reference only — but the cumulative-exposure normalization $C^t(i)/E^{\le t}(i)$ is structurally the same move as debiasing a frequency proxy by its exposure history: resolved as a transferable mechanism.
  • Simulated click model with fixed rank-only acceptance (no item relevance in the click probability shown): missing link — real feedback depends on relevance, so amplification dynamics may differ.
  • Single-round retraining of MF on appended clicks vs. production-scale continual training: reference only.
  • Equality-of-exposure target (uniform over items) vs. merit-proportional exposure (Singh & Joachims): missing link — the paper's constraint assumes uniform is the goal; our setting needs merit-anchored targets.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the diagnosed root cause is that the static constraint is memoryless with respect to accumulated exposure, and the fix injects exactly that missing state ($E^{\le t}$) into the constraint, rather than patching the observed metric downstream; scope is narrow (one method, empirical only) but the mechanism targets the cause it names.
