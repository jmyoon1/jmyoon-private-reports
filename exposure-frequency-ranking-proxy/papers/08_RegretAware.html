Regret-aware Re-ranking for Guaranteeing Two-sided Fairness and Accuracy in Recommender Systems

Ye et al. · arXiv · 2025

arXiv:2504.14550 · PDF · SP: SP1

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

구간별 exposure 예산을 Talmud 규칙으로 배분하고 사용자별 효용 하한을 regret 기반 오목 만족으로 보장하려는 양측 공정 re-ranking(BankFair+)을 제안한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Provider-fair re-ranking methods that guarantee minimum provider exposure while maximizing average user accuracy (including the authors' own BankFair, CIKM 2024) concentrate the accuracy loss on a subset of users: on KuaiRand-1K, even on the best day, ~41% of users receive accuracy below the platform's minimum-accuracy threshold while the average sits above it. This individual unfairness worsens monotonically as the provider-fairness degree is raised (Fig. 1c), and no prior method jointly controls all three of {average accuracy, individual (per-user) accuracy fairness, provider exposure fairness}.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

BankFair+ = BankFair's Module 1 (bankruptcy/Talmud-rule allocation of per-interval minimum provider exposure under fluctuating traffic, unchanged) + a new Module 2 with two steps:

  1. Regret-aware satisfaction (Step 1): replace linear user utility with $Z(\pi_u) = q_{\pi_u} + 1 - \exp(-\delta(q_{\pi_u} - q^*_{\pi_u}))$, making per-user accuracy loss convexly expensive so the optimizer equalizes accuracy across users.
  2. Fuzzy-programming re-ranking (Step 2): express per-user satisfaction and provider unfairness as fuzzy goals with membership functions, reduce to $\max_X \frac{1-\lambda}{|U|}\sum_u Z'(\pi_u) + \lambda G'(\boldsymbol{e})$ subject to assignment constraints, then solve online via the Lagrangian dual and dual mirror descent on per-provider dual variables $\boldsymbol{\mu}_t$ (Algorithm 1): each arriving user's slate is $\arg\max_X \{\frac{1-\lambda}{|U|} Z'(\pi_{u_t}) - \boldsymbol{\mu}_t^\top A^\top X_t \boldsymbol{p}\}$, and $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ is updated from the gap between target and realized provider exposure.

Decision variable $X_{u,i,k} \in \{0,1\}$ places item $i$ at position $k$; exposure uses position bias $p(k) = 1/\log_2(k+1)$. Scores $s_{u,i}$ from the base ranker are consumed as-is — the base model is never modified.

Key Results주요 결과
  • KuaiRand-1K, NDCG@10 at matched provider fairness (Gini@10 < 0.1): BankFair+ NDCG is ≥ 0.2 higher than the best baseline (P-MMF); at ESP@10 = 80%, +0.18 NDCG over the best baseline.
  • Individual fairness at matched provider fairness (Gini = 0.1, ESP = 0.7): BankFair+ keeps MMR@K > 0.7 while no baseline exceeds 0.5.
  • Case study (best-accuracy config with ESP@10 ≥ 75%): MMR 0.741 (BankFair+) vs. 0.493 (BankFair), 0.5247 (Welf), 0.065 (CPFair).
  • Accuracy variance across users stays below 0.002 at all provider-fairness levels, which baselines reach only when provider fairness is poor (Gini > 0.2, ESP < 0.6).
  • Regret coefficient $\delta$↑ → MMR→1 and NDCG→max, but provider fairness degrades (ESP↓, Gini↑) — the three-way trade-off is explicit, tunable via $(\delta, \lambda, g_0)$.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: BankFair+ "guarantees" individual fairness, user accuracy, and provider fairness simultaneously; outperforms all baselines on all three axes.
  • Demonstrated: Pareto dominance over 7 baselines on 2 datasets (KuaiRand-1K: 933 users; Huawei-Video: 19,355 users) across K ∈ {5,10,20} and two base rankers (LightGCN, NeuMF).
  • Gap: "guarantee" is soft — fuzzy goals provide no hard per-user accuracy bound (the paper explicitly abandons hard constraints as infeasible), and no theoretical guarantee (e.g., regret bound for the online dual algorithm) is proven for individual fairness; the evidence is empirical Pareto frontiers only. Venue status is also unconfirmed (TOIS-format preprint with placeholder DOI; only the predecessor BankFair is published, at CIKM 2024). Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

BankFair / sequential bankruptcy exposure allocation

  • What it computes: given a total minimum-exposure requirement $\boldsymbol{m}_p$ per provider and fluctuating per-interval user traffic, it allocates a per-interval minimum exposure $\boldsymbol{M}_{p,n}$ using the Talmud rule of the bankruptcy problem (Eq. 10): remaining requirement $\hat{\boldsymbol{m}}_p(n)$ is split across future intervals proportionally-with-caps against a predicted demand vector.
  • Why it was created: fixed per-interval exposure constraints sacrifice user accuracy during low-traffic periods; borrowing "surplus fairness" from high-traffic intervals offsets deficits in low-traffic ones.
  • Concrete example: if $\hat{\boldsymbol{m}}_p(n) \le \mathbf{1}^\top \boldsymbol{D}_p(n)/2$, each interval gets $\min\{D_{p,i}(n)/2, \theta\}$ where $\theta$ balances the total to $\hat{\boldsymbol{m}}_p(n)$.
  • How it is used in this paper: Module 1 of BankFair+, carried over unchanged from the authors' prior CIKM 2024 paper (BankFair). This paper's novelty is entirely in Module 2.

Regret-rejoice satisfaction function (regret theory applied to re-ranking)

  • What it computes: user perceived satisfaction $Z(\pi_u) = q_{\pi_u} + 1 - \exp(-\delta(q_{\pi_u} - q^*_{\pi_u}))$ — the ranking quality $q_{\pi_u}$ (DCG) plus a concave penalty comparing the delivered list against the accuracy-maximal list $\pi^*_u$ (Eq. 13). $\delta > 0$ is the regret-avoidance coefficient.
  • Why it was created: prior re-ranking methods assume satisfaction is linear in accuracy (expected-utility view), so the optimizer happily concentrates the accuracy loss from fairness constraints on a few users. A concave (non-linear) satisfaction makes low-accuracy users disproportionately costly, forcing the loss to be spread evenly.
  • Concrete example: with $\delta$ large, dropping one user from NDCG 0.9→0.5 costs far more objective value than dropping four users 0.9→0.8; Fig. 8 shows MMR (min/max accuracy ratio) rising toward 1 as $\delta$ increases from 0 to 20.
  • How it is used in this paper: Step 1 of Module 2 — replaces the linear utility inside the re-ranking objective.

Fuzzy-programming re-ranking formulation

  • What it computes: replaces hard per-user accuracy constraints ($q_{\pi_u} \ge \phi\ \forall u$) with fuzzy goals $Z(q_{\pi_u}) \gtrsim Z(q^*_{\pi_u})$ and $G(\boldsymbol{e}) \lesssim G(\boldsymbol{e}^*)$, converted via membership functions (normalized satisfaction for users; a sigmoid on exposure variance for providers) into a single weighted objective $\max \frac{1-\lambda}{|U|}\sum_u Z'(\pi_u) + \lambda G'(\boldsymbol{e})$ (Theorem 1, Eq. 21–22).
  • Why it was created: one hard accuracy constraint per user makes the LP huge and frequently infeasible (constraints conflict with the provider-exposure constraint); fuzzy goals degrade gracefully instead of failing.
  • Concrete example: fairness membership $G'(\boldsymbol{e}) = 1 - 1/(1+\exp(-k(\mathrm{Var}(\boldsymbol{e}) - g_0/2)))$ — a smooth 1→0 transition around the "unacceptable unfairness" level $g_0$.
  • How it is used in this paper: Step 2 of Module 2; solved online per arriving user via Lagrangian dual + dual mirror descent (Theorem 2, Algorithm 1) [SKIM - proofs of Theorems 1–2 in appendix not verified line-by-line].

ESP@K / MMR@K metrics

  • What it computes: ESP = fraction of providers whose (position-decayed) cumulative exposure meets their minimum guarantee; MMR = min-over-users NDCG divided by max-over-users NDCG (individual-fairness measure).
  • Why it was created: ESP measures minimum-exposure-guarantee satisfaction directly; MMR exposes the accuracy disparity that average NDCG hides.
  • Concrete example: in the case study (KuaiRand-1K, ESP@10 ≥ 75%), MMR is 0.741 for BankFair+ vs. 0.493 (BankFair) and 0.065 (CPFair).
  • How it is used in this paper: primary evaluation axes alongside NDCG@K and Gini@K.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: base ranker scores (any model — tested with LightGCN, NeuMF), item→provider mapping, per-provider exposure targets, traffic history for Module 1's demand prediction; an assignment solver per arriving user + dual mirror descent (lightweight).
  • Code: not available (no repository link found in the paper).
  • Modifications: for a pure impression-frequency setting, keep the dual exposure-targeting loop (lines 20–23 of Algorithm 1) but restrict the action space from full slate permutation to include/exclude (cap/boost) decisions; hyperparameters $(\delta, \lambda, g_0, k, \beta)$ need retuning per platform.
  • Unknowns: missing link: per-user solve latency at industrial QPS; missing link: sensitivity of Module 1 to traffic-prediction error; missing link: whether the fuzzy relaxation ever violates the provider minimum exposure badly in adversarial traffic.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Ye et al., "Guaranteeing Accuracy and Fairness under Fluctuating User Traffic: A Bankruptcy-Inspired Re-ranking Approach" (CIKM 2024) — the predecessor (BankFair); Module 1 here is identical, and it is the published venue anchor for this line.
  • Xu et al., "P-MMF: Provider Max-min Fairness Re-ranking in Recommender System" (WWW 2023) — the strongest baseline; same online-dual exposure-allocation machinery under a max-min objective.
  • Xu et al., "FairSync: Ensuring Amortized Group Exposure in Distributed Recommendation Retrieval" (WWW 2024) — same "arbitrarily specified minimum exposure" setting, at the retrieval stage; relevant for extending exposure control beyond re-ranking.
  • Patro et al., "FairRec" (WWW 2020) and Wu et al., "TFROM" (SIGIR 2021) — canonical two-sided fairness baselines this paper measures against; core SP1 references for exposure-guarantee re-ranking.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = frozen preference scores $s_{u,i}$ from a base ranker + item-provider mapping + per-provider minimum exposure requirements; outputs = per-user top-K slates (position assignment $X_{u,i,k}$); conditions = online sequentially-arriving users, fluctuating traffic, exposure measured with position bias $p(k)=1/\log_2(k+1)$, provider-side fairness = minimum exposure guarantee, user-side = per-user accuracy equalization. Our setting (SP1): a black-box recommender whose scores cannot be transformed; the only lever is exposure frequency (impression capping/boosting) used as a proxy for controlling the induced ranking. Differences:

  • Score immutability: BankFair+ never modifies $s_{u,i}$; it only re-permutes slates → resolved: fits the black-box constraint exactly (re-ranking as exposure allocation over positions).
  • Intervention type: paper controls exposure via full slate re-ranking (position assignment), our framing is impression-frequency adjustment → resolved (partially): position-decayed exposure $\sum p(\sigma(i))$ is a frequency-like exposure budget, but the paper assumes it may reorder every slate, a stronger lever than pure frequency capping/boosting.
  • Objective: paper targets a specific two-sided fairness triple; SP1 is about exposure control as a general ranking-control proxy → reference only: the mechanism (dual-variable exposure targeting per provider, Algorithm 1 lines 20–23) transfers; the specific fairness objectives need not.
  • Scale/latency: per-user argmax over an integer assignment each arrival; feasibility at industrial scale asserted (Huawei dataset) but wall-clock cost not reported → missing link: runtime/scalability numbers.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the paper correctly identifies the mechanism behind individual unfairness (a linear-in-accuracy objective lets the optimizer dump fairness costs on a few users) and fixes that mechanism directly with a concave satisfaction function; however, the promised individual-accuracy guarantee is delivered only as a soft fuzzy goal with empirical Pareto evidence, so the hard-guarantee problem it frames in Eq. (3) is relaxed away rather than solved.
