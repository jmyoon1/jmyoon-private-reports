Unintended Consequences of Recommender System Interventions: Evidence from a Field Experiment

Luo et al. · arXiv · 2026

arXiv:2606.08265 · PDF · SP: SP7

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

무작위 필드 실험으로, 일시적 exposure 부스트가 production recommender를 지속적으로 재학습시켜 개입 목표를 반전시킴을 보인다(+14.75%, 지속). 정적 immutability가 동적으로는 성립하지 않음을 보인, 조사의 프레임을 바꾼 논문.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Platform content interventions on recommender systems (boosts, demotions, CSR campaigns) are evaluated as static nudges with short-term campaign metrics. This ignores that the recommender adaptively learns from the user behavior the intervention itself induces, so the evaluation misses persistent, system-level policy shifts — including ones that reverse the intervention's stated goal.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Empirical finding from a large-scale randomized field experiment on a major short-video platform. A "sleep reminder" campaign temporarily boosted ranking scores of celebrity-recorded sleep-reminder videos between 1–5 a.m. to reduce late-night usage. Instead, treated users' late-night usage rose 14.75% and overall engagement rose 2.18% during the campaign week, and the positive engagement effects persisted for weeks after the boost was removed. The authors explain this via a preference-discovery / forced-exploration mechanism: the boost exposed under-supplied content, users' responses revealed high latent demand, and the recommender's post-campaign policy update reweighted related content — the intervention effectively retrained the algorithm. A three-period conceptual model (inaction zone → exposure shock → post-campaign update) organizes the empirical tests: exposure verification, short-run and post-campaign treatment effects, category-level feed reallocation, conversion-rate patterns, and heterogeneity by baseline exposure. Key Results주요 결과
  • Sleep-reminder boost (1–5 a.m.) increased treated users' late-night usage by +14.75% during the campaign week (goal was a decrease).
  • Overall platform engagement rose +2.18% during the campaign week.
  • Positive engagement effects persisted for multiple weeks after the campaign ended, i.e., after the ranking boost was removed — the signature of a policy update rather than a transient nudge.
  • Supporting evidence chain: increased exposure → conversion patterns → downstream feed reallocation → heterogeneity by baseline exposure to the promoted content. [SKIM - specific numbers for these secondary results not yet extracted]

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Forced-exploration mechanism (intervention as exploration)

  • What it computes: a platform-run ranking-score boost for a content category → users are exposed to content the baseline policy would have filtered out → user responses to that content become new training signals → the recommender updates its post-campaign policy toward the category. Input: exogenous exposure shock; output: durable policy shift.
  • Why it was created: prior evaluations treat content interventions as static "nudges" and measure only immediate campaign metrics, ignoring that the recommender learns from intervention-induced behavior.
  • Concrete example: boosting celebrity "sleep reminder" videos between 1–5 a.m. increased treated users' late-night usage by 14.75% and overall engagement by 2.18%, with effects persisting for weeks after the boost ended.
  • How it is used in this paper: as the causal mechanism explaining why an engagement-reducing campaign paradoxically increased engagement; formalized in a three-period conceptual model (Appendix A).

Inaction zone

  • What it computes: a state of the recommender in which it under-supplies a content category, therefore observes too little informative user behavior about that category to justify a policy update — routine feedback cannot escape it. [SKIM - verify formal definition in Appendix A model]
  • Why it was created: explains why a temporary intervention has persistent effects — only an exogenous exposure shock generates the data needed to trigger the update.
  • Concrete example: pre-campaign, celebrity sleep-reminder-adjacent content was under-supplied to some users despite high latent demand; routine behavior never revealed that demand.
  • How it is used in this paper: period-1 state of the three-period framework (pre-campaign incumbent policy → campaign exposure shock → post-campaign policy update).

Embedding bias

  • What it computes: a misalignment between the system's prior beliefs about a user's preferences (its learned representation) and the user's true preferences. [SKIM - verify operationalization in deep read]
  • Why it was created: names the structural error that the forced-exploration data corrects; distinguishes this paper's "correction" story from prior feedback-loop work that emphasizes error propagation.
  • Concrete example: baseline system under-weights celebrity content for users who, once exposed, convert at high rates.
  • How it is used in this paper: the quantity the intervention-generated data corrects, allowing the algorithm to escape a suboptimal equilibrium and improve content–user matching.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: partnership with a platform running an adaptive recommender; randomized user-level assignment; granular exposure/engagement logs spanning pre-, during-, and post-campaign periods.
  • Code: not available (field experiment on proprietary platform data). [SKIM - check for replication materials in deep read]
  • Modifications: for our purposes this is evidence, not a method to run — the transferable artifact is the three-period framework (inaction zone → shock → update) as a template for reasoning about when exposure frequency stops being a valid preference proxy.
  • Unknowns: missing link — which algorithm family the platform's recommender uses and how fast its update cycle is (determines generality of the persistence result); missing link — magnitude thresholds for the inaction zone.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = randomized user-level assignment to a temporary ranking-score boost on one content category on a short-video platform; outputs = causal treatment effects on exposure, engagement, and post-campaign feed composition; conditions = proprietary platform logs, an adaptive production recommender, single intervention, single platform. Our setting (SP7 — exposure frequency as a ranking/preference proxy): analyzing when exposure frequency is a valid proxy for ranking quality or user preference, given that exposure is itself policy-determined. Differences:

  • The paper directly demonstrates that exposure is endogenous to the learning loop — intervention-induced exposure changes future policy, so exposure frequency at time t is not a stable preference readout: resolved — this is exactly the evidence SP7 needs that exposure–preference decoupling is not just simulation-based but field-experimentally documented.
  • The paper studies a deliberate platform intervention; our setting concerns organically arising exposure-frequency signals: missing link — whether routine (non-intervention) exposure variation triggers comparable policy updates is what the "inaction zone" concept suggests it does not; [SKIM - verify].
  • Production short-video recommender with proprietary logs vs. general ML research setting with public data: reference only — the finding transfers as evidence, the experimental apparatus does not.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상
