A Game-Theoretic Approach to Recommendation Systems with Strategic Content Providers

Ben-Porat & Tennenholtz · NeurIPS 2018 · 2018 · 88 citations

arXiv:1806.00955 · PDF · SP: SP9

P0Track C

한 줄 요약

전략적 콘텐츠 provider가 있는 추천을 게임 이론으로 분석한 NeurIPS 2018 논문. relevance-argmax는 어떤 완전(complete) 공정 mediator로도 순수 Nash 균형이 없으며, 공정성+안정성+효율을 동시에 만족하려면 불완전 표시(show/don't-show 무작위화, Shapley mediator)가 공리적으로 필요함을 증명한다 — 빈도 제어의 규범적 기초.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Recommendation systems with strategic content providers face instability: providers choose what content to offer based on how the RS allocates exposure, and under standard relevance-maximizing recommenders (show each user the most-relevant item), the induced game between providers may have no pure Nash equilibrium — providers cycle among content choices indefinitely. Prior fairness-aware RS work imposed static fairness constraints without modeling this strategic response at all.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Model recommendation as a game where provider payoff = expected number of displays (exposure). Main results:

  1. Impossibility (Thm 1): no mediator satisfying the fairness axioms F and stability S can be "Complete" (always display some item). Proven via a 3-user cyclic satisfaction matrix forcing any fair mediator into a Rock-Paper-Scissors-like game with no PNE.
  2. The Shapley mediator: set display probabilities to Shapley values of a per-user cooperative game with $v_i(\mathcal{C}) = \max_{j \in \mathcal{C}} \sigma_i(X_j)$. Total display probability to $u_i$ equals $\max_j \sigma_i(X_j)$ (< 1 in general — sometimes shows nothing).
  3. Stability (Thm 2): games under SM are exact potential games (reduction to congestion games), so a PNE always exists and any better-response dynamics converges.
  4. Tractability (Thm 3): closed-form $O(N)$ computation, despite Shapley values being intractable in general.
  5. Uniqueness (Thm 4): SM is the only mediator satisfying F + S + Efficiency ($\sum_j \mathbb{P}(\cdot=j) = \sigma_i(\boldsymbol{X})$).
Key Results주요 결과
  • No mediator satisfies fairness (F), stability (S), and Completeness simultaneously (Thm 1) — standard relevance-maximizing recommenders (TOP, BTL/softmax-style) admit no pure Nash equilibrium among strategic providers.
  • The Shapley mediator satisfies F and S, runs in $O(N)$ per user (Thms 2, 3), and is the unique mediator satisfying F + S + Efficiency (Thm 4).
  • Provider-welfare Price of Anarchy under SM: $PoA_{SM} \le \frac{2N-1}{N} < 2$, tight (Thm 5).
  • User Price of Anarchy: unbounded for SM with zero default utility (Prop 2) — but also unbounded for TOP and BTL (Prop 3), so no completeness-based alternative does better; with optimal-plain-content default ($\sigma_i(\emptyset)=1$), $UPoA_{SM} \le 4$ analytically, $\approx 1.76$ numerically.
  • Extends to personalized multi-item strategies (each provider selects up to $k_j$ items, per-user targeting) with all main theorems intact (Appendix C).
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: theoretical (mechanism design / game theory for RS) Context: builds on Shapley value (Shapley 1952), potential games (Monderer & Shapley 1996), congestion games (Rosenthal 1973); positions against fairness-in-ML and multi-stakeholder RS literature (Burke 2017, Zheng 2017); adversarial IR strand (Ben Basat et al. 2015, Raifer et al. 2017). Earliest game-theoretic treatment of RS with strategic providers in this survey's corpus — foundational ancestor of the Track-C strategic-provider papers. Correctness: full proofs in appendix (impossibility, potential-game reduction, uniqueness induction, PoA bounds); no experiments — purely theoretical, so no statistical-rigor concerns. Assumptions (known $\sigma_i$, finite item sets, exposure-linear payoffs) are strong but stated explicitly. Contributions: (1) impossibility theorem for fair+stable+complete recommenders; (2) the Shapley mediator with linear-time implementation and uniqueness characterization; (3) Price-of-Anarchy analysis for both provider welfare and user utility. Clarity: fully reproducible — Algorithm 1 is 5 lines; all proofs included.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: traditional RSs fail fairness+stability; SM is fair, stable, linear-time, and uniquely characterized; SM "is not inferior to traditional approaches" on user utility.
  • Demonstrated: all game-theoretic claims are proven, not just claimed. The user-utility claim is the softest: SM's UPoA is unbounded in the zero-default case (Prop 2), and "not inferior" rests on TOP/BTL also being unbounded — a symmetric-failure argument rather than a positive guarantee.
  • Gap: no empirical validation on any real recommendation data; whether real providers behave as payoff-maximizing item-selectors from a fixed finite catalog is untested. [SKIM - verify in deep read whether appendix adds empirical content — from full text scan, it does not]
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Recommendation game

  • What it computes: input = users $\mathcal{U}$ with satisfaction functions $\sigma_i:\mathcal{L}\to[0,1]$, players $[N]$ each choosing one item $X_j$ from a finite catalog $\mathcal{L}_j$; a mediator $\mathcal{M}$ maps a strategy profile $\boldsymbol{X}$ and user $u_i$ to a distribution over $[N]\cup\{\emptyset\}$ (which provider's item to display, or nothing) → output = provider payoff $\pi_j(\boldsymbol{X})=\sum_i \mathbb{P}(\mathcal{M}(\boldsymbol{X},u_i)=j)$, i.e. expected exposure count.
  • Why it was created: prior fairness-aware RS work treated fairness as a static constraint, ignoring that content providers change behavior in response to the recommendation policy.
  • Concrete example: 2 players, 3 users; player 1 chooses between items $l_1, l_2$; under the top-relevance mediator, player 1 deviates from the socially better item $l_2$ (user welfare 2.6) to $l_1$ (welfare 2.0) because it doubles her exposure from 1 to 2 displays.
  • How it is used in this paper: the formal model within which fairness (F), stability (S), completeness, and efficiency of mediators are defined and proven.

Shapley mediator (SM)

  • What it computes: input = a strategy profile and one user's satisfaction vector $(\sigma_i(X_1),\dots,\sigma_i(X_N))$ → for each provider $j$, sets display probability equal to $j$'s Shapley value in the induced cooperative game $v_i(\mathcal{C};\boldsymbol{X})=\max_{j\in\mathcal{C}}\sigma_i(X_j)$ → output via closed form $\mathbb{P}(\text{SM}=j)=\sum_{m=1}^{\rho_i^j}\frac{\sigma_i^m-\sigma_i^{m-1}}{N-m+1}$ (sorted satisfaction levels), computable in $O(N)$ time (Algorithm 1: draw $Y\sim U(0,1)$, show nothing if $Y>\max_j\sigma_i(X_j)$, else uniform among items with $\sigma_i\ge Y$).
  • Why it was created: Theorem 1 shows no mediator that always displays something ("Complete") can satisfy fairness + equilibrium existence; SM escapes the impossibility by making total display probability equal $\sigma_i(\boldsymbol{X})$ (sometimes showing nothing).
  • Concrete example: with satisfaction levels (0.8, 0.9) for user $u_1$, SM shows player 1 with probability $(0.8-0)/2 = 0.4$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the central proposed mechanism; proven fair, stable (exact potential game — via reduction to congestion games), and unique among fair+stable+efficient mediators.

Fairness axioms F (Null Player, Symmetry, User-Independence, Leader Monotonicity) — adaptation of Shapley's cooperative-game axioms to display probabilities; used to prove both the impossibility result and SM's uniqueness. [SKIM - deep read would detail each axiom's role in Theorem 4's induction]

User Price of Anarchy (UPoA) — ratio of best achievable user utility (any mediator, any profile) to worst-equilibrium user utility under the mediator in question; introduced to quantify what fair/stable exposure allocation costs the users.

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: only the satisfaction matrix $\sigma_i(l)$ per user-item pair; Algorithm 1 is ~5 lines, $O(N)$ per impression. Trivial compute.
  • Code: not available (no repo mentioned; but reimplementation from Algorithm 1 is immediate).
  • Modifications: for a modern ranking setting one needs (a) estimated relevance in place of exact $\sigma_i$, (b) extension from single-slot to slate/position-weighted exposure, (c) provider payoff models beyond raw display counts.
  • Unknowns: missing link: robustness of the equilibrium/uniqueness guarantees when $\sigma_i$ is noisy or learned; missing link: how SM interacts with position bias in ranked lists.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = known, exact user-satisfaction functions $\sigma_i \in [0,1]$ for every user-item pair; providers pick one item (or a small set) from a fixed finite catalog; mediator displays at most one item per user impression; provider payoff = expected display count (exposure IS the payoff, by assumption). Our setting (SP9, exposure/frequency as ranking proxy): exposure frequency arises from learned ranking policies over estimated relevance scores; providers produce/modify content continuously; ranked slates rather than single-slot display; provider utility may be clicks/revenue, not raw displays. Differences:

  • Exposure defined as payoff by construction (1 display = 1 unit) vs. exposure as an emergent proxy in our setting: reference only — the paper assumes the exposure-payoff link the SP interrogates, making it the clean theoretical baseline for O3's mechanism-design view.
  • Known satisfaction functions vs. estimated relevance from interaction data: missing link — SM's guarantees require exact $\sigma_i$; behavior under estimation noise is unaddressed.
  • Single-slot display vs. ranked lists / position effects: missing link — no position-bias or slate structure in the model.
  • Finite fixed item catalogs vs. continuous content-creation strategy spaces: missing link here; resolved by successor exposure-game papers (Ben-Porat et al. follow-ups; Jagadeesan et al. supplier competition) that move to continuous content spaces.
  • Displaying nothing as a mechanism requirement: reference only — commercially awkward but theoretically necessary; a key structural insight (fairness+stability forces incomplete display).
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper identifies the structural root cause of provider-side instability (any complete, fair display rule creates payoff cycles) via an impossibility theorem, and resolves it with a uniquely-characterized mechanism rather than patching the observable symptom.
