Fairness of Exposure in Rankings

Singh & Joachims · KDD 2018 · 2018 · 685 citations

arXiv:1802.07281 · PDF · SP: SP1 · SP3 · SP4 · SP5 · SP8 · SP9

P0Track A

한 줄 요약

ranking이 배분하는 희소 자원은 슬롯 수가 아니라 attention이라는 관점에서 exposure를 공정성의 대상으로 정의하고, doubly stochastic 행렬 위의 LP와 Birkhoff-von Neumann 분해로 exposure 제약을 만족하는 확률적 ranking을 구성한다. 이 분야 전체의 기원이 되는 논문이다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

The Probability Ranking Principle (sort by probability of relevance) maximizes user utility but induces a winner-take-all allocation of exposure to the ranked items: because position bias decays steeply (e.g. $1/\log(1+j)$), an arbitrarily small relevance difference between groups produces a large exposure difference (0.03 relevance gap → 0.32 exposure gap in the running example). Prior fairness-in-ranking work (Yang & Stoyanovich 2017; Zehlike et al. FA*IR; Celis et al. 2017) only expresses parity constraints on the count of protected-group items in ranking prefixes, which neither models attention/exposure directly nor links exposure to relevance or economic impact.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

A framework: (1) generalize ranking to probabilistic rankings represented as doubly stochastic matrices $P$ ($P_{i,j}$ = prob. of doc $i$ at rank $j$), on which expected utility is linear: $U(P|q) = u^T P v$; (2) express fairness as linear constraints $f^T P g = h$ (e.g. demographic parity: $f_i = \frac{\mathbb{1}_{d_i \in G_0}}{|G_0|} - \frac{\mathbb{1}_{d_i \in G_1}}{|G_1|}$, $g = v$, $h = 0$); (3) solve the LP $\max_P u^T P v$ s.t. $P$ doubly stochastic + fairness constraints; (4) recover an implementable distribution over rankings via Birkhoff-von Neumann decomposition and sample. Sampled rankings maximize expected utility while satisfying the fairness constraint in expectation. PRP is recovered as the special case with no fairness constraint.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Job-seeker example ($N=6$): demographic-parity-fair ranking DCG 3.8031 vs. unconstrained PRP 3.8193 (−0.4%); the fair $P$ decomposes into 2 permutations at weight 0.5 each.
  • Disparate treatment: unconstrained PRP has DTR 1.7483; constrained solution reaches DTR 1.0000 at DCG 3.8044 (vs. 3.8193).
  • Disparate impact: unconstrained PRP has DIR 1.8193; constrained solution reaches DIR 1.0000 at DCG 3.8025.
  • Yow news dataset ($N=25$): disparate-treatment constraint nearly free (DCG 5.1983 vs. 5.2027) because the unconstrained ranking was already almost DTR-fair; demographic parity costs more (DCG 5.1360).
  • Cost-of-fairness is constraint-dependent and unbounded for demographic parity: utility of the fair ranking approaches zero if all relevant documents are in one group and the other group grows — parity ignores relevance entirely.
  • Feasibility caveat: the disparate-treatment LP is infeasible when the group utility ratio lies outside the exposure ratio range achievable by any $P$ (explicit bound derived in §5).
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: new method (conceptual + computational framework). Context: builds on the Probability Ranking Principle (Robertson 1977), supervised-learning fairness notions (Dwork et al. individual fairness; Hardt et al. equalized odds; Calders et al. demographic parity), position-bias/DCG measurement, and Recommendations-as-Treatments (Schnabel et al. 2016). Concurrent with Biega et al. "Equity of Attention" (individual amortized fairness — a special case of this framework with groups of size 1, solved by ILP instead of LP+BvN). Foundational Track-A paper for this survey: it is the origin of "exposure" as the quantity fairness/allocation arguments are made over. Correctness: LP optimality and BvN feasibility guarantees are standard and sound; constraints hold in expectation, not per-realization. Experiments are illustrative (one toy 6-item example, one small Yow-news subset), no error bars. Assumes true relevances $u(d|q)$ are known; the paper acknowledges estimated relevances carry their own biases. [SKIM — no issues spotted, but appendix-level detail not verified.] Contributions: (1) exposure allocation as the object of fairness in rankings, with position bias as the exposure model; (2) LP-over-doubly-stochastic-matrices + BvN sampling algorithm with provable utility-optimality under linear fairness constraints; (3) demonstration that demographic parity, disparate treatment (DTR), and disparate impact (DIR) are all expressible as linear constraints. Clarity: reproducible from the paper alone — the LP, constraint vectors, and BvN step are fully specified, and the BvN implementation used is cited (github.com/jfinkels/birkhoff).

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장

[SKIM - verify in deep read] Preliminary observation: the framework's generality claim ("broad range of fairness constraints") is demonstrated only for three constraint types on two small ranking problems (6 items, 25 items); scalability of the $N^2$-variable LP and BvN to production-size candidate sets is not evaluated. Constraints are satisfied in expectation only — per-user realized rankings can be arbitrarily unfair.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Exposure (of a document/item under a probabilistic ranking)

  • What it computes: probabilistic ranking (doubly stochastic matrix $P$, where $P_{i,j}$ = probability document $d_i$ is placed at rank $j$) + position-bias vector $v_j$ → weighted sum → $\text{Exposure}(d_i|P) = \sum_{j=1}^{N} P_{i,j} v_j$, the expected attention item $i$ receives.
  • Why it was created: prior fairness-in-ranking work used parity of item counts in ranking prefixes; this paper argues the scarce resource a ranking allocates is attention, which drops off steeply with position, so fairness must be defined on exposure, not slot counts.
  • Concrete example: with position bias $v_j = 1/\log(1+j)$ (the DCG discount), 3 male applicants (relevances 0.80/0.79/0.78) ranked above 3 female applicants (0.77/0.76/0.75) get 30% more exposure despite only a 0.03 average relevance gap (paper's Figure 1: 0.32 exposure difference from a 0.03 relevance difference).
  • How it is used in this paper: the quantity all three fairness constraints (§4) are defined over.

Probabilistic ranking via doubly stochastic matrix + LP formulation

  • What it computes: utility vector $u$ ($u_i$ = expected relevance of doc $i$), position vector $v$ → linear program $\max_P u^T P v$ subject to $P$ doubly stochastic and linear fairness constraints $f^T P g = h$ → optimal fair probabilistic ranking $P$.
  • Why it was created: rankings are combinatorial objects; searching them under constraints is exponential. Utility and exposure both depend only on the marginal rank distribution, so optimizing over doubly stochastic matrices ($N^2$ variables) makes the problem an efficiently solvable LP.
  • Concrete example: job-seeker example, $N=6$: unconstrained LP solution is the PRP permutation with DCG 3.8193; adding a demographic-parity constraint yields a stochastic $P$ with DCG 3.8031.
  • How it is used in this paper: the core computational framework (§3.3).

Birkhoff-von Neumann (BvN) decomposition for ranking sampling

  • What it computes: doubly stochastic matrix $P$ → decomposition $P = \sum_i \theta_i P_i$ into ≤ $(N-1)^2+1$ permutation matrices with convex weights → sample ranking $r_i$ with probability $\theta_i$.
  • Why it was created: an LP solution is a marginal-probability matrix, not a ranking; BvN converts it into a distribution over actual deterministic rankings that satisfies the fairness constraints in expectation.
  • Concrete example: the fair demographic-parity ranking in the job-seeker example decomposes into 2 permutations with weights 0.5/0.5 (Fig. 3).
  • How it is used in this paper: step 3–4 of the algorithm (§3.5); implementation from github.com/jfinkels/birkhoff.

Demographic Parity / Disparate Treatment / Disparate Impact constraints (ranking versions)

  • What it computes: each is a linear constraint $f^T P v = h$ on the LP. Demographic parity: equal average exposure per group. Disparate treatment: group exposure proportional to group average utility, measured by DTR $= \frac{\text{Exposure}(G_0|P)/U(G_0|q)}{\text{Exposure}(G_1|P)/U(G_1|q)}$. Disparate impact: expected group clickthrough rate (exposure × relevance under a simple examination click model) proportional to group average utility, measured by the analogous DIR.
  • Why it was created: transplants the three classic supervised-learning fairness notions to exposure allocation, going beyond the prefix-count parity constraints of prior ranking-fairness work.
  • Concrete example: job-seeker example, unconstrained PRP has DTR = 1.7483 and DIR = 1.8193; constrained solutions bring both to 1.0000 at DCG cost of only ~0.015 (3.8193 → 3.8044 / 3.8025).
  • How it is used in this paper: the three instantiations demonstrating expressiveness of the $f^T P g = h$ constraint language (§4.1–4.3).
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: relevance scores per item, a position-bias vector, group labels, an LP solver (interior point), and a BvN decomposition routine; $O(N^2)$ LP variables.
  • Code: no official release found in the paper; BvN component available at https://github.com/jfinkels/birkhoff. [SKIM - check for later reference implementations]
  • Modifications: for our setting, replace known relevances with estimates from a (debiased) LTR model; replace the DCG discount with empirically estimated exposure/position-bias values; scale beyond small $N$ likely requires the LP-free or amortized reformulations of follow-up work.
  • Unknowns: missing link: LP scalability to large candidate sets; missing link: behavior under noisy/estimated relevance (sensitivity of the fairness guarantee).
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Equity of Attention: Amortizing Individual Fairness in Rankings (Biega, Gummadi & Weikum, SIGIR 2018) — concurrent work; the individual-fairness, amortized-over-time counterpart (exposure proportional to relevance, groups of size 1); directly relevant to exposure-as-proxy over repeated rankings.
  • Policy Learning for Fairness in Ranking (Singh & Joachims, NeurIPS 2019) — same authors' follow-up that learns ranking policies with exposure-fairness objectives from data, removing the known-relevance assumption.
  • FA*IR: A Fair Top-k Ranking Algorithm (Zehlike et al., CIKM 2017) — the prefix-count-parity predecessor this framework generalizes beyond.
  • Unbiased Learning-to-Rank with Biased Feedback (Joachims, Swaminathan & Schnabel, WSDM 2017) — supplies the debiased relevance estimates this framework assumes as input; the bridge to click/frequency data.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = known true relevances $u(d|q)$ per document for a single query, known position-bias curve $v_j$, group memberships; outputs = distribution over rankings of $N$ items ($N \le 25$ in experiments); conditions = static single-query optimization, fairness in expectation, groups defined by a sensitive attribute. Our setting: exposure/frequency used as a proxy signal within ranking systems (survey topic: exposure-frequency-ranking-proxy) — [SKIM - verify against SP definitions] likely dynamic/feedback settings where exposure both measures and causes ranking outcomes. Differences:

  • True relevances assumed known vs. estimated (and feedback-loop-biased) relevance in our setting: missing link — paper explicitly defers to counterfactual LTR (Joachims et al. 2017) for unbiased estimates.
  • Static one-shot ranking vs. repeated/amortized exposure over sessions: reference only — amortized individual fairness is delegated to Biega et al. (concurrent work).
  • Position bias as a fixed known $v_j$ (DCG discount) vs. exposure measured from logged frequency: missing link — paper suggests intervention experiments to estimate $v_j$ but does not do so.
  • Group fairness vs. individual: resolved within the framework (groups of size one), though disparate-impact individual fairness is noted as ill-defined.
  • Expectation-level guarantee vs. per-realization guarantees: missing link.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper identifies the structural root cause (PRP's winner-take-all exposure allocation under steep position bias) and changes the allocation mechanism itself via constrained optimization over the exposure quantity, rather than post-hoc patching ranked lists; the remaining gaps (estimated relevances, expectation-only guarantees) are acknowledged, not obscured.
