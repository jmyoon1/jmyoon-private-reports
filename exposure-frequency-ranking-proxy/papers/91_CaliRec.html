Calibration-Disentangled Learning and Relevance-Prioritized Reranking for Calibrated Sequential Recommendation

Jeon et al. · CIKM 2024 · 2024

arXiv:2408.02156 · PDF · SP: SP8

P0Track B

한 줄 요약

calibration-분리 학습과 relevance 우선 re-ranking으로 보정된 순차 추천을 달성한다(CIKM 2024).

Problem해결하려는 문제

Calibrated sequential recommendation must keep the per-user category proportions of the recommendation list close to the (drifting) proportions in the interaction history, without hurting top-of-list relevance. Prior post-processing methods (CaliRec greedy, MIP, MCF) rerank a relevance-only model, so calibration adjustments scramble a ranking that was never trained to survive them; the end-to-end alternative DACSR optimizes calibration over the entire item set (for differentiability) rather than the top-$k$ that actually matters, and none of them resolves the direct conflict where a highly relevant item from a previously unexplored category is suppressed by its miscalibration penalty.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

LeapRec = two phases:

  1. Training: backbone sequential model (SASRec default) trained with $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{BPR} + \gamma\,\mathcal{L}_{CD\text{-}BPR}$ — the second term enforces the pairwise preference order after subtracting each item's miscalibration score, so the ranking is stable under later calibration adjustment (equations above).
  2. Reranking: greedy selection with the rank-dependent objective $(1-\lambda^{1/k}) r^u_{i,T+1} - \lambda^{1/k} \Delta S_{KL}(i|C^u)$ ("relevance priority property": higher ranks prioritize relevance, lower ranks calibration), using sequential (recency-weighted) miscalibration rather than static.
Key Results주요 결과
  • Consistently better accuracy–calibration trade-off curves (nDCG@10 / HR@10 vs. $S_{KL}@10$) than CaliRec, CaliRec+, MIP, MCF, DACSR on ML-1M, Goodreads, Grocery, Steam; Grocery annotations show +31.3% accuracy and ~98.4× calibration improvement at matched points [SKIM - exact anchor points of the annotated deltas need deep read].
  • LeapRec (base) beats plain SASRec (base) in accuracy before any reranking — the CD-BPR loss alone improves the backbone.
  • Reranking speed matches greedy CaliRec: 24s on ML-1M vs. MIP 1,793s and MCF 330s; 3,925s on Steam vs. MIP 92,245s (Table 3). Complexity $O(nkm)$ vs. MIP $O(nm^k)$, MCF $O(n(k+c)m^2\log m)$.
  • On Goodreads, accuracy increases with calibration weight up to $\lambda = 0.7$ (nDCG@10 0.0258 → 0.0266), i.e. calibration and accuracy are not strictly antagonistic under this scheme.
  • Ablations: removing CD training, or replacing $\lambda^{1/k}$ with uniform $\lambda$ or reversed $\lambda^k$, all degrade the trade-off; reversed (calibration-first at top ranks) degrades most.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Sequential miscalibration $S_{KL}(u)$

  • What it computes: user's interaction history $S^u$ + recommendation list $R^u$ → KL divergence $KL(p\|\tilde q)$ between (a) the recency-weighted category distribution of past interactions, $p(c|u) \propto \sum_t \alpha^{T-t} p(c|s^u_t)$ with decay $\alpha$ (e.g. 0.9), and (b) the smoothed category distribution of the recommended list, $\tilde q = (1-\beta)q + \beta p$ → a scalar miscalibration score (lower = better calibrated).
  • Why it was created: Steck's static miscalibration weights all past interactions equally, but Figure 1 of the paper shows category preferences drift over time (KL between windowed category distributions grows with sequence interval on ML-1M and Steam), so a sequential setting needs recency weighting.
  • Concrete example: with $\alpha=0.9$, an interaction 10 steps ago contributes weight $0.9^{10} \approx 0.35$ relative to the most recent one; $\beta=0.01$ smoothing keeps the KL finite.
  • How it is used in this paper: both as the calibration term inside the reranking objective and as the evaluation metric ($S_{KL}@10$).

Calibration-disentangled learning-to-rank loss ($\mathcal{L}_{CD\text{-}BPR}$)

  • What it computes: for each observed (positive $i$, negative $j$) pair at step $t$, a BPR-style loss on calibration-adjusted scores: $\mathcal{L}_{CD\text{-}BPR} = \sum -\log\sigma\big(r^u_{i,t} - KL(p^u_{t-1}\|p_i) - r^u_{j,t} + KL(p^u_{t-1}\|p_j)\big)$, combined as $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{BPR} + \gamma\,\mathcal{L}_{CD\text{-}BPR}$ ($\gamma = 0.1$).
  • Why it was created: pure post-hoc reranking degrades accuracy because the trained ranking $r^u_{i,t} > r^u_{j,t}$ gives no guarantee the preference order survives once a calibration penalty is subtracted; training on both orders makes rankings robust to later calibration adjustment. Adds no parameters (model-agnostic; backbone SASRec by default).
  • Concrete example: if item $i$'s categories diverge sharply from the user's recent category preference, the model is pushed to raise $r^u_{i,t}$ enough to compensate for its high $KL(p^u_{t-1}\|p_i)$ penalty.
  • How it is used in this paper: the training-phase half of LeapRec; ablation (Only CD-BPR vs. Only BPR vs. both) shows either loss alone substantially underperforms the combination.

Relevance-prioritized reranking (rank-dependent trade-off $\lambda^{1/k}$)

  • What it computes: greedy list construction; at position $k$ it selects $\arg\max_{i \notin C^u} (1-\lambda^{1/k})\, r^u_{i,T+1} - \lambda^{1/k}\, \Delta S_{KL}(i|C^u)$ — the position index $k$ modulates the balance so top ranks weight relevance more, bottom ranks weight calibration more ($\lambda \in [0,1]$, $\lambda^{1/k} \to$ larger as $k$ grows).
  • Why it was created: a uniform weighted sum (CaliRec/MIP/MCF style) can bury a highly relevant item from a new category because its miscalibration penalty is large — precisely the emerging-interest case sequential recommendation should catch.
  • Concrete example: case study (Grocery): at $\lambda = 0.3$ the ground-truth item "English breakfast tea" stays at rank 1 while Candy & Chocolate items enter lower ranks; $S_{KL}@10$ drops from 1.5920 ($\lambda=0$) to 0.1001.
  • How it is used in this paper: the second phase of LeapRec; greedy because exact optimization of the list objective is NP-hard; complexity $O(nkm)$, same as greedy CaliRec.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: standard sequential-rec stack (PyTorch, SASRec-scale model: embedding dim 50, 2 attention blocks), item category labels, public datasets used in the paper. Reranking is cheap ($O(nkm)$ greedy).
  • Code: available at https://github.com/jeon185/LeapRec
  • Modifications: for the survey's exposure-frequency setting, replace the per-user category distribution $p(c|u)$ with the target exposure distribution and $\Delta S_{KL}$ with the marginal exposure-divergence gain; the greedy $\lambda^{1/k}$ scaffold carries over unchanged.
  • Unknowns: missing link: sensitivity of CD-BPR to the scale mismatch between relevance scores and KL penalties (scores are unbounded, KL is not normalized); missing link: whether the trade-off gains hold when the calibration target is cross-user (exposure) rather than per-user.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Steck (2018), "Calibrated recommendations" (RecSys) — origin of the calibration-as-KL framing and the greedy baseline; foundational for SP8.
  • Abdollahpouri et al. (2023), "Calibrated Recommendations as a Minimum-Cost Flow Problem" (WSDM) — the strongest non-greedy post-hoc competitor; relevant as an alternative O2 mechanism.
  • Chen et al. (2022), "DACSR" (Applied Sciences) — the end-to-end alternative whose whole-item-set calibration limitation motivates this paper; useful contrast for training-time vs. rerank-time calibration on the O2 axis.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = per-user item interaction sequences + item category labels; outputs = top-$K$ ($K=10$) recommendation list per user; conditions = per-user calibration target defined as the recency-weighted category distribution of that user's own history; offline leave-one-out evaluation on ML-1M / Goodreads / Grocery / Steam. Our setting (SP8 / O2 axis): enforcing an exposure-frequency target distribution over items or providers at ranking time, where exposure frequency serves as the ranking proxy, while preserving the relevance order at top ranks. Differences:

  • Calibration target is a per-user category distribution, whereas our concern is item/provider exposure frequency aggregated over rankings: missing link — the KL machinery transfers, but the target distribution and the per-user vs. cross-user aggregation differ.
  • The rank-dependent $\lambda^{1/k}$ trade-off is exactly a score-preserving-at-top re-ranking template: resolved — directly reusable as the O2 mechanism (swap $S_{KL}$'s target for an exposure distribution).
  • Training-phase disentanglement assumes we control model training; a pure post-hoc exposure-calibration setting cannot use $\mathcal{L}_{CD\text{-}BPR}$: reference only for post-hoc-only pipelines.
  • Sequential (recency-weighted) target vs. typically static exposure quotas: resolved — set $\alpha = 1$ to recover the static case.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the CD-BPR training loss addresses the root cause of post-hoc reranking degradation (rankings were never trained to survive calibration adjustment), but the $\lambda^{1/k}$ position schedule is a heuristic patch on the relevance-vs-calibration conflict itself: it decides where in the list each objective wins rather than resolving when a calibration-driven swap is actually justified.
