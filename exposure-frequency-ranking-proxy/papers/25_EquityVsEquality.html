Equity vs. Equality: Optimizing Ranking Fairness for Tailored Provider Needs

Yiteng Tu et al. · arXiv · 2026

arXiv:2602.00495 · PDF · SP: SP2

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

provider마다 필요(need)가 다르다는 전제에서 equity(필요 비례)와 equality(균등)를 구분해 ranking 공정성 목표를 맞춤화한다. exposure=효용 가정에 대한 독립적 반례 계열.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Existing provider-side fairness in ranking is equality-based: it allocates exposure so that providers with similar content/relevance receive similar exposure (amortized exposure fairness), assuming a uniform exposure→utility relationship for all providers. Real providers have heterogeneous objectives — e.g., established brands prioritize sales/CVR while emerging brands prioritize visibility — so identical exposure does not translate into comparable benefit, and exposure-based fairness misrepresents what providers actually care about.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

[SKIM - verify] An equity-oriented fairness framework where each provider $g$ has explicit preference weights over two outcomes — exposure (examination gain $v^g_e$) and sales (purchase gain $v^g_b$) — and fairness is measured over need-weighted provider gains rather than raw exposure. The metric generalizes: it extends to additional utility dimensions and reduces to amortized exposure fairness as a special case. EquityRank is a gain-based, gradient-based ranking algorithm that jointly optimizes user-side effectiveness and this provider-side equity objective, evaluated in offline (known relevance) and online (dynamically inferred relevance) simulation.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Equity-oriented fairness metric (provider-need-weighted gain fairness)

  • What it computes: per-provider preference weights over two outcome types (exposure gain $v^g_e$ and sales gain $v^g_b$) + realized exposure/sales from rankings → aggregation into a per-provider "gain" $G$ → a fairness score measuring whether realized gains match each provider's individualized expected gain ratio $y^g$, rather than whether raw exposure is equalized. [SKIM - exact metric equation is in §4, not yet read]
  • Why it was created: amortized exposure fairness assumes every provider benefits identically per unit of exposure; in reality established brands prioritize conversion/sales while emerging providers prioritize visibility, so equal exposure does not mean equal utility.
  • Concrete example: a well-known brand with high CVR values a purchase event at high $v^g_b$ and exposure at low $v^g_e$; a new merchant weights the reverse — the metric scores each against its own preference vector. [SKIM - verify numeric instantiation in deep read]
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the optimization target of EquityRank and the evaluation metric in offline/online simulations; the paper states it recovers amortized exposure fairness as a special case (all providers' preference = pure exposure).

EquityRank

  • What it computes: candidate items + estimated relevance + provider preference profiles → gradient-based joint optimization of user-side ranking effectiveness and the equity metric → a top-$K$ ranked list per request. [SKIM - algorithm steps in §4/§5 not yet read]
  • Why it was created: all prior provider-fairness ranking algorithms (FairCo, MMF, PG-Rank, PL-Rank, MCFair, TFROM) optimize equality-oriented (exposure-equalizing) objectives; none optimizes heterogeneous provider needs.
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - check experiments for a worked trade-off number]
  • How it is used in this paper: the main proposed method, evaluated in offline (known relevance labels) and online (relevance inferred dynamically over time steps $T$) simulation settings on multiple large-scale recommender datasets.

Interaction hypothesis (examine-then-buy factorization)

  • What it computes: binary variables e (examined), r (relevant), b (bought) with $b = r$ if $e{=}1$ else $0$, giving $P(b{=}1) = P(r{=}1)P(e{=}1)$; examination probability is position-dependent ($p_{rank(\tau|\pi)}$, position bias) and zero for unshown items (selection bias).
  • Why it was created: standard click-model assumption (from Chuklin et al.) reused to link exposure allocation to sales outcomes so provider gain can be computed from rankings.
  • Concrete example: an item at rank 1 with $p_1 = 0.9$ and relevance $r = 0.5$ yields purchase probability $0.45$; the same item unshown yields $0$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the simulation model connecting ranking positions to the two provider outcomes (exposure and sales) that the equity metric aggregates.

Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = ranking requests over items owned by $m$ providers, each provider with a 2-dimensional preference vector (exposure gain, sales gain); outputs = top-$K$ rankings; conditions = e-commerce-style recommender simulation, position-bias click model, purchase = relevance × examination, offline (oracle relevance) and online (estimated relevance) modes. Our setting: exposure frequency used as a ranking/utility proxy — the survey (SP2) examines when exposure-based allocation is a valid proxy for the quantity actually being optimized. Differences:

  • The paper is direct evidence for SP2's premise: it argues exposure is a lossy proxy for provider utility and replaces it with need-weighted gain — reference only (it critiques the proxy rather than validating it).
  • Utility restricted to two outcomes (exposure, sales) linked by a fixed click model; our setting may involve outcomes not expressible as examination × relevance — missing link.
  • Provider preferences assumed known/explicit per provider; how they are elicited or estimated in practice — [SKIM - verify] missing link.
  • Simulation-only evaluation (no live deployment) — reference only.
