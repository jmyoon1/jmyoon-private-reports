Introducing the Expohedron for Efficient Pareto-optimal Fairness-Utility Amortizations in Repeated Rankings

Till Kletti et al. · WSDM 2022 · 2022 · 20 citations

arXiv:2202.03237 · PDF · SP: SP2 · SP4

P2Track C

한 줄 요약

expohedron — 위치 기반 모델 하에서 단일 ranking들이 도달 가능한 exposure 벡터의 볼록 폴리토프 — 을 도입해 Caratheodory 분해로 Pareto 최적 amortization을 O(n^2 log n)에 계산한다. balanced-word(stride) 전달로 실현 오차를 창 당 m=N-1로 결정론적으로 묶는, 조사 전체에서 유일한 open-loop 실현 메커니즘도 담고 있다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Amortized fairness of exposure in repeated rankings: given fixed relevance scores ρ, deliver a sequence of rankings whose average per-item exposure hits a merit-proportional target while losing as little consumer utility (nDCG) as possible. Prior work (Singh & Joachims 2018 and follow-ups) solves this with an LP over n² bistochastic-matrix variables plus a BvN decomposition — total complexity O(n⁵), empirically too slow at scale, recovering only a single point (the zero-unfairness endpoint) of the utility-fairness trade-off rather than the whole frontier, and yielding distributions over up to (n−1)²+1 rankings that are then delivered by slow-converging random sampling.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Replace the Birkhoff polytope with the expohedron Π(γ) = Conv{πγ | π ∈ Sₙ} — the convex hull of all item-exposure vectors achievable by rankings under a PBM. Three algorithmic pieces:

  1. Pareto front (Alg. 2): solve max_ℰ ρ⊤ℰ, min_ℰ ‖ℰ − ℰ*‖₂² s.t. ℰ ≺ γ geometrically — walk from the target exposure ℰ* in the direction of ρ, projecting onto each face hit until utility is maximal; the piecewise-linear path of at most n breakpoints is the entire Pareto curve. O(n² log n).
  2. Decomposition (Alg. 1): express any chosen Pareto point as a convex sum of at most n vertices (= rankings) via a GLS-style Carathéodory decomposition using the majorization membership test x ∈ Π(γ) ⟺ x ≺ γ. O(n² log n).
  3. Delivery: schedule the n rankings deterministically with an m-balanced-word generator (stride scheduling) instead of sampling, reducing variance of realized exposure at finite horizons.

Applicable to any merit that is a non-decreasing function of relevance, and any PBM exposure vector (DCG, RBP, ...).

Key Results주요 결과
  • Full Pareto front (Expo) computed faster than the LP baseline computes a single endpoint; at n=100, endpoint computation is near-instant (≈0.0014–0.0018 s per query) while BvN alone reaches ~70 s (Fig. 4, Table 1).
  • End-to-end (TREC 2020 Fairness Track, T=1000): Expo+GLS+BW = 0.155 s vs LP+BvN+rand = 2.39 s; on MSLR: 0.56 s vs 31.35 s — a ~15–56× speedup (Table 1).
  • Expo's Pareto points coincide with the QP oracle's provably Pareto-optimal points on both MSLR and TREC (Fig. 5); Plackett-Luce is dominated except near nDCG=1.
  • Balanced-word delivery converges to target exposure roughly an order of magnitude faster than random sampling (normalized unfairness ~10⁻⁴ vs ~10⁻³ at T=1000, Fig. 6).
  • The heuristic controller (Ctrl) is empirically near-Pareto-optimal and faster for horizons under a few thousand rankings, but has no optimality guarantee; Expo wins for T ≳ 10 000 (Table 1: Ctrl 0.22–0.25 s vs Expo total ~0.16–0.57 s at T=10 000).

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Expohedron (γ-expohedron)

  • What it computes: input = a PBM exposure vector γ ∈ R^n (e.g. DCG weights 1/log₂(k+1)); operation = take the convex hull of all n! permutations πγ of that vector; output = a polytope of dimension n−1 whose points are exactly the expected item-exposure vectors achievable by any distribution over rankings.
  • Why it was created: prior work (Singh & Joachims 2018) optimized over the n²-dimensional Birkhoff polytope of bistochastic matrices via LP (total O(n⁵)), which is too slow at scale; the expohedron reduces the search space to the n-dimensional exposure vectors that actually matter.
  • Concrete example: for n=3 items with DCG exposures (1/log₂2, 1/log₂3, 1/log₂4), the expohedron is a hexagon in a 2-D hyperplane of R³ whose 6 vertices are the exposures of the 6 possible rankings (Fig. 1b).
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the entire optimization domain — Pareto-front tracing (Alg. 2) and decomposition into rankings (Alg. 1) both operate inside it.

Carathéodory decomposition via GLS in the expohedron

  • What it computes: input = any target exposure vector x inside the expohedron; operation = repeatedly draw a half-line from a vertex through the point to the boundary (GLS method), using a majorization test (x ≺ γ iff x is inside) for boundary intersection; output = a convex combination of at most n vertices, i.e. a distribution over at most n rankings whose expected exposure equals x. Complexity O(n² log n).
  • Why it was created: the Birkhoff-von-Neumann decomposition used by prior work needs up to (n−1)²+1 permutation matrices and is empirically slow (~70 s at n=100); existing permutohedron decompositions do not extend to general γ.
  • Concrete example: at n=100 items, GLS decomposition takes ≈0.5–0.8 s vs ≈29–70 s for BvN (Table 1 / Fig. 4).
  • How it is used in this paper: converts each Pareto-optimal exposure vector into a deliverable ranking distribution.

Geometric Pareto-front tracing [SKIM - verify details in deep read]

  • What it computes: input = target exposure ℰ*, relevance vector ρ, exposure vector γ; operation = start at ℰ* and walk in the direction of ρ (projected onto successively lower-dimensional faces) until utility is maximal, computing each face intersection analytically via majorization prefix sums; output = at most n breakpoints whose connecting segments are the whole utility-unfairness Pareto curve. O(n² log n).
  • Why it was created: the LP approach recovers only one endpoint of the Pareto front (minimal unfairness) and is slower than this method's recovery of the entire front.
  • Concrete example: Fig. 3 shows the piecewise-linear Pareto curve for ρ=(0.55, 0.6, 0.65) inside the n=3 DCG expohedron.
  • How it is used in this paper: solves the two-objective problem max ρ⊤ℰ, min ‖ℰ−ℰ*‖² s.t. ℰ ≺ γ (Eq. 10).

Balanced-word delivery (m-balanced words / stride scheduling)

  • What it computes: input = a distribution over N rankings; operation = deterministic scheduling (argmin of accumulated inverse-density counters) generating a sequence where any ranking's frequency in equal-length substrings differs by at most m = N−1; output = a delivery sequence tracking target proportions much more tightly than i.i.d. sampling.
  • Why it was created: random sampling converges slowly to target exposure proportions at low time horizons (e.g. 10 fair coin flips give exactly 5/5 only ~25% of the time); n-support distributions permit m = n−1 balance vs (n−1)² for BvN-sized supports.
  • Concrete example: in Fig. 6, balanced-word delivery (Expo bw) reaches normalized unfairness ~10⁻⁴ by T=1000 while sampled delivery (Expo rand, LP rand) stays around 10⁻³.
  • How it is used in this paper: step 3 of the pipeline — delivering the computed distribution as an actual sequence of rankings.

Position Based Model (PBM) — standard click-model background: an item's exposure depends only on its rank, via the fixed vector γ. Included because the entire method's validity is conditional on it.

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: only relevance scores per query and a PBM exposure vector; NumPy-level linear algebra and sorting; laptop CPU sufficient (all experiments on an i7-8650U).
  • Code: available at https://github.com/tillkletti/expohedron.
  • Modifications: for our setting, swap the meritocratic target ℰ* for whatever exposure target the proxy analysis requires; extension beyond PBM (cascade models) or beyond ~100 items needs new numerics/geometry.
  • Unknowns: missing link: numerical robustness of face identification for large n (paper acknowledges convergence failures without the 10⁻⁶ tolerance hack); missing link: behavior when relevance estimates are noisy rather than fixed ground truth.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = a fixed, trusted relevance vector ρ ∈ [0,1]ⁿ per query and a PBM exposure vector γ; outputs = a distribution over ≤ n rankings plus a deterministic delivery schedule; conditions = repeated identical queries (many impressions to amortize over), individual item-level fairness, Euclidean unfairness to a decision-maker-chosen merit-proportional target, exposure depends on rank only. Our setting: exposure frequency as a ranking proxy — allocating/interpreting item exposure under an immutable scorer, where observed exposure frequencies stand in for or feed back into ranking decisions (SP2, SP4). Differences:

  • Relevance scores assumed fixed and correct (scorer immutable and trusted): matches our immutable-scorer premise — resolved: the expohedron is exactly the mechanism-level characterization of what exposure allocations are feasible given a frozen scorer.
  • PBM-only exposure (rank-determined, no inter-item dependence): missing link — cascade/DBN-style exposure where higher-ranked items affect lower ones is explicitly future work.
  • Requires many repetitions of the same query to amortize: missing link [SKIM - verify] — our setting may involve heterogeneous or few-shot query streams.
  • Fairness target chosen exogenously by a decision-maker (merit = affine function of relevance): reference only — our proxy question concerns what exposure reveals or induces, not which target is normatively right.
  • Scale cap at ~100 items per query due to numerical issues: missing link for large catalogs.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct. The paper identifies the actual structural bottleneck of prior amortized-fairness work — optimizing in the n²-dimensional Birkhoff polytope when only the n-dimensional exposure marginals matter — and replaces it with an exact characterization (the expohedron plus the majorization membership criterion) that provably contains all and only the achievable exposure vectors, solving the trade-off problem at its geometric root rather than accelerating the old formulation.
