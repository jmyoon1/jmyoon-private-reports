SP1: Fairness of Exposure in Rankings under Fixed Scores

15 papers · targets O1, O2 · 2018-2026

How the literature allocates exposure via ranking distributions when relevance scores are fixed — the taxonomy backbone for O1 and the first access-level labels for O2's feasibility map under the black-box / immutable-scores / frequency-only constraint.

SP1: 고정 score 하의 exposure 공정성

15편 논문 · O1, O2 목표 대상 · 2018-2026

relevance score가 고정되어 있을 때 exposure를 ranking 분포로 배분하는 문헌 — O1 taxonomy의 골격과, black-box·score 불변·빈도 전용 제약에 대한 O2 접근 수준 라벨의 출발점이다.

Entry Assumption vs. What Was Found

Expected (original rationale — no revisions): The densest, most directly on-point field: given fixed relevance/scores, allocate exposure via ranking distributions to satisfy exposure constraints. Expected to contain the seed papers (Singh & Joachims 2018, FairRec) and their entire successor line, giving the taxonomy backbone for O1 and the access-level labels for O2.

Found: A single shared mechanism answers the SP question: utility and exposure are both linear in the marginal rank-probability matrix, so exposure control reduces to convex optimization, realized as a distribution over rankings served across repeated impressions — literally an impression-frequency schedule. 9 of 15 papers are score-read-only.

Assessment: Surprised — the field was expected to contain literal show/don't-show methods, but almost every method presumes per-instance slate-reordering rights; only FairRec operates on raw inclusion counts, and its EF1/MMS guarantees provably do not transfer to position-weighted exposure. The 2026 SOTA (HEA) even moves away from black-box feasibility.

사전 가정과 실제 발견

기대 (최초 rationale — 수정 없음): 가장 밀도 높고 직접적인 분야로서, 고정 score 하에서 ranking 분포를 통해 exposure 제약을 만족시키는 seed 논문들(Singh & Joachims 2018, FairRec)과 그 후속 계보 전체를 담고 있어 O1 taxonomy의 골격과 O2 접근 수준 라벨을 제공할 것으로 기대하였다.

발견: 효용과 exposure가 모두 주변 순위 확률 행렬에 선형이므로 exposure 제어가 볼록 최적화로 환원되고, 그 해는 반복 impression에 걸쳐 서빙되는 ranking 분포 — 곧 impression-frequency 스케줄 — 로 실현된다. 15편 중 9편이 score-read-only였다.

평가: 예상과 다름 — 문자 그대로의 show/don't-show 방법이 있을 것으로 기대했으나, 대부분의 방법이 인스턴스별 slate 재배열 권한을 전제한다. 순수 포함 횟수로 동작하는 것은 FairRec뿐이며, 그 보장은 position 가중 exposure로 이전되지 않는다. 2026년 SOTA(HEA)는 오히려 black-box 실행 가능성에서 멀어진다.

What SP1 Establishes

The SP question — given fixed scores, how is exposure allocated to satisfy exposure constraints — is answered fully and by a single shared mechanism: because both user utility and item exposure are linear in the marginal rank-probability matrix, the combinatorial problem of choosing rankings collapses to convex optimization over that matrix, and the optimized marginals are realized as a distribution over concrete rankings executed across repeated impressions ([01] FoE: LP over doubly stochastic matrices + Birkhoff–von Neumann sampling; [05] IncompleteExposure: the top-$k$ generalization; [10] ParetoRepeatedRankings: the same geometry in exposure space at $O(n^2\log n)$ instead of $O(n^5)$). A stochastic ranking policy is an impression-frequency schedule — each item's expected exposure equals its frequency of appearing at each position across servings — so this entire line operates through exactly the knob the user has ([05] IncompleteExposure states this equivalence explicitly).

For the black-box lens: nearly every method here is score-read-only — it consumes the scorer's outputs as an immutable merit vector and never transforms them, which fits the user's constraint (scores must be used, cannot be modified). The genuinely score-free alternative (score-agnostic randomization) exists but is demonstrably dominated: score-aware Plackett–Luce randomization beats score-agnostic rank-transposition at every disparity level ([15] ExpectedExposure). What the SP1 literature does not answer: (a) how policy probabilities map onto discrete impression caps/boosts in a production serving stack, and (b) whether any guarantee survives when the black-box scores are themselves miscalibrated — [14] FairPGRank shows post-processing on biased estimated relevances can make exposure less fair, and no SP1 paper bounds this sensitivity.

SP1이 확립한 것

SP1은 다음을 확립한다. 효용과 exposure가 모두 주변 순위 확률 행렬에 선형이라는 사실 때문에, 고정 score 하의 exposure 제어는 그 행렬 위의 볼록 최적화로 붕괴하며(FoE의 LP+BvN, IncompleteExposure의 top-k 일반화, expohedron의 O(n² log n) 기하), 최적화된 주변 확률은 반복 impression에 걸친 구체적 ranking들의 분포로 실현된다. 확률적 ranking 정책은 곧 impression-frequency 스케줄이다. 거의 모든 방법이 score-read-only이므로 사용자의 score 불변 제약과 부합하지만, (a) 정책 확률을 production의 이산적 impression 예산으로 사상하는 문제와 (b) black-box score 자체가 miscalibrated일 때 보장이 유지되는가는 SP1 문헌이 답하지 못한다.

Dominant Approach지배적 접근

The dominant family is score-read-only post-processing over a fixed merit vector, in two realizations that are formally the same intervention at different timescales. (1) Within-query stochastic policies: solve a convex program over marginal rank probabilities under linear exposure constraints, then serve a distribution over rankings ([01] FoE, [05] IncompleteExposure, [10] ParetoRepeatedRankings, [15] ExpectedExposure). (2) Cross-query amortization: keep a per-item or per-provider cumulative exposure ledger and re-rank each arriving request to repay exposure deficits, via ILP ([11] EquityOfAttention), online dual/pacing variables ([04] PMMF, [08] RegretAware), velocity re-scoring with a per-user utility floor ([09] FairSort), or greedy round-robin allocation of inclusion slots ([13] FairRec). The family became dominant because it is model-agnostic — any scorer's outputs plug in as the merit vector — and because the linear structure gives optimality proofs, feasibility bounds, and $O(T^{1/2})$ online regret guarantees ([04] PMMF) that in-processing methods cannot match without retraining.

Its key limitation is what it trusts and what it assumes it can touch. Every method trusts the fixed scores as ground-truth merit; [14] FairPGRank demonstrates on a controlled synthetic that when scores carry representation bias, post-processing on those scores becomes less fair as the fairness weight increases — the one in-processing paper in this SP exists precisely because of that failure mode, and it costs full retraining. Every method also assumes a known position-bias curve; [05] IncompleteExposure shows that when inter-item dependencies (e.g. visual outliers) break the position-only exposure model, policy fairness can no longer even be verified. And almost every method assumes the right to reorder entire slates per instance — a stronger lever than pure impression-frequency control. Only [13] FairRec operates on raw inclusion counts (show/don't-show, uniform attention), and it explicitly concedes its guarantees do not transfer to position-weighted exposure.

For the survey objectives this means: O1's taxonomy backbone is complete from SP1 (constraint-based stochastic policies; amortized ledger controllers; allocation-theoretic guarantees; in-processing policy learning as the retraining contrast class). For O2, the feasible subset under the user's constraint is the score-read-only, frequency-level column — with one sharp caveat: if the user cannot even reorder slates and can only cap/boost impressions, the literal match shrinks to FairRec-style inclusion allocation plus the interpretation of stochastic policies as frequency schedules, and the mapping from policy weights to discrete impression budgets is nowhere worked out in this SP.

Convergences수렴 지점
  • A single ranking cannot satisfy exposure constraints; a distribution over rankings (equivalently, an exposure-frequency schedule) can. Reached independently via the LP/BvN construction ([01] FoE), the impossibility argument that motivates amortization ([11] EquityOfAttention), and the equal-expected-exposure principle that no deterministic ranker meets ([15] ExpectedExposure). Mechanism: position bias is fixed per slate, so redistribution requires varying slates across impressions.
  • Merit-proportional exposure is the shared normative target. [01] FoE (disparate treatment ratio), [11] EquityOfAttention (attention ∝ relevance), [14] FairPGRank (exposure-to-merit ratios), [15] ExpectedExposure (target exposure from relevance grades), and [12] RecFairnessSurvey (calibrated fairness as one of two dominant concepts) all normalize exposure by merit rather than demanding parity.
  • The utility cost of exposure control is governed by the flatness of the relevance distribution. Near-free when ties are abundant (real Airbnb data: amortization at zero NDCG loss allowed, [11] EquityOfAttention; DCG cost −0.4% in [01] FoE); collapses when relevance decays steeply (exponential-relevance synthetic makes any quality constraint $\theta\ge0.5$ infeasible, [11] EquityOfAttention; parity cost unbounded, [01] FoE §5).
  • Cumulative-ledger dual/pacing control is the scalable online realization. [04] PMMF and [08] RegretAware independently use per-provider dual variables updated from exposure deficits — functionally an impression-pacing controller — with inference cost tied to provider count, not catalog size (17–40ms flat to 200k items, [04] PMMF).
Divergences분기 지점
  • Equality vs. one-sided exposure-to-merit constraints. [01] FoE and [11] EquityOfAttention enforce equality-form proportionality; [14] FairPGRank proves the equality form infeasible whenever low-merit items must still occupy slots (overabundant exposure) and replaces it with one-sided constraints that only cap above-merit exposure. Likely cause: the equality papers optimize per-query where infeasibility is masked by expectation-level satisfaction; the learning paper must handle every training query. Adjudicated in favor of one-sided forms for general settings, but the field largely still uses equality metrics.
  • What "exposure" is: inclusion counts vs. position-discounted attention. [13] FairRec defines exposure as uniform-attention list membership (its EF1/MMS guarantees hold on counts only); [09] FairSort, [11] EquityOfAttention, [04] PMMF-adjacent work use log-discounted position weights. [13] FairRec itself concedes its guarantees do not transfer under position weighting. Unresolved — and directly load-bearing for O2, since pure frequency control governs counts, not positions.
  • Are aggregate exposure metrics sufficient? [06] HEA shows the standard baselines (FairRec, CPFair, FairDual, Multi-FR) improve inter-group exposure shares while raising within-group Gini ("pseudo-fairness"), and [02] JME constructs six systems with identical II-F that differ arbitrarily on group-level metrics. Both contradict the implicit sufficiency claim of single-scalar evaluation in the earlier line. Cause: aggregate KL/variance objectives are blind to distribution within groups.
  • Is post-processing on estimated scores safe? The whole re-ranking line treats it as adequate; [14] FairPGRank demonstrates the opposite when the score representation is group-corrupted (its post-processing baseline gets less fair as its fairness weight rises). Cause: post-processing can only reallocate exposure among items as scored; it cannot see which scores are biased. No SP1 paper adjudicates this on real data.
Prior Beliefs and Surprises (full)사전 믿음과 놀라움 (전문)

What the entry rationale expected to find:

"The densest, most directly on-point field: given fixed relevance/scores, allocate exposure via ranking distributions to satisfy exposure constraints. Contains the seed papers and their entire successor line, giving the taxonomy backbone for O1 and the access-level labels for O2."

Confirmed:

  • The taxonomy backbone is fully buildable from SP1 alone: constraint-based stochastic policies, amortized ledger controllers, allocation-theoretic guarantees, and the in-processing contrast class are all present with their succession structure (FairRec → P-MMF → BankFair+/FairSort/HEA baseline chains visible in the citation graph).
  • Access-level labeling worked: 9 of 15 papers are cleanly score-read-only, confirming the field's post-processing center of gravity.

Surprised:

  • (a) Assumed: the field would contain literal impression-frequency (show/don't-show) methods. Found instead: almost every method presumes full per-instance slate-reordering rights; only [13] FairRec operates on raw inclusion counts, and it disclaims position-weighted guarantees. Why wrong: the fairness literature formalized exposure via position bias from the start ([01] FoE), which makes position assignment — not impression frequency — the natural decision variable.
  • (b) Assumed: the newest work would move toward the black-box constraint. Found instead: the 2026 SOTA in this line ([06] HEA) requires internal embedding access and modifies scores — it moves away from black-box feasibility, while the retraining-free-under-black-box direction apparently lives in SP2's frontier papers instead. Why wrong: "post-hoc" in this literature means "after training", not "without internal access".
  • (c) Unexpected bonus: [15] ExpectedExposure provides a quantified answer to "how much does score-free intervention cost" (PL dominates RT at every disparity level) — an O2 datum the rationale did not anticipate finding inside SP1.

KB impact: No prior KB claims affected (knowledge_base.json did not exist at synthesis time; new claims logged in rqs_1.json for seeding).

Key Concepts핵심 개념

Exposure (position-bias-weighted attention)

  • What it computes: a ranking (or ranking distribution) + a position-bias vector $v_j$ (e.g. $1/\log_2(1+j)$) → weighted sum over positions → the expected attention item $i$ receives, $\text{Exposure}(d_i|P) = \sum_j P_{i,j} v_j$.
  • Why it was created: prior ranking-fairness work constrained counts of items in list prefixes; the scarce resource a ranking allocates is attention, which decays steeply with position, so a 0.03 relevance gap can produce a 0.32 exposure gap ([01] FoE Fig. 1).
  • Concrete example: an item at rank 1 earns $1/\log_2 2 = 1.0$ exposure; the same item at rank 3 earns $1/\log_2 4 = 0.5$ ([09] FairSort).
  • Role in this SP: the quantity every method allocates and every constraint is written over; the formal bridge between "rank position" and "impression frequency" for the survey's proxy framing.

Stochastic ranking policy realized by BvN decomposition

  • What it computes: a doubly stochastic marginal matrix $P$ ($P_{i,j}$ = probability item $i$ sits at rank $j$), obtained by solving $\max_P u^\top P v$ under linear exposure constraints, then decomposed as $P = \sum_i \theta_i P_i$ into ≤ $(N-1)^2+1$ permutation matrices; ranking $P_i$ is served with frequency $\theta_i$.
  • Why it was created: searching permutations under constraints is exponential; utility and exposure depend only on marginals, so an LP with $N^2$ variables suffices, and BvN converts the solution into servable rankings ([01] FoE).
  • Concrete example: [01] FoE's demographic-parity-fair job-seeker ranking decomposes into 2 permutations served at frequency 0.5 each — literally an impression-frequency schedule over two slates.
  • Role in this SP: the canonical mechanism ([01] FoE, [05] IncompleteExposure, [10] ParetoRepeatedRankings, [15] ExpectedExposure); its non-uniqueness is itself an intervention surface — FELIX re-samples the decomposition to avoid problematic rankings at zero fairness/utility cost.

Amortized (equity-of-attention) fairness

  • What it computes: over a sequence of rankings, requires cumulative attention proportional to cumulative relevance, $\sum_l a_i^l / \sum_l r_i^l$ equal across items; unfairness = $\sum_i |A_i - R_i|$ over cumulated distributions.
  • Why it was created: no single ranking can equalize attention/relevance ratios — position bias forces large attention gaps for tiny relevance gaps — so fairness must accumulate over time by rotating who occupies top slots ([11] EquityOfAttention).
  • Concrete example: 100 equally relevant items under a winner-take-all attention model return to exactly zero unfairness every 100 servings as each takes the top slot once.
  • Role in this SP: the second realization of exposure-frequency control (rotate rankings over time, per-instance decisions driven by a cumulative exposure ledger); adopted by [04] PMMF, [08] RegretAware, [09] FairSort as running exposure/deficit ledgers.

Merit-proportional (calibrated) target exposure

  • What it computes: a target exposure vector where each item's exposure share is proportional to its merit share (merit = relevance or a function of it); deviation measured by squared error ([15] ExpectedExposure's $\|\epsilon-\epsilon^*\|^2$), ratios ([01] FoE's DTR), or KL/L1 divergences ([12] RecFairnessSurvey).
  • Why it was created: uniform parity ignores relevance entirely and has unbounded utility cost ([01] FoE §5); tying exposure to merit makes the constraint compatible with ranking quality.
  • Concrete example: under RBP with $\gamma=0.5$ and 2 equally relevant documents, target exposure is 0.75 each, versus 1.0/0.5 under any static ranking ([15] ExpectedExposure).
  • Role in this SP: the shared normative target across [01] FoE, [11] EquityOfAttention, [14] FairPGRank, [15] ExpectedExposure; [14] FairPGRank proves the equality form infeasible in general and replaces it with one-sided constraints.

Expohedron

  • What it computes: the convex polytope in $\mathbb{R}^n$ whose vertices are the exposure vectors attainable by single rankings under a position-based model; interior points are expected exposures of ranking distributions, decomposable via Carathéodory in $O(n^2\log n)$.
  • Why it was created: BvN decomposition of the $n\times n$ Birkhoff-polytope representation costs $O(n^5)$; working directly in $n$-dimensional exposure space removes that bottleneck ([10] ParetoRepeatedRankings, after Kletti et al. 2022).
  • Concrete example: MSLR decomposition time drops from 29.56s (BvN) to 0.54s (Carathéodory) ([10] ParetoRepeatedRankings Table 2).
  • Role in this SP: the scaling path for the stochastic-policy mechanism; also yields the exact utility–fairness Pareto front rather than one point per hyperparameter.

Open Questions After SP1SP1 이후 미해결 질문

  • Policy-to-impression-budget discretization: no paper maps stochastic-policy weights $\theta_i$ (or marginal exposure targets) onto the discrete impression caps/boosts a production serving stack actually implements. Flagged as a missing link in [05] IncompleteExposure ("how policy probabilities map onto discrete impression-frequency budgets") and implicit in [01] FoE's unaddressed LP scalability; the ads/pacing literature (SP7) uses that vocabulary but is citation-disconnected from this cluster. What would resolve it: a construction (with rounding-error bounds) from a fair marginal matrix to per-item impression budgets under a traffic forecast.
  • Guarantee sensitivity to score error: every score-read-only method trusts $s_{u,i}$ as ground-truth merit; [04] PMMF, [09] FairSort, [11] EquityOfAttention, [13] FairRec each list miscalibrated/exposure-contaminated scores as an explicit unknown, and [14] FairPGRank shows the failure mode exists. No paper bounds how far exposure-fairness guarantees degrade as a function of score bias. What would resolve it: a perturbation analysis of the LP/dual solutions under bounded score error, or empirical stress tests with controlled score corruption.
  • Per-realization (not expectation-level) exposure guarantees under frequency-only control: [01] FoE and [05] IncompleteExposure both guarantee fairness only in expectation over the policy; low-traffic items/queries never accumulate enough servings for the expectation to bind ([05] IncompleteExposure states this applies only to head queries). What would resolve it: concentration bounds or deterministic rotation schedules with worst-case per-window exposure floors.
  • Certifying realized exposure without a validated user model: [05] IncompleteExposure (inter-item dependencies break position-only estimation), [15] ExpectedExposure (browsing-model misspecification unstudied), and [06] HEA (log-decay curve never validated against attention data) all hit the same wall — the controlled quantity (model-based exposure) and the actual quantity (user attention) are linked only by assumption. What would resolve it: joint estimation of position bias and exposure allocation from the same logged impressions, or eye-tracking/intervention validation of the exposure model under frequency interventions.
  • Exposure variance across lists: [12] RecFairnessSurvey flags that all amortized-exposure work constrains the average exposure and none constrains its variance across lists/windows; no SP1 paper addresses it.
