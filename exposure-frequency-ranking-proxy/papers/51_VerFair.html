Vertical Allocation-based Fair Exposure Amortizing in Ranking

Yang et al. · arXiv · 2022

arXiv:2204.03046 · PDF · SP: SP5

P4Track C

한 줄 요약

원래의 amortization 메커니즘이 상위 슬롯에서 비효율적임을 보이고 수직 배분 기반 공정 exposure amortizing(VerFair)을 제안한다. 실현 오차를 position weight 1개 미만으로 묶는다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Existing amortized-exposure-fairness methods (Biega et al.'s per-round ILP, Singh & Joachims' LP over probabilistic rankings, Morik et al.'s FairCo proportional controller) allocate exposure in an online/horizontal manner that assumes no prior knowledge of the consumer population. In settings where the system CAN pre-compute all ranklists at once (email advertising, offline recommendation), this assumption is wasteful: horizontal allocation creates avoidable conflicts for relevant items at top ranks, yielding a suboptimal fairness-relevance tradeoff (lower NDCG at top positions for the same fairness level). ILP methods are additionally too slow for large-scale serving (NP-complete; ~44–147 s per 1k ranklists vs VerFair's 0.1–0.2 s).

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

VerFair, a model-agnostic post-processing algorithm with three phases (Algorithm 1), operating on a pre-computed relevance matrix R(d,u) for all m consumers at once:

  1. Anchor-point search (Algorithm 2): scan slots backwards from (m,k) until accumulated position exposure ≥ α · E_total; start allocation there.
  2. Allocation Phase: vertical (column-first) greedy allocation from the anchor point — at each slot, among items whose group still has quota remaining (set H) intersected with the consumer's unused items F(u), pick the most relevant; update the group's allocated exposure.
  3. Appending Phase: fill remaining empty slots purely by personal relevance (no fairness constraint).
  4. Re-sorting Phase: sort each consumer's list by personal relevance. Because allocation started at the bottom-anchored region, quota-satisfying items can only move UP, so the minimum-exposure guarantee E(G) ≥ Quota(G|α) survives (proof sketch in §4.4.2: either all quotas are met exactly, or the per-group shortfall is bounded by one position's examination probability p_{c*,k} < 1).

Individual fairness is the special case where every item is its own group. Complexity O(m·n·k), same as FairCo/FairRec but empirically faster.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Vertical allocation

  • What it computes: given an m-consumer x k-rank grid of ranklist slots and a relevance matrix R(d,u), it fills rank position r for ALL m consumers before moving to rank r+1 (column-by-column), greedily picking the most relevant still-feasible item per slot — versus the standard "horizontal" order that fills all k ranks for one consumer before moving to the next.
  • Why it was created: horizontal allocation (used by the Biega ILP / FairCo line) creates conflicts at top ranks — relevant items get "used up" on early consumers' full lists — lowering NDCG at top positions for a given fairness level.
  • Concrete example: with 3 consumers, 3 items of equal average relevance 0.70, k=2, exposure 1 per slot, α=1 (each item must appear exactly twice): vertical allocation reaches NDCG@1 = 1.000 vs horizontal's 0.989 (paper Table 3).
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the core of VerFair's Allocation Phase (Algorithm 1) and the basis of the theoretical argument (§4.4.1) that greedy column-first filling maximizes top-rank DCG.

Exposure Quota (relevance-induced minimum exposure)

  • What it computes: Quota(d|α) = R(d) · α · E_total / Σ_{d'} R(d'), i.e. item d's fair share of the fixed total exposure, scaled by a tolerance parameter α ∈ [0,1]; the constraint enforced is E(d) ≥ Quota(d|α) (and the group analogue with R(G), E(G)).
  • Why it was created: prior amortized-fairness formulations (Biega et al., Singh & Joachims) impose the strict equality "exposure proportional to relevance"; the quota relaxes this to a minimum-guarantee inequality, with α controlling how much of total exposure is reserved for fairness (α=1 recovers the strict amortized-fairness equality).
  • Concrete example: 3 items of average relevance 0.80 each, total exposure 6, α=0.5 → each item's quota = 1 (must appear at least once at exposure-1 positions; paper Table 4).
  • How it is used in this paper: defines the fairness constraint VerFair provably satisfies, and α is the tradeoff knob swept to produce the fairness-vs-NDCG curves.

Anchor point

  • What it computes: the grid position (ĉ, r̂) found by scanning backwards from the last consumer's last rank (m,k) toward (1,1), accumulating position exposure until the running sum first reaches α · E_total (Algorithm 2); vertical allocation starts there instead of at (1,1).
  • Why it was created: if fairness-constrained allocation starts at the top of ranklists, the later relevance-based re-sorting phase can only push quota-satisfying items DOWN (to lower-exposure ranks), breaking the minimum-exposure guarantee; starting from the bottom-anchored region means re-sorting can only move them UP, preserving the guarantee.
  • Concrete example: in Table 4 (3 consumers, k=2, α=0.5), starting allocation at rank 2 (the anchor) instead of rank 1 keeps every item's exposure ≥ 1 after re-sorting; starting from the origin breaks it for item C.
  • How it is used in this paper: step 7 of Algorithm 1; the exposure-guarantee proof (§4.4.2) relies on it.

Amortized fairness itself (exposure distribution should match relevance distribution, individual- and group-level) is inherited from Biega et al. 2018 and Singh & Joachims 2018 — the Track A seeds of this survey (defined in the corresponding Track A notes, e.g. Equity of Attention / Fairness of Exposure).

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: a dense consumer-item relevance matrix (they impute via Surprise SVD, lr 5e-3, L2 2e-2, 100-d factors), position examination probabilities (NDCG-discount PBM), CPU only (Xeon E5-2640; sub-second per 1k ranklists).
  • Code: available at https://github.com/Taosheng-ty/sigirAP-VerFair.git; datasets: Yahoo! R3, Google Local (via FairRec repo), Movielens-Groups (via FairCo/Dynamic-Fairness repo).
  • Modifications: for our setting one would need (a) an online/streaming variant (limit U to active consumers per timestamp, as they suggest but do not implement); (b) estimated rather than oracle propensities; (c) robustness analysis when R(d,u) is noisy, since both the quota and NDCG use the same estimated R.
  • Unknowns: missing link: sensitivity of the quota guarantee to relevance-estimation error; missing link: behavior at industrial catalog scale (n >> 1000); [SKIM] appendix-level proof details not checked.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = a fully pre-computed consumer-item relevance matrix (MF/SVD-imputed ratings) for m ≈ 10k–15k consumers, n = 100–1000 items; outputs = m ranklists of length k = 10; conditions = offline/batch serving, exposure = position-bias examination probability (PBM with known η), fairness target = exposure distribution ∝ (estimated) relevance distribution, post-processing only (relevance estimation and propensity estimation both assumed solved). Our setting: study of exposure frequency as it is governed by ranking systems where estimated relevance serves as the merit proxy for allocating exposure (SP5: the amortized-fairness Biega/FairCo lineage and its limitations).   • Batch/offline serving with the full consumer set known a priori vs typical online serving where consumers arrive sequentially: missing link — the paper's core advantage depends on this assumption; online extension is future work.
  • True relevance assumed given (imputed by MF and then treated as ground truth for both allocation and NDCG evaluation) vs our concern that estimated relevance is itself a noisy/biased proxy: reference only — the paper explicitly scopes relevance estimation out (§5.1.2), so it inherits, and does not examine, the exposure-∝-relevance proxy assumption central to this survey.
  • Known, homogeneous position-bias (PBM, shared η, known examination probabilities) vs settings where propensities must be estimated: reference only — scoped out (§5.1.3).
  • Small item catalogs (≤1000 items) vs industrial-scale catalogs: missing link — O(m·n·k) per full batch; scalability beyond thousands of items unreported.
  • Groups defined by producer/company, explicitly NOT sensitive attributes vs sensitive-attribute group fairness: reference only — the paper flags the extension as future work.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — for the problem it names (horizontal/online allocation wastes the available prior knowledge of the consumer set, causing avoidable top-rank relevance loss under a fairness constraint), the paper fixes the actual structural cause: it redesigns the allocation ORDER (vertical, anchor-started) and proves the quota guarantee survives re-sorting, rather than merely tuning a tradeoff knob. Caveat relevant to this survey: it leaves the upstream proxy assumption (exposure should be proportional to estimated relevance) entirely intact.
