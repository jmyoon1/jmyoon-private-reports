Pairwise Fairness for Ranking and Regression

H. Narasimhan et al. · arXiv · 2019 · 130 citations

arXiv:1906.05330 · PDF · SP: SP3

P2Track A

한 줄 요약

쌍별(pairwise) 비교 기반의 공정성 정의를 ranking과 회귀에 도입한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Statistical fairness notions (equal opportunity, equal accuracy, statistical parity) were well developed for binary classification with efficient in-training enforcement via rate constraints, but had no trainable analogue for ranking and regression models. Prior fair-ranking work was mostly listwise and exposure-based (Singh & Joachims 2018, Biega et al. 2018, Zehlike & Castillo 2018), often applied as post-processing re-ranking rather than model training, and the closest pairwise approach (Beutel et al. 2019) offered only a fixed regularizer with no way to control the fairness–accuracy trade-off, limited to two groups. Continuous protected attributes were handled only by bucketing.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Reduce ranking (and regression) fairness to constraints on pairwise comparison accuracies, then train with constrained or robust optimization. Core training formulation (cross-group equal opportunity for ranking):

$$\max_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \text{AUC}(f) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad A_{G_i>G_j}(f) - A_{G_k>G_l}(f) \le \epsilon \;\; \forall i \neq j, k \neq l$$

or the robust variant $\max_{f, \xi} \xi$ s.t. $\xi \le \text{AUC}(f), \; \xi \le A_{G_i>G_j}(f) \; \forall i \neq j$. Each pairwise accuracy is rewritten as a rate constraint $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{1}_{f(x)-f(x')>0} \mid \mathcal{S}_{ij}]$ on score differences, so the proxy-Lagrangian two-player framework of Cotter et al. (hinge surrogates for the model player, exact indicators for the multiplier player) solves it out-of-the-box, yielding a stochastic model that satisfies constraints in expectation. Regression replaces AUC with MSE; continuous attributes replace group membership with attribute ordering.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Simulated ranking (2 groups): constrained optimization reaches fairness violation 0.01 at AUC 0.86 vs. unconstrained 0.92 (0.28 violation); Beutel et al.: 0.84 (0.04).
  • W3C Experts (gender): unconstrained model has near-total violation 0.96 at AUC 0.53; constrained gets 0.10 at AUC 0.54 — constraints act as regularizers and improve test AUC here. Beutel et al. wins this one (0.55 AUC, 0.09 violation).
  • Wiki Talk Pages toxicity ranking ('gay' term as group): violation 0.10 → 0.01 at AUC 0.97 → 0.96.
  • Law School regression (gender): violation 0.30 → 0.02 at MSE 0.142 → 0.143 (essentially free); strictly beats Beutel et al. (0.167 MSE, 0.06 violation).
  • Head-to-head vs. Beutel et al. 2019: of 4 cases where one strictly dominates, this method wins 3. Post-processing baselines (Kallus & Zhou, Singh & Joachims) consistently pay more AUC for the same fairness or fail feasibility on sparse-pair datasets.

Category: new method (metric family + in-processing training procedure) Context: builds on rate-constraint fair classification (Goh et al. 2016, Cotter et al. 2019a/b, Hardt et al. 2016); direct sibling of Beutel et al. 2019 pairwise fairness; positioned against exposure-based listwise fair ranking (Singh & Joachims 2018, Biega et al. 2018) — the exposure line is central to this survey's SP3. Correctness: [SKIM] — experiments report point estimates only, no error bars visible in the main tables; convergence guarantee inherited from Cotter et al. 2019a stated in appendix. Contributions: (1) pairwise-accuracy analogues of equal opportunity / equal accuracy / statistical parity for ranking and regression; (2) extension to continuous protected attributes via attribute-ordered pairs; (3) constrained/robust optimization training that enforces the constraints directly, with user-controllable slack $\epsilon$. Clarity: method reproducible — formulations are explicit and code is public (TensorFlow constrained optimization toolbox).

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Group-dependent pairwise accuracy $A_{G_i > G_j}$

  • What it computes: for a scoring function $f: \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$, the probability $P(f(x) > f(x') \mid y > y', (x,y) \in G_i, (x',y') \in G_j)$ — i.e., among same-query pairs where the group-$i$ item is labeled better than the group-$j$ item, the fraction the model ranks correctly. With $K$ groups this yields a $K \times K$ accuracy matrix; the unconditioned version is standard AUC.
  • Why it was created: listwise fairness definitions (exposure parity etc.) require full ranked lists and are hard to optimize during training; classification fairness notions (equal opportunity, statistical parity) had no direct ranking/regression analogue.
  • Concrete example: on their simulated 2-group ranking data, the unconstrained model has $A_{G_0>G_1} = 0.980$ but $A_{G_1>G_0} = 0.705$ — a 0.28 cross-group fairness violation.
  • How it is used in this paper: the fairness metric family (equal opportunity, equal accuracy, statistical parity variants) is defined as equality constraints between entries of this matrix, then enforced during training.

Pairwise equal opportunity / marginal equal opportunity

  • What it computes: pairwise equal opportunity requires $A_{G_i>G_j} = \kappa$ for all $i,j$ (cross-group variant: only $i \neq j$); marginal equal opportunity relaxes to row-averages $A_{G_i>:} = \kappa$ for all $i$, i.e., each group's "should-win" pairs are ranked correctly at the same rate.
  • Why it was created: the full $K^2$ constraint set can be statistically insignificant or infeasible; the marginal version is a coarser, feasible criterion.
  • Concrete example: Business-matching experiment constrains $|A_{chain>:} - A_{nonchain>:}| \le 0.01$.
  • How it is used in this paper: one of the constraint menus users can pick from when training.

Continuous-attribute pairwise accuracies $A_>$, $A_<$

  • What it computes: $A_> = P(f(x) > f(x') \mid y > y', z > z')$ and $A_< = P(f(x) > f(x') \mid y > y', z < z')$ for a continuous protected feature $z$ (age, income, race %); equal opportunity becomes $A_> = A_<$.
  • Why it was created: prior fairness work assumed categorical groups; bucketing continuous attributes creates arbitrary bin boundaries and significance problems.
  • Concrete example: Communities & Crime with % black population as $z$: unconstrained $A_> = 0.904$ vs. $A_< = 0.577$; constrained training brings these to 0.777 vs. 0.745.
  • How it is used in this paper: extends every pairwise fairness notion to continuous protected attributes without discretization.

Proxy-Lagrangian constrained optimization for pairwise constraints — reused from Cotter et al. 2019a/b (rate-constraint framework), applied here by rewriting each pairwise accuracy as a positive-prediction rate on score differences $f(x) - f(x') > 0$ over pair subsets. Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: pairwise-comparable labeled data (same-query pairs for ranking, all pairs for regression), group/attribute annotations, TensorFlow constrained-optimization library; pair enumeration is $O(N^2)$ (they subsample or minibatch at 100).
  • Code: available at https://github.com/google-research/google-research/tree/master/pairwise_fairness
  • Modifications: to serve exposure-frequency analysis, the pairwise metrics would need position-weighted pair selection (the paper's own conclusion suggests weighting pairs by presentation-order side information — directly relevant hook for SP3).
  • Unknowns: missing link: how pairwise-accuracy parity relates quantitatively to exposure parity in the induced rankings — the paper never measures exposure of its trained models.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Beutel et al., "Fairness in Recommendation Ranking through Pairwise Comparisons" (KDD 2019) — the closest predecessor; correlation-regularizer variant of the same pairwise idea, main experimental competitor.
  • Singh & Joachims, "Fairness of Exposure in Rankings" (KDD 2018) — the exposure-based listwise counterpart this paper positions against; core to SP3's exposure axis.
  • Kallus & Zhou, "The Fairness of Risk Scores Beyond Classification: Bipartite Ranking and the xAUC Metric" (NeurIPS 2019) — pairwise AUC-based fairness metrics with post-processing; complementary metric family.
  • Cotter et al., "Two-Player Games for Efficient Non-Convex Constrained Optimization" (ALT 2019) — supplies the proxy-Lagrangian machinery this paper's training rests on.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = query–candidate feature vectors with relevance labels (or regression labels) + protected group/attribute; outputs = scoring function $f$ trained from scratch; conditions = fairness defined on pairwise ordering accuracy, position/exposure of items in the displayed ranking is entirely absent from the metric. Our setting: SP3 — exposure frequency in rankings as the fairness/measurement object; position bias and how often items are shown matter directly. Differences:

  • Fairness object is pairwise accuracy, deliberately ignoring rank position and exposure weighting (paper explicitly contrasts itself with the exposure line and shows Singh & Joachims performs poorly on pairwise metrics — metric mismatch, not superiority on exposure): reference only — this paper is the non-exposure alternative on the O2 access axis, useful as a contrast class.
  • In-processing (train model from scratch) vs. post-processing re-ranking of an existing system: reference only [SKIM - verify relevance to SP3 framing].
  • Supports continuous protected attributes; most exposure-based work assumes discrete groups: missing link — whether exposure-based notions extend to continuous attributes is not addressed here.
  • No user/click feedback loop; labels assumed given i.i.d., no exposure→click→label feedback: missing link for our dynamic-exposure setting.
