Equity of Attention: Amortizing Individual Fairness in Rankings

Biega et al. · SIGIR 2018 · 2018 · 556 citations

arXiv:1805.01788 · PDF · SP: SP1 · SP3 · SP4 · SP5 · SP9 · SP8

P0Track C

한 줄 요약

단일 ranking으로는 attention/relevance 비율을 균등화할 수 없으므로, ranking 열 전체에 걸쳐 누적 attention을 누적 relevance에 비례시키는 amortized 개별 공정성을 제안한다. 누적 exposure ledger 개념의 출발점이다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Position bias means top-ranked subjects absorb most searcher attention nearly irrespective of true relevance, so attention received systematically diverges from relevance-implied "deserved" attention — e.g., attention decays geometrically with rank while relevance may decay linearly or be nearly flat. Prior fair-ranking work (diversification, group-exposure constraints) (a) measures fairness only at the group level, leaving individual-level harm invisible, and (b) treats single static rankings, which structurally cannot be individually fair because tiny relevance differences force large attention differences. When rankings mediate income (Airbnb, Uber, LinkedIn, Spotify), this repeated mismatch becomes economic starvation of low-ranked subjects.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Redefine fairness as amortized individual equity — cumulative attention proportional to cumulative relevance across a ranking series — and enforce it online: at each ranking, solve an ILP that permutes subjects to minimize $\sum_i |A_i - R_i|$ (L1 distance between cumulated attention and relevance distributions) subject to an NDCG-quality lower bound $\theta$ on the current ranking, with candidate pre-filtering (top-k by relevance + largest attention-deficit subjects) to keep the $n^2$-variable ILP tractable. Key Results주요 결과
  • On Airbnb single-query data (Boston/Geneva/Hong Kong; 3944/1728/4529 subjects), the relevance-only baseline's unfairness grows linearly without bound, while the ILP with $\theta = 0$ and the Objective heuristic hold unfairness near 0 with periodic returns to ~0 every n iterations (n = dataset size).
  • Real relevance distributions are near-uniform with many ties at the top, so substantial fairness amortization is achievable at little or no NDCG loss — even $\theta = 1$ (zero quality loss allowed) performs well on multi-query Airbnb data by reshuffling within ties.
  • On the synthetic exponential-relevance dataset, the ILP cannot satisfy any quality constraint $\theta \geq 0.5$ and collapses to relevance-only ranking — steep relevance decay leaves no room to trade quality for fairness.
  • Actual NDCG loss is typically below the threshold $\theta$ (Fig. 17); quality periodically returns to 1 as top-relevant subjects regain priority.
  • On StackExchange (~20K queries, 500 candidates each), the same trends hold, but subjects appearing in few rankings need a longer amortization horizon.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Equity of attention (individual fairness for rankings)

  • What it computes: for every pair of ranked subjects, requires the ratio of received attention to relevance be equal: $a_{i_1}/r_{i_1} = a_{i_2}/r_{i_2}\ \forall u_{i_1}, u_{i_2}$ — attention proportional to relevance at the individual level.
  • Why it was created: prior fair-ranking work measured fairness only over demographic groups; position bias harms individuals regardless of group membership, and group-based perturbation cannot address it.
  • Concrete example: two Airbnb listings with near-identical rating scores (0.95 vs 0.94) can sit at ranks 1 and 5; under a geometric attention model (p=0.5) rank 1 gets ~0.5 of all attention and rank 5 gets ~0.03 — a 16x attention gap for a 1% relevance gap.
  • How it is used in this paper: the normative definition (Def. 1) that the whole mechanism is built to approximate; shown to subsume group fairness and equality-of-attention as special cases.

Equity of amortized attention

  • What it computes: over a sequence of rankings $\rho^1..\rho^m$, requires cumulative attention proportional to cumulative relevance: $\sum_l a^l_{i_1} / \sum_l r^l_{i_1} = \sum_l a^l_{i_2} / \sum_l r^l_{i_2}$.
  • Why it was created: no single ranking can satisfy Def. 1 — attention is dictated by position bias while relevance is dictated by the data — so fairness must be accumulated over time by rotating who occupies top positions.
  • Concrete example: with 100 equally-relevant subjects and a singular attention model (all attention to rank 1), unfairness returns to exactly 0 every 100 iterations as each subject takes the top slot once.
  • How it is used in this paper: the operational fairness target; unfairness is measured as the L1 distance $\sum_i |A_i - R_i|$ between the cumulated attention and cumulated relevance distributions (Eq. 1).

ILP-based online attention amortization

  • What it computes: at each incoming ranking, solves an integer linear program over $n^2$ binary assignment variables $X_{i,j}$ (subject $u_i$ → position $j$), minimizing $\sum_{i,j} |A^{l-1}_i + w_j - (R^{l-1}_i + r^l_i)| \cdot X_{i,j}$ subject to NDCG-quality@k of the reordered ranking staying $\geq \theta$ times the original.
  • Why it was created: the fairness-quality tradeoff needs a mechanism that runs online (one query at a time, no knowledge of future load) with an interpretable quality guarantee.
  • Concrete example: $\theta = 1$ still permits reshuffling within relevance ties; $\theta = 0$ permits any permutation; experiments use Gurobi with a pre-filtered candidate set of 100 subjects (top-k by relevance plus the subjects with largest attention deficit $A_i - (R_i + r_i)$).
  • How it is used in this paper: the core mechanism evaluated on synthetic, Airbnb, and StackExchange data.

Position-bias attention models (geometric / singular) — attention weight per rank: geometric $w_j = p(1-p)^{j-1}$ up to cutoff $k$ (a cascade-model special case), or singular (rank 1 gets everything, motivated by Uber-style top-1 presentation). Standard click-model material, used here as the exposure→attention proxy. [SKIM - the paper assumes these generic forms rather than fitting empirical click data]

  • Modifications: for an exposure-as-proxy study, replace assumed geometric weights with empirically estimated exposure/click curves; add the relevance-feedback loop the paper abstracts away; LP relaxation or greedy heuristic needed beyond ~10^3 subjects.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = a stream of rankings with system-provided [0,1]-normalized relevance scores per subject + fixed per-position attention weights (geometric/singular click model); outputs = a permuted ranking per query; conditions = platform controls the ranker, attention weights known a priori, relevance assumed proportional to true worthiness, populations of ~100–4.5K subjects, ILP solved per query. Our setting: exposure frequency used as a proxy signal in ranking/evaluation pipelines — where accumulated exposure both results from and feeds back into rank position. [SKIM - verify against SP definitions] Differences:

  • Paper treats attention as an outcome to be allocated given exogenous relevance; our setting treats exposure/frequency as a proxy input that may contaminate the relevance estimate itself: missing link — the paper explicitly assumes relevance ∝ worthiness and flags ranker-score calibration as future work.
  • Attention weights are assumed known generic click models, not estimated from data: missing link for any empirical exposure measurement.
  • Feedback loop (exposure → clicks → future relevance scores) is outside the model — rankings are amortized but relevance is static per query: missing link.
  • Individual-level exposure accounting via cumulated $A_i$ vs $R_i$ distributions: directly reusable as a measurement construct — resolved: adopt the L1 unfairness measure as an exposure-discrepancy metric.
  • Scale (thousands of subjects, ILP per query with 100-candidate pre-filter): reference only for web-scale settings.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the paper identifies and directly resolves one root cause (a single static ranking structurally cannot equalize attention/relevance ratios, so it moves the fairness target to the amortized sequence level — a genuine reframing, not a metric patch), but it deliberately leaves the deeper proxy problem untouched: system relevance is taken as ground-truth worthiness ("we assume the two are proportional"), so any bias already baked into relevance scores — including exposure-driven feedback into ratings — is propagated, not corrected.
