Reach Measurement, Optimization and Frequency Capping In Targeted Online Advertising Under k-Anonymity

Gao et al. · arXiv · 2025 · 1 citations

arXiv:2501.04882 · PDF · SP: SP7

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

k-익명성 하의 targeted 광고에서 도달 측정·최적화·frequency capping을 다룬다(2025).

Problem해결하려는 문제

Frequency capping — showing an ad to a given user at most $c$ times — requires per-user impression counts, but under k-anonymity the ad system only observes which group of $k$ users a request came from. Prior reach-optimization work (Mehta et al. 2007; Buchbinder et al. 2014; Hojjat et al. 2017) assumes known user identities; prior privacy work focuses on cross-publisher measurement with DP aggregation, not first-party serving under group-level anonymity. No prior work addresses reach measurement, reach-optimizing bidding, and frequency capping when per-user exposure counts are only probabilistically known.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Model the unobservable per-user exposure count as $X_i \sim \mathcal{B}(\sum_{j \in G_i} n_j, 1/k)$ given observed group impression counts. This yields:

  1. Measurement: closed-form expected reach under any cap $c$ (Theorem 1), exact variance/Chebyshev concentration bounds for $c=1$ (Theorem 2), expected over-exposed user count (Theorem 3); streaming algorithms with $O(kc^2)$ time per impression.
  2. Optimization: budget-constrained bid maximization $\max_b \sum_t p_t W_t(b_t)$ s.t. $\sum_t H_t(b_t) \le B$, where $p_t$ (probability of incremental reach, Theorem 5) probabilistically discounts each bid — recovering deterministic capping at $k=1$.
  3. Non-uniform extension: replace uniform $1/k$ with anonymized within-group visit-probability vectors (Theorems 4, 6) when groups don't overlap.
Key Results주요 결과
  • Privacy-efficiency tradeoff: relative ROAS drops ~33% moving from $k=1$ (no privacy) to $k=2$; further privacy is nearly free — at the extreme of one all-user group (traditional-media-like), efficiency is 62% of fully personalized serving.
  • Reach-measurement variance jumps sharply at $k=2$ then grows minimally with larger $k$ (Fig. 2C); $k=1$ is deterministic.
  • Real-data evaluation (groups of 20, 10 campaigns, $c=1$): non-uniform approach D achieves 94.12% mean relative ROAS (variance 0.005) vs. unique-impressions 85.99%, unique-groups 88.42%, uniform-reach 86.95%; D also has the lowest measurement error (Fig. 5).
  • Uniform within-group assumption systematically overestimates reach (uniform distribution maximizes the reach expression on the probability simplex).
  • ROAS increases almost linearly with targeted-user coverage of groups (8.33% → 100% coverage; Appendix B) — group-formation alignment with targeting segments matters.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

k-anonymous privacy protocol (for ad serving)

  • What it computes: user id at ad-request time → lookup in a user-group identification system → returns only a group id (group of $k$ users sharing attributes like geolocation/interests); the ads ranking system sees group-level information only.
  • Why it was created: individual-level tracking (the basis of traditional frequency capping) is being phased out for privacy; platforms (LinkedIn, Google) group users so any individual is indistinguishable from $k-1$ others.
  • Concrete example: $k=6$: 120 targeted users partitioned into 20 groups of 6; each ad request reveals only which of the 20 groups it came from.
  • How it is used in this paper: the operating constraint for all measurement and optimization results — per-user impression counts $X_i$ become latent Binomial random variables $X_i \sim \mathcal{B}(\sum_{j \in G_i} n_j, 1/k)$ given observed group impression counts $n_j$.

Probabilistic discounting (probabilistic frequency capping)

  • What it computes: for ad request $t$ from group $j_t$, computes $p_t = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i \in U_{j_t}} F(c-1; \sum_{j \in G_i} n_{tj}, 1/k)$ — the probability the request comes from a user who has seen the ad fewer than $c$ times (Theorem 5) — and multiplies it into the bid: $b_t^*(\lambda) = (h_t/w_t)^{-1}(p_t/\lambda)$.
  • Why it was created: hard per-user frequency capping is impossible when the user is unidentifiable; discounting the bid by the probability of incremental reach recovers capping as the special case $p_t \in \{0,1\}$ when $k=1$.
  • Concrete example: unique-reach campaign ($c=1$), group of 2 targeted users, 2 requests: $p_1 = 1$; after winning the first, $p_2 = 0.5$ (either user A or B has already seen the ad).
  • How it is used in this paper: the core optimization mechanism — plugged into a budget-constrained bid optimizer (Lagrangian $\lambda$ updated online) and evaluated in simulated second-price auctions and on a real ad platform dataset.

Expected reach under a frequency cap (Theorem 1)

  • What it computes: group impression counts $\{n_j\}$ → Binomial model per user → expected reach $\sum_{i=1}^N (\sum_{l=1}^c l f(l; \sum_{j\in G_i} n_j, 1/k) + c - cF(c; \cdot))$; streaming algorithm updates it in $O(kc^2)$ time per impression.
  • Why it was created: reach reporting must continue under k-anonymity, where the true per-user counts are unobservable.
  • Concrete example: $c=1$ corollary: expected unique reach $= \sum_i (1 - (1-1/k)^{\sum_{j \in G_i} n_j})$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the reported reach metric; also extended to concentration bounds (Chebyshev via exact covariance, Lemma 1 — Hoeffding inapplicable because per-user reach indicators are negatively correlated within a group) and over-exposure counts (Theorem 3).

Group property vector (non-uniform arrival relaxation)

  • What it computes: per group $j$, a sorted probability vector $(q_{j1},\dots,q_{jk})$, $\sum_l q_{jl}=1$, of within-group user visit frequencies, unlinked to specific user identities → replaces the uniform $1/k$ in reach and $p_t$ formulas (Theorems 4, 6).
  • Why it was created: uniform within-group arrival is unrealistic (real groups have a few heavy visitors); the uniform assumption provably overestimates reach (uniform maximizes the reach expression on the simplex).
  • Concrete example: property vector $(0,0,\dots,0,1)$ = one active user, rest inactive; system knows this without knowing which user.
  • How it is used in this paper: approach D in the real-data evaluation — most accurate measurement and best ROAS (94.12% of no-privacy performance).
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: group membership structure ($U_j$, $G_i$), group-level impression logs, and for the non-uniform variant, anonymized within-group visit-frequency vectors; trivial compute (streaming $O(kc^2)$/impression).
  • Code: not available (no repository mentioned).
  • Modifications: to use as an exposure-frequency proxy in ranking, replace auction win-probability $W_t$ with a ranking score and reuse $p_t$ as an exposure-aware multiplier; needs a mapping from cohort-level exposure logs to the group model.
  • Unknowns: missing link: sensitivity of $p_t$ to errors in estimated property vectors (paper acknowledges it ignored this); missing link: behavior with overlapping groups in the non-uniform setting (theory restricted to non-overlapping).
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Sang, Kant & Karlsson, "Feedback Control of In-Target Rate for Probabilistic Frequency Cap in Online Advertising" (ACC 2022) — the closest prior probabilistic-capping mechanism (control-theoretic, consent-based partial tracking); useful contrast for SP7's approximate-count capping thread.
  • Epasto et al., "Clustering for private interest-based advertising" (KDD 2021) — how the k-anonymous groups are actually formed; determines the coverage variable this paper shows drives ROAS almost linearly.
  • Wright et al. 2021 / Kreuter et al. 2020 (Google technical reports) — DP-based cross-publisher reach/frequency estimation; the alternative privacy mechanism whose tradeoff curve the authors name as future work.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = group-level impression counts (group size $k$, group id per ad request), auction win/cost curves, campaign budget; outputs = expected reach + bids; conditions = single campaign, second-price auctions, groups formed by the publisher, per-user counts latent. Our setting (SP7 — exposure frequency as a ranking proxy): ranking/recommendation systems where per-item or per-user exposure frequency is used as a signal or constraint, and where exact exposure counts may be unavailable, aggregated, or privatized. Differences:

  • Objective is auction-bid reach maximization, not ranking-score construction: reference only — but the probabilistic-discount multiplier $p_t$ is structurally a frequency-based down-weighting of a candidate's score, directly analogous to exposure-frequency de-boosting in ranking. [SKIM - verify mapping in deep read]
  • Uncertainty source is k-anonymity grouping, whereas our setting's exposure-count noise may come from logging/sampling/DP: missing link — the Binomial-given-group-counts model transfers only when the aggregation structure is known and uniform-within-group.
  • Brand-ad reach (unique users under cap $c$) vs. general ranking utility: reference only.
  • Single-campaign, non-overlapping-group assumptions in the best-performing variant (D): missing link for multi-item ranking with overlapping cohorts.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper models the root uncertainty exactly (per-user exposure as a Binomial induced by the k-anonymity mechanism) and derives capping/measurement from that model, rather than heuristically patching the reach metric; the residual uniform-arrival simplification is itself identified and relaxed (Theorems 4/6).
