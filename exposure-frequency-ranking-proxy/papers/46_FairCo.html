Controlling Fairness and Bias in Dynamic Learning-to-Rank

Morik et al. · SIGIR 2020 · 2020

arXiv:2005.14713 · PDF · SP: SP5

P0Track A

한 줄 요약

동적 learning-to-rank에서 score + λ·(누적 격차)로 정렬하는 비례 제어기(FairCo)를 제안한다. 임의의 λ>0과 유계 고정 scorer에 대해 amortized disparity가 O(1/τ)로 0에 수렴함을 증명한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Dynamic learning-to-rank systems that re-rank based on accumulated feedback have two coupled deficiencies: (1) click counts are inconsistent relevance estimators because presentation position biases which items collect feedback, producing rich-get-richer dynamics — items lucky early stay on top regardless of true relevance; (2) even with true relevances known, relevance-sorted ranking allocates exposure disproportionally to merit (a 2% relevance edge can yield a 10-position exposure gap). Existing fair-ranking methods (Singh & Joachims 2018; Biega et al. 2018) assume merit is known a priori and do not handle the dynamic setting where merit is still being learned from biased feedback.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

FairCo, a proportional controller layered on top of an unbiased relevance estimate [SKIM - convergence proof (Appendix B) and LP baseline details not deep-verified]:

  1. Estimate merit unbiasedly from biased clicks via IPS: $\hat{R}^{IPS}(d) = \frac{1}{\tau}\sum_t \frac{c_t(d)}{p_t(d)}$ (global) or the unbiased cardinal regression objective (Eq. 14, personalized).
  2. Track amortized exposure(or impact)-to-merit disparity $\hat{D}_{\tau-1}(G_i, G_j)$ between all group pairs.
  3. Rank by $\sigma_\tau = \mathrm{argsort}_d\,[\hat{R}(d|x) + \lambda\,\mathrm{err}_\tau(d)]$ where $\mathrm{err}_\tau(d) = (\tau-1)\max_{G_i}\hat{D}_{\tau-1}(G_i, G)$ — a post-hoc additive error-correction term on the score, zero for the most-over-exposed group and growing with accumulated disparity for others.

Theorem 7.1: for any $\lambda > 0$, any bounded relevance model, and a feasibility condition (Eq. 18: ranking an under-exposed group entirely above another reduces its disparity), overall exposure disparity converges to zero at $O(1/\tau)$ regardless of unfairness accumulated before merit estimates converge.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Semi-synthetic news simulation (30 articles, 2 polarity groups, PBM with DCG-style drop-off, 100 trials): FairCo(Imp) drives impact unfairness near 0 while Naive stays ~0.1–0.15 and D-ULTR(Glob) only marginally better; NDCG cost vs. D-ULTR(Glob) is small (roughly 0.70 vs. 0.72 range, Fig. 1).
  • IPS relevance-estimation error → 0 with more users; Naive click-count error stagnates at ~0.25 (Fig. 2).
  • Adversarial head-start (first x users all right-leaning): Naive unfairness grows with x; FairCo stays low across the whole range 0–400 (Fig. 3) — controller corrects arbitrary accumulated unfairness, matching Theorem 7.1.
  • FairCo matches the adapted LinProg baseline (Singh & Joachims 2018 LP) in NDCG/unfairness across $\lambda$ sweep while being far cheaper (LP has $O(n^2+|G|^2)$ variables per step) (Fig. 4).
  • MovieLens ML-20M (100 movies, 5 production-company groups, $10^4$ users, personalized NN scorer): FairCo(Exp) and FairCo(Imp) control their respective unfairness with small NDCG reduction; optimizing impact fairness can increase exposure unfairness — the two criteria are substantively different (Figs. 8–10).

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: first dynamic LTR algorithm that is unbiased and fair while merit is still being learned; rigorous theoretical foundation and convergence guarantees; practical, robust, easy to implement.
  • Demonstrated: unbiasedness of both estimators (algebraic proof); $O(1/\tau)$ disparity convergence for FairCo(Exp) only (Theorem B.3) — impact-fairness convergence is explicitly a conjecture ("we conjecture that analogous proofs apply to other fairness criteria"). Empirics are simulation-only: semi-synthetic news + MovieLens with simulated PBM clicks and known propensities.
  • Gap: no real user-interaction data; position-bias model is assumed given and correctly specified (propensity misspecification untested); FairCo(Imp) has no convergence proof despite being the variant used in most news experiments. Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Amortized merit-based exposure disparity $D^E_\tau(G_i, G_j)$

  • What it computes: input = per-timestep group exposures $Exp_t(G_i) = \frac{1}{|G_i|}\sum_{d \in G_i} p_t(d)$ (marginal examination probabilities from the position-bias model) and group merits $Merit(G_i) = \frac{1}{|G_i|}\sum_{d \in G_i} R(d)$ → operation = difference of time-averaged exposure-to-merit ratios: $D^E_\tau(G_i,G_j) = \frac{\frac{1}{\tau}\sum_t Exp_t(G_i)}{Merit(G_i)} - \frac{\frac{1}{\tau}\sum_t Exp_t(G_j)}{Merit(G_j)}$ → output = a signed scalar; zero means exposure was allocated proportional to relevance, amortized over the whole interaction history.
  • Why it was created: prior fairness-of-exposure work (Singh & Joachims 2018; Biega et al. 2018) assumed relevance/merit is known; in dynamic LTR merit is itself being learned from biased clicks, so fairness must be defined amortized over the learning process with estimated merit.
  • Concrete example: two news groups where 51% of users like $G_{right}$ and 49% like $G_{left}$ — relevance-sorted ranking puts $G_{right}$ in positions 1–10, so a 2% merit difference produces a vastly larger exposure difference; $D^E_\tau$ quantifies exactly this gap.
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the controller's error signal (Eq. 10) and the evaluation metric ("Exposure Unfairness", via the pairwise average $\overline{D}_\tau$, Eq. 16). An impact-based variant $D^I_\tau$ replaces exposure with IPS-clicks.

FairCo (P-controller ranking policy)

  • What it computes: input = current relevance estimate $\hat{R}(d|x)$ and previous-step disparity estimates → operation = sort by $\hat{R}(d|x) + \lambda\, \mathrm{err}_\tau(d)$, where $\mathrm{err}_\tau(d) = (\tau-1)\cdot\max_{G_i}(\hat{D}_{\tau-1}(G_i, G))$ for $d \in G$ → output = the ranking $\sigma_\tau$ shown at step $\tau$. The error term pushes items from under-exposed groups upward, proportional to accumulated disparity.
  • Why it was created: existing fair-LTR algorithms are static (assume known relevance); an LP-based per-step solution is expensive ($O(n^2 + |G|^2)$ variables per request). A proportional controller is cheap, needs no exploration interventions, and provably drives amortized disparity to zero even from arbitrary initial unfairness.
  • Concrete example: $\lambda = 0.01$ works across all experiments; higher $\lambda$ oscillates, lower converges slower but any $\lambda > 0$ suffices asymptotically (Theorem 7.1: $\overline{D}^E_\tau \to 0$ at rate $O(1/\tau)$).
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the main contribution — the ranking policy in Eq. 17, instantiated as FairCo(Exp) and FairCo(Imp).

Unbiased cardinal relevance estimators (IPS)

  • What it computes: (a) global: $\hat{R}^{IPS}(d) = \frac{1}{\tau}\sum_t c_t(d)/p_t(d)$ — clicks reweighted by examination propensity → unbiased estimate of average relevance $R(d)$; (b) conditional: a regression objective $\mathcal{L}^c(w) = \sum_t \sum_d \hat{R}_w(d|x_t)^2 + \frac{c_t(d)}{p_t(d)}(c_t(d) - 2\hat{R}_w(d|x_t))$ whose expectation equals the least-squares objective on the unobserved true relevances (Eq. 14).
  • Why it was created: prior unbiased LTR (Joachims et al. 2017) gives ordinal (ranking-only) estimates; the fairness disparities are cardinal — merit must be estimated on an absolute scale, so a cardinal, position-bias-corrected estimator is required.
  • Concrete example: on the semi-synthetic news data, the Naive click-count estimator's error stagnates at ~0.25 absolute relevance error while $\hat{R}^{IPS}$'s error approaches 0 as users increase (Fig. 2).
  • How it is used in this paper: $\hat{R}^{IPS}(d)$ feeds the merit denominator of the disparities and the global ranking criterion; $\hat{R}^{Reg}(d|x)$ (a 1-hidden-layer NN trained on Eq. 14) is the personalized scorer in the MovieLens experiments.

Position-Based Model (PBM) exposure — reused from Craswell et al. 2008 / Joachims et al. 2017: examination probability depends only on rank, here $p_t(d) = 1/\log_2(\mathrm{rank}(d|\sigma_t)+1)$; treated as a known input, with estimation delegated to prior work.

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: position-bias/propensity model (input, assumed given); running counters for per-group cumulative exposure and IPS merit; any bounded relevance scorer; single hyperparameter $\lambda$ (0.01 used throughout). Compute is trivial — sort plus counter updates per request.
  • Code: available at https://github.com/MarcoMorik/Dynamic-Fairness
  • Modifications: for a fixed-scorer setting, drop the online regression (Eq. 14) and plug the frozen scores into Eq. 17; replace merit-proportional disparity with the desired exposure target; supply or estimate propensities.
  • Unknowns: missing link: sensitivity to misspecified propensities; missing link: behavior with many groups / individual-level fairness at scale; missing link: FairCo(Imp) convergence (conjectured only).
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Singh & Joachims, "Fairness of Exposure in Rankings" (KDD 2018) — origin of the merit-proportional exposure criterion and the LP formulation FairCo adapts as baseline.
  • Biega, Gummadi & Weikum, "Equity of Attention: Amortizing Individual Fairness in Rankings" (SIGIR 2018) — source of the amortized-over-time fairness notion.
  • Joachims, Swaminathan & Schnabel, "Unbiased Learning-to-Rank with Biased Feedback" (WSDM 2017) — the IPS de-biasing framework FairCo extends from ordinal to cardinal estimation.
  • Yadav, Du & Joachims, "Fair Learning-to-Rank from Implicit Feedback" (arXiv 2019) — closest contemporaneous fair-LTR-from-clicks work, cited as related.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = stream of user requests with biased click feedback under a known PBM position-bias model; a scorer being learned online (or a global IPS estimate); disjoint item groups; outputs = per-step ranking; conditions = simulated users, 30–100 items, group-level merit-proportional exposure target, amortized over time. Our setting (SP5): a fixed (pre-trained) scorer with cumulative exposure control — using exposure/frequency as the controlled quantity on top of estimated relevance, per the survey's fixed-scorer + cumulative-exposure-control framing. Differences:

  • Scorer learned online vs. fixed scorer: resolved — FairCo's controller term is additive and post-hoc on top of any $\hat{R}(d|x) \in [0,1]$ (Theorem 7.1 holds for any relevance model), so it transfers directly to a fixed scorer; the online-learning machinery is separable.
  • Known examination propensities $p_t(d)$: missing link — our setting may not have a calibrated position-bias model; FairCo's exposure bookkeeping and merit estimates both depend on it.
  • Group-level fairness target vs. item-level control: resolved — the paper notes individual fairness is the special case of one item per group, though convergence constants degrade with $m$. [SKIM - verify scaling with many groups]
  • Merit-proportional exposure as the target allocation: reference only — our proxy target may differ; the paper notes alternative disparity definitions plug in, but only proportionality is evaluated.
  • Simulated clicks vs. real feedback: missing link — no real-world interaction validation.
