Manipulating Recommender Systems: A Survey of Poisoning Attacks and Countermeasures

Nguyen et al. · ACM Computing Surveys 2024 · 2024 · 74 citations

arXiv:2404.14942 · PDF · SP: SP11

P2Track B

한 줄 요약

recommender poisoning 공격과 대응책을 정리한 ACM Computing Surveys 2024 survey. 입력 신호가 위조 가능하고 방어가 뒤처짐을 확인한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Existing surveys of attacks on recommender systems focus on classic heuristic (shilling-era) attacks and their detection methods only. AI-based attacks (GAN, RL, surrogate-model based) of the last ~7 years are largely uncovered, and — the central gap — countermeasures have never been systematically linked to the properties of the attacks they defend against, making it impossible to assess which mitigation works against which attack, or which attacks evade which defenses.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

A systematic survey (Scopus/DBLP/Google Scholar, top-tier-venue focus) that:

  1. Builds a 5-dimension taxonomy (goal, knowledge, capability, impact, approach) and formalizes each dimension as an optimization problem over the rating matrix (Eqs. 4–10).
  2. Organizes 31 attacks under it: 12 model-agnostic (shilling → PoisonRec RL → GAN-based AUSH/TrialAttack/Leg-UP → KG-enhanced KGAttack) and 19 model-intrinsic (grouped by target: MF, graph, neighborhood, deep-learning, federated recommenders).
  3. Reviews 43 countermeasures split into detection (supervised/semi-supervised/unsupervised over the RDMA-family features, Table 6) and prevention (robust optimization E = L + λ·R, robust M-estimators, GCN dual-task GraphRfi, differential privacy, adversarial training APT, provable PORE).
  4. Maps countermeasure↔attack effectiveness in both directions (Fig. 4: which defenses catch which attacks; Fig. 5: which attacks are resilient against which defenses).
  5. Releases a companion repository (github.com/tamlhp/awesome-recsys-poisoning) with papers, code, datasets.
Key Results주요 결과
  • Organizes 31 attacks (12 model-agnostic, 19 model-intrinsic) and 43 countermeasures (35 detection, 8 prevention) — the largest coverage among the 8 compared surveys (Table 1: only this one covers AI-based prevention, unified taxonomy, and attack↔countermeasure links).
  • Targeted (promotion/demotion) attacks dominate over untargeted; ~2/3 of attacks aim at availability, ~1/3 at replication of the victim model.
  • Fake users + fake ratings is the near-universal capability; fake links (knowledge graphs) and fake images (cold-start visual channels) are each used by only a single attack — flagged as an emerging side-information vulnerability.
  • HR@k is the dominant attack-evaluation metric (~2/3 of studies); MovieLens used in >2/3 of studies, Amazon in ~half — evidence of narrow evaluation ecology.
  • Only a small set of countermeasure↔attack weakness dependencies exist (Fig. 5), e.g. unnoticeability-oriented GAN attacks (AUSH, TrialAttack, Leg-UP) evade classic feature-based detectors (CM-01, CM-04); recent-years imbalance: many new attacks, few new countermeasures (§6.4).

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Poisoning attack (on a recommender system)

  • What it computes: adversary-crafted training data (fake users + fake ratings R′) → injected into the rating matrix R before/while the model trains → a corrupted model whose recommendation output favors the adversary's goal (promote/demote items) persistently, across users and sessions.
  • Why it was created: distinguishes training-time manipulation from adversarial (inference-time) attacks, which only perturb inputs temporarily without altering the model.
  • Concrete example: crowdsourced fake 5-star reviews injected into an e-commerce platform's training data so a target product enters top-K lists for a broad user population.
  • How it is used in this paper: the paper's entire subject; formalized as constrained optimization, e.g. untargeted attack min_{R′} ||R′|| s.t. f(⟨u,i⟩;θ) ≠ r_ui, and promotion attack minimizing loss on the target item t only.

Five-dimension attack taxonomy (adversary's goal / knowledge / capability / impact / approach)

  • What it computes: a classification scheme mapping each of 31 surveyed attacks onto 5 axes — goal (untargeted vs. targeted promotion/demotion/ancillary), knowledge (white/grey/black-box), capability (fake users, fake ratings, fake co-occurrence, fake links, fake images), impact (availability, replication, unnoticeability), approach (injection vs. simulation).
  • Why it was created: prior surveys covered mostly classic heuristic attacks and detection only; no unified taxonomy existed to link attack properties to which countermeasures work against them.
  • Concrete example: PipAttack (MI-15) = targeted promotion / grey-box / fake users + ratings / injection / unnoticeability — it exploits popularity bias in federated recommenders by making fake users mimic popular items.
  • How it is used in this paper: organizes Tables 2–3 (30+ attacks) and drives the §5.3/§5.4 attack↔countermeasure effectiveness mapping.

Model-agnostic vs. model-intrinsic attacks

  • What it computes: a partition of attacks by whether they need knowledge of the target's learning algorithm. Model-agnostic: min ||R′ − R||² over injected ratings, works against any system. Model-intrinsic: optimizes a combined objective E = α·L + β·A (recommendation loss + attack objective) against a known model family (MF, graph, neighborhood, deep, federated).
  • Why it was created: effectiveness and required knowledge differ sharply; agnostic attacks are weaker but realistic, intrinsic attacks are stronger but need grey/white-box access.
  • Concrete example: shilling attack (Lam & Riedl 2004) is model-agnostic; Fang et al.'s graph-attack (MI-03) optimizes fake rating scores specifically for random-walk-based recommenders.
  • How it is used in this paper: top-level split of §4 (12 agnostic + 19 intrinsic attacks).

Detection features: RDMA / WDMA / LengthVar / DegSim (model-agnostic) and MeanVar / FMTD / FAC / FMD (model-intrinsic)

  • What it computes: per-user statistics over the rating matrix that separate fake from genuine profiles — e.g. RDMA_u = (Σ|r_u^i − r̄_i| / n_{r_i}) / n_u, the user's average rating deviation from item means weighted by inverse item rating-frequency.
  • Why it was created: injected profiles exhibit statistical anomalies (extreme target ratings, near-mean filler ratings, unusually long profiles, abnormally high similarity to neighbors) that genuine users do not.
  • Concrete example: a fake profile rating a target item 5 while item mean is 2.1, with filler items rated at the mean, yields high RDMA + high FMTD simultaneously.
  • How it is used in this paper: columns of Table 6 organizing 35 detection methods (CM-01…CM-35) by which features/traits they use.

Detection traits: crowdability / skewed ratings / co-occurrence / user-user graph — trait taxonomy (extending Sundar et al. 2020) grouping the signals detectors exploit: rating-frequency spikes on target items, deviation of fake ratings from item averages, injected co-visitation patterns, and graph-similarity clusters of colluding profiles. Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: nothing to run for the survey itself; individual attacks/defenses need CF training pipelines + MovieLens/Amazon datasets.
  • Code: companion repository at https://github.com/tamlhp/awesome-recsys-poisoning (papers, code links, datasets for the reviewed studies).
  • Modifications: for our SP, the useful extraction is the capability/impact taxonomy re-read through an exposure-frequency lens (fake co-occurrence and popularity-bias exploitation as frequency-signal forgery); no method transfer needed.
  • Unknowns: missing link: whether any surveyed attack quantifies its effect on exposure distribution (their ER@k metric suggests at least one does — identify which in deep read).
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Zhang et al., "PipAttack: Poisoning Federated Recommender Systems for Manipulating Item Promotion" (WSDM 2022) — exploits popularity bias directly; the clearest single instance of frequency-proxy gaming in this survey.
  • Yang et al., "Fake Co-visitation Injection Attacks to Recommender Systems" (NDSS 2017) — poisons co-occurrence frequency counts, the exact proxy class SP11 studies.
  • Sundar et al., "Understanding Shilling Attacks and Their Detection Traits" (IEEE Access 2020) — the detection-trait framework this survey extends; relevant for frequency-anomaly (crowdability) detection.
  • Jia et al., "PORE: Provably Robust Recommender Systems against Data Poisoning Attacks" (2023) — the only provable-robustness defense reviewed; candidate for whether robustness guarantees carry over to exposure metrics.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = user-item rating matrix (explicit or implicit CF), outputs = top-K recommendation lists; adversary injects fake users/ratings at training time; evaluation on MovieLens/Amazon-class datasets with HR@k, NDCG@k. Our setting (SP11, exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy): systems where an item's exposure/impression frequency serves as (or feeds) the ranking signal; we care whether frequency-derived proxies are manipulable and how manipulation of interaction counts distorts exposure allocation. Differences:

  • Signal attacked: this survey's attacks poison ratings/interactions, which are upstream of popularity/exposure statistics — directly instantiates the "engagement counts are forgeable" failure mode of frequency proxies: resolved (PipAttack MI-15 explicitly exploits popularity bias; fake co-occurrence attacks inflate co-visit frequencies).
  • Objective framing: paper frames outcomes as item promotion/demotion in top-K, our SP frames it as exposure-share distortion; mapping promotion success (HR@k, ER@k — note "exposure rate at rank k" appears in their metric Table 4) onto exposure-frequency distortion: missing link — the survey does not quantify exposure-distribution shifts, only hit-rate style metrics.
  • Defense relevance: detection features (RDMA, crowdability = abnormal rating-frequency spikes on a target item) are frequency-anomaly detectors, i.e., defenses that themselves treat frequency as the signal: resolved (usable as evidence that frequency statistics are both the attack surface and the detection signal).
  • Domain: general CF recommenders vs. our broader ranking-proxy scope (search, feeds, LLM-era rankers): reference only.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the gap the paper targets is organizational (countermeasures never linked to attack properties), and it resolves exactly that by building the taxonomy and the bidirectional attack↔countermeasure maps rather than patching around it; note this is a Direct solution to a survey-level gap, not a solution to poisoning itself (which it explicitly leaves open, §6.4).
