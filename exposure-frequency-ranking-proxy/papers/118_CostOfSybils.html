The Cost of Sybils, Credible Commitments, and False-Name Proof Mechanisms

Mazorra & Della Penna · arXiv · 2023 · 6 citations

arXiv:2301.12813 · PDF · SP: SP11

P4Track A

한 줄 요약

Sybil 비용과 신뢰 가능한 커밋의 관점에서 false-name-proof mechanism을 분석한다. 개수 비례 보상 분할은 pie-shrinking 없이는 항상 신원 부풀리기가 가능하고, 저항은 지수적 후생 감소(r(n)=nR/2^{n-1})를 요구함을 증명한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Mechanisms that cannot observe the true number of participants must rely on self-reported identities. Prior work splits into three incompatible cost regimes (mechanism design: identity cost infinite; Sybil-attack security: cost zero/finite for one attacker only; false-name proofness: cost zero for all), so there was no single framework to ask when a mechanism resists strategic identity multiplication at a given creation cost $c$, and no analysis of Sybil-proof collusion (bidding rings) or fair-division (cake-cutting) mechanisms, nor of what happens when Sybils can credibly commit to strategies.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

[SKIM - verify details in deep read] The paper (1) defines the Sybil extension game with a variable identity-creation cost unifying the three literatures; (2) gives the necessary-and-sufficient dominance condition for Sybil-proofness and shows mechanisms from the literature (VCG/Shapley/Hybrid cost sharing, McAfee–McMillan bidding rings, proportional reward splitting) fail it; (3) constructs optimal Sybil-proof mechanisms via "pie shrinking with crowding": reward schedule $r(n) = \frac{n}{2^{n-1}}R$ is Sybil-proof for all $c \ge 0$ and welfare-optimal in its class; (4) defines Sybil-commitment games and proves that credible commitment can break otherwise Sybil-proof mechanisms, with equilibrium welfare bounded $O(n/2^n)$ for Sybil-commitment-proof games.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Optimal prior-free Sybil-proof reward distribution: $r(n) = \frac{n}{2^{n-1}}R$; consequently no efficient prior-free Sybil-proof reward mechanism exists — welfare must decay exponentially in the number of participants (Proposition 3).
  • Without Sybil-proofing, the reward-sharing game with identity cost $c$ has equilibrium welfare $\Theta(R/n)$, a welfare loss ratio $\Theta(n)$ ("Price of Identity") vs. optimum (Proposition 1).
  • No truthful Sybil-proof cake-cutting mechanism is $\alpha$-proportional for $\alpha > 1/2^{n-1}$; worst-case social welfare of truthful Sybil-proof cake-cutting is exactly $n/2^{n-1}$ (Propositions 7–8).
  • Every efficient bidding ring in second-price auctions is not Sybil-proof, but profitable Sybil-proof bidding rings exist (Propositions 9–10).
  • VCG, Shapley, and Hybrid cost-sharing mechanisms for public excludable goods are not Sybil-proof (Proposition 11); Sybil-proof games (e.g. concave pro-rata, Cournot) can fail Sybil-commitment-proofness when $c = O(1/n^2)$ (Theorem 12).

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Sybil extension game

  • What it computes: takes a symmetric normal-form game $U$ over action space $A$ and a Sybil cost function $C(x,y)$ (cost for a player creating $x$ identities when others create $y$; typically $C(x,y)=cx$) → extends the action space so each player chooses a number of identities plus an action per identity → player $i$'s payoff is the sum of its Sybils' payoffs minus the creation cost: $\tilde U_i(a) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal I_i} U_j(a) - C(|\mathcal I_i|, |\cup_{k \ne i}\mathcal I_k|)$.
  • Why it was created: classical mechanism design implicitly assumes identity-creation cost $c=\infty$ for everyone; the Sybil-attack literature assumes $c$ finite for one attacker and $\infty$ for honest players; the false-name-proof literature assumes $c=0$. The variable-cost game unifies all three streams in one model.
  • Concrete example: reward sharing $U(x,y) = \frac{x}{x+y}R - cx$ with $R=10$, $c=0.1$, $y=3$: reporting 1 identity yields $2.4$, reporting 2 yields $3.8$ — so single-identity reporting is not dominant.
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the base framework; every mechanism studied (reward distribution, cake-cutting, bidding rings, cost sharing) is analyzed as a Sybil extension game.

Sybil-proofness / false-name proofness (unified, cost-parameterized)

  • What it computes: a mechanism is Sybil-proof if for every player, every Sybil set, and every action tuple, some single-identity strategy weakly dominates the multi-identity strategy (Eq. 1–2 of the paper). Proof for $C=0$ implies proof for all $C$.
  • Why it was created: the two literatures used incompatible cost assumptions; this gives one necessary-and-sufficient condition covering both.
  • Concrete example: second-price auctions with atomless valuation distributions are Sybil-proof; VCG for combinatorial settings is not.
  • How it is used in this paper: the test applied to mechanisms from the literature (cost sharing, bidding rings, cake-cutting) to show which fail.

Pie shrinking with crowding

  • What it computes: a reward schedule $r : \mathbb N \to [0,R]$ that decreases the total distributed welfare as more identities are reported; the optimal Sybil-proof schedule is $r(n) = \frac{n}{2^{n-1}}R$ (Proposition 3).
  • Why it was created: naive proportional splitting ($r(n)=R$) invites identity inflation; shrinking the pie makes single-identity self-reporting a strictly dominant strategy.
  • Concrete example: with $n=3$ reported identities the pool shrinks to $\frac{3}{4}R$; each player gets $r(n)/n = R/2^{n-1}$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the constructive tool for Sybil-proof reward distribution, cake-cutting ($1/2^{n-1}$-proportional mechanism), and Sybil-proof bidding rings in second-price auctions.

Sybil-commitment game [SKIM - verify in deep read]

  • What it computes: a two-phase extension where players first commit Sybil identities with declared (possibly fake) utility functions — e.g. via smart contracts — and then the Sybils play a complete-information game.
  • Why it was created: credible commitment devices (blockchains) let an attacker's fake identities behave as independent rational agents, which breaks mechanisms that are Sybil-proof without commitment.
  • Concrete example: Cournot oligopoly with $n=10$, $\beta=10$, $c=0.001$: best response is to commit 11 Sybils.
  • How it is used in this paper: Theorem 12 shows any symmetric Sybil-commitment-proof game with small cost $c = O(1/l^2)$ has equilibrium welfare $O(n/2^n)$; pro-rata and Cournot games are Sybil-proof but not Sybil-commitment-proof.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: pure theory; no data or compute. Reproducing the one simulation needs the pro-rata arbitrage-game setup from Johnson et al. 2022.
  • Code: available at https://github.com/BrunoMazorra/CostsOfSybils (simulation only).
  • Modifications: applying to a ranking-proxy setting requires mapping "identities reported" to "exposure units created" and defining the analog of the reward $R$ (visibility/rank mass) and cost $c$ (cost of generating an exposure unit). [SKIM]
  • Unknowns: missing link: whether the pie-shrinking construction has a meaningful analog when the "mechanism" (a ranking function) does not control a transferable budget.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = self-reported identities + actions in a symmetric game; outputs = allocations/payments; conditions = players can mint identities at cost $c$ per identity, mechanism cannot observe true player count, permissionless/anonymous environment (blockchain-motivated), game-theoretic equilibrium analysis, no data. Our setting (SP11, exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy survey): ranking/scoring systems that use frequency-of-exposure or occurrence counts as a proxy signal; the concern is whether count-based proxies can be inflated by an actor who controls how many "units" (identities, mentions, documents) present themselves to the measuring mechanism. Differences:

  • Domain: auction/reward mechanisms vs. ranking/retrieval proxies — reference only; the transferable content is the abstract result that any count-based allocation without pie-shrinking is manipulable, and that resistance costs exponential efficiency.
  • The paper assumes strategic rational agents with known cost $c$; ranking-proxy settings usually lack an explicit cost model for creating exposure units — missing link.
  • The paper's mechanisms control payoffs (can shrink the pie); a ranking proxy typically cannot redesign the reward it implicitly hands out (visibility) — missing link [SKIM - verify].
  • Equilibrium/incentive analysis vs. empirical measurement of proxy validity — reference only.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper attacks the root cause (the incentive to multiply self-reported identities under a count-sensitive allocation rule) by characterizing exactly which mechanisms are incentive-compatible against it and proving the unavoidable efficiency price, rather than patching detection of fake identities downstream.
