Modeling Content Creator Incentives on Algorithm-Curated Platforms

Hron et al. · ICLR 2023 · 2023 · 58 citations

arXiv:2206.13102 · PDF · SP: SP9

P0Track C

한 줄 요약

알고리즘 큐레이션 플랫폼에서 creator 인센티브를 모델링한 ICLR 2023 논문. 배분 규칙의 온도 파라미터에 따라 콘텐츠 균질화 상전이(τ-임계값)가 발생함을 보인다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Prior game-theoretic models of exposure-maximizing content creators restrict creators to choosing from a predefined finite item catalog, which excludes the dominant class of real deployed rankers — matrix factorization and (deep) two-tower models whose continuous embedding spaces admit infinitely many possible items. Separately, platforms have no ex-ante tool for assessing how an algorithm update shifts creator incentives: A/B tests cannot randomize producers into treatment groups and content adaptation lags deployment, so misaligned incentives (junk/divisive content, as in the 2018 Facebook feed update) surface only after harm and are handled post-hoc by moderation.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Two contributions:

  1. Exposure games (Definition 1): $n$ producers each pick a unit-norm embedding $s_i \in S^{d-1}$ to maximize expected exposure $u_i(s) = \mathbb{E}_{c \sim P_c}\big[\mathrm{softmax}(\tau^{-1}[\langle c, s_j\rangle]_{j=1}^n)_i\big]$ against a fixed demand distribution $P_c$ of user embeddings. Theory: mixed NE always exist (Thm 1); pure NE need not exist for either hardmax or softmax (Thm 2); for large enough $\tau$, all producers collapsing onto the normalized mean consumer $\bar{c}$ is an $\epsilon$-PNE with $\epsilon_\tau \le \epsilon/\tau$ (Thm 3); the same demand distribution can yield zero, one, multiple, or infinitely many $\epsilon$-NE depending solely on $\tau$ (Cor 1). Non-negative games (embeddings in the positive orthant, e.g. NMF) behave differently from unconstrained ones — e.g. PNE always exist for $n=d=2$ non-negative hardmax games but may not otherwise (Prop 1).
  2. Pre-deployment audit: fit a real recommender (PMF/NMF on MovieLens-100K, LastFM-360K), take its user embeddings as $P_c$, run simultaneous projected gradient ascent (Eq. 4) to find $\epsilon$-LNE, and read off properties of the incentivized content (diversity, group targeting) as a function of design choices ($\tau$, $d$, rating model).

Headline qualitative finding: more algorithmic exploration (higher $\tau$) incentivizes homogeneous, broadly appealing content; less exploration incentivizes niche specialization — the opposite of the intuition that exploration promotes diversity.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Pure NE can fail to exist even with $n=2$ producers in $d=2$ (Thm 2), while mixed NE always exist (Thm 1) — so stable creator content configurations are not guaranteed and persistent oscillation is possible at low $\tau$.
  • For $\tau$ above a threshold $\tau_0$, all-producers-at-$\bar{c}$ is an $\epsilon$-PNE with $\epsilon_\tau \le \epsilon/\tau$, and all better-responses concentrate within $\delta = 2\epsilon/(\|\hat{c}\|-\epsilon)$ of $\bar{c}$ (Thm 3): exploration → homogeneous mainstream content.
  • On MovieLens and LastFM, producer strategies collapse to ~1 cluster already at $\tau = 1$ for both $d=3$ and $d=50$; at $\tau = 0.01$ the clusters-to-producers ratio is high (specialization) — Figure 4.
  • Gender-targeting bias of incentivized content grows with embedding dimension ($d = 50$ ≫ $d = 3$), and NMF incentivizes more biased content than PMF relative to the pre-adaptation baseline on MovieLens (users 71% male) — Figure 5.
  • On LastFM, incentivized content sits closer to male creators' baseline embeddings, more so at $d = 50$ and (for the distance metric) under PMF — quantifying that strategic adaptation is easier for the majority creator group (Figure 6).
  • Hardmax ($\tau = 0$, i.e. deterministic top-1) NE-finding reduces to an NP-complete hitting-set problem; only exponential worst-case algorithms known (Appendix B).

Category: theoretical (game-theoretic model + existence/characterization results) with a system/audit demonstration. Context: builds on facility-location / catalog-constrained creator games (Hotelling 1929; Ben-Porat et al. 2018–2020; Raifer et al. 2017), strategic classification / performative prediction (Hardt et al. 2016; Perdomo et al. 2020), and concurrent work of Jagadeesan et al. 2022 (supply-side equilibria; hardmax non-negative special case with production cost instead of norm constraint). Directly relevant to this survey's creator-side track: it formalizes how exposure allocation rules reshape the content distribution itself. Correctness: proofs provided in appendix for all claims; experiments use 5 seeds × 10 restarts with a Riemannian second-order filter, and the appendix separately reports the stricter confirmed-LNE subset. Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: seemingly innocuous algorithmic choices significantly affect equilibria; exploration incentivizes uniformity; the audit can flag incentive misalignment pre-deployment.
  • Demonstrated: existence/sensitivity theorems proven for the stylized model; the exploration→homogenization effect confirmed empirically on two datasets; bias findings shown via aggregate statistics of predicted ratings.
  • Gap (acknowledged by the authors): the behavior model assumes rational, omniscient producers with full control of their item embeddings, linear utility in exposure, a fixed attention pool, and no recommendation diversification; no validation against real creator behavior (platform data inaccessible to academics). The audit's predictions are illustrative, not validated against observed post-deployment content shifts. [SKIM - verify appendix caveats in deep read]
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Exposure game

  • What it computes: input = embedding dimension $d$, demand distribution $P_c$ over user embeddings, $n$ producers each choosing a unit-norm item embedding $s_i \in S^{d-1}$; operation = each producer maximizes expected exposure $u_i(s) = \mathbb{E}_{c\sim P_c}[p_i(c)]$ where $p_i(c) = \mathrm{softmax}([\tau^{-1}\langle c, s_{i'}\rangle]_{i'=1}^n)_i$; output = a strategy profile (and its Nash equilibria) describing what content the ranking algorithm implicitly incentivizes.
  • Why it was created: prior creator-incentive game models (Ben-Porat et al., Raifer et al.) constrain creators to a predefined finite item catalog, which excludes factorization-based and two-tower rankers whose continuous embedding space allows infinitely many possible items.
  • Concrete example: with $n = d = 2$ and $P_c$ placing mass $1/3$ on three consumers $2\pi/3$ apart on the circle, no pure Nash equilibrium exists at any temperature (Theorem 2's counterexample).
  • How it is used in this paper: the central object — all theory (Section 2) characterizes its equilibria; the audit (Section 3) finds its local equilibria numerically on MovieLens/LastFM.

Temperature $\tau$ as exploration knob

  • What it computes: input = inner-product scores $\langle c, s_i\rangle$; operation = softmax with temperature $\tau \ge 0$; output = exposure probabilities. $\tau = 0$ is hardmax (deterministic top-1); $\tau > 0$ spreads exposure over lower-ranked items, modeling ranked position effects and algorithmic exploration.
  • Why it was created: to let a single model interpolate between deterministic top-k ranking and stochastic/exploratory rankers, and to isolate exploration's effect on creator incentives.
  • Concrete example: at $\tau = 1$ producers collapse into one cluster near the average consumer embedding on both MovieLens and LastFM, regardless of $d$ and $n$; at $\tau = 0.01$ they scatter into distinct niches.
  • How it is used in this paper: the main axis of both theory (Theorem 3, Corollary 1) and experiments (Figures 4–6).

Pre-deployment (ex-ante) incentive audit

  • What it computes: input = trained recommender (user embeddings define $P_c$) + a creator behavior model (exposure game); operation = run simultaneous gradient ascent on producer utilities with reparametrization $s_i = \theta_i/\|\theta_i\|$ (Eq. 4), filter candidates with a Riemannian second-derivative test; output = approximate local Nash equilibria = the content the algorithm incentivizes, examined before deployment.
  • Why it was created: A/B testing cannot measure producer impact because producers cannot be randomized into treatment groups and content adaptation lags deployment; harms are otherwise handled only post-hoc via filtering/moderation.
  • Concrete example: the MovieLens/LastFM audit used gradient ascent runs (5 recommender seeds × 10 restarts per configuration, 50K iterations) over $\{$PMF, NMF$\}$ × $d \in \{3, 50\}$ × $\tau \in \{0.01, 0.1, 1.0\}$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the second headline contribution; Section 3 demonstrates it end-to-end.

$\epsilon$-local Nash equilibrium ($\epsilon$-LNE) [SKIM - verify formal definition chain in deep read]

  • What it computes: a strategy profile where no producer can gain more than $\epsilon$ utility by a small (local) unilateral deviation; found by first-order local search and certified (sufficient condition only) via the Riemannian gradient/Hessian test on $S^{d-1}$ (Definition 3).
  • Why it was created: pure NE need not exist (Theorem 2) and hardmax NE-finding requires solving an NP-complete hitting-set problem, so a weaker, gradient-findable solution concept is needed for the practical audit.
  • Concrete example: runs are ruled out if any Riemannian gradient norm exceeds $10^{-5}\sqrt{d}$ or the Riemannian Hessian has a positive eigenvalue.
  • How it is used in this paper: the solution concept actually computed in all experiments.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: a trained factorization/two-tower recommender's user embeddings; Python + JAX/numpy/scikit-surprise/reclab; the paper's full sweep used ~50K CPU hours (72 AWS 4-core machines × 5h for final runs), but single-configuration audits are far cheaper.
  • Code: referenced as provided (config.py, manifold.py, optimisation.py named in appendix); [SKIM - locate repository URL].
  • Modifications: for this survey's setting one would need (a) a feature-space (not embedding-space) creator action model, (b) closing the loop with recommender retraining on the strategically produced content, (c) extending exposure to explicit position-bias/top-k models.
  • Unknowns: missing link: how well $\epsilon$-LNE of the stylized game predict real creator adaptation; missing link: equilibrium behavior in the $\beta \to \infty$ limit connecting to Jagadeesan et al.'s production-cost model; missing link: non-negativity effects beyond $d = 2$.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Supply-Side Equilibria in Recommender Systems (Jagadeesan, Garg & Steinhardt, 2022) — concurrent complementary model (hardmax non-negative exposure games with production cost instead of unit-norm constraint); studies genre formation and profitability; a natural sibling read for SP9.
  • Content Provider Dynamics and Coordination in Recommendation Ecosystems (Ben-Porat et al., NeurIPS 2020) — the finite-catalog predecessor line this paper generalizes; covers repeated interactions and user welfare that exposure games omit.
  • Towards Content Provider Aware Recommender Systems (Zhan et al., TheWebConf 2021) — designs rankers optimizing both user and provider utility; the "fix" side to this paper's "diagnose" side.
  • Top-K Off-Policy Correction for a REINFORCE Recommender System (Chen et al., WSDM 2019) — source of the softmax exposure model used here in a deployed YouTube system; grounds Eq. (2)'s realism.
  • Strategic Ranking (Liu, Garg & Borgs, 2022) — closest strategic-adaptation-under-finite-resources analysis; single-score competitors vs. this paper's multi-dimensional embedding scores.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = fixed trained factorization recommender (user embeddings → empirical demand distribution $P_c$) on MovieLens-100K / LastFM-360K; outputs = ($\epsilon$-local) Nash equilibria of creator embedding choices; conditions = creators fully control unit-norm item embeddings, exposure follows a softmax over inner-product scores, static one-shot game, fixed user population. Our setting (SP9 — creator-side failure modes of exposure/frequency as a ranking proxy): real ranking pipelines where exposure frequency both measures and confers quality, creators manipulate content features (not embeddings directly), interactions are repeated, and the user population and attention pool shift. Differences:

  • Creators control features, not embeddings; the feature→embedding map is unknown to them: missing link (paper explicitly abstracts this away as its least realistic assumption).
  • Static one-shot equilibrium vs. repeated ranker-retraining feedback loops (exposure → engagement data → retrained ranker): missing link — the paper fixes the recommender; the exposure→training-signal loop central to this survey is outside the model.
  • Softmax exposure (Eq. 2) vs. real position-bias/top-k cascade exposure: resolved: partially — $\tau$ interpolates toward hardmax and the softmax form is used in real systems (YouTube, Chen et al. 2019), but exact top-k with position curves is not covered (hardmax case is computationally intractable here).
  • Fixed finite attention pool vs. growing/shifting platform usage: reference only (acknowledged limitation).
  • Rating-prediction factorization models vs. modern LLM-based or multi-objective rankers: reference only — the theory needs only inner-product scoring, so two-tower LLM retrieval embeddings are formally covered, but nothing beyond inner-product scoring is.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper attacks the root cause of gaming-like content shifts (the incentive structure the exposure allocation rule itself induces on creators) and proposes auditing that structure ex-ante, rather than patching the symptom (junk content) with post-hoc filtering/moderation, which it explicitly positions itself as complementing.
