A Unified Approach to Fair Online Learning via Blackwell Approachability

Chzhen et al. · NeurIPS 2021 · 2021 · 15 citations

arXiv:2106.12242 · PDF · SP: SP6

P0Track A

한 줄 요약

Blackwell 접근가능성으로 공정 온라인 학습을 통일하는 이론 틀을 제공한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Fair online learning results were fragmented: each objective-constraint pair (no-regret + equal payoffs in Blum et al. 2018; group-wise calibration in Hébert-Johnson et al. 2018 and Gupta et al. 2021; equal opportunity under partial feedback in Bechavod et al. 2019) was handled with different tools and different information models, with no general criterion for when a learning objective is even compatible with a fairness constraint — let alone what the optimal trade-off is when they are incompatible. Additionally, classical Blackwell approachability assumes the payoff structure (here, dependent on the context distribution $Q$) is known, which fails when the Player is unaware of sensitive contexts and $Q$ must be estimated.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Cast fair online learning as Blackwell approachability with stochastic contexts:

  1. Setting: repeated game; each round, $(x_t, s_t) \sim Q$ i.i.d.; the Player sees only $x_t$ (unawareness), Nature sees $G(x_t,s_t)$ ($=x_t$ or $=(x_t,s_t)$); both pick actions; Player observes reward and $s_t$ after acting.
  2. Reduction: each objective and each fairness constraint is a (vector payoff, closed convex target set) brick; combine by product. Objective + constraint compatible $\iff$ product set approachable.
  3. Main characterization (Theorem 1): under Assumptions 1 (sequential estimation of $Q$ with $\sum_t \frac{1}{t}\sqrt{E[TV^2(\hat{Q}_t,Q)]} < \infty$) and 2 (bounded payoff), $C$ closed convex is approachable iff

$$\forall (q^{G(x,s)})\ \exists (p^x)\ \text{s.t.}\ \int_{X\times S} m(p^x, q^{G(x,s)}, x, s)\, dQ(x,s) \in C,$$ with explicit $L^2$ rate $E[d_T^2] \le \sqrt{K/T} + 4\|m\|_{\infty,2}\bar\Delta_T$.

  1. Strategy: Blackwell's projection-based strategy with $\hat{Q}_t$ plugged in place of $Q$ (Eq. 1); Section 6 extends to unknown target sets estimated at doubling epochs (Theorem 2).
Key Results주요 결과
  • Theorem 1: closed convex $C$ approachable iff Blackwell condition (Eq. 2) holds under estimated $Q$; rate $E[d_T^2] \le \sqrt{K/T} + 4\|m\|_{\infty,2}\bar\Delta_T$ where $\bar\Delta_T \to 0$ under Assumption 1.
  • Vanilla calibration + demographic parity: always simultaneously achievable (Example 1) — a constant forecast at the population mean satisfies both up to $1/(2N)$.
  • Group-wise no-regret with unaware Player: achievable only in two cases (Prop. 1) — sensitive context irrelevant to payoffs, or group-conditional supports disjoint ($supp(Q^s)$ pairwise disjoint); counter-examples otherwise.
  • Pareto frontier (Props. 2-3): with Nature aware, $\varepsilon^\star(\delta_\tau) \in [1 - \tau t^*,\ 1 - \tau t^* + 1/N]$ where $t^* = TV(Q^0,Q^1)$ — group-wise calibration at $1/N$ is impossible unless $t^* = 1$; with Nature unaware, $\varepsilon^\star(\delta_\tau) \in [(1-\tau)t^*,\ (1-\tau)t^* + 1/N]$ — calibration achievable at $\tau=1$, and both goals jointly achievable iff $t^* = 0$ (contexts independent of sensitive attribute).
  • Theorem 2: the same characterization holds when the target set itself is unknown and estimated at doubling epochs (Assumption 3: convexity, high-probability super-set, $L^2$-Hausdorff control).
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: theoretical (new framework + characterization theorems; no experiments). Context: builds on Blackwell (1956), the approachability-calibration-regret equivalences (Abernethy et al. 2011; Perchet 2014; Mannor & Stoltz 2010); unifies fair-online-learning results of Blum et al. 2018, Hébert-Johnson et al. 2018 (multicalibration), Gupta et al. 2021, Bechavod et al. 2019. Correctness: full proofs in appendix; assumptions explicit and weak (estimation rate $1/\ln^3 T$ suffices; boundedness). No empirical claims to scrutinize. [SKIM - proof details not verified line-by-line] Contributions: (1) necessary-and-sufficient approachability condition under unknown context distribution; (2) unified (im)possibility results for objective-constraint pairs; (3) exact Pareto frontier for group-wise calibration vs. demographic parity, governed by $TV(Q^0,Q^1)$; (4) approachability theory for unknown target sets. Clarity: highly reproducible in the mathematical sense — strategies are explicit (Eqs. 1, 7), though the per-round min-max over measurable families of distributions is computationally nontrivial (authors note complexity at least linear in the number of groups).

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: a unified approach covering fair online learning objectives and constraints; explicit criteria for tractability plus explicit strategies.
  • Demonstrated: full proofs for the stated theorems; instantiations limited to two objectives (no-regret, calibration), three constraints (group-wise, DP, equal payoffs), and mostly $|S| = 2$ groups for the trade-off results.
  • Gap: (acknowledged in Section 7) the trade-off characterization is only for two groups; the generic strategy is computationally expensive (at least linear, possibly polynomial, in the number of groups) versus e.g. Gupta et al.'s surrogate-loss strategy with log-in-groups sample complexity; no ranking or exposure setting is treated; monitoring is bandit-level, extension to partial monitoring left open. Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Blackwell approachability (with stochastic contexts and unknown context distribution)

  • What it computes: input = a repeated vector-payoff game between Player and Nature with per-round contexts $(x_t, s_t) \sim Q$ (non-sensitive + sensitive), a vector payoff function $m: A \times B \times X \times S \to \mathbb{R}^d$, and a closed target set $C \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$; operation = the Player picks mixed actions minimizing the worst-case inner product $\langle \bar{m}_t - \bar{c}_t, \int m\, d\hat{Q}_t \rangle$ using a sequentially estimated distribution $\hat{Q}_t$; output = average payoff vector $\bar{m}_T$ converging to $C$ a.s. (or a certificate that no strategy can achieve this).
  • Why it was created: classical Blackwell approachability (1956) assumes no stochastic contexts / known distribution; fair online learning requires contexts (sensitive attribute unseen by the Player at decision time) whose joint distribution $Q$ is unknown and must be estimated online.
  • Concrete example: approximate calibration with $N$ forecast bins corresponds to approaching $C_{cal} = \{v \in \mathbb{R}^N : \|v\|_1 \le 1/N\}$ under payoff $m_{cal}(k,b) = ((a^{(k')} - b)\mathbb{I}\{k=k'\})_{k'}$.
  • How it is used in this paper: Theorem 1 gives a necessary-and-sufficient condition (Eq. 2) for approachability under estimated $Q$; Assumption 1 requires only $\sum_t \frac{1}{t}\sqrt{E[TV^2(\hat{Q}_t, Q)]} < \infty$ (a $1/\ln^3 T$ estimation rate suffices).

Objective/constraint "bricks" as product target sets

  • What it computes: input = a learning objective (no-regret, calibration) and a fairness constraint (demographic parity, equal average payoffs, group-wise control); operation = each is written as an (payoff function, convex target set) pair, combined by taking products $m = (m_{obj}, m_{fair})$, $C = C_{obj} \times C_{fair}$; output = a single approachability problem whose feasibility answers "are objective and constraint simultaneously satisfiable?"
  • Why it was created: prior fair-online-learning results (Blum et al. 2018; Hébert-Johnson et al. 2018; Gupta et al. 2021; Bechavod et al. 2019) used disparate ad-hoc tools per objective-constraint combination; the product-set formulation makes (im)possibility results derivable "nearly effortlessly" from one geometric condition.
  • Concrete example: demographic parity with two groups is $m_{DP}(k,s) = (a^{(k)}\mathbb{I}\{s=0\}/\gamma_0,\ a^{(k)}\mathbb{I}\{s=1\}/\gamma_1)$ with $C_{DP} = \{(u,v): |u-v| \le 1/N\}$.
  • How it is used in this paper: Section 4 instantiates the condition on vanilla calibration + DP (achievable), group-wise no-regret (achievable only when sensitive context is irrelevant or group supports are disjoint), and no-regret + equalized payoffs (mirrors Blum et al.'s impossibility).

Pareto frontier between calibration error and DP violation via total variation

  • What it computes: input = trade-off parameter $\tau \in [0,1]$; operation = relax targets to $C^\varepsilon_{gr\text{-}cal}$ and $C^\delta_{DP}$ and solve the max-min of Eq. (6); output = the exact frontier $\varepsilon^\star(\delta_\tau)$ with $\delta_\tau = \tau \cdot TV(Q^0, Q^1)$.
  • Why it was created: group-wise calibration and demographic parity are generally incompatible; prior work checked compatibility but did not characterize the optimal trade-off in the online setting.
  • Concrete example: with Nature unaware ($G(x,s)=x$), $\varepsilon^\star(\delta_\tau) = (1-\tau)\cdot TV(Q^0,Q^1)$ up to $+1/N$ discretization error (Prop. 3); with Nature aware, $\varepsilon^\star(\delta_\tau) = 1 - \tau\cdot TV(Q^0,Q^1)$ up to $1/N$ (Prop. 2).
  • How it is used in this paper: Section 5's main result; the trade-off is governed entirely by the total variation distance between the group-conditional context distributions.

Approachability of an unknown target set [SKIM - verify details in deep read]

  • What it computes: input = target set $C$ depending on unknown quantities ($\gamma_0, \gamma_1$, $TV(Q^0,Q^1)$); operation = move all unknowns from the payoff into the target set, estimate $\hat{C}_r$ at doubling epochs $T_r = 2^r$ with a high-probability super-set guarantee (Assumption 3), and project onto $\hat{C}_r$ in the Blackwell strategy; output = approachability iff the same Blackwell condition holds (Theorem 2).
  • Why it was created: the group-wise payoffs $m_{gr\text{-}cal}, m_{DP}$ contain unknown group marginals $\gamma_s$, and the Pareto-optimal $(\varepsilon,\delta)$ pair depends on unknown $TV(Q^0,Q^1)$ — the target itself must be learned.
  • Concrete example: $\tilde{C}^\varepsilon_{gr\text{-}cal} = \{(v_0,v_1): \|v_0\|_1/\gamma_0 + \|v_1\|_1/\gamma_1 \le \varepsilon\}$ estimated via plug-in $\hat{\gamma}_{s,t}$ and empirical-TV $\hat{M}_t$ with confidence widths $\alpha_1(t), \alpha_2(t)$.
  • How it is used in this paper: Section 6 + Appendix D; makes the whole framework fully agnostic to $Q$.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: no data or training; implementing the strategy requires solving a per-round min-max over distribution families (linear programming for finite $X$, $A$, $B$) plus a density/frequency estimator $\hat{Q}_t$ and (for unknown targets) plug-in set estimates with confidence widths.
  • Code: not available (no code release mentioned; purely theoretical NeurIPS paper).
  • Modifications: to apply to exposure-based ranking fairness, one must (a) encode exposure statistics as vector-payoff coordinates, (b) handle combinatorially large action sets (rankings) — the paper's complexity is already at least linear in group count and would blow up in slate size; (c) replace demographic parity of forecasts with exposure parity of items.
  • Unknowns: missing link — whether the approachability condition remains checkable (and the min-max solvable) for ranking-structured action sets; missing link — finite-sample constants $K$, $\|m\|_{\infty,2}$ for realistic exposure payoffs.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Gupta et al., "Online Multivalid Learning: Means, Moments, and Prediction Intervals" (2021) — the computationally efficient group-wise calibration alternative the paper itself benchmarks its generality against; relevant if SP6 needs practical per-group calibration.
  • Hébert-Johnson et al., "Multicalibration" (ICML 2018) — the batch group-wise calibration objective this paper puts into the online approachability frame.
  • Blum et al., "On Preserving Non-Discrimination When Combining Expert Advice" (NeurIPS 2018) — the no-regret + equal-payoffs impossibility this paper reproduces and generalizes (Appendix B.2).
  • Bechavod et al., "Equal Opportunity in Online Classification with Partial Feedback" (NeurIPS 2019) — the partial-monitoring extension the paper names as its main open direction (Section 7).
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = per-round stochastic contexts $(x_t, s_t) \sim Q$ unknown, finite action set (forecast bins or discrete actions), single Player vs. adversarial Nature; outputs = per-round action/forecast; conditions = Player unaware of $s_t$ at decision time, bandit monitoring, fairness = group-level statistics of the Player's own action distribution (demographic parity of average forecasts), asymptotic a.s. guarantees. Our setting (SP6, exposure/frequency as ranking proxy): inputs = a ranking or recommendation system where item exposure frequency serves as the observable proxy for relevance/quality; outputs = rankings/exposure allocations over many items; conditions = fairness of exposure across item groups or producers, typically position-based exposure models, finite-sample regimes. Differences:

  • Action space: single scalar forecast/action per round vs. rankings over item sets — missing link (paper's finite-$A$ machinery does not directly encode permutation/exposure structure).
  • Fairness target: demographic parity of the Player's average action across user groups vs. exposure parity across item groups — reference only (structurally analogous — both are group-averaged linear statistics of the decision, so $m_{DP}$-style bricks could encode exposure parity — but not instantiated in the paper).   • Trade-off currency: paper's frontier is governed by $TV(Q^0,Q^1)$ between group-conditional context distributions; an exposure-fairness analogue would need the corresponding separability quantity for item groups — missing link.
  • Guarantee type: asymptotic a.s. convergence with slow estimation-driven rates vs. finite-horizon ranking metrics — reference only.
  • Unknown-target-set machinery (Section 6): directly relevant if the fair-exposure target depends on unknown popularity/marginals — resolved: the plug-in + doubling-epoch construction transfers conceptually.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper resolves the root question (when are a learning objective and a fairness constraint jointly satisfiable at all, and what is the exact optimal trade-off when not) via a necessary-and-sufficient geometric characterization, rather than proposing an algorithm that merely improves an observed fairness metric.
