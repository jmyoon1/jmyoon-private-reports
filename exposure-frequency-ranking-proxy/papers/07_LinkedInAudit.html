An External Fairness Evaluation of LinkedIn Talent Search

Behzad et al. · arXiv · 2025 · 4 citations

arXiv:2511.10752 · PDF · SP: SP1

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

LinkedIn Talent Search를 외부에서 반복 질의로 감사하여 exposure 격차를 측정하는 black-box 측정 프로토콜을 제시한다. 개입 없이 측정만 수행하는 audit 연구이다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

LinkedIn Talent Search — the recruiter-facing ranking that directly shapes who gets seen, contacted, and hired — has never been independently audited. LinkedIn's self-reports (Geyik et al., 2019) claim fairness improvements, but external validation is absent, and existing audit methodology largely covers ad delivery (Meta) rather than candidate-search ranking. Additionally, most ranking-fairness audits use static single-snapshot metrics, missing temporal disparities in exposure and retention.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

[SKIM - verify] An external, black-box empirical audit: (1) collect LTS rankings for a diverse set of occupational queries, repeated over five consecutive days; (2) infer gender and race for returned users via a labeling pipeline built on external APIs/datasets; (3) measure static exposure disparity with deviation-from-group-proportions and MinSkew@k; (4) measure temporal disparity in rank persistence/volatility per group; (5) contrast findings with LinkedIn's published self-audits and discuss which disparities are hard or impossible to eliminate at early ranks.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Under-representation of minority groups at early ranks (first 100 ranks / 4 pages) across many occupational queries. [SKIM - exact numbers in Section 5]
  • Demographic disparities in temporal stability: some groups show greater day-to-day rank volatility, affecting cumulative selection probability. [SKIM - exact numbers in Section 6]
  • Some early-rank disparities are argued to be difficult or impossible to fully eliminate. [SKIM - verify argument in deep read]

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

MinSkew@k

  • What it computes: for a ranked list truncated at position k → compares the proportion of a demographic group in the top-k against that group's proportion in the full candidate pool → outputs the minimum (worst-case, most under-represented) skew ratio across groups. Originates from LinkedIn's own Skew@k family (Geyik et al., 2019); this paper reuses it as an audit metric.
  • Why it was created: prefix-representation constraints on a single ranking do not summarize how badly the worst-off group is under-represented at early ranks.
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - verify in deep read] — the paper includes a worked Min-Skew example in Appendix C.
  • How it is used in this paper: one of the two exposure-disparity metrics applied to the collected LTS ranking dataset (Section 5.2).

Deviation from group proportions

  • What it computes: top-k group share minus the group's share in the reference population → a signed per-query disparity value.
  • Why it was created: gives a direct, interpretable measure of over/under-representation at early ranks, complementing the ratio-based Skew@k.
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - verify in deep read]
  • How it is used in this paper: primary static analysis across occupational queries (Section 5.1).

Temporal subgroup fairness / retention disparity

  • What it computes: for identical queries issued over five consecutive days → tracks each candidate's persistence and rank volatility across days → outputs per-group differences in exposure stability and retention.
  • Why it was created: static single-snapshot metrics miss the fact that staying ranked over multiple days compounds a candidate's selection probability; temporal volatility can itself be demographically skewed.
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - verify in deep read] — abstract states some groups experience greater volatility in ranked positions than others.
  • How it is used in this paper: Section 6 temporal-disparity analysis; positioned as a dimension "more difficult to audit for in practice."

Black-box external audit pipeline for a deployed ranker

  • What it computes: occupational queries issued to LinkedIn Talent Search over 5 consecutive days → ingested rankings enriched with demographic inference (gender, race) via external APIs/datasets → exposure- and temporal-disparity analysis.
  • Why it was created: LinkedIn's fairness claims rest on self-audits with internal access; no independent audit of the recruiter-facing candidate search existed.
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - verify in deep read] — first ~100 ranks (4 result pages) per query collected.
  • How it is used in this paper: the entire data-collection methodology (Sections 3–4).

Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = live LinkedIn Talent Search rankings (top ~100 per query, 5 consecutive days, occupational queries); outputs = per-group exposure and retention disparity measurements; conditions = black-box access, demographic attributes inferred (noisy), two protected attributes (gender, race). Our setting: exposure frequency over repeated rankings used as a proxy signal for ranking behavior (SP1 — exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy). [SKIM - verify SP1 framing] Differences:

  • Paper measures group-level exposure disparity for fairness; our interest is exposure frequency as a measurable proxy for underlying ranking mechanics: reference only — but the repeated-query temporal design (5-day persistence/volatility tracking) is directly reusable as an exposure-frequency measurement protocol. [SKIM - verify]
  • Demographic attribute inference is central to the paper; likely irrelevant to our proxy question: reference only.
  • Black-box repeated-query data collection against a live commercial ranker: resolved — the paper's pipeline (Section 4.1) is a concrete template for collecting exposure-frequency data without internal access.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Symptom — the audit measures observable exposure disparities and discusses potential causes, but by design (black-box, external) it cannot identify or fix the root cause inside the ranking pipeline; the paper itself concedes some disparities may be impossible to eliminate at early ranks.
