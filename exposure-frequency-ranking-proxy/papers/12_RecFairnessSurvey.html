A Survey on the Fairness of Recommender Systems

Wang et al. · ACM TOIS · 2022 · 453 citations

arXiv:2206.03761 · PDF · SP: SP1

P1Track C

한 줄 요약

consistent/calibrated, 단발/amortized, treatment/impact 축으로 recommender 공정성 연구를 정리한 survey. 본 조사의 O1 taxonomy 어휘를 제공한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Fairness research in recommendation is scattered: multiple allocation processes (information to users, exposure to items), 7+ incompatible fairness definitions, dozens of ad-hoc metrics, and methods spread across the whole recommendation pipeline, with no unified view. Prior surveys either predate the field's growth (Castillo 2019, pre-2018 only), cover ML fairness without recommendation's two-sided/dynamic/personalized specifics (Caton & Haas 2020; Mehrabi et al. 2021), or treat fairness as a side topic of bias (Chen et al. 2020, no measurements or datasets).

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

A systematic review of 60+ papers (2017–2022, TOIS/SIGIR/WWW/KDD etc.) organized along five layers:

  1. Definitions: process vs. outcome fairness; outcome fairness split by target

(group / individual) and by concept (consistent, calibrated, counterfactual, envy-free, Rawlsian maximin, maximin-shared) — each given a formal statement.

  1. Views: subject (user / item / joint), granularity (single / amortized),

optimization object (treatment / impact).

  1. Measurements: ~30 metrics tabulated with formulas and applicability scope

(Table 5).

  1. Methods: taxonomy by pipeline position — data-oriented (resampling, antidote

data), ranking (regularization, adversarial, RL, other), re-ranking (slot-wise, user-wise, global-wise) — with representative equations for each family.

  1. Datasets: 21 datasets with fairness-related attributes used per prior study.
Key Results주요 결과

Survey findings rather than experimental numbers:

  • Consistent fairness (CO) is the most common definition; group-level fairness dominates over individual-level (Table 1, 60+ papers).
  • Amortized fairness dominates over single-list fairness, and treatment-based dominates over impact-based optimization — because per-list constraints are often infeasible and user feedback is uncontrollable.
  • User fairness and item fairness conflict: improving item fairness can only worsen or preserve user fairness (citing Wu et al. 2021, TFROM); joint fairness is handled by only ~4 of 60+ papers.
  • Open gap flagged: all amortized-exposure work equalizes the average exposure; none considers exposure variance across lists.
  • Only one surveyed paper (Zhu et al. 2021, popularity-opportunity bias) theoretically proves a model class causes unfairness; explanation of unfairness causes is nearly untouched.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

The survey introduces no new methods; it systematizes existing terminology. The following organizing concepts are the ones load-bearing for this SP.

Calibrated fairness (merit-based fairness)

  • What it computes: for individuals/groups $i, j$, requires $V(C_h(i))/M(i) = V(C_h(j))/M(j)$ — the value of the allocated outcome (e.g., exposure) divided by a merit function must be equal across subjects.
  • Why it was created: consistent fairness ("treat everyone alike") ignores that items differ in relevance/quality; calibration ties allocation to merit instead of uniformity.
  • Concrete example: item exposure share $p(v_i) = f(v_i)/\sum_j f(v_j)$ compared against a merit share $p_f(v_i) = Merit(v_i)/\sum_j Merit(v_j)$ via KL-divergence or L1-norm; merit is often measured as CTR or historical relevance.
  • How it is used in this paper: one of the two dominant fairness concepts in the taxonomy (Table 3); most item-exposure fairness work reviewed falls here.

Single vs. amortized fairness (fairness granularity)

  • What it computes: single fairness constrains each individual recommendation list; amortized fairness constrains the cumulative effect over many lists (e.g., average exposure of a group within a day).
  • Why it was created: per-list fairness is often infeasible (a top-10 list cannot proportionally represent thousands of items); amortization makes exposure constraints satisfiable over time (Biega et al. 2018).
  • Concrete example: male/female-author book exposure need not be 5/5 in each list, only ~equal summed over all lists in a period.
  • How it is used in this paper: a classification axis in Table 1/Table 4; the survey notes most work is amortized and that no prior work considers the variance of exposure across lists, only the average.

Treatment-based vs. impact-based fairness (optimization object)

  • What it computes: treatment-based fairness constrains what the system does (predicted scores, allocated exposure); impact-based fairness constrains the downstream user feedback (e.g., CTR distribution across item groups).
  • Why it was created: equalizing exposure alone can backfire — the system can dump discriminated items on inactive users, raising exposure while CTR collapses.
  • Concrete example: treatment: exposure of item groups follows a fair distribution (Geyik et al. 2019); impact: CTR of item groups follows a fair distribution (Morik et al. 2020).
  • How it is used in this paper: classification axis; most surveyed work is treatment-based because user feedback cannot be directly controlled.

Exposure-fairness metrics (rND/rKL/rRD, NDKL, MinSkew/MaxSkew, Overall Disparity)

  • What it computes: position-discounted divergences between the realized group distribution in top-$i$ prefixes of a ranking and a target (uniform or merit-based) distribution, e.g. $NDKL@K = \frac{1}{Z}\sum_i^K \frac{1}{\log(i+1)} D^i_{KL}$.
  • Why it was created: scalar averages (Absolute Difference, Gini) ignore where in the ranking exposure lands; these metrics weight top positions more, mirroring NDCG.
  • Concrete example: rND sums $|\,|S^+_{1..i}|/i - |S^+|/N\,|$ at cutoffs $i = 10, 20, \dots$ with $1/\log_2 i$ discounting.
  • How it is used in this paper: cataloged in Table 5 with applicability scope (two-group / multi-group / individual; user / item; single / amortized; treatment / impact).
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: nothing to run — reference work. The metric formulas (Eq. 1–31) are directly implementable from the text with standard NumPy.
  • Code: not available (no repo; survey has no artifacts).
  • Modifications: for our SP, repurpose the calibrated-fairness formalism: replace merit $M(\cdot)$ with an exposure-independent relevance estimate to quantify how far exposure-derived rankings drift from merit-derived ones.
  • Unknowns: missing link: which of the ~30 cataloged metrics are robust when the merit function itself is estimated from exposure-biased logs — the survey does not address metric validity under feedback loops.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Biega et al., "Equity of Attention: Amortizing Individual Fairness in Rankings" (SIGIR 2018) — origin of amortized exposure fairness; the position-based attention model is the core exposure-quantification primitive for SP1.
  • Singh & Joachims, "Fairness of Exposure in Rankings" (KDD 2018) — probabilistic ranking / exposure allocation via LP; canonical formalization of exposure as an allocatable quantity.
  • Zhu et al., "Popularity-Opportunity Bias in Collaborative Filtering" (WSDM 2021) — the one theoretical proof cited that MF prefers popular items at equal true preference; directly on the exposure-frequency-as-distorted-proxy mechanism.
  • Morik et al., "Controlling Fairness and Bias in Dynamic Learning-to-Rank" (SIGIR 2020) — dynamic setting where exposure feeds back into learned relevance; treatment/impact distinction in action.
  • Wu et al., "TFROM" (SIGIR 2021) — formal statement of the user-vs-item fairness conflict; relevant if SP1 needs the two-sided trade-off.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = user–item interaction logs with (optionally) sensitive attributes; outputs = ranked recommendation lists; conditions = two-sided recommender platforms, fairness defined over users and/or items, mostly offline evaluation on public datasets (MovieLens, Amazon, Last.FM, Xing). Our setting: exposure frequency used as a proxy signal in ranking — asking when accumulated exposure counts stand in for (or distort) relevance/merit in ranking decisions. Differences:

  • Fairness as the objective vs. exposure-as-proxy as the object of study: the survey treats exposure as the resource to allocate fairly; we treat it as a measurement proxy for merit. The calibrated-fairness machinery (merit function $M(\cdot)$, exposure/merit ratio) is directly reusable as the formal language for proxy mismatch — resolved: adopt $p$ vs $p_f$ divergence framing.
  • The survey's merit functions (CTR, historical interactions) are themselves exposure-contaminated (popularity feedback loop, its refs [119], [32]) — this is exactly our SP1 concern but the survey only names it, does not resolve it: missing link.
  • Single/amortized and treatment/impact axes transfer directly to classifying exposure-proxy failure modes — resolved: reuse as classification axes.
  • Domain is recommendation only; our SP may extend to search/LLM ranking — reference only.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper's stated problem is that fairness research is scattered, and it resolves that root cause by supplying a unified formal taxonomy (definitions → views → metrics → methods → datasets) rather than patching around it; note however it explicitly leaves the causes of unfairness (the field's deeper root cause) as an open direction.
