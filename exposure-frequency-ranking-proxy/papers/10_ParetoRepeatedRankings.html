Towards Efficient Pareto-optimal Utility-Fairness between Groups in Repeated Rankings

Mai et al. · arXiv · 2024 · 1 citations

arXiv:2402.14305 · PDF · SP: SP1 · SP2 · SP4

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

expohedron 기하 위에서 Caratheodory 분해로 BvN의 O(n^5)를 O(n^2 log n)으로 줄이고, 반복 ranking에서 효용-공정성의 정확한 Pareto front 전체를 계산한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Computing the utility–fairness trade-off for repeated rankings (stochastic ranking policies) is standardly done by scalarized LP/QP over bi-stochastic matrices followed by Birkhoff–von Neumann decomposition to obtain a distribution over permutations. BvN costs $O(n^5)$ in the number of items, which is impractical at scale, and the LP-with-fairness-constraint formulation produces only a single Pareto point (at the target fairness level) rather than the full trade-off curve a decision-maker needs.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Reformulate the multi-objective problem $\max_x U(x) = x\rho$, $\min_x F(x) = \|Gx - \epsilon^*\|_2$ s.t. $x \preceq \gamma$ (majorization) directly in exposure space (the Expohedron), then:

  1. P-Expo: prove the Pareto set is a chain of line segments on Expohedron facets (dimension $\le n-g-1$) and trace it exactly by iterated facet-walking from the target-fairness optimum to the max-utility vertex.
  2. Sphere-Expo: approximate that chain by geodesics on the Expohedron's circumscribed $n$-sphere, refined by $K$ QP-computed marked points; total complexity $O(Kn^3\sqrt{n})$, with $K$ trading accuracy for runtime. Ranking distributions are then recovered by Caratheodory decomposition ($O(n^2\log n)$) instead of BvN.

Applicable to any PBM exposure model with non-decreasing ranking merit in item relevance (DCG, RBP). [SKIM - verify] proof details of Theorems 1–2 and the asymptotic-closeness claim.

Key Results주요 결과
  • BvN decomposition replaced: decomposition time on MSLR 29.56s (BvN) vs 0.54s (Caratheodory); on TREC 2.25s vs 0.07s (Table 2).
  • Pareto-front generation for T=1000 rankings: Sphere-Expo K=1 takes 0.28s (TREC) / 0.56s (MSLR) vs QP 20-point 1.23s / 4.45s.
  • On small synthetic data, QP and P-Expo produce identical Pareto fronts (both exact), confirming the facet-chain corollary; Sphere-Expo with 7 marked points nearly matches the true front.
  • Controller (Ctrl) baseline converges to a single point after T=1000 with very high unfairness on MSLR (normalized unfairness 0.93; Table 1).

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Expohedron (introduced by Kletti et al. 2022; heavily relied upon here)

  • What it computes: a convex polytope in $\mathbb{R}^n$ (a permutahedron) whose vertices are the exposure vectors $\pi\gamma$ attainable by single rankings under a Position-Based Model; interior points = expected exposure vectors of distributions over rankings.
  • Why it was created: replaces the $n \times n$ bi-stochastic-matrix (Birkhoff polytope) representation, whose BvN decomposition costs $O(n^5)$, with an $n$-dimensional exposure space decomposable via Caratheodory in $O(n^2 \log n)$.
  • Concrete example: for $n=3$ items with DCG exposure $\gamma = (1, 0.63, 0.5)$, the Expohedron is the hexagon of all permutations of $\gamma$; the point $(0.71, 0.71, 0.71)$ (uniform expected exposure) lies inside it.
  • How it is used in this paper: the entire Pareto curve between user utility and group-exposure unfairness is profiled on the Expohedron's facets.

P-Expo (exact Pareto profiling)

  • What it computes: relevance vector $\rho$, group matrix $G$, target group exposure $\epsilon^*$, exposure vector $\gamma$ → walks facet-by-facet from the max-utility-at-target-fairness point along the optimal trade-off direction → the exact Pareto set as consecutive line segments across Expohedron facets connected at "break" points.
  • Why it was created: scalarization + LP/QP on bi-stochastic matrices yields one Pareto point per hyperparameter value and needs the expensive BvN step; P-Expo characterizes the whole front geometrically (Theorem 1: Pareto solutions lie on facets of dimension at most $n-g-1$).
  • Concrete example: Figure 1 (n=3, g=2) shows the Pareto curve as green segments on the hexagonal Expohedron boundary.
  • How it is used in this paper: as the theoretical exact solution, validated on a small synthetic dataset; its runtime grows with the (unknown) number of facets crossed, so it serves mainly to verify theory.

Sphere-Expo (geodesic approximation)

  • What it computes: same inputs plus $K$ (number of marked points) and a sample count → relaxes the piecewise-linear Pareto curve onto geodesics of the Expohedron's circumscribed $(n-2)$-sphere, recursively bisecting with QP-solved "marked points" → an approximate Pareto set, asymptotically approaching the true front as $K$ grows.
  • Why it was created: P-Expo's facet-walk runtime is uncontrollable; the geodesic relaxation gives total complexity $O(Kn^3\sqrt{n})$ and a tunable accuracy-vs-time knob.
  • Concrete example: with $K=7$ marked points on the small synthetic dataset, the approximation curve is visually near-identical to the true Pareto front (Fig. 4).
  • How it is used in this paper: the main practical method, benchmarked on TREC 2020 Fair Ranking and MSLR against QP+BvN and the Controller.

Supporting reused concepts: Position-Based Model (exposure depends only on rank position via $\gamma$), doubly bi-stochastic ranking matrix + BvN decomposition (Singh & Joachims 2018), expected-exposure unfairness $F(\mathcal{D}, \epsilon^*) = \|G E(\mathcal{D}) - \epsilon^*\|_2$ with a meritocratic target exposure (Diaz et al. 2020).

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = per-query relevance vector $\rho \in [0,1]^n$ from a black-box scorer, disjoint item-group matrix $G$, PBM exposure $\gamma$, decision-maker-specified target group exposure $\epsilon^*$; outputs = a distribution over rankings realizing any chosen point on the utility–unfairness Pareto curve, delivered over repeated impressions of the same query; conditions = $n \le 100$ items/query (larger queries discarded for solver numerics), static relevance, group membership exclusive.

Our setting (exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy survey): we study exposure frequency in repeated/stochastic rankings as a measurable proxy for item merit or ranking quality, likely with observational exposure counts rather than decision-maker-set targets.

Differences:

  • Target exposure $\epsilon^*$ is exogenous (decision-maker/meritocratic affine function of relevance) vs. our interest in exposure as an emergent measured quantity: reference only — the paper optimizes toward a given target, it does not estimate merit from exposure.
  • Group-level exposure fairness vs. item-level exposure frequency: missing link — item-level (individual) fairness is delegated to Kletti et al. 2022; this paper aggregates by group.
  • Repeated identical query with known relevance vs. dynamic/estimated relevance: missing link — relevance is assumed given and static per query.
  • PBM exposure assumption (exposure depends only on position): resolved within the paper's scope — explicitly stated; extension to DBN click models is future work.
