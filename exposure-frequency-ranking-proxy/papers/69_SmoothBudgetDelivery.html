Real Time Bid Optimization with Smooth Budget Delivery in Online Advertising

Lee et al. · arXiv · 2013 · 142 citations

arXiv:1305.3011 · PDF · SP: SP7

P2Track A

한 줄 요약

고정 impression 예산을 시간에 걸쳐 매끄럽게 소진하는 실시간 입찰 최적화의 고전(2013). pacing의 원형.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Prior online-allocation solutions do not fit production RTB bid optimization: online-knapsack strategies (Zhou et al.) assume unlimited impression supply, which fails for tightly targeted campaigns; multi-armed-bandit pricing (Babaioff et al.) needs fast feedback but conversion feedback in display advertising is delayed up to ~7 days; online LP duals (Agrawal et al.) require frequent re-solving when values/costs are estimated, which is too costly at ~1M requests/second with <50 ms latency. Meanwhile the practical constraint — spend the daily budget smoothly, neither prematurely exhausting it nor fluctuating — must hold jointly with a performance goal (CPC/CPA/CTR/AR) under rare, delayed conversion events.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

An online feedback-control pipeline (skimmed from pp. 1–9):

  1. Split the day into $T$ slots with per-slot budget targets $b_t$ ($\sum b_t = B$), set either uniformly (Eq. 6) or proportional to historical per-slot performance (Eq. 8).
  2. Update the pacing rate recursively from last slot's spend, traffic, and win rate (Eq. 4) so realized spend tracks $b_t$.
  3. Flat CPM campaigns: filter incoming requests by a predicted CTR/AR threshold $\tau(t)$ (Eq. 9) with a Gaussian confidence band; randomize at the boundary with probability = pacing rate.
  4. dCPM campaigns: set base bid $u_i = \text{AR} \times G$, then shade (safe region), keep (critical), or linearly boost toward the exchange cap (danger region, Eq. 11) depending on where the pacing rate sits relative to $\beta_1, \beta_2$.
  5. CTR/AR estimated via (user, publisher, advertiser) hierarchy smoothing + per-leaf logistic regression (following Lee et al. 2012); cold start handled with contextual epsilon-greedy exploration; hard monitors stop/pause campaigns on overspend.
Key Results주요 결과
  • Uniform-pacing simulation: actual spend tracks ideal spend within \$0.56 average deviation across slots, <1% of daily budget (Fig. 4).
  • Performance-based-pacing simulation: \$0.17 average deviation, ~2.3% of daily budget (Fig. 6).
  • 7 flat CPM campaigns: average CTR lift +123% vs. baseline (constant-factor adaptive threshold control); per-campaign lift 47%–286% (Table 1).
  • 10 CPC dCPM campaigns: average CPC improvement 19.02%; 10 CPA campaigns: average CPA improvement 30.87% vs. pacing-only baseline without bid-price adjustment (Tables 2–3; one campaign at -2.23%).

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Pacing rate

  • What it computes: input = eligible incoming ad requests in time slot $t$; operation = ratio bids(t)/reqs(t), updated recursively as $\text{pacing\_rate}(t{+}1) = \text{pacing\_rate}(t) \cdot \frac{b_{t+1}}{s(t)} \cdot \frac{\text{reqs}(t)}{\text{reqs}(t{+}1)} \cdot \frac{\text{win\_rate}(t)}{\text{win\_rate}(t{+}1)}$; output = the fraction of eligible requests the campaign bids on in the next slot.
  • Why it was created: naive budget spending either exhausts budget prematurely or fluctuates wildly; a feedback-controlled bid fraction ties spend to a target per-slot schedule $b_t$.
  • Concrete example: if slot $t$ spent \$2 against a target $b_{t+1}=\$1$ with unchanged traffic and win rate, the pacing rate is halved for slot $t+1$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the core control variable — drives request selection (§3.2), bid-price scaling regions (§3.3), and overspend pausing (§4.2).

Performance-based pacing (vs. uniform pacing)

  • What it computes: input = historical per-slot click/conversion probabilities $p_1..p_T$ (a discrete pdf, $\sum p_t = 1$) and remaining budget; operation = $b^p_{t+1} = (B - \sum_{m\le t} s(m)) \cdot \frac{p_{t+1}}{\sum_{m>t} p_m}$; output = the next slot's spend target, allocating more budget to historically higher-performing hours.
  • Why it was created: uniform pacing ignores that traffic volume and quality vary through the day, forcing purchases of low-quality impressions in bad hours.
  • Concrete example: with equal slot lengths and remaining budget \$10, if $p_{t+1}=0.2$ and remaining mass $\sum_{m>t}p_m = 0.5$, then $b^p_{t+1} = \$4$.
  • How it is used in this paper: one of the two pacing strategies simulated (Fig. 6; 2.3% deviation from the ideal spend curve).

CTR/AR threshold filtering with confidence bounds

  • What it computes: input = empirical histogram $q_t(x)$ of predicted CTR/AR values over historical requests in slot $t$ and the required request count $\text{reqs}^*(t)$; operation = solve $\tau(t) = \arg\min_x |\int_x^1 q_t(s)ds - \text{reqs}^*(t)|$, then wrap $\tau(t)$ in a Gaussian 95% confidence band via online mean/variance updates; output = keep requests above the upper bound, drop below the lower bound, randomize (with prob = pacing rate) in between. [SKIM - verify band details in deep read]
  • Why it was created: a raw threshold oscillates across slots because the historical histogram mismatches current traffic; the confidence band damps oscillation while preserving exploration at the quality boundary.
  • Concrete example: with $\gamma = 1.96$ and $d$ days of history, bounds are $\mu_\tau(t) \pm 1.96\,\sigma_\tau(t)/\sqrt{d}$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the impression-selection rule for flat CPM campaigns (§3.2).

Three-region bid-price adjustment (safe / critical / danger)

  • What it computes: input = current pacing rate and thresholds $0 \le \beta_1 \le \beta_2 \le 1$; operation = if pacing rate $\le \beta_1$ (safe), shade the bid down to $\hat{c}_i = \theta^* u_i$ where $\theta^*$ is a low percentile of the paid-price/bid-price ratio histogram; if between (critical), bid the base price $u_i = \text{AR} \times G$ (predicted action rate times CPA goal); if $\ge \beta_2$ (danger), boost linearly toward the exchange bid cap via $\rho^* = 1 + \frac{C/c^*-1}{1-\beta_2}(\text{pacing\_rate}(t)-\beta_2)$; output = the submitted bid price for dCPM campaigns.
  • Why it was created: pacing rate alone controls bid frequency but not win probability; underdelivering campaigns need higher bids, overdelivering ones can shade bids to improve eCPA.
  • Concrete example: $\theta^*$ = bottom 1–2 percentile of $\theta = c_i/\hat{c}_i$ among converting impressions.
  • How it is used in this paper: the bid-pricing rule for dynamic CPM campaigns (§3.3), evaluated in Tables 2–3.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: streaming per-slot aggregates (spend, requests, wins), a CTR/AR prediction model, historical per-slot performance distributions; the controller itself is a handful of arithmetic updates per slot — trivially cheap.
  • Code: not available (proprietary Turn Inc production system).
  • Modifications: replace bid-price machinery with exposure-probability throttling for a ranking setting; choose slot length so per-slot price/quality variance stays small (paper's stated condition); pick $\beta_1, \beta_2$, $T$ empirically since values are unstated.
  • Unknowns: missing link: parameter values ($\beta_1,\beta_2$, slot count $T$, histogram refresh rate) and baseline details are not fully specified; missing link: behavior under nonstationary traffic shifts beyond what the confidence band absorbs.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = per-request features (user, publisher, ad) + historical per-slot spend/traffic/win-rate + predicted CTR/AR; outputs = bid/no-bid decision and bid price per ad request; conditions = DSP-side RTB second-price auctions, ~1M requests/sec, <50 ms latency, daily budget constraint, delayed and rare conversion feedback. Our setting: exposure frequency as a proxy signal in ranking systems (SP7) — deciding how often an item/ad is exposed and how pacing/frequency interacts with ranking-quality signals, rather than computing exchange bid prices. Differences:

  • Second-price auction bidding vs. ranking-slate exposure allocation: the bid-price machinery (§3.3) does not transfer directly — reference only.
  • Pacing rate as an exposure-frequency throttle: directly analogous — a feedback-controlled fraction of eligible opportunities is the same primitive an exposure-frequency controller needs — resolved: the recursive Eq. 4 controller is the reusable core.
  • Delayed/rare feedback (conversions up to 7 days): shared condition with exposure-outcome attribution in ranking — resolved: historical-distribution-based allocation (Eq. 8) is their answer.
  • Per-campaign daily budget vs. per-item exposure quota: structurally similar constraint but different semantics — [SKIM - verify] whether SP7 needs hard quotas or soft smoothness.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the pacing-rate feedback loop directly addresses the root cause of unsmooth delivery (spend is mechanically tied to a per-slot schedule via a closed-loop controller), but the performance-optimization side patches symptoms: the stated online LP (Eq. 1) is never solved or approximated with guarantees; instead heuristic thresholding, percentile bid shading, and a linear boost formula stand in for optimality, and the delayed-feedback problem is sidestepped by relying on multi-day historical distributions rather than resolved.
