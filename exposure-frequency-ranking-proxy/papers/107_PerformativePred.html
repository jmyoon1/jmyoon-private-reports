Performative Prediction

Perdomo et al. · ICML 2020 · 2020 · 473 citations

arXiv:2002.06673 · PDF · SP: SP10

P0Track A

한 줄 요약

예측이 예측 대상 분포를 바꾸는 performative prediction의 기초(ICML 2020). scorer 내생성 이론의 골격을 제공한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Predictions that support decisions change the distribution of the outcome they predict (credit scores raise default risk via interest rates; recommendations shape consumption; crime prediction reallocates policing). Prior supervised-learning theory assumes a fixed distribution; the practice of repeatedly retraining on newly induced data had no theory — no notion of what retraining converges to, when it converges, or whether its fixed points are any good. Concept-drift work treats the shift as arbitrary and exogenous; strategic-classification work depends on specific agent cost functions.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

A decision-theoretic framework built on a distribution map $\mathcal{D}(\theta)$: deploying $f_\theta$ induces distribution $\mathcal{D}(\theta)$. Two solution concepts: performative optimality ($\arg\min \text{PR}(\theta)$) and performative stability (fixed point of retraining). Main theorems:

  1. Theorem 3.5: if the loss is $\beta$-jointly smooth and $\gamma$-strongly convex and $\mathcal{D}(\cdot)$ is ε-sensitive with $\varepsilon < \gamma/\beta$, RRM contracts with rate $\varepsilon\beta/\gamma$ and converges linearly to a unique stable point. Tight: dropping any one assumption admits a divergence counterexample (Prop. 3.6) — notably, plain convexity is NOT enough, unlike static learning.
  2. Theorem 3.8: RGD likewise converges linearly for $\varepsilon < \frac{\gamma}{(\beta+\gamma)(1+1.5\eta\beta)}$.
  3. Theorem 4.3: any stable point is within $2L_z\varepsilon/\gamma$ of any performative optimum — so retraining's fixed point approximately minimizes performative risk.
  4. Corollary 5.1: strategic classification is a special case (Stackelberg equilibria = performative optima); this yields the first sufficient conditions for retraining to overcome strategic feedback.
Key Results주요 결과
  • RRM converges linearly to a unique performatively stable point iff loss is β-jointly smooth, γ-strongly convex, and $\varepsilon < \gamma/\beta$; removing any single assumption permits non-convergence (Thm 3.5 + Prop 3.6).
  • Strong convexity is necessary — for merely convex smooth losses, RRM can oscillate forever (e.g. iterates 1, −1, 1, −1, …) for any $\varepsilon > 0$.
  • $\|\theta_{PO}-\theta_{PS}\|_2 \leq 2L_z\varepsilon/\gamma$ — stable points approximately minimize performative risk (Thm 4.3).
  • PR($\theta$) can be concave even when all convergence assumptions hold (Prop 4.2), so direct PR minimization is harder than finding stable points.
  • Credit-scoring simulation (Kaggle dataset, n=18,357, logistic loss): RRM converges within ~10 iterations for small ε, diverges for large ε, matching theory.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Performative prediction

  • What it computes: a risk-minimization framework where model parameters $\theta$ → distribution map $\mathcal{D}(\theta)$ → the data distribution itself. Deploying model $f_\theta$ induces the distribution $\mathcal{D}(\theta)$ over instances $Z=(X,Y)$; the prediction causally changes the target it predicts.
  • Why it was created: supervised learning assumes a fixed distribution $\mathcal{D}$; when predictions drive decisions (credit scoring, traffic, recommendations), the deployed model shifts the data distribution, which prior work treated as generic "distribution shift" handled by ad-hoc retraining.
  • Concrete example: biased coin flip with $Y|X \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\frac{1}{2}+\mu X+\varepsilon\theta X)$ — the outcome probability depends on the deployed score $\theta$; the supervised-learning optimum $\theta_{SL}=\mu$ differs from the performative optimum $\theta_{PO}=\mu/(1-2\varepsilon)$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the central framework; all results are stated over the map $\mathcal{D}(\cdot)$.

Performative risk (PR) and decoupled performative risk (DPR)

  • What it computes: $\text{PR}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{Z\sim\mathcal{D}(\theta)}\,\ell(Z;\theta)$ — loss of $\theta$ evaluated on the distribution $\theta$ itself induces. $\text{DPR}(\theta,\theta') = \mathbb{E}_{Z\sim\mathcal{D}(\theta)}\,\ell(Z;\theta')$ decouples which model generates data from which is evaluated.
  • Why it was created: classical risk is undefined when the distribution depends on the argument being optimized.
  • Concrete example: in the coin-flip example, $\text{PR}(\theta)=\frac{1}{4}-2\theta\mu+(1-2\varepsilon)\theta^2$ — which is concave in $\theta$ for $\varepsilon\geq\frac{1}{2}$ (Prop. 4.2), so PR can be non-convex even under strong regularity.
  • How it is used in this paper: $\theta_{PO}=\arg\min \text{PR}(\theta)$ defines performative optimality; DPR expresses both solution concepts.

Performative stability

  • What it computes: $\theta_{PS} = \arg\min_\theta \mathbb{E}_{Z\sim\mathcal{D}(\theta_{PS})}\ell(Z;\theta)$ — a model optimal for the distribution it induces; a fixed point of retraining.
  • Why it was created: retraining was seen as a "cat and mouse" nuisance chasing a moving target; stability recasts it as an equilibrating dynamic with well-defined fixed points. Stability ≠ optimality in general (coin example: $\theta_{PS}=\mu/(1-\varepsilon)$ vs $\theta_{PO}=\mu/(1-2\varepsilon)$).
  • Concrete example: at a stable point, retraining returns the same parameters — the model is calibrated against the future outcomes its own deployment produces.
  • How it is used in this paper: the convergence target of repeated risk minimization / gradient descent.

ε-sensitivity of the distribution map

  • What it computes: $W_1(\mathcal{D}(\theta),\mathcal{D}(\theta')) \leq \varepsilon\|\theta-\theta'\|_2$ — Lipschitzness of the map from parameters to distributions, in Wasserstein-1 distance.
  • Why it was created: some regularity on $\mathcal{D}(\cdot)$ is provably necessary — without it stable points may not exist and retraining oscillates (Example 3.4).
  • Concrete example: in strategic classification with quadratic manipulation cost $\frac{1}{2\varepsilon}\|x'-x\|^2$, the best response $x'_S = x_S - \varepsilon\theta_S$ gives exactly an ε-sensitive map.
  • How it is used in this paper: the key assumption in every convergence theorem; the threshold $\varepsilon < \gamma/\beta$ governs convergence.

Repeated risk minimization (RRM) / repeated gradient descent (RGD)

  • What it computes: RRM: $\theta_{t+1}=\arg\min_\theta \mathbb{E}_{Z\sim\mathcal{D}(\theta_t)}\ell(Z;\theta)$ — retrain fully on the distribution the previous model induced. RGD: one projected gradient step per round.
  • Why it was created: formalizes the ubiquitous practice of retraining on freshly collected data, so its long-term behavior can be analyzed.
  • Concrete example: in the credit-scoring simulation, RRM converges in <10 iterations for $\varepsilon=0.01$ and diverges for $\varepsilon=1000$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the two algorithms whose convergence to $\theta_{PS}$ is the main result; finite-sample versions (RERM/REGD) also analyzed.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: any retraining loop + a way to sample from the induced distribution; convexity/smoothness constants to check the $\varepsilon<\gamma/\beta$ condition; ε itself must be estimated or modeled.
  • Code: available at https://github.com/zykls/performative-prediction (simulator also in WhyNot package).
  • Modifications: for SP10 the distribution map must model exposure→engagement feedback (top-k selection), which breaks Lipschitzness assumptions; strong convexity requires regularized/linear surrogate rankers.
  • Unknowns: missing link: how to verify or estimate ε-sensitivity for a deployed ranking system; missing link: behavior under stateful (non-memoryless) feedback.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Stochastic Optimization for Performative Prediction (Mendler-Dünner et al., NeurIPS 2020) — greedy vs. lazy deployment in the stochastic setting; relevant to online ranker updates.
  • Performative Prediction in a Stateful World (Brown, Hod & Kalemaj, AISTATS 2022) — removes the memoryless-map assumption, closest to real recommender feedback loops.
  • Outside the Echo Chamber: Optimizing the Performative Risk (Miller, Perdomo & Zrnic, ICML 2021) — directly minimizing PR instead of settling for stable points.
  • Strategic Classification (Hardt et al., ITCS 2016) — the predecessor setting this paper strictly subsumes.
  • Fairness Without Demographics in Repeated Loss Minimization (Hashimoto et al., ICML 2018) — repeated retraining's disparity effects; cited as closest prior on retraining dynamics.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = parametric model $\theta$ on convex $\Theta$, loss smooth + strongly convex; conditions = distribution map $\mathcal{D}(\theta)$ fixed, memoryless, ε-sensitive in $W_1$; outputs = convergence guarantees for retraining. Our setting (SP10, exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy): a ranking/recommendation system whose exposure decisions shape the engagement data that trains the next ranker — exposure frequency acting as a self-reinforcing proxy for relevance. Differences:

  • Ranking feedback loops ARE performative prediction (paper's own Appendix A lists recommendations as a canonical instance): resolved — this paper supplies the formal language (PR, stability, ε-sensitivity) for SP10.
  • Memoryless map $\mathcal{D}(\theta)$ vs. real recommender loops with state (user preference drift, item cold-start history accumulate over deployments): missing link — the framework excludes path dependence; successors (stateful performative prediction) needed. [SKIM - verify]
  • Strong convexity + parametric θ vs. modern neural rankers (non-convex) and discrete exposure/ranking outputs: missing link — the convergence guarantees do not transfer directly.
  • Paper analyzes convergence of retraining, not whether the stable point is biased toward frequently exposed items: reference only — stability can enshrine popularity bias; the paper's own PS-vs-PO gap ($\theta_{PS}\neq\theta_{PO}$) is the formal seed of that concern.
  • Wasserstein ε-sensitivity plausibility in ranking (small ranker change → small exposure-distribution change) can fail under top-k discontinuity: missing link.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper identifies the root cause of prediction-induced distribution shift (the model-dependent map $\mathcal{D}(\theta)$), formalizes it, and proves exactly when the standard remedy (retraining) resolves it and when it provably cannot, rather than patching the shift symptomatically.
