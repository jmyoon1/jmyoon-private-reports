Don't Let Bandit Feedback Pull Continual LLM-Recommender Updates Off Target

Kim et al. · arXiv · 2026

arXiv:2605.18899 · PDF · SP: SP10

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

bandit feedback으로 계속 갱신되는 production LLM recommender의 3개월 A/B(2026). 클릭되지 않은 item을 아예 생성하지 못하게 되는 generation-space support collapse를 보고한다 — serving 측 floor가 구조적으로 불충분할 수 있는, 조사 11개 SP 중 최초의 설정.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Continually updating a deployed generative LLM recommender from its own deployment logs, where feedback exists only for items the prior policy exposed. Prior update strategies either train only on positive responses (reinforcing historically exposed successes → popularity bias) or treat no-responses as hard negatives (abundant false negatives → the policy stops generating those items and diversity collapses). The paper shows empirically (Fig. 1) that naive RLVR/GRPO updates can make the updated model worse than not updating at all on both HR@5 and diversity.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

ABPO = GRPO with three modifications: (1) insert the logged exposed item as a fixed anchor into each rollout group so group-relative normalization is calibrated against the action the prior policy actually took (anchor affects only baseline mean/std, gets no gradient); (2) weight the anchor's baseline contribution by a self-normalized inverse-propensity ratio e_old/e0 computed at the item level over the candidate set, stratified by feedback type; (3) asymmetric rewards — positive responses get a binary item-matching reward, no-responses get a matching penalty tempered by a self-certainty (output-confidence) bonus. Final objective (Eq. 7) is the standard clipped GRPO surrogate applied only to the G−1 policy-sampled rollouts.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Across 5 Amazon domains + MovieLens and 4 backbones (Rec-SAVER, Rec-R1, GDPO, G²RPO), ABPO gets best or second-best HR@1/HR@5/NDCG@5 in the large majority of cells; e.g. Rec-SAVER Fashion HR@5 51.45 vs 39.60 no-update, 44.69 best baseline (RL-Rec).
  • No-update beats several update baselines in many settings (e.g. UL-Rec frequently below no-update) — updating on bandit logs without bias treatment can be net harmful.
  • Diversity (popularity-weighted −log p(x)): ABPO highest in most domain/backbone cells (e.g. Fashion GDPO Div@1 37.07 vs 26.6–30.7 for baselines).
  • Ablation (Rec-R1 backbone): anchoring is the largest single contributor; SNIPS and self-certainty each add further gains; full method best.
  • Online A/B (Feb–Apr 2026, production): only ABPO shows monotonic CTR growth 7.23% → 7.48% → 7.62%; beats GRPO-Update by +3.3–3.6pp every month (same backbone) and ML-Retrain by +0.34pp aggregate (z=6.74).

Category: new method (industrial system paper with a small theory appendix). Context: builds on GRPO/RLVR (DeepSeekMath, DeepSeek-R1), Rec-R1-style LLM-Rec RL training, off-policy IPS/SNIPS correction from the bandit-learning literature; compares against DEALRec, RLRec, ULRec update strategies. Correctness: offline bandit feedback is synthetic (y=1 iff the sampled exposed item equals the next observed item in a 200-candidate set) — propensities are exact by construction, which is much cleaner than any real log; online A/B has heavily unequal traffic shares (production allocation), though user-level randomization and z-tests are reported. Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: mitigates exposure bias and feedback ambiguity in continual LLM-Rec updates; stable production improvement.
  • Demonstrated: consistent offline gains on synthetic bandit logs where propensities are known exactly; monotonic CTR in one production system.
  • Gap: the offline no-response label is "exposed item ≠ next observed item," i.e. a simulator artifact — the claimed handling of real ambiguous no-responses (inattention, position bias) is only indirectly validated through the online test, where confounds (unequal traffic, business-logic-customized updates per month) are acknowledged. Theory covers baseline-shift direction only, not convergence or unbiasedness (explicitly disclaimed). [SKIM - verify appendix D details in deep read]
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Policy-shaped contextual bandit feedback

  • What it computes: deployment logs → record (context, exposed item a^log, response y ∈ {1,0}, logging propensity e0(a^log|x)) only for the item the prior serving policy chose to expose → a partial, exposure-biased training signal.
  • Why it was created (as a framing): naive updates on such logs learn the prior policy's exposure distribution rather than true user preference, creating a self-reinforcing loop that amplifies popularity bias.
  • Concrete example: Fig. 2 — with a +1/−1 item-matching reward, GRPO's matching rate on positive-response exposures climbs to ~60% (self-reinforcement) while no-response items stop being generated entirely (over-penalization).
  • How it is used in this paper: the problem setting (Definition 2); motivates every component of ABPO.

Anchored Bandit Policy Optimization (ABPO) / anchor-augmented rollout group

  • What it computes: for each logged prompt, a GRPO rollout group of size G where rollout 1 is replaced by the logged exposed item a^log (a fixed "anchor"); the anchor enters only the group-relative baseline mean/std, never the surrogate gradient itself.
  • Why it was created: without anchoring, sampled rollouts rarely match the logged item in a 200-item candidate space, so the sparse bandit reward never reliably enters group normalization; treating a^log as a label to imitate causes self-reinforcement instead.
  • Concrete example: expected anchored baseline = α·r(a^log) + (1−α)·V^ρ(q) with α = ω/(ω+G−1) — a positive-response anchor raises the baseline (dampening copying of past successes), a no-response anchor lowers it (buffering penalties).
  • How it is used in this paper: the core method (§3.2, Eq. 7); ablation shows anchoring alone is the key stabilizing component.

SNIPS anchor reweighting

  • What it computes: stop-gradient item-level propensity ratio w = e_old(a^log|q)/e0(a^log|q) (both softmaxes over the same candidate set of length-normalized LLM token scores), self-normalized separately within positive- and no-response mini-batch strata; weights only the anchor's contribution to the group baseline.
  • Why it was created: both feedback types are observed only through prior-policy exposure, so the anchor's reward is off-policy relative to the current rollout distribution.
  • Concrete example: Eq. (4)–(6); the paper explicitly limits scope — SNIPS makes the anchor contribution exposure-calibrated but does NOT make the full ABPO objective an unbiased counterfactual estimator.
  • How it is used in this paper: §3.3; ablation variant (D).

Self-certainty reward for no-responses

  • What it computes: per-rollout mean KL(Uniform ‖ π_θ(·|prefix)) over output tokens → a verifier-free model-confidence scalar added (weight λ_sc) to the reward only for no-response cases.
  • Why it was created: a no-response is ambiguous (inattention, position effects, timing) — treating it as a hard negative injects false negatives and collapses diversity.
  • Concrete example: reward for no-response rollouts = −I[a^j = a^log] + r_fmt + λ_sc·r_sc; borrowed from Zhao et al. 2025 ("Learning to reason without external rewards").
  • How it is used in this paper: §3.4.1; ablation variant (E). [SKIM - λ_sc value and sensitivity not checked]
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: an SFT'd LLM-Rec backbone (Gemma-3-4B-IT in the paper), logged (context, exposed item, response, propensity) tuples, GRPO training stack (rollout group 16, LoRA r=8, DeepSpeed ZeRO-2); paper used 8× H200.
  • Code: available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/ABPO-5CD8 (anonymous link; data also released).
  • Modifications: for our setting, would need estimated (not exact) propensities and a slate/position exposure model; the anchor-baseline trick could be tested on any group-relative update rule.
  • Unknowns: missing link: sensitivity to propensity misestimation; missing link: λ_sc choice and self-certainty behavior when the backbone is poorly calibrated.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = user interaction history + 200-item candidate set in an LLM prompt; outputs = generated next-item recommendation; conditions = continual RL post-training of a deployed Gemma-3-4B LLM-Rec from logged one-step bandit feedback with known item-level propensities. Our setting (SP10 — feedback loops when exposure frequency serves as the ranking/relevance proxy): how exposure-conditioned observation corrupts learned rankings, and what corrections break the loop. Differences:

  • Known, exact logging propensities (softmax over a controlled candidate set): reference only — real ranking systems must estimate propensities; the paper's correction quality is an upper bound.
  • Generative LLM policy updated by GRPO vs. classical ranking-model retraining: missing link — the anchor-calibration idea is GRPO-group-specific; transferring it to non-group-relative learners is not addressed.
  • One-step bandit (single exposure, binary response) vs. slate/position-ranked exposure: missing link — position bias is named as a source of no-response ambiguity but never modeled.
  • Direct evidence for SP10's core claim: resolved (as evidence) — Figs. 1–2 quantify the exposure-feedback self-reinforcement loop (matching rate → 60% on past exposures; diversity collapse), a concrete measured instance of exposure frequency substituting for relevance.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the anchor + SNIPS component targets the root cause (the log's sampling distribution is the prior policy's exposure distribution, and it corrects for that mismatch with propensity weighting), but the self-certainty tempering of no-responses is a symptom patch: it softens penalties by model confidence without identifying why a no-response occurred (no examination/position model), and the exactness of propensities in the synthetic setup sidesteps the hardest part of the root cause in practice.
