Mitigating Exposure Bias in Online Learning to Rank Recommendation: A Novel Reward Model for Cascading Bandits

Mansoury et al. · arXiv · 2024 · 14 citations

arXiv:2408.04332 · PDF · SP: SP6

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

cascading bandit의 보상 모델을 바꿔 온라인 LTR의 exposure bias를 완화한다. 탐색 노이즈 자체는 exposure를 debias하지 않음을 보여, 탐색에만 기대는 debiasing 주장을 의심하게 만드는 근거.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Cascading bandit algorithms for online learning-to-rank reward all clicked items identically regardless of list position and penalize all examined-unclicked items identically. Because users disproportionately click already-over-exposed top items, this position-blind reward assignment lets a feedback loop amplify exposure bias over rounds: the paper's RQ1 analysis shows LinUCB's built-in UCB exploration improves exposure fairness only during the early exploration phase, and once exploration decays (~10K rounds at α=2) exposure bias stops decreasing — larger exploration coefficients α do not yield fairer exposure. Prior exposure-bias work targets static/offline recommenders; the online, dynamically-updated setting was underexplored.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Exposure-Aware Cascading Bandit (EACB): reformulate the reward so that examined items are rewarded/penalized as a function of their position,

$$w^{EA}_t(\mathcal{L}(k)) = \mathcal{F}_{t,k}\times\mathbb{1}[C_t=k] \;-\; \gamma\,\mathcal{F}_{t,k}\times\mathbb{1}[C_t<k],\quad k\in[1,\min\{K,C_t\}]$$

so clicked items lower in the list are rewarded more (they required user effort → likely genuinely relevant and under-exposed) and unclicked items higher in the list are penalized more (the model over-estimated them → downgrade over-exposed items). Integrated into CascadeLinUCB's ridge-regression update (only the B_t vector update changes: $+\mathcal{F}_{t,k}x_{\mathcal{L}(k)}$ on click, $-\gamma\mathcal{F}_{t,k}x_{\mathcal{L}(k)}$ otherwise). Theorem 1: for $\gamma \le 1/\mathcal{F}_{t,k} - 1$, the n-step regret keeps the same $\tilde{O}(dK\sqrt{n})$ upper bound as the original cascading bandit — the intervention costs nothing in regret order. Key Results주요 결과
  • MovieLens, d=10, K=10 (Table 4): EALinUCB-Log reaches Equality^P 0.514 vs. LinUCB 0.3656, EARSLinUCB 0.3848, FRMLinUCB 0.4157, with clicks nearly preserved (0.3605 vs. 0.3722).
  • Yahoo Music, d=10, K=5 (Table 3): EALinUCB-Linear Equality^P 0.5879 vs. LinUCB 0.3316 (+77% relative), clicks 0.1706 vs. 0.1802.
  • RQ1: higher exploration α does not monotonically improve fairness; α=0.25 was best across all metrics — built-in exploration is an inadequate exposure-bias mitigator.
  • γ sensitivity (RQ3): small γ (≈0.005) helps all exposure metrics; γ=0.2 collapses clicks toward 0 (penalty term dominates since unclicked items vastly outnumber clicked ones).
  • Theorem 1: same-order regret bound $R(n) \le 2\alpha K\sqrt{dn\log[1+nK/(d\sigma^2)]/\log(1+1/\sigma^2)} + 1$ as vanilla cascading bandits.
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: new method (reward-model modification for an existing bandit algorithm) + empirical analysis. Context: builds on cascading bandits (Kveton et al. 2015; Zong et al. 2016 linear variant); baselines are post-processing re-ranking (EARSLinUCB, Jeunen & Goethals 2021) and fairness-regret formulation (FRMLinUCB, Wang et al. 2021). Same authors' earlier workshop/preprint line (Mansoury et al. 2021/2022) on exposure bias in cascade bandits. Correctness: significance tested (p < 0.01 daggers in Tables 3–4); evaluation is a simulator built from offline MovieLens/Yahoo data with heavy preprocessing (1000 most active users; 9% / 1% of ratings retained) — findings may reflect dense-subset behavior. [SKIM - simulator fidelity not scrutinized] Contributions: (1) empirical demonstration that UCB exploration alone does not fix exposure bias long-run; (2) position-aware reward/penalty model (EACB) intervening at the update step rather than the ranking step; (3) regret bound showing the modification preserves $\tilde{O}(dK\sqrt{n})$. Clarity: Algorithm 1 plus Eq. 6 make the method reproducible; code released.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Cascading Bandit (CB) / cascade click model

  • What it computes: given a ranked list L of K items, assumes the user scans top-to-bottom, clicks the first attractive item at position C_t, and stops; items above the click are labeled unattractive (weight 0), the clicked item attractive (weight 1), items below unobserved. The bandit learns per-item attraction probabilities from these partial-feedback rounds.
  • Why it was created: standard bandits treat each slot's feedback independently; the cascade model handles position bias in list feedback (a non-click below a click carries no signal).
  • Concrete example: K=10, click at position 3 → items 1–2 get weight 0, item 3 gets weight 1, items 4–10 are unobserved and their estimates are not updated.
  • How it is used in this paper: the baseline algorithm class (linear cascading bandit of Zong et al. 2016, "CascadeLinUCB") whose exposure distribution the paper analyzes and modifies.

Exposure-Aware (EA) reward model

  • What it computes: replaces the binary cascade weight (Eq. 2) with a position-weighted one: $w^{EA}_t(\mathcal{L}(k)) = \mathcal{F}_{t,k}\cdot\mathbb{1}[C_t{=}k] - \gamma\,\mathcal{F}_{t,k}\cdot\mathbb{1}[C_t{<}k]$, where $\mathcal{F}_{t,k}$ is a position weight function (Log: $\log(1{+}k)$; RBP: $\beta^{k-1}$; Linear: $\beta k$) and $\gamma$ scales the penalty on examined-but-unclicked items.
  • Why it was created: vanilla CB rewards a click at position 1 and position K identically, so highly exposed top items keep accumulating reward and the feedback loop amplifies exposure bias.
  • Concrete example: with the Log function, a click at position 10 earns weight log(11) ≈ 2.4 vs. log(2) ≈ 0.69 at position 1 — a bottom-of-list click updates the model ~3.5× more strongly.
  • How it is used in this paper: plugged into the linear cascading bandit's parameter update (B_t update, Algorithm 1 lines 16–23) to produce EALinUCB.

Equality/Equity exposure-fairness metrics (1 − Gini family)

  • What it computes: four notions of per-item exposure — binary vs. position-discounted ($1/\log_2(1{+}k)$), each optionally divided by item merit (average MF relevance score) — then 1 − Gini index over the item-exposure distribution; 1 = perfectly uniform (fairest).
  • Why it was created: to separate "was the item shown at all" from "how prominently" and "proportionally to merit" when quantifying exposure bias.
  • Concrete example: Equality^P for item i sums $1/\log_2(1{+}k)$ over all lists containing i at position k, then 1 − Gini over all items.
  • How it is used in this paper: the four evaluation metrics for exposure bias (Tables 3–4, Figures 2–3).
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: MovieLens 1M / Yahoo Music (public), matrix-factorization embeddings, a cascade-click simulator; single-machine compute (50K rounds, 1000 users).
  • Code: available at https://github.com/masoudmansoury/ealinucb (full results website linked in paper).
  • Modifications: only the B_t update of any CascadeLinUCB-style learner changes; porting to Thompson-sampling or neural bandits is claimed as future work, untested.
  • Unknowns: missing link: whether the position-weighted reward remains beneficial under real (non-cascade) click behavior; missing link: γ tuning appears dataset-sensitive.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: online top-K recommendation with linear cascading bandits; inputs = item feature vectors (MF embeddings, d ∈ {10,20}) + simulated cascade-model clicks from binarized MovieLens 1M / Yahoo Music ratings; outputs = ranked list of K ∈ {5,10}; conditions = 50K simulated rounds, exposure measured by 1−Gini over four exposure notions. Our setting (SP6): exposure frequency acting as a de-facto proxy for quality/relevance in ranking systems — how repeated exposure feeds back into rank position and whether frequency-driven feedback loops can be corrected. Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the method intervenes at the learning-update step where the feedback loop actually operates (closer to the root than post-hoc re-ranking baselines), but the position weight $\mathcal{F}_{t,k}$ is a heuristic incentive rather than an unbiased estimator of true attraction probability, so the underlying identification problem (recovering item merit from exposure-confounded clicks) is incentive-patched, not solved.
