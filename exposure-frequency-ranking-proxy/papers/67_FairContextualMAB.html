Fair Contextual Multi-Armed Bandits: Theory and Experiments

Chen et al. · arXiv · 2020 · 69 citations

arXiv:1912.08055 · PDF · SP: SP6

P2Track C

한 줄 요약

공정 contextual bandit의 이론과 실험을 제공한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Prior fair-task-allocation bandit algorithms (Claure et al., Li et al., Patil et al.) define fairness as a minimum selection rate per arm, but (a) assume each arm's losses are drawn i.i.d. from a fixed arm-specific distribution — failing when users/arms have context-dependent skill (a worker good at cutting wire but bad at measuring it) — and (b) cannot handle non-stationary or adversarial loss generation.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Fair CB: cast fair contextual bandits as FTRL with an entropy regularizer over the convex feasible set $\Omega$ of joint per-context arm distributions whose $q$-weighted marginal pull probability of each arm is at least $v$. No assumption on loss generation (losses may depend on the full history). Regret against the best fixed feasible $P^*$ is $O(\sqrt{TMK\ln K})$ — the proof rewrites the algorithm as FTRL in $\mathbb{R}^{MK}$ and bounds the Bregman-divergence terms; the fairness constraint costs essentially nothing in the rate. Key Results주요 결과
  • Fair CB achieves $\mathrm{Reg} = O(\sqrt{TMK\ln K})$ under the marginal fairness constraint — same order as contextual bandits without any fairness constraint (Theorem 4.1).
  • Fairness costs performance only when the constraint forces a suboptimal arm: with even contexts and each arm best in one context, performance is flat across all $v$; with one arm dominant, regression gives predicted performance $= 0.37 - 0.11v$ ($p < .0001$, 66.1% variance explained).
  • With uneven contexts ($q(j_1)=0.9, q(j_2)=0.1$), performance degrades only once $v > q(j_2) = 0.1$ — i.e., once the mandated rate exceeds the natural exposure the arm's "good" context provides.
  • Against non-contextual FTRL at $v=0.45$: significant advantage ($F(1,198)=1197.43$, $p<0.0001$) because Fair CB imposes the fairness burden in the context where the arm gap is smallest.
  • Under adversarial (switching) losses with $M=1$: Fair CB mean performance 0.496 vs Fair UCB 0.45 ($p<0.001$); Fair UCB is exploited by the adversary at low fairness levels.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Minimum-rate fairness constraint (marginal pull-probability constraint)

  • What it computes: given per-context arm distributions $P = (p^1, \dots, p^M)$ and a known context distribution $q \in \Delta_M$, it requires the marginal probability of pulling each arm to satisfy $\sum_{j=1}^{M} q(j)\, p^j_t(i) \ge v$ for every arm $i$ and every round $t$, with $v \in (0, 1/K)$.
  • Why it was created: prior fair-bandit work (Claure et al.; Li et al.; Patil et al.) imposed minimum selection rates only in the non-contextual stochastic setting; this generalizes the constraint to hold marginally across contexts, so the algorithm can choose in which context to satisfy fairness cheaply.
  • Concrete example: with 2 arms, 2 contexts, $q(j_1)=q(j_2)=0.5$ and $v=0.45$: if arm 1 is better in both contexts, it is eventually pulled with probability 0.55 (its opponent gets the mandated 0.45); if each arm is best in one context, the constraint is satisfied for free at no performance cost.
  • How it is used in this paper: defines the feasible set $\Omega$ (a convex set) over which the whole algorithm optimizes; regret is measured against the best fixed $P^* \in \Omega$.

Fair CB algorithm (FTRL over the fairness-constrained joint decision set)

  • What it computes: at each round, $P_t = \arg\min_{P \in \Omega} \sum_{s<t} \langle p^{j_s}, \hat{l}_s \rangle + \frac{1}{\eta}\sum_{j,i} p^j(i)\ln p^j(i)$ — Follow-the-Regularized-Leader with entropy regularizer, jointly over all M context-distributions instead of one FTRL instance per context, using importance-weighted loss estimators $\hat{l}_s(i) = \frac{l_s(i)}{p^{j_s}_s(i)}\mathbb{1}\{i_s = i\}$.
  • Why it was created: the fairness constraint couples contexts (it is marginal over $q$), so the standard "run one Exp3 per context" decomposition no longer applies; joint FTRL over $\Omega$ satisfies the constraint by construction.
  • Concrete example: unlike Exp3, $P_t$ has no closed form; the paper solves it with a standard convex-optimization toolbox each round.
  • How it is used in this paper: the core contribution; proven to achieve $\mathrm{Reg} = O(\sqrt{TMK \ln K})$ with $\eta = \sqrt{M \ln K / (TK)}$ — essentially matching the unconstrained bound.

No other new concepts — Exp3, FTRL, and UCB are standard; "Fair UCB" is the baseline from Claure et al. (stochastic UCB with a minimum-pull-rate constraint).

Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Patil et al., "Achieving Fairness in the Stochastic Multi-Armed Bandit Problem" (2020) — the unfairness-tolerance variant of the same minimum-rate constraint; cited as [21] and used as a Fair UCB reference implementation.
  • Claure et al. (2019/2020), Fair UCB with strict minimum-rate constraint [9] — the direct predecessor whose stochastic assumption this paper removes; also the source of the Tetris trust user study motivating the fairness definition.
  • Li, Liu & Ji, "Combinatorial Sleeping Bandits with Fairness Constraints" (2019) — the in-expectation minimum-rate variant [18], closer to exposure-in-expectation notions used in ranking fairness.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = discrete context $j_t \in [M]$ (M small, finite) with known distribution $q$, adversarial loss vectors in $[0,1]^K$; outputs = one arm pulled per round; condition = fairness as a minimum marginal selection rate $v$ per arm, one user served at a time, losses = task performance. Our setting (SP6, exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy): items ranked in slates for many users; fairness target is minimum exposure (impression frequency weighted by position), where exposure frequency stands in as a proxy for ranking quality/relevance signal. Differences:

  • Single arm pulled per round vs ranked slate with position-weighted exposure: missing link — the marginal-probability constraint is structurally the bandit analog of a minimum-exposure floor, but no position/slate model here.
  • Selection rate = fairness currency vs exposure frequency = proxy for relevance: reference only — this paper treats the minimum rate as the fairness definition itself, and its analysis of "when does the constraint cost performance" (only when $v$ exceeds an arm's naturally-earned rate) transfers as intuition for exposure floors.
  • Finite small M contexts, known q vs high-dimensional user/query features: missing link [SKIM - verify Section 7 unknown-q extension].
  • Adversarial losses vs stochastic click/relevance feedback: resolved — adversarial guarantee subsumes the stochastic case.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — for the problem it poses (guaranteeing a minimum selection rate under contextual, assumption-free losses), it solves the root cause by building the constraint into the decision set and proving the regret rate is unchanged, rather than post-hoc patching an unconstrained policy.
