Joint Multisided Exposure Fairness for Recommendation

Wu et al. · arXiv · 2022 · 80 citations

arXiv:2205.00048 · PDF · SP: SP1 · SP9

P2Track A

한 줄 요약

개별 item-user 쌍을 넘어 item 집단이 user 집단에 체계적으로 과소/과다 노출되는 문제를 다루는 여섯 가지 다면적 exposure 공정성 지표 체계를 제안한다. 동일한 개별 지표 값에서도 집단 수준 지표가 임의로 달라질 수 있음을 보인다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Prior exposure-fairness work (Diaz et al. 2020's expected exposure framework) measures disparities only from individual users to individual items or groups of items — it cannot detect systemic disparities where item groups are under/over-exposed to user groups or to all users (e.g. a job recommender steering women toward low-paying jobs, or under-exposing jobs from minority-owned businesses to everyone). Multisided fairness had been argued for conceptually (Burke 2017; Ekstrand et al. 2021) but had no formal metric formulation.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Extend the expected-exposure framework with group attributes on both the consumer (user) and producer (item) side, yielding six metrics — II-F, IG-F, GI-F, GG-F, AI-F, AG-F — all mean squared deviations $(\mathsf{E}_{ij}-\mathsf{E}^*_{ij})^2$ aggregated at different granularities (individual / group / all, on each side). GG-F generalizes all others via trivial group definitions. Each metric decomposes into disparity + relevance components measured against random exposure $\mathsf{E}^\sim$. The metrics are differentiable, so a recommender (MF base model) can be trained on $\mathcal{L} = \text{II-F} + \alpha\,\text{GG-F}$ via PL sampling + Gumbel + smooth ranks.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Six systems can have identical II-F (0.0625 in the toy example) while differing arbitrarily on the other five dimensions — II-F alone masks systemic group-level disparity.
  • Metric implications: II-fair ⟹ all six fair; IG-fair or GI-fair ⟹ GG-fair; GI-fair ⟹ AI-fair; GG-fair or AI-fair ⟹ AG-fair; no reverse implications.
  • Kendall rank correlation between II-F and the other five metrics is low (0.307–0.504 across MovieLens1M model instances), confirming the metrics rank systems differently.
  • Optimizing $\text{II-F} + \alpha\,\text{GG-F}$ at $\alpha=1$: statistically significant GG-F drop (p < 0.01) with non-significant II-F increase; NDCG@50 degrades only 0.3703→0.3692 (ML100K) and 0.2741→0.2736 (ML1M).
  • Best disparity-relevance trade-off model differs per metric: WRMF wins GI/GG/AI/AG AUC, SLIM wins II (0.6408).

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: first formal joint multisided exposure-fairness family; metrics capture distinct systemic biases; stochastic policies can be optimized toward them; framework extends to search "without loss of generality."
  • Demonstrated: metric definitions + toy-example separation + correlation analysis on MovieLens (gender/age × genre groups); optimization demo with MF on MovieLens100K/1M for one combination (II-F + GG-F) only.
  • Gap: implication proofs omitted; only demographic-style group attributes on one dataset family (MovieLens); the claimed extension to search/other IR tasks untested; target exposure assumes true relevance labels, which the authors themselves flag as circular under historically biased data. Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Expected exposure matrix E

  • What it computes: stochastic ranking policy $\pi_u$ per user → expectation over sampled rankings of a browsing-model exposure probability → matrix $\mathsf{E} \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{U}|\times|\mathcal{D}|}$ with $\mathsf{E}_{ij} = p(\epsilon\,|\,\mathcal{D}_j, \pi_{\mathcal{U}_i})$, the probability item $j$ is seen by user $i$.
  • Why it was created: static rank positions do not quantify how much attention items actually receive under randomized policies; Diaz et al. (2020) introduced it, this paper reuses it. (Introduced in prior work — re-defined here because that prior paper is not yet analyzed in this survey.)
  • Concrete example: under the RBP browsing model $p_{RBP}(\epsilon|d,\sigma) = \gamma^{\rho_{d,\sigma}-1}$ with patience $\gamma=0.8$, the item at rank 1 has exposure 1, rank 2 has 0.8, rank 3 has 0.64.
  • How it is used in this paper: the primitive from which all six fairness metrics are computed as deviations from target exposure $\mathsf{E}^*$.

JME-fairness metric family (II-F, IG-F, GI-F, GG-F, AI-F, AG-F)

  • What it computes: system exposure matrix $\mathsf{E}$, target exposure $\mathsf{E}^*$, plus group partitions on users and/or items → mean squared deviation aggregated at the chosen granularity → one scalar per metric (lower = fairer). E.g. $\text{II-F} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}||\mathcal{U}|}\sum_{j}\sum_{i}(\mathsf{E}_{ij}-\mathsf{E}^*_{ij})^2$; GG-F first averages the deviation within a user group × item group cell, then squares.
  • Why it was created: Diaz et al.'s II-F cannot distinguish whether exposure deviations are spread uniformly or concentrated systemically on specific user/item groups (e.g. women disproportionately shown low-paying jobs).
  • Concrete example: toy 4-user × 4-job system (Fig. 1) — six exposure matrices all with II-F = 0.0625 but differing on the other five metrics (one matrix has all six = 0.0625, another has only II-F nonzero).
  • How it is used in this paper: defined in §4, analyzed for stochasticity trade-offs in §5, used as differentiable training objectives in §6.

**Disparity–relevance decomposition (-D / -R)**

  • What it computes: each JME metric → algebraic expansion of the squared deviation using $\mathsf{E}^\delta = \mathsf{E}-\mathsf{E}^\sim$ (deviation of system from random exposure) and $\mathsf{E}^\Delta = \mathsf{E}^*-\mathsf{E}^\sim$ → a disparity component (-D) plus a relevance component (-R) plus a system-independent constant.
  • Why it was created: to expose the inherent trade-off — adding ranking stochasticity lowers disparity but also lowers relevance; measuring against random exposure $\mathsf{E}^\sim$ (rather than a flat distribution) makes the decomposition consistent when groups have unequal sizes.
  • Concrete example: AUC of the disparity–relevance curve on MovieLens1M: WRMF is best on GI/GG/AI/AG (e.g. GG AUC 0.3186) but SLIM is best on II (0.6408).
  • How it is used in this paper: §4.3 and §5 (RQ1/RQ2 analysis of stochasticity and cross-metric correlation).

Plackett-Luce randomization with Gumbel reparameterization + smooth rank — sampling rankings via softmax-with-temperature $\beta$ over relevance scores; made differentiable by Gumbel noise plus a smooth (sigmoid-sum) rank approximation. Follows Diaz et al. (2020); used here to optimize $\mathcal{L} = \text{II-F} + \alpha \cdot \text{GG-F}$. [SKIM - established techniques, not new here]

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: user-item interaction data with relevance labels, group attributes on both sides, a base ranker producing relevance scores; light compute (MF, embedding size 64, MovieLens scale).
  • Code: available at https://github.com/haolun-wu/JMEFairness (code + datasets).
  • Modifications: for a proxy-signal study, replace target exposure $\mathsf{E}^*$ with observed exposure logs; swap RBP for the browsing model matching the surface being studied; group attributes optional if only AI-F/II-F granularities used.
  • Unknowns: missing link — sensitivity of the metrics to browsing-model choice (paper uses only RBP, γ=0.8); missing link — behavior when relevance labels are absent or implicit-only.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Diaz et al., "Evaluating stochastic rankings with expected exposure" (CIKM 2020) — the parent framework this paper extends; defines expected exposure, II-F, and the Gumbel optimization approach.
  • Oosterhuis, "Computationally Efficient Optimization of Plackett-Luce Ranking Models for Relevance and Fairness" (SIGIR 2021) — more efficient PL optimization applicable to the same objectives.
  • Singh & Joachims, "Fairness of Exposure in Rankings" (KDD 2018) — producer-side exposure fairness baseline the JME family generalizes beyond.
  • Ekstrand et al., "Fairness and Discrimination in Information Access Systems" (2021) — survey articulating the multisided-fairness motivation this paper formalizes.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = user-item relevance labels + user/item group attributes (MovieLens 100K/1M: gender, age × genre); outputs = stochastic ranking policies + scalar fairness metrics; conditions = explicit browsing model (RBP), known group memberships, offline evaluation, recommendation task. Our setting (SP1/SP9 — exposure frequency as a ranking proxy): we care about how exposure frequency functions as a proxy signal for ranking quality/position, potentially without ground-truth relevance or explicit group labels. Differences:

  • Paper requires a target exposure $\mathsf{E}^*$ built from true relevance labels; our setting may lack ground-truth relevance: missing link
  • Paper's exposure is model-derived (RBP over sampled rankings), giving an analytic exposure-frequency ↔ rank-position mapping ($\gamma^{\rho-1}$) directly usable as a proxy formalism: resolved — the RBP/expected-exposure machinery is the formal bridge from rank position to exposure frequency
  • Paper assumes known user/item group attributes; our setting may not have group partitions: reference only [SKIM - verify against SP definitions]
  • Recommendation (users × items) vs. our possibly search/LLM-ranking setting; authors claim users→queries substitution works: reference only
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the metric family directly formalizes the previously unmeasurable root phenomenon (systemic group-level exposure disparity), but the mitigation is a symptom-level patch: injecting model-independent stochasticity and linearly weighting objectives trades disparity against relevance rather than removing the source of the disparity, and the target-exposure-from-biased-labels circularity is acknowledged but left unsolved.
