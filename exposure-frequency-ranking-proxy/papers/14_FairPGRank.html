Policy Learning for Fairness in Ranking

Singh & Joachims · arXiv · 2019 · 250 citations

arXiv:1902.04056 · PDF · SP: SP1 · SP3 · SP4 · SP6

P2Track C

한 줄 요약

exposure-to-merit 비율의 등식 제약이 일반적으로 실행 불가능함을 증명하고 한쪽 방향(상한) 제약으로 대체하는 in-processing 정책 학습을 제안한다. 편향된 추정 relevance 위의 post-processing이 fairness 가중치를 올릴수록 오히려 덜 공정해지는 실패 사례를 보인다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Fairness-of-exposure work prior to this paper (Singh & Joachims 2018 LP post-processing; Biega et al. 2018 amortized equity of attention) assumes the true relevances of all items are known and only re-ranks given scores. Two concrete gaps: (1) no LTR algorithm existed that learns a ranking function under merit-based exposure constraints; (2) post-processing applied on top of estimated relevances is structurally blind to bias baked into the learned representation — if the relevance estimator exploits a feature that is corrupted for a minority group, post-processing on those estimates can make exposure less fair.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Cast fair LTR as constrained ERM over stochastic ranking policies: maximize empirical utility subject to empirical disparity $\le \delta$, solved in Lagrangian form (Eq. 1 above) with a chosen $\lambda$. Policies are Plackett-Luce distributions induced by any differentiable scoring model (linear or neural net). Both the utility term and the disparity term are expectations over sampled rankings, so both gradients follow from the REINFORCE log-derivative trick and Monte-Carlo estimation. Fairness measures are one-sided exposure-to-merit ratio constraints, in individual ($\mathcal{D}_{ind}$, Eq. 3) and group ($\mathcal{D}_{group}$, Eq. 4) forms. Key Results주요 결과
  • PG-Rank ($\lambda=0$) on Yahoo LTR Set 1: NDCG@10 0.76145 (linear) vs. RankSVM 0.75924; neural net 0.77082; GBDT baseline 0.79013 (ERR: PG-Rank NN 0.45440 vs. RankSVM 0.43680, GBDT 0.46201).
  • Synthetic biased-feature dataset (minority group's second feature zeroed): increasing $\lambda$ drives the learned linear model toward $\theta_1 \gg \theta_2$, i.e., it detects and discounts the biased feature; the Singh & Joachims 2018 post-processing baseline on estimated relevances becomes less fair as its $\lambda$ increases, and Zehlike & Castillo 2018's heuristic barely moves disparity.
  • Yahoo individual fairness: $\mathcal{D}_{ind}$ goes to ~0 as $\lambda \to 10^3$–$10^4$ with NDCG@10 degrading from ~0.76 to ~0.6; train/test curves overlap (generalizes).
  • German Credit group fairness (gender): smooth NDCG-vs-$\mathcal{D}_{group}$ trade-off; baselines fail to trade off.

Clarity: high — objective, PL model, gradients, and hyperparameters (appendix B) are all stated; reproducible from the paper.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장

Merit is set to $M(rel)=rel$ (identity) in all experiments despite the framework claiming general merit functions.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Fair-PG-Rank (policy-gradient fair LTR)

  • What it computes: training set of (query features, relevance labels) → SGD on a Lagrangian objective $\hat{\pi}^*_\lambda = \arg\max_\pi \frac{1}{N}\sum_q U(\pi|q) - \lambda \frac{1}{N}\sum_q \mathcal{D}(\pi|q)$ over stochastic ranking policies → a Plackett-Luce ranking policy that trades off user utility (e.g., NDCG) against exposure disparity.
  • Why it was created: prior fairness-of-exposure methods (Singh & Joachims 2018; Biega et al. 2018) assume known relevances and post-process rankings — they cannot handle the learning problem or detect biased features in the representation.
  • Concrete example: 5 male candidates with relevance 0.89 vs. 5 female candidates with 0.88 — relevance-sorted ranking puts all females at positions 6–10, allocating drastically less exposure for a 0.01 merit gap.
  • How it is used in this paper: the core contribution; evaluated on synthetic data, Yahoo LTR, and German Credit.

Exposure (of a document under a stochastic policy)

  • What it computes: position bias vector $v_j$ (fraction of users examining position $j$) + policy $\pi(r|q)$ → $v_\pi(d_i) = \mathbb{E}_{r\sim\pi}[v_{r(d_i)}]$ → expected attention a document receives.
  • Why it was created: makes the item-side impact of a ranking a first-class measurable quantity, rather than a by-product of utility maximization.
  • Concrete example: paper sets $v_j \propto 1/\log_2(1+j)$, the DCG gain curve.
  • How it is used in this paper: the quantity constrained by all fairness measures.

Merit-proportional inequality constraints (one-sided exposure-to-merit)

  • What it computes: for all pairs with $M_i \ge M_j > 0$: enforce $\frac{v_\pi(d_i)}{M_i} \le \frac{v_\pi(d_j)}{M_j}$; disparity $\mathcal{D}_{ind}$ averages the hinge violations $\max(0, v_\pi(d_i)/M_i - v_\pi(d_j)/M_j)$ over such pairs.
  • Why it was created: strict proportionality Exposure $\propto$ Merit is infeasible when low-merit documents must still occupy positions (overabundant exposure); the equality-based Disparate Exposure (Singh & Joachims 2018) and Equity of Attention (Biega et al. 2018) both share this defect. One-sided constraints only prevent higher-merit items from getting exposure beyond merit-ratio, which counteracts rich-get-richer dynamics.
  • Concrete example: one document with relevance 1 and nine with relevance $\epsilon$: any ranking over-exposes the $\epsilon$-documents, so equality constraints are unsatisfiable.
  • How it is used in this paper: instantiated as $\mathcal{D}_{ind}$ (individual) and $\mathcal{D}_{group}$ (group-averaged exposure/merit ratios), both plugged into the training objective.

PG-Rank (REINFORCE over Plackett-Luce rankings)

  • What it computes: scores $h_\theta(x^q)$ → Plackett-Luce distribution over permutations (product of softmaxes over remaining docs) → log-derivative-trick gradient $\mathbb{E}_{r\sim\pi_\theta}[\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(r|q)\,\Delta(r, rel^q)]$ estimated with $S$ Monte-Carlo sampled rankings, plus baseline subtraction and entropy regularization.
  • Why it was created: conventional LTR optimizes smoothed surrogates or heuristic bounds (RankSVM, RankNet, SoftRank, ListNet); PG directly optimizes any IR metric, including non-decomposable and IPS-weighted ones.
  • Concrete example: $S=10$ sampled rankings per query on Yahoo; $S=25$ on German Credit.
  • How it is used in this paper: standalone LTR contribution ($\lambda=0$ case) and the optimization engine for the fairness term.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성

Modest compute.

Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Singh & Joachims (2018), "Fairness of Exposure in Rankings" (KDD) — the predecessor whose disparity framework and LP post-processing this paper generalizes to learning; likely also in this survey's citation graph.
  • Biega, Gummadi & Weikum (2018), "Equity of Attention" (SIGIR) — amortized individual fairness; shares the infeasible-equality-constraint defect this paper fixes.
  • Zehlike & Castillo (2018), "Reducing Disparate Exposure in Ranking" — the heuristic in-training baseline Fair-PG-Rank outperforms.
  • Joachims, Swaminathan & Schnabel (2017), "Unbiased LTR with Biased Feedback" (WSDM) — the IPS bridge from this paper's known-relevance setting to our exposure-confounded-feedback setting.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: supervised LTR with known, trusted relevance labels (Yahoo editorial judgments 0–4; German Credit binary creditworthiness); known position-bias curve; fairness enforced per-query in expectation over a stochastic policy; merit = relevance. Our setting (exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy survey): concerned with exposure/impression frequency acting as a proxy or feedback signal for ranking quality — settings where relevance is not directly observed but inferred from exposure-dependent feedback, and where exposure itself distorts the training signal. Differences:

  • Relevance labels known and unbiased here vs. exposure-confounded implicit feedback in our setting: missing link — the paper explicitly does not address biased/partial feedback for the fairness term (though PG-Rank mentions IPS-weighted metrics for utility).
  • Exposure treated as an allocation target (what the ranker gives items) here vs. exposure as a measurement proxy (what the signal is contaminated by) in our setting: reference only — the exposure formalism $v_\pi(d)=\mathbb{E}[v_{r(d)}]$ and position-bias vocabulary transfer directly.
  • Static per-query fairness in expectation vs. dynamic/amortized feedback loops (rich-get-richer over time): partially resolved — the one-sided constraint is explicitly motivated as counteracting "unmerited rich-get-richer dynamics," but only within a stationary training distribution; no feedback-loop dynamics modeled.
  • Position bias assumed known ($1/\log_2(1+j)$): missing link for our setting where the exposure→attention mapping is itself estimated (paper cites eye-tracking, swap experiments, intervention harvesting as external estimators).
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper attacks the root cause it targets: unfair exposure arises during learning (biased representations), so it moves the fairness constraint inside the training objective rather than patching rankings post hoc, and demonstrates on synthetic data that the post-processing alternative fails exactly where the root cause (a group-corrupted feature) lives. Caveat for our survey: it takes relevance labels as ground-truth merit, so the upstream question of whether relevance itself is exposure-contaminated is out of scope.
