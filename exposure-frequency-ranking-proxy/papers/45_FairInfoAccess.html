Fairness in Information Access Systems

Ekstrand et al. · Foundations and Trends in Information Retrieval 2022 · 2022

arXiv:2105.05779 · PDF · SP: SP4

P1Track A

한 줄 요약

정보 접근 시스템의 공정성을 체계화한 Foundations and Trends 단행본급 survey.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Fairness concepts developed for classification (one-shot, independent, user-independent, objective-outcome decisions about a single stakeholder class) do not transfer to information access. Rankings make decisions non-independent (a slot given to one item is denied to others), repeated over time, personalized, evaluated against subjective relevance, and multistakeholder. Before this monograph there was no integrated taxonomy relating the fast-growing fair-ranking/fair-recommendation literature to these structural differences.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

A survey + taxonomy (not a new method). Core moves:

  1. Ch. 4 taxonomy — dimensions of fair information access: category of harm (representational vs. distributional), stakeholder (consumer/provider/subject/side), mechanism (misrepresentation, unfair result-set composition, unfair benefit distribution), vantage point, time scale, pipeline stage, and five explicit ways classifier fairness "breaks" in IA (Table 4.2).
  2. Ch. 5–6 literature synthesis — consumer fairness (disaggregated utility, disparate effectiveness) and provider fairness (representation metrics vs. exposure/utility metrics vs. pairwise-accuracy metrics), with a normalized-notation comparison of fair ranking constructs (Table 6.2: PreF, AWRF, FAIR, DP, EUR, RUR, IAA, EEL/EER/EED, Pair).
  3. Ch. 7–8 — dynamic fairness (feedback loops amplifying exposure inequity) and open problems / methodological recommendations (define the harm, operationalize it, use appropriate data, report limitations).
Key Results주요 결과
  • No empirical results of its own; key synthesized claims:
  • Five structural breaks between classification fairness and IA fairness (Table 4.2): ranked non-independent decisions; repeated decisions over time; personalization; subjective outcomes; multiple stakeholder classes.
  • Fair exposure is generally achievable only in expectation (stochastic policies) or amortized over sequences — not within a single ranking (§6.2).
  • Groupwise exposure metrics that aggregate exposure and relevance separately can be gamed by exposing the wrong items of a group; per-item over/under-exposure aggregated by group (adopted by TREC 2022 Fair Ranking) fixes this (§6.2).
  • Positive feedback loops (popularity bias, presentation bias) make systems "tend naturally toward unfairness," requiring continuous counteraction (§7.1).
  • Fairness–accuracy tradeoff is not inherent; observed tradeoffs often arise from biased evaluation data (§4.8, citing Dutta et al. 2020, Bigdeli et al. 2021).

Category: survey (177-page FnTIR monograph). Context: builds on classifier-fairness literature (Mitchell et al. 2020, Barocas & Selbst 2016, Friedler et al. 2021) and integrates the fair-ranking canon: Biega et al. 2018, Singh & Joachims 2018/2019, Diaz et al. 2020, Zehlike et al. 2017, Sapiezynski et al. 2019, Beutel et al. 2019. Within this survey it is the foundational Track-A reference tying together the exposure-metric papers. [SKIM] Contributions: (1) taxonomy of fair-IA problem space; (2) precise articulation of why classification fairness breaks in ranking (non-independent, repeated, personalized, subjective, multistakeholder); (3) normalized comparison of exposure/representation/pairwise fair-ranking metrics. Clarity: high — designed as an entry point for both IR and fairness audiences; notation is unified across surveyed metrics.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: provides a taxonomy and survey enabling scholars from either parent field to work on fair IA; claims the taxonomy is useful, not complete ("overlapping lenses," §4.10).
  • Demonstrated: comprehensive coverage of the pre-2022 literature with unified notation; the metric comparison (Table 6.2) is derived from Raj & Ekstrand (2022)'s empirical comparison.
  • Gap: the taxonomy's usefulness is asserted, not evaluated; several identified harms are explicitly hypothetical ("not every harm we discuss has been documented in the wild"). [SKIM — acceptable for a survey; verify no over-claiming in deep read if needed.]
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Information access system

  • What it computes: a repository of items + a user information need → retrieval/ranking/rendering pipeline → ranked (possibly stochastic) result lists presented to users.
  • Why it was created: umbrella abstraction unifying IR, recommender systems, and filtering so that fairness concepts can be discussed once for all of them.
  • Concrete example: a music platform mapping a listener's history to a ranked list of artist recommendations.
  • How it is used in this paper: the framing device for the entire monograph (Ch. 2).

Provider / consumer / subject fairness (multistakeholder taxonomy)

  • What it computes: a classification of who can be harmed — consumers (users receiving results), providers (item creators receiving exposure), subjects (people the items are about), plus side stakeholders.
  • Why it was created: classifier fairness assumes one decision-subject class; information access has multiple stakeholder classes with distinct, sometimes competing, fairness claims.
  • Concrete example: in job-candidate search, recruiters are consumers and candidates are providers; the roles reverse in job-listing recommendation on the same platform.
  • How it is used in this paper: organizes Chapters 5 (consumer) and 6 (provider, with subjects in §6.4).

Exposure (attention) as the allocable resource

  • What it computes: item's rank position → browsing-model discount δ(r) → probability mass of user attention the item receives; aggregated over a stochastic policy this is expected exposure EE(d|π).
  • Why it was created: single rankings cannot be fair (position 1 is a rivalrous good); measuring exposure in expectation or amortized over time makes fair allocation achievable.
  • Concrete example: under rank-biased precision with γ, an item at rank r receives δ(r) = (1−γ)γ^(r−1) exposure.
  • How it is used in this paper: the central quantity of Ch. 6.2, connecting Diaz et al. (2020) EEL/EED, Biega et al. (2018) amortized attention, and Singh & Joachims (2018) DP/EUR/RUR. [SKIM — the survey synthesizes rather than introduces these; each metric originates in a cited paper.]

Disparate effectiveness

  • What it computes: per-group aggregation of a utility metric (nDCG, satisfaction) → statistically significant between-group gaps.
  • Why it was created: "disparate impact" doesn't fit — the system makes no decision about consumers; the harm is unequal quality of service.
  • Concrete example: Mehrotra et al. (2017) found search satisfaction gaps across age groups that vanished after matching on query difficulty.
  • How it is used in this paper: Ch. 5.3's construct for consumer-side group fairness.

WYSIWYG vs. WAE assumptions

  • What it computes: two axiomatic bases for fairness constructs — "what you see is what you get" (observed data unbiased) vs. "we're all equal" (group differences in observations are discrimination artifacts).
  • Why it was created: makes explicit the normative assumption each fairness metric silently imports (from Friedler et al.).
  • Concrete example: statistical parity presumes WAE; error-parity metrics presume WYSIWYG labels.
  • How it is used in this paper: Ch. 3.2 uses them to organize all group-fairness constructs.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: nothing to run — it is a reference work; Appendix A lists data (TREC Fair Ranking, PIReT book data) and software (Terrier xQuAD, librec-auto).
  • Code: n/a (survey); pointers to experiment scripts of surveyed papers included.

Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Diaz et al., "Evaluating Stochastic Rankings with Expected Exposure" (CIKM 2020) — the expected-exposure metric family central to SP4.
  • Biega, Gummadi & Weikum, "Equity of Attention" (SIGIR 2018) — amortized individual-fairness exposure; origin of the exposure∝relevance principle.
  • Singh & Joachims, "Fairness of Exposure in Rankings" (KDD 2018) — DP/EUR/RUR group exposure constraints via stochastic policies.
  • Raj & Ekstrand, "Measuring Fairness in Ranked Results" (SIGIR 2022) — empirical/analytical comparison of the fair-ranking metrics this survey tabulates.
  • Sapiezynski et al., "Quantifying the Impact of User Attention on Fair Group Representation in Ranked Lists" (WWW 2019) — AWRF, attention-weighted representation.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = the fair-IA literature to ~2021; outputs = taxonomy + metric synthesis; conditions = ranking/recommendation systems, group and individual fairness, mostly offline evaluation. Our setting (SP4): analyzing exposure/frequency-of-appearance as a proxy for ranking value or fairness — how exposure metrics are defined, what they assume, and where the proxy breaks. Differences:

  • The survey treats exposure as the resource to allocate fairly, exactly our object of study: resolved — Ch. 6.2 gives the definitional foundations (browsing-model discounts, expected/amortized exposure) SP4 needs.
  • It documents proxy failure modes (exposure ≠ engagement; RUR vs. EUR distinction; group aggregation gaming): resolved — directly usable.
  • It does not quantify how well exposure predicts realized provider utility (revenue, clicks) empirically: missing link — SP4 needs empirical papers for that.
  • Consumer-side and dynamic-fairness chapters: reference only for SP4.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the monograph attacks the root conceptual mismatch (why classification-fairness constructs structurally fail under ranked, repeated, personalized, multistakeholder decisions) rather than proposing another metric patch; its taxonomy is built from those structural causes.
