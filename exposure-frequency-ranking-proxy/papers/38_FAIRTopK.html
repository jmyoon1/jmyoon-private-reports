FA*IR: A Fair Top-k Ranking Algorithm

Meike Zehlike et al. · CIKM 2017 · 2017 · 545 citations

arXiv:1706.06368 · PDF · SP: SP3

P2Track C

한 줄 요약

접두사마다 보호 집단 최소 비율 p를 보장하는 fair top-k ranking(FA*IR). merit-blind 목표의 대표로, score가 임의로 오염되어도 제약 만족 자체는 구조적으로 유지된다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Given a pool of n >> k scored candidates and one binary legally-protected attribute, existing ranking pipelines produce top-k lists whose protected-group proportion fluctuates arbitrarily across prefixes (e.g., XING queries showing 90%/10% male/female in the top 10 but 73%/27% in the top 40), so a consumer stopping at any depth may see systematic under-representation. Prior fair-ranking work either only measured this (Yang & Stoyanovich's averaged, discounted parity measures; Kulshrestha et al.'s audit framework) or assumed a constraint matrix as given without saying how to build one (Celis et al.). No prior method offered a statistically grounded per-prefix criterion plus an algorithm that constructs a ranking satisfying it.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Post-processing re-ranking in three pieces (skim-level view):

  1. Fair representation test (Def. 3.1): a set with τ_p protected members among k fairly represents the group at proportion p and significance α iff F(τ_p; k, p) > α, with F the binomial CDF — i.e., a one-sided binomial test with null "protected proportion ≥ p".
  2. Ranked group fairness (Def. 3.2): the test must hold on every prefix i = 1..k, each at corrected significance α_c = c(α, k, p) computed by the O(k²) AdjustSignificance procedure (binary-searched so family-wise Type-I error is α). This yields a table m_{α,p}(i) of minimum protected counts per position.
  3. **FA*IR construction**: greedily build the top-k maximizing utility subject to meeting m_{α,p}(i) at every position. [SKIM - verify construction details and utility guarantees in §4]

The paper claims this is the first ranking-bias mitigation algorithm grounded in statistical tests.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Fair rankings produced by FA*IR show "small distortions" in utility relative to the color-blind (pure score-ordered) ranking across existing and newly released datasets. [SKIM - exact utility/NDCG numbers are in §5, not yet read]
  • Naive per-prefix binomial testing at α = 0.1 yields family-wise Type-I error well above 0.1 (Figure 1); the AdjustSignificance correction restores it exactly, e.g. α_c = 0.0096 for k = 1000, p = 0.5.
  • The significance adjustment runs in O(k²) once per (k, p), dataset-independent; verification of ranked group fairness for a given list is efficient.
  • Claimed first statistically-test-grounded algorithm for mitigating under-representation along a ranked list (545 citations support its foundational status).

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Ranked group fairness condition

  • What it computes: input = a top-k ranking τ, a minimum protected-group proportion p, a significance level α → for every prefix τ|i (i = 1..k), run a one-sided binomial test comparing the observed count of protected candidates τ_p against Bin(i, p) → output = accept/reject. The prefix passes if F(τ_p; i, p) > α_c, where F is the binomial CDF.
  • Why it was created: prior group-fairness notions for rankings (Yang & Stoyanovich 2017) averaged representation over a few prefix cut-offs with a logarithmic discount but gave no statistically grounded accept/reject criterion at every position; a recruiter stopping at any depth could still see arbitrarily skewed proportions.
  • Concrete example: with p = 0.4, α = 0.1, the minimum-protected-count table m_{α,p}(k) requires ≥ 1 protected candidate in the top 5 and ≥ 2 in the top 9 — so a top-10 with the first protected candidate at position 10 fails.
  • How it is used in this paper: it is both the fairness constraint the FA*IR algorithm enforces during construction and the test used to audit XING job-search rankings in the motivating example.

Adjusted significance α_c (AdjustSignificance algorithm)

  • What it computes: input = k, p, α → an O(k²) iterative block-wise computation over the binomial pmf (Algorithm 1) → output = the per-test significance α_c such that the family-wise probability of rejecting a genuinely fair ranking across all k dependent prefix tests equals the target α.
  • Why it was created: running k prefix tests naively at level α inflates the Type-I error above α (Figure 1); Šidák's independent-tests correction is too loose because the prefix tests are positively dependent.
  • Concrete example: for k = 1000, p = 0.5, α = 0.1, the corrected per-test level is α_c = 0.0096; for small (k, p) such as k = 40, p ≤ 0.4 no valid α_c exists.
  • How it is used in this paper: computed once per (k, p) — dataset-independent — and plugged into every prefix test inside the ranked group fairness condition.

Selection utility and ordering utility

  • What it computes: two loss criteria on a re-ranked list: selection utility penalizes including a candidate less qualified than an excluded one; ordering utility penalizes any in-list pair ranked against qualification order. [SKIM - exact loss definitions in §3.4, not yet read]
  • Why it was created: to make "distortion relative to the pure score-ordered (color-blind) ranking" measurable per individual, unlike positional-discount utilities that hide who was displaced.
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - verify in deep read]
  • How it is used in this paper: the two objectives FA*IR maximizes subject to the ranked group fairness constraint, and the axes of the experimental comparison against the color-blind ranking.

**FA*IR algorithm**

  • What it computes: input = candidate pool with qualification scores q_i and a binary protected attribute, plus (k, p, α) → greedy construction of a top-k list that satisfies the ranked group fairness condition while maximizing selection/ordering utility → output = a fair top-k ranking. [SKIM - algorithm mechanics are in §4, not yet read; likely two per-group priority queues merged against the m(k) table]
  • Why it was created: Celis et al.'s constrained-ranking formulation left open how to construct the position-wise constraint matrix; FA*IR derives it from the statistical test.
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - verify in deep read]
  • How it is used in this paper: the paper's main algorithmic contribution, evaluated in §5.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: candidate scores + binary protected attribute; the m_{α,p}(k) table (O(k²) one-time computation); a greedy merge — trivially cheap at serving time.
  • Code: [SKIM] — widely reimplemented (FA*IR is available in ranking-fairness libraries, e.g. fairsearch); verify the official repository in a deep read.
  • Modifications: to serve our setting, the per-prefix count constraint would have to be re-derived over an exposure distribution (impressions across sessions) instead of a single list's prefixes; the binomial-test machinery could in principle test observed exposure shares against a target p per time window.
  • Unknowns: missing link: behavior when the protected group has fewer than the required candidates (feasibility condition), utility guarantees of the greedy construction, and multi-group extension — all in §4/§6, not yet read.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Singh & Joachims, "Fairness of Exposure in Rankings" (KDD 2018) — replaces prefix-count representation with position-bias-weighted exposure, the notion our survey's setting actually controls; direct successor line.
  • Zehlike et al., "Fairness in Ranking" surveys (2021/2022, ACM CSUR) — same first author; situates FA*IR within the score-based vs. supervised fair-ranking taxonomy used by SP3's access-assumption labeling.
  • Celis, Straszak & Vishnoi, "Ranking with Fairness Constraints" (ICALP 2018) — the constrained-ranking LP formulation whose open constraint-matrix question FA*IR answers; relevant as the optimization-based branch of SP3.
  • Geyik, Ambler & Kenthapadi (KDD 2019, LinkedIn) — deployed re-ranking with per-prefix representation constraints heavily influenced by FA*IR; industrial evidence for SP3 feasibility.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = a static pool of n scored candidates with one binary protected attribute; output = a single re-ordered top-k list; conditions = scores readable but not modified, one-shot ranking, group-level (binary) fairness target expressed as a minimum proportion p, position-count notion of representation (no exposure/attention model). Our setting: black-box recommender whose scores cannot be transformed; the only lever is exposure frequency (impression capping/boosting) over repeated servings; fairness/control target is exposure allocation, potentially item-level, amortized over time. Differences:

  • Score access: FA*IR reads scores only to order candidates within/across groups and never modifies them — compatible with the score-immutable constraint: resolved (post-processing, score-read-only).
  • Intervention type: FA*IR reorders positions in a single list, whereas our setting adjusts how often items are shown across many impressions; a single-list re-ranker gives no amortization mechanism: missing link.
  • Representation notion: prefix membership counts vs. graded exposure/attention (position-bias-weighted): prefix counts are a coarse binary proxy for exposure: missing link.
  • Fairness unit: one binary protected group vs. item-level or multi-group exposure targets (paper explicitly defers multiple attributes to future work): reference only.
  • Statistical-test framing (accept/reject per prefix with family-wise correction) as a template for auditing exposure distributions over time: reference only — a transferable idea, not a direct solution.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct (skim-level judgment) — for the problem it poses (guaranteed per-prefix group representation in a top-k), the paper attacks the root cause: it derives the position-wise constraint from an explicit statistical model with a correct multiple-testing adjustment rather than heuristically re-shuffling until a metric looks better, and it closes the constraint-construction gap Celis et al. left open. For our survey's problem it addresses only the static-ranking symptom of exposure imbalance, not repeated-impression exposure itself.
