Computationally Efficient Optimization of Plackett-Luce Ranking Models for Relevance and Fairness

Oosterhuis · SIGIR 2021 · 2021

arXiv:2105.00855 · PDF · SP: SP4

P0Track A

한 줄 요약

Plackett-Luce ranking 모델의 relevance/공정성 최적화를 O(n log n) 표집으로 효율화한다. 단, 고정 score 아래에서 PL 표집은 온도 1-파라미터 곡선족만 표현하므로 임의 목표에는 score 학습이 필요하다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

PL ranking models are attractive for both relevance and exposure-based fairness because they are fully differentiable and can distribute exposure probabilistically — but computing their gradient exactly requires iterating over every permutation of items. Existing practice approximates it with generic REINFORCE-style policy gradients (Singh & Joachims 2019, Bruch et al. 2020), which ignore the structure of both the PL model and ranking metrics: they multiply the whole-ranking log-probability gradient by the whole-ranking reward, need on the order of $N = 1000$ sampled rankings to converge, and are computationally costly — a particular problem in online/dynamic settings where the model is re-optimized continuously as interaction data arrives (Morik et al. 2020).

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Three novel unbiased estimators of $\frac{\delta}{\delta m}\mathcal{R}(q)$, derived by progressively exploiting structure:

  1. Placement policy gradient (Eq. 14): decomposes the ranking log-probability into per-placement terms and pairs each placement's gradient only with the following reward $\sum_{x=k}^{K}\theta_x\rho_{y_x}$ (items placed after rank $k$ cannot affect reward already earned).
  2. PL-Rank-1 (Eq. 20): substitutes the analytic softmax derivative of the PL placement probability, yielding a closed-form per-item weight — (reward following placement) − (risk imposed by placement probability) — computable in $O(N \cdot K \cdot D)$ without autodiff through the sampling.
  3. PL-Rank-2 (Eq. 23): separates the item's own expected direct reward $\sum_{k=1}^{\text{rank}(d,y)} \pi(d|y_{1:k-1})\theta_k\rho_d$ from the following reward, so relevant-but-unsampled items still get positive weight → better sample-efficiency at low $N$, same complexity.

Fairness extension (Eq. 27–28): since $\mathcal{E}_d$ has the same form as $\mathcal{R}(q)$ with indicator relevances, any exposure-based fairness metric $\mathcal{F}$ (or linear combination with relevance) is optimized by running the same Algorithm 1 with $\rho_d$ replaced by $\frac{\delta\mathcal{F}(q)}{\delta\mathcal{E}_d}$.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Sample-efficiency (RQ1): PL-Rank-2 converges near-optimally at $N=10$–$100$ where the basic policy gradient needs $N=1000$ and has severe convergence failures at $N=10$; LambdaLoss plateaus at suboptimal DCG regardless of $N$ (Fig. 1).
  • Time per epoch (Table 1, $N=1000$): PL-Rank-1 is fastest everywhere — e.g. MSLR 18.48 min vs. policy gradient 24.20 and LambdaLoss 1669.59; Yahoo 5.70 vs. 8.27 / 10.25.
  • Equal-time comparison (Table 2, dynamic $N = 10 + 90\cdot\text{epoch}/40$): PL-Rank-2 significantly best ($p<0.01$) in all cases except a tie with PL-Rank-1 on MSLR — DCG@5 11.42 (Yahoo), 8.39 (MSLR), 19.38 (Istella) vs. LambdaLoss 11.11 / 7.80 / 18.75.
  • Fairness (RQ3, Fig. 3): PL-Rank methods and the placement policy gradient reduce mean disparity error considerably faster than LambdaLoss and the basic policy gradient; PL-Rank's edge over the placement policy gradient is small for fairness.

Context: builds on Plackett-Luce choice models (Plackett 1975, Luce 1959), REINFORCE (Williams 1992), policy learning for fair ranking (Singh & Joachims 2019), stochastic LTR (Bruch et al. 2020), expected-exposure evaluation (Diaz et al. 2020), dynamic fairness control (Morik et al. 2020). Baselines: basic policy gradient, placement policy gradient, LambdaLoss (Wang et al. 2018). Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: PL-Rank is the first LTR method exploiting joint PL/metric structure; greater sample- and time-efficiency; enables industry-scale fair stochastic ranking.
  • Demonstrated: efficiency claims are backed with numbers on three large public LTR datasets with significance tests (see Key Results).
  • Gap: the "enables the industry for real-world ranking systems" framing is extrapolation — experiments use offline expert-labeled relevance ($\rho_d = 2^{\text{label}}-1$), a small 2×32 sigmoid MLP, and single-CPU timing; no online/production experiment. Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Plackett-Luce (PL) ranking model

  • What it computes: item log-scores $m(d)$ from a scoring model → sequential softmax choice without replacement: $\pi(d|y_{1:k-1}, \mathcal{D}) = \frac{e^{m(d)}\mathbb{1}[d \notin y_{1:k-1}]}{\sum_{d' \in \mathcal{D}\setminus y_{1:k-1}} e^{m(d')}}$ → a probability distribution over full rankings $\pi(y) = \prod_{k=1}^K \pi(y_k|y_{1:k-1})$.
  • Why it was created: deterministic sort-by-score ranking is non-differentiable and can never give two items equal probability of the top slot; a stochastic ranking distribution is differentiable and can spread exposure.
  • Concrete example: with scores $m = (2, 2, 0)$, items 1 and 2 each get probability $\approx 0.47$ of rank 1 — a deterministic sorter must pick one of them 100% of the time.
  • How it is used in this paper: the model class whose gradient PL-Rank estimates; prior work (Luce 1959, Plackett 1975) — this paper only optimizes it.

Gumbel top-K sampling for PL rankings

  • What it computes: per-item Gumbel noise $\gamma_d$ added to log-score $\hat{m}_d = m(d) + \gamma_d$ → sorting by $\hat{m}$ → an exact sample from the PL distribution in $O(|\mathcal{D}|\log|\mathcal{D}|)$, with no probability renormalization.
  • Why it was created: naive sequential sampling recomputes the softmax denominator at each rank.
  • Concrete example: $\gamma_d = -\log(-\log(\zeta_d))$, $\zeta_d \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$.
  • How it is used in this paper: samples the $N$ rankings all gradient estimators average over (from Bruch et al. 2020 / Gumbel 1954; reused, not new here).

PL-Rank-1 / PL-Rank-2 gradient estimators (the paper's core contribution)

  • What it computes: $N$ sampled rankings + relevances $\rho$ + metric rank-weights $\theta$ → a per-item weight $\lambda_d$ such that $\frac{\delta}{\delta m}\mathcal{R}(q) \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_d \lambda_d \frac{\delta m(d)}{\delta m}$, computed in $O(N \cdot K \cdot D)$. PL-Rank-1 weights each item by (reward following its placement) minus (risk: placement probability × following reward at each earlier rank). PL-Rank-2 additionally splits out the item's expected direct reward $\pi(d|y_{1:k-1})\theta_k\rho_d$, so items never sampled into the top-K can still receive positive weight.
  • Why it was created: exact PL gradients require summing over all $K!$-scale permutations; the existing REINFORCE-style policy gradient is an unstructured Monte Carlo estimate that needs $N \approx 1000$ samples and is computationally slow.
  • Concrete example: with $N=10$ samples on Yahoo, PL-Rank-2 converges near-optimally where the basic policy gradient diverges (Fig. 1).
  • How it is used in this paper: the proposed method, evaluated for DCG@5 and an exposure-disparity fairness metric.

Exposure $\mathcal{E}_d$

  • What it computes: ranking distribution $\pi$ + examination weights $\theta_k$ (position-bias model) → $\mathcal{E}_d = \mathbb{E}_y[\sum_k \theta_k \mathbb{1}[y_k = d]]$, the expected probability a user examines item $d$.
  • Why it was created: fairness-of-ranking metrics need a quantity measuring how much attention an item receives, since position bias makes lower ranks near-invisible.
  • Concrete example: with DCG weights, an item always at rank 1 gets $\mathcal{E}_d = 1/\log_2 2 = 1$; always at rank 5, $\mathcal{E}_d = 1/\log_2 6 \approx 0.39$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the fairness target; PL-Rank optimizes any exposure-based metric $\mathcal{F}$ via chain rule by substituting $\frac{\delta\mathcal{F}}{\delta\mathcal{E}_d}$ for $\rho_d$ in Algorithm 1. Concept originates in Singh & Joachims 2018/2019, Biega et al. 2018, Diaz et al. 2020.

Squared cross-exposure disparity (novel here)

  • What it computes: pairwise disparity $D(d,d') = (\mathcal{E}_{d'}\rho_d - \mathcal{E}_d\rho_{d'})^2$, averaged over all item pairs.
  • Why it was created: the standard exposure-to-merit ratio $\mathcal{E}_d/\rho_d$ (Singh & Joachims) is undefined when many items have zero relevance, as in the LTR benchmark datasets.
  • Concrete example: if $\rho_d = \rho_{d'}$ but $\mathcal{E}_d = 2\mathcal{E}_{d'}$, the pair contributes $(\mathcal{E}_{d'}\rho_d)^2 > 0$; equal treatment ($\mathcal{E}_d\rho_{d'} = \mathcal{E}_{d'}\rho_d$) contributes 0.
  • How it is used in this paper: the fairness objective optimized in the RQ3 experiments (Fig. 3).
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: any scoring model producing per-item log-scores; NumPy for Algorithm 1 + Gumbel sampling; standard LTR datasets. Experiments ran on a single Intel Xeon CPU — very light.
  • Code: available at https://github.com/HarrieO/2021-SIGIR-plackett-luce (public data only, per paper).
  • Modifications: to use in an exposure-frequency-proxy setting, replace label-derived $\rho_d$ with estimated relevance (or plug in $\frac{\delta\mathcal{F}}{\delta\mathcal{E}_d}$ for a chosen exposure metric); exposure values would need estimation from logged data rather than 1000 policy samples.
  • Unknowns: missing link — variance behavior of the estimators when $\rho_d$ is itself a noisy estimate; missing link — behavior at large $K$ (all experiments are top-5).

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = query-document feature vectors with expert relevance labels (Yahoo 29.9k / MSLR 30k / Istella 33.1k queries); outputs = a stochastic top-K ($K=5$) ranking policy; conditions = true relevance $\rho_d$ known from labels, exposure defined by fixed metric rank-weights $\theta_k$, offline supervised optimization. Our setting (SP4, exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy survey): exposure/frequency arising from a deployed ranking loop, where item merit is not given by clean labels and exposure both drives and is driven by the ranking (feedback), and exposure frequency may serve as a proxy signal for ranking quality/relevance. Differences:

  • Known merit $\rho_d$ from expert labels vs. merit estimated from interaction data contaminated by position bias: missing link — the paper takes $\rho_d$ as given; combining with unbiased-LTR estimation is pointed to (Oosterhuis & de Rijke 2021, Morik et al. 2020) but not done here.
  • Static one-shot optimization vs. dynamic re-ranking feedback loops: reference only — the paper motivates efficiency by the online setting but evaluates offline.
  • Exposure defined analytically via $\theta_k$ under a position-bias model vs. observed exposure frequencies: resolved in part — Eq. 26 gives the exact bridge from ranking policy to expected exposure, which is the mechanism our SP needs; but empirical exposure estimation from logs is out of scope.
  • Fairness metric: paper uses its own squared cross-exposure disparity, chosen for zero-merit robustness vs. the merit-proportional exposure standard: [SKIM - verify] how results transfer to other exposure-fairness definitions.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the targeted problem is the computational intractability of the exact PL gradient, and the solution is an exact structural reformulation of that gradient (unbiased by algebraic derivation, complexity reduced to $O(N \cdot K \cdot D)$), rather than a heuristic surrogate objective like LambdaLoss.
