Item-side Fairness of Large Language Model-based Recommendation System

Jiang et al. · WWW 2024 · 2024 · 51 citations

arXiv:2402.15215 · PDF · SP: SP1

P0Track A

한 줄 요약

LLM 기반 recommender의 item 측 공정성을 처음으로 측정하고, 학습 데이터 편향과 pre-training 내용 prior라는 두 가지 편향 원천을 분해한다. exposure 대 학습 이력 비교 지표(GU/MGU/DGU)를 제안한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Item-side fairness (fair exposure across item groups) is unstudied for LLM-based recommenders, whose properties differ from conventional recommenders: they infer preference from semantic clues, are instruction-tuned, and generate recommendations rather than score candidates. The only prior touchpoint (LLMRank) noted a recommendation-frequency vs. item-popularity correlation qualitatively, with no quantitative metrics and no mitigation. Additionally, conventional in-processing fairness methods assume access to per-item ranking scores, which generative LRS training does not provide.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Two-part contribution:

  1. Diagnostic study (BIGRec/LLaMA vs. SASRec on MovieLens1M and Steam, sequential setting, all-ranking protocol): LRS over-recommends the most-popular item quintile at top-1 far more than SASRec (MGU@1 0.1044 vs. 0.0238 on MovieLens1M); genre-level unfairness also exceeds SASRec's. A genre-deletion experiment (remove all Comedy items from fine-tuning data; model still recommends comedies) shows the bias has two sources: imbalanced fine-tuning interactions and semantic priors from LLM pre-training. The grounding step partially masks popularity bias at large $K$; beam search instead amplifies it.
  1. IFairLRS framework: (a) in-learning sample reweighting $W_G = \text{GH}_{tr}(G)/\text{GH}_{ta}(G)$ applied to the instruction-tuning loss; (b) post-learning reranking that divides the grounding L2 distance by $(1-\hat{U}_i)^\alpha$, where $\hat{U}_i$ aggregates validation-set GU@$K$ across $K$ with weights favoring large $K$.
Key Results주요 결과
  • Popularity groups, MovieLens1M: BIGRec MGU@1 = 0.1044 vs. SASRec 0.0238 (~4.4× worse); but BIGRec MGU@20 = 0.0128 vs. SASRec 0.0279 — grounding dilutes the skew as $K$ grows.
  • Steam: BIGRec worse at every $K$ (MGU@20 0.0900 vs. 0.0157; DGU@20 0.3050 vs. 0.0445) — unfairness grows with $K$ on the more popularity-skewed dataset.
  • Genre-deletion probe: model recommends Comedy titles never seen in fine-tuning (e.g. "Airplane! (1980)") — direct evidence that pre-training semantic priors, independent of the fine-tuning interaction distribution, drive part of the exposure skew.
  • IFairLRS(RW): MGU@1/DGU@1 improvements of 5%/8.4% (MovieLens1M) and 9.7%/11.1% (Steam) on popularity groups; up to 30%/28.3% on genre groups (MovieLens1M).
  • IFairLRS(RR): strongest at large $K$ (Steam MGU@5 0.0590 → 0.0179; MovieLens1M genre DGU@20 −52%); accuracy (NDCG@5, HR@5) stays within ~1–5% of BIGRec for all variants.
  • Beam search in place of grounding significantly amplifies unfairness (Figure 3b), echoing bias-amplification findings for beam search in NLP.
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: empirical analysis + new method (fairness framework) Context: builds directly on BIGRec (Bao et al. 2023, bi-step grounding) as the LRS backbone; SASRec as the conventional baseline; calibrated recommendations (Steck 2018) for the metric design; positions against LLMRank (qualitative-only) and FaiRLLM (user-side, not item-side fairness). Correctness: experiments run 3× with averages reported; no CIs/std reported in main tables. [SKIM] — appendix-level rigor not fully assessed. Contributions: (1) first quantitative item-side fairness metrics (GU/MGU/DGU) for generative LRS; (2) causal decomposition of LRS unfairness into interaction-imbalance vs. pre-training semantic priors (genre-deletion evidence); (3) IFairLRS reweighting + reranking adapted to the generative (non-scoring) setting. Clarity: metrics and both strategies are fully specified with equations; code released (github.com/JiangM-C/IFairLRS). Reproducible in principle.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: LRS unfairness stems from both historical-interaction imbalance and inherent LLM semantic biases; IFairLRS yields "significant" fairness improvements without accuracy loss.
  • Demonstrated: the two-source decomposition is supported by the genre-deletion experiment (qualitative + Figure 3a); fairness gains are real but modest for popularity groups at top-1 (5–10%), larger for genre groups and large-$K$ reranking (up to ~50%). Accuracy preservation is demonstrated.
  • Gap: the semantic-prior component is identified but neither quantified nor mitigated — the paper concedes "it is challenging to quantify and alleviate such impact." Reweighting explicitly fails to fix grounding-stage issues (MGU@20 worsens); reranking fails at small $K$. Only one LLM backbone (LLaMA-1) and one LRS paradigm (BIGRec) tested, so generality across LRS architectures is claimed by argument ("BIGRec embodies the fundamental elements"), not shown. Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Group Unfairness (GU) — calibration-style exposure gap

  • What it computes: for an item group $G$, GU$(G) = \text{GP}(G) - \text{GH}(G)$, where GP$(G)$ is the proportion of group-$G$ items in the top-$K$ recommendations across all users, and GH$(G)$ is the group's proportion in users' historical interaction sequences. Output: a signed scalar per group; $>0$ = over-recommended, $<0$ = overlooked.
  • Why it was created: prior LRS fairness work (LLMRank) only observed the recommendation-frequency vs. popularity correlation qualitatively; GU turns the exposure-vs-history discrepancy into a per-group quantitative measure grounded in calibrated-recommendation ideas (Steck 2018).
  • Concrete example: on Steam, BIGRec's top-1 recommendations give the most-popular quintile a GP far above its GH (Figure 1b), yielding DGU@1 = 0.1586 vs. SASRec's 0.0647.
  • How it is used in this paper: aggregated into MGU (mean $|$GU$|$ over groups) and DGU (max − min GU), computed at $K \in \{1,5,10,20\}$ under the all-ranking protocol; these are the paper's core fairness metrics and also feed the reranking penalty.

BIGRec grounding step (L2 embedding grounding)

  • What it computes: the fine-tuned LLM generates a free-text item description ("oracle"); the system computes $D_i = \|\text{emb}_i - \text{oracle}\|_2$ against every catalog item's token-embedding and returns the $K$ nearest items as the ranked list.
  • Why it was created: generated descriptions may not exactly match any real catalog item; grounding maps generation into the actual item space.
  • Concrete example: LLM outputs "Mighty Ducks, The (1992)" (not in catalog); grounding maps it to the nearest embedding "The Mighty Ducks (1992)".
  • How it is used in this paper: shown to be a de facto fairness mechanism — as $K$ grows, grounding (popularity-agnostic in embedding space) dilutes the LLM's popularity skew, which explains why BIGRec's MGU@20 can be better than SASRec's while MGU@1 is 4× worse. Replacing grounding with beam search amplifies unfairness.

IFairLRS reweighting (in-learning strategy)

  • What it computes: per-group weight $W_G = \text{GH}_{tr}(G) / \text{GH}_{ta}(G)$ (group proportion in training sequences ÷ proportion in target items); per-sample weight $W_i$ = mean of $W_G$ over the target item's groups; the instruction-tuning loss is scaled by $W_i$.
  • Why it was created: conventional in-processing IF methods need ranking scores over item candidates, which a generative LRS (token-likelihood training) does not produce; pre-computed sample weights sidestep this.
  • Concrete example: MovieLens1M MGU@1 drops 0.1044 → 0.0990 (−5%), DGU@1 0.3917 → 0.3585 (−8.4%).
  • How it is used in this paper: applied during LLaMA instruction-tuning on MovieLens1M and Steam.

IFairLRS reranking (post-learning strategy)

  • What it computes: penalty $U_G = \sum_{K} \gamma_K \cdot \text{GU}(G)@K$ with $\gamma_K \propto K$ (computed on validation data), normalized to $[-1,1]$, averaged over an item's groups into $\hat{U}_i$; the grounding distance is rescaled as $\tilde{D}_i = D_i / (1-\hat{U}_i)^{\alpha}$ so over-recommended items get pushed down.
  • Why it was created: to correct residual unfairness after training without retraining, and to handle grounding's over-correction at large $K$ (hence the $K$-weighted aggregation).
  • Concrete example: Steam MGU@5 drops 0.0590 → 0.0179 with reranking.
  • How it is used in this paper: applied on top of BIGRec's grounding-based ranking at inference.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: LLaMA checkpoint + instruction-tuning stack (PyTorch, Adam, lr 1e-3, batch 128), MovieLens1M and Steam datasets, embedding-distance grounding infra; GU computation needs only group labels + interaction logs.
  • Code: available at https://github.com/JiangM-C/IFairLRS.git
  • Modifications: for our exposure-frequency-proxy setting, the GU/GP/GH machinery ports directly to any ranker output; the reranking penalty assumes a distance-based grounding score (Eq. 14) — would need adaptation for logit- or prompt-based rankers.
  • Unknowns: missing link — sensitivity to group granularity (5 popularity quintiles is a design choice); missing link — behavior on LLM backbones newer than LLaMA-1; missing link — how validation-set GU estimates transfer when the exposure distribution shifts.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Bao et al., "A Bi-Step Grounding Paradigm for LLMs in Recommendation Systems" (BIGRec, 2023) — the backbone whose grounding step turns out to be a hidden fairness modulator; foundational for interpreting this paper's $K$-dependence.
  • Hou et al., "Large Language Models are Zero-Shot Rankers for Recommender Systems" (LLMRank, 2023) — the only prior item-side observation (frequency–popularity correlation) in LRS; covers the zero-shot regime this paper omits.
  • Zhang et al., "Is ChatGPT Fair for Recommendation? (FaiRLLM, RecSys 2023)" — user-side counterpart; together they span both fairness sides of LRS.
  • Steck, "Calibrated Recommendations" (RecSys 2018) — origin of the calibration-to-history framing that GU operationalizes; relevant if our SP formalizes exposure frequency against a base-rate reference.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = user interaction sequences verbalized as instructions; outputs = generated item description grounded to catalog via L2 embedding distance, top-$K$ list; conditions = fine-tuned LLaMA (BIGRec), MovieLens1M + Steam, sequential recommendation, group fairness over popularity quintiles and genres, exposure measured against historical-interaction proportions. Our setting (SP1 — exposure frequency as a ranking proxy): whether the frequency with which an LLM surfaces an item in its ranked/generated output can serve as (or contaminates) a ranking-quality signal; interest is in the mechanism linking training-corpus/interaction frequency to output exposure frequency. Differences:

  • Paper measures exposure skew as a fairness harm; our SP treats exposure frequency as a proxy signal to characterize or exploit — same underlying quantity (GP vs. GH gap), different use: resolved — GU/MGU/DGU are directly reusable as exposure-frequency-vs-base-rate proxies.
  • Paper's exposure source is a fine-tuned + grounded pipeline; a prompting-only or scoring LLM ranker may show different frequency dynamics: missing link — top-1 results isolate the LLM (Appendix B shows grounding barely alters top-1), but zero-shot ranking is not tested here (LLMRank covers that qualitatively).
  • Pre-training semantic prior as a frequency driver independent of in-domain interaction counts: resolved (existence) via genre-deletion, missing link (magnitude) — no quantification.
  • Domains limited to movies/games with title-only item text: reference only.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the diagnostic half genuinely isolates two root causes (interaction imbalance vs. pre-training semantic priors, via the genre-deletion probe), but the mitigation half calibrates the observable exposure distribution (loss reweighting, distance-rescaling rerank) without touching the semantic-prior mechanism, which the authors admit remains unquantified and unmitigated.
