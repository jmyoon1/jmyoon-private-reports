Observations and Remedies for Large Language Model Bias in Self-Consuming Performative Loop

Wang et al. · arXiv · 2026 · 1 citations

arXiv:2601.05184 · PDF · SP: SP10

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

LLM의 자기 소비 performative loop에서 편향을 관찰하고 교정한다(2026). 배포 LLM이 실제로 쓰는 incremental fine-tuning이 편향 증폭을 가속함을 보인다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Prior self-consuming-loop studies for LLMs (Wang et al. 2024c; Briesch et al. 2023; Kazdan et al. 2024) analyze bias/collapse under a fixed data-generating process and only in the retraining-from-base-model setting. They do not model (a) performative feedback — deployed models changing which users keep supplying queries, so group representation drifts as a function of model performance — or (b) the incremental fine-tuning regime that practitioners actually use when original training data is inaccessible.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

[SKIM - verify] Empirical-framework contribution: define the SCPL (Algorithms 1–2), where group-wise sample selection each generation is governed by a controlled dynamic (disadvantaged-group ratio decreasing linearly from its initial value, vs. fixed-ratio and non-dynamic baselines). Run the loop under two training regimes (retraining vs. incremental fine-tuning) and three data cycles (full-synthetic, accumulation, real-data replacement) on three tasks (news continuation / political lean, creative preference, math problem solving). Findings: the loop amplifies preference bias (model's inclination toward the advantaged group) while disparate bias (accuracy gap between groups) decreases; performative dynamics accelerate preference-bias growth mainly in the incremental fine-tuning loop; accumulation slows both preference-bias growth and quality decline. Mitigation: reward-based rejection sampling with group reweighting (Eq. 2).

Key Results주요 결과
  • Preference bias increases faster in SCPL (dynamic) than in the non-dynamic self-consuming loop under incremental fine-tuning; under retraining the dynamic-vs-non-dynamic difference is marginal (Fig. 2a, news continuation). [SKIM - exact numbers in deep read]
  • Replacing synthetic data with real data yields a stable bias trajectory with minimal variation across generations.
  • Disparate performance bias (accuracy gap on easy-vs-hard math) tends to decrease over the loop — group performance converges.
  • Accumulation (retaining $D_0 \cup \dots \cup D_t$) slows preference-bias growth, mitigates generation-quality decline, and helps preserve math ability.
  • Mitigation effectiveness numbers: [SKIM] — past page 5.

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Self-Consuming Performative Loop (SCPL)

  • What it computes: at each generation $t$, the model $M_{t-1}$ generates synthetic prompt-response data $D_t$ whose group composition is shifted by simulated human feedback $H_{t-1}$ (users from the group the model serves worse submit fewer queries); $M_t$ is then trained on $D_t$ via the performative fine-tuning loss $L(D_t(\theta_{t-1}, H_{t-1}); \theta_t) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{(x,y)\in D_t} L(x,y;\theta_t)$.
  • Why it was created: prior self-consuming-loop work (Shumailov et al. 2023, Wang et al. 2024c) treats the data distribution as static across generations; real deployed LLMs change the distribution of future queries via user engagement (performative prediction, Perdomo et al. 2020), and that coupling was unmodeled for LLM retraining.
  • Concrete example: news-continuation task starts from 5,000 real samples (3,000 left-leaning = disadvantaged, 2,000 right-leaning = advantaged); the disadvantaged group's share of sampled prompts decreases linearly over generations to simulate disengagement.
  • How it is used in this paper: the central experimental framework — Algorithm 1 (loop) + Algorithm 2 (performative sampling with disadvantage ratio $r_d = F(S_a, S_d)$ computed from group-wise test scores).

Performative Incremental Fine-tuning Loop

  • What it computes: instead of retraining from the base model each generation (the standard retraining loop), $M_t$ is obtained by fine-tuning $M_{t-1}$ directly on the newly generated $D_t$.
  • Why it was created: retraining from scratch assumes access to original training data, which may be restricted (expired licenses, deletion requests); incremental fine-tuning is the practically common regime but was underexplored in self-consuming-loop studies.
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - verify in deep read] — experiments use Qwen2.5-1.5B/7B, Llama2-7B, Llama3.1-8B, Qwen2.5-Math-7B over $T$ generations.
  • How it is used in this paper: one of the two loop types compared; the key finding is that performative feedback accelerates preference-bias amplification in this loop more than in the retraining loop.

Reward-based rejection sampling with reweighting (mitigation)

  • What it computes: for each candidate prompt $x$, generate $k$ responses from $M_{t-1}$, score each with $R(x,\tilde y) = \lambda_1 r_1(x,\tilde y) + \lambda_2 r_2(x,\tilde y) + r_3$ ($r_1$ = generation quality, $r_2$ = preference match with input, $r_3$ = extensible task rule), select high-scoring samples, and reweight selection to oversample the disadvantaged group (larger $k$ / adjusted criteria).
  • Why it was created: naive rejection sampling curates for quality only and does not counteract the group-composition drift induced by performative feedback.
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - verify in deep read] — Algorithm 3 details and effectiveness numbers are past page 5.
  • How it is used in this paper: the proposed remedy, compared against naive rejection-sampling baselines; notably it uses proxy bias metrics, not ground-truth group labels.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = LLM-generated synthetic prompt-response pairs whose group composition drifts under a simulated, controlled linear feedback schedule; outputs = bias trajectories (preference bias score, disparate accuracy gap) over $T$ generations; conditions = two-group setup, single provider, LLM fine-tuning (SFT/DPO), 1.5B–8B models. Our setting (SP10): exposure/interaction frequency used as a ranking-relevance proxy in retrieval/recommendation — the feedback loop runs through a ranker's exposure allocation, and the quantity of interest is item-level exposure distortion, not group-level generation bias in an LLM. Differences:

  • Feedback mechanism: paper simulates user disengagement via a hand-set linear group-ratio schedule; our setting has feedback mediated by ranked exposure positions — missing link (the paper does not model position/exposure-driven feedback).
  • Unit of bias: demographic/content group preference in generated text vs. per-item exposure frequency in a ranking — reference only (structural analogy: performative loop where model output shapes future training signal).
  • Training regime: LLM SFT/DPO generations vs. ranking-model updates on logged interactions — reference only.
  • Mitigation transferability: reward-based reweighted sampling of the disadvantaged group is structurally analogous to inverse-propensity-style exposure reweighting — resolved: the reweighting idea maps onto exposure-debiasing, though only as a design analogy.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the paper directly names and models the root feedback structure (performative coupling between deployment and future training data), but its remedy reweights the sampling to offset the drift rather than breaking or correcting the performative mechanism itself, so the mitigation side treats the symptom.
