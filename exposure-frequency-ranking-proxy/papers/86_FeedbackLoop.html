Feedback Loop and Bias Amplification in Recommender Systems

Mansoury et al. · CIKM 2020 · 2020

arXiv:2007.13019 · PDF · SP: SP8

P0Track A

한 줄 요약

추천이 학습 데이터보다 평균적으로 더 인기 편향될 때(라운드 당 uplift θ^t>0) exposure→클릭→재학습 루프를 통해 편향이 단조 증폭됨을 정식화한다(Eq. 4). 동적 immutability의 형식적 골격.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Popularity bias in recommenders had been studied almost exclusively in single-shot (one train/test round) settings. What was missing is a quantified account of the dynamic case: recommendations are consumed, the consumption is logged, and the model retrains on its own biased output — so an initially small popularity bias may compound. Prior loop studies used synthetic data (Chaney et al. 2018) or non-recommender domains (D'Amour et al. 2020); this paper measures the loop on real rating data with standard CF algorithms and asks who is harmed most.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Empirical/mechanism contribution, two parts:

  1. Formal propagation argument (Eq. 4 above): as long as recommendations are on average more popular than the training data ($\theta^t > 0$) and some are accepted ($K > 0$), average data popularity strictly increases every iteration, so recommendation popularity does too — amplification is baked into the loop, not an artifact of one algorithm.
  2. Offline simulation of 20 recommendation–consumption–retrain iterations on MovieLens 1M with UserKNN, BPR, and MostPopular, using the rank-based exponential acceptance model plus IRT rating estimation, then tracking average recommendation popularity, aggregate diversity, per-user KLD taste drift, and male/female group KLD.
Key Results주요 결과
  • Average recommendation popularity rises monotonically over 20 iterations for all three algorithms; BPR shows the steepest amplification slope (Fig. 2 left).
  • Aggregate diversity (catalog coverage) declines over iterations for all algorithms, most sharply for BPR, which starts with the highest coverage (Fig. 2 right).
  • Per-user KLD between iteration-$t$ and initial genre distributions increases monotonically for all algorithms — users' represented tastes drift from their initial preferences (Fig. 3 left).
  • $KLD(G_M || G_F)$ between male and female genre distributions drops dramatically over iterations for all algorithms — group homogenization (Fig. 3 right).
  • Females (minority: 1,709 users / 246K ratings vs. 4,331 males / 754K ratings) drift toward the male-dominated population distribution; $KLD(G || G_F)$ falls while $KLD(G || G_M)$ slightly rises, and female per-user deviation is significantly larger than male (Fig. 4).
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: empirical mechanism study (simulation) with a light formal model Context: builds on Chaney et al. 2018 (algorithmic confounding/homogenization, synthetic data), D'Amour et al. 2020 (long-term fairness simulation), Sinha et al. 2016 (feedback deconvolution — source of the IRT rating model), Jiang et al. 2019 (degenerate feedback loops). Within this survey it is the Track-A motivation paper for SP8's exposure-frequency intervention. Correctness: the formal argument is a simple but valid expectation calculation; empirical results are trajectories without error bars or repeated runs; single dataset (MovieLens 1M); user behavior is simulated by an assumed acceptance model, so magnitudes should be read qualitatively. [SKIM note: no CIs reported anywhere in the paper] Contributions: (1) formal proof-sketch that popularity bias monotonically amplifies through the feedback loop; (2) empirical demonstration that amplification shrinks aggregate diversity, drifts users' represented tastes, and homogenizes user groups; (3) finding that the minority group (females) suffers the strongest representation drift, being pulled toward the majority-dominated population distribution. Clarity: fully reproducible from the description — algorithms, equations, dataset, and iteration count are all stated (exact $\alpha$ value is not reported).

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: feedback loops amplify popularity bias across algorithms; amplification causes diversity decline, taste-representation shift, and homogenization; minority users are hit hardest.
  • Demonstrated: all of the above, but on one dataset (MovieLens 1M), three algorithms, one simulated choice model, no repeated runs.
  • Gap: the future-work hypothesis that per-iteration popularity-bias mitigation would shrink long-run amplification (the direct bridge to SP8's intervention question) is stated but not tested. The acceptance-probability model is assumed, not validated against real click behavior. "Generally stronger for minority users" is shown for one gender split on one dataset.
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Note: this is a 5-page short paper, so the skim (pages 1–5) covered the full text; concept definitions below are complete, not abstract-only.

Feedback loop simulation (iterated offline retrain loop)

  • What it computes: rating matrix $D^t$ → (80/20 train/test split → train recommender → generate top-k lists $R^t$ → users probabilistically accept items → accepted (item, estimated rating) pairs appended to profiles) → $D^{t+1}$; repeated for $T = 20$ iterations
  • Why it was created: single-shot offline evaluation cannot show how recommendation → consumption → retraining dynamics compound bias over time, and real-platform online testing was inaccessible
  • Concrete example: MovieLens 1M (6,040 users, 3,706 movies, ~1M ratings) run through 20 iterations with UserKNN, BPR, and MostPopular
  • How it is used in this paper: the entire experimental apparatus; every result is a trajectory over the 20 iterations

Acceptance probability

  • What it computes: item rank in a user's recommendation list → $prob(i|R_u^t) = e^{\alpha \times rank_i}$ with $\alpha < 0$ → selection probability (higher-ranked items exponentially more likely to be consumed)
  • Why it was created: the simulator needs a user-choice model; borrowed from Abdollahpouri et al. (2019) multistakeholder work to encode position bias in what users actually consume
  • Concrete example: with $\alpha = -0.5$, rank 1 gets $e^{-0.5} \approx 0.61$ relative weight vs. rank 5 at $e^{-2.5} \approx 0.08$
  • How it is used in this paper: step 3 of each simulation iteration, deciding which recommended items enter the next round's training data

Rating estimation via Item Response Theory

  • What it computes: user profile mean $\bar{s}_u$, profile std $sd(s_u)$, item mean rating $\bar{s}_i$, Gaussian noise $\eta_{u,i} \sim N(0,1)$ → $\omega = \bar{s}_u + sd(s_u)\times \bar{s}_i + \eta_{u,i}$, then $\hat{s}_{u,i} = \max(\min(round(\omega), b), a)$ → integer rating in $[a,b]$
  • Why it was created: the acceptance probability only selects items; rating-based algorithms (UserKNN) also need a plausible rating value for each accepted item
  • Concrete example: rating clamped to [1, 5] for MovieLens
  • How it is used in this paper: generates the rating attached to each accepted item before appending to the profile

Popularity propagation model (Eq. 4)

  • What it computes: $\overline{P}_{R^t} \propto \overline{P}_{D^t} + \theta^t$, where $\theta^t$ is the recommender's per-iteration popularity uplift over the data; adding $K$ accepted interactions gives $\overline{P}_{D^{t+1}} = \overline{P}_{D^t} + \frac{K\theta^t}{|D^t|+K}$, hence $\overline{P}_{R^{t+1}} > \overline{P}_{R^t}$ by transitivity
  • Why it was created: to show formally (not just empirically) that any recommender with a positive popularity lift ($\theta^t > 0$) monotonically amplifies data popularity through the loop
  • Concrete example: if the recommender's lists average 10% more popular than the data ($\theta^t$) and $K$ new interactions are logged, data popularity rises by $K\theta^t/(|D^t|+K)$ each round
  • How it is used in this paper: Section 3; also grounds the future-work hypothesis that per-iteration debiasing shrinks long-run amplification

Aggregate diversity (catalog coverage) and KLD-based taste-shift / homogenization metrics

  • What they compute: (a) % of catalog items appearing at least once across all users' lists per iteration; (b) KL divergence between a user's genre distribution at iteration $t$ vs. $t=1$ (taste-representation drift), and $KLD(G_M || G_F)$ between male/female group genre distributions (group homogenization)
  • Why created: to translate "bias amplification" into measurable downstream harms (shrinking catalogs, drifting profiles, converging groups)
  • Concrete example: multi-genre movies split probability uniformly (genres {a, b} → 0.5 each) over 18 MovieLens genres
  • How used: Figures 2–4 trajectories over 20 iterations
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: MovieLens 1M (public), implementations of UserKNN/BPR (e.g., LibRec/Implicit), the 3-step simulation loop; trivial compute (20 retrain rounds on 1M ratings)
  • Code: not available (no repository linked in the paper)
  • Modifications: for SP8, insert an exposure-frequency-controlling re-ranker between step 2 and step 3 of the loop and compare amplification slopes against the uncontrolled baseline; replace explicit-rating IRT with implicit-feedback acceptance for modern settings
  • Unknowns: missing link: the exact $\alpha$ used in the acceptance probability is not reported; missing link: sensitivity of conclusions to the choice model (top-1 / random selection variants are named but untested)
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Chaney, Stewart & Engelhardt, "How algorithmic confounding in recommendation systems increases homogeneity and decreases utility" (RecSys 2018) — the synthetic-data predecessor for homogenization; this paper's real-data counterpart
  • Sinha, Gleich & Ramani, "Deconvolving feedback loops in recommender systems" (NeurIPS 2016) — source of the IRT rating-estimation step; recovers true preferences from loop-contaminated ratings
  • Jiang et al., "Degenerate feedback loops in recommender systems" (AIES 2019) — theoretical treatment of loop degeneracy complementing this paper's empirical trajectories
  • Abdollahpouri & Mansoury, "Multi-sided Exposure Bias in Recommendation" (KDD IRS Workshop 2020) — same group's framing of exposure bias, closest bridge to SP8's exposure-frequency proxy
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = explicit-rating matrix (MovieLens 1M), outputs = top-k lists from classic CF (UserKNN, BPR, MostPopular), conditions = offline simulation, synthetic user choice via rank-exponential acceptance, 20 retrain iterations, popularity measured as average item interaction share. Our setting (SP8, exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy): exposure frequency as the controlled quantity in a ranking policy, aiming to break or bound the popularity-amplification loop this paper quantifies; modern ranking stacks (learned rankers / LLM-era pipelines), implicit feedback. Differences:

  • Diagnosis only vs. intervention: the paper measures amplification but tests no exposure-side control — missing link (its own future-work item; SP8's target)
  • Simulated acceptance model vs. real user click behavior: reference only (validity of $e^{\alpha \cdot rank}$ untested against logs)
  • Explicit ratings + classic CF vs. implicit feedback + modern rankers: missing link (amplification slopes may differ; Eq. 4 argument itself is algorithm-agnostic, which partially transfers) [SKIM - verify transferability]
  • Popularity measured on data averages vs. exposure frequency per item: resolved conceptually — the paper's $\theta^t$ (recommendation-over-data popularity uplift) is precisely an exposure-frequency excess, giving SP8 its intervention handle
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper explicitly models the mechanism producing amplification (Eq. 4: any positive per-iteration exposure uplift $\theta^t$, once logged, compounds) rather than only reporting degraded metrics; it is a diagnosis paper, so "solving" is out of scope, but the diagnosis targets the root cause.
