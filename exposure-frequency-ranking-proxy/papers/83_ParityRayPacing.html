The Parity Ray Regularizer for Pacing in Auction Markets

Celli et al. · arXiv · 2021 · 19 citations

arXiv:2106.09503 · PDF · SP: SP7

P2Track C

한 줄 요약

경매 시장 pacing을 위한 parity ray 정규화기. (v_t − ⟨λ_t, c_t⟩)/(1+μ_t) 입찰은 공정성 측 deficit dual과 동일한 subgradient dual 갱신이다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Online budget-pacing literature assumes the bidder optimizes an additively separable objective (total clicks/revenue), so existing pacing algorithms cannot express a common non-separable advertiser goal: making the realized distribution of won impressions across user categories close to a target distribution (e.g. uniform reach for surveys, balanced two-sided-market reach). Practitioners currently resort to complex manual sub-campaign segmentation (Gelauff et al. 2020); the closest general framework (Balseiro et al. 2020, regularized online allocation) offers a max-min fairness regularizer that would require knowing the input distribution and win-scale in advance to parameterize.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Add the parity ray regularizer $R(\bar{x}) = -\min_{\gamma\in[0,1]} D(\gamma\hat{x};\bar{x})$ (any jointly convex, Lipschitz pseudo-distance $D$; $\ell_p$ norms and Lipschitz-clipped f-divergences qualify) to the budget-constrained utility, then solve the regularized online pacing problem with a dual online-mirror-descent scheme (Algorithm 1): at each step, (i) primal decision maximizes opportunity-cost-adjusted reward $f_t(x) - \mu_t p_t x - \langle\lambda_t, c_t x\rangle$ subject to remaining budget; (ii) dual variables $(\mu, \lambda)$ are updated by mirror descent on an unbiased subgradient $(\rho - p_t\hat{x},\ \bar{x}_t - c_t\hat{x})$. The dual subgradient descent of Balseiro et al. (2020) is recovered as a special case of the OMD scheme. [SKIM - full proofs in Appendix B not traced step-by-step]

Key Results주요 결과
  • Algorithm 1 attains regret $O(T^{1/2})$ vs. hindsight-optimal allocation under i.i.d. inputs — the optimal order for this stochastic setting (Theorem 2; bound $C_1 + \frac{G^2\eta}{\sigma}T + \frac{1}{\eta}C_2$ with $\eta \sim T^{-1/2}$).
  • On real ad-log data ($10^6$ requests, $T$ up to 5000, 20 trials): regularized algorithm keeps TVD from target $\hat{x}$ near 0.01–0.02 for the binary breakdown, vs. ~0.125 for adaptive pacing (which tracks the input mix, ~0.12).
  • Hard 3-category case (empirical mix (.5,.3,.2), target (.1,.3,.6)): regularized TVD stays near 0 while AP sits near 0.4 — AP is even farther from target than the raw input distribution.
  • With artificially unbalanced category prices, AP degenerates to winning only cheap-category impressions (TVD ≈ 0.4–0.5); the regularized algorithm steers to target without sacrificing reward.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: tractable modeling of distributional preferences; optimal-order regret; easy incorporation into standard pacing systems; effectiveness on real-world data.
  • Demonstrated: regret theorem with explicit constants; simulation on one advertiser's log data from one platform, two breakdowns ($m=2,3$), $T \le 5000$.
  • Gap: the "easily incorporated into existing pacing systems" claim is shown by algebraic reduction (Section 6), not by a deployment or multi-bidder equilibrium experiment; single-bidder stochastic model — market-level effects when many bidders adopt the regularizer are untested. Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Parity ray regularizer

  • What it computes: input = realized average category-allocation vector $\bar{x} \in \Delta^m_+$ (fraction of won impressions per user category) and a target distribution $\hat{x} \in \Delta^m$; operation = $R(\bar{x}) := -\min_{\gamma \in [0,1]} D(\gamma\hat{x}; \bar{x})$, i.e. the negated pseudo-distance from $\bar{x}$ to the ray segment $\{\gamma\hat{x} : \gamma \in [0,1]\}$; output = a concave penalty in $[-\bar{r}, 0]$ added to the bidder's objective.
  • Why it was created: directly constraining the realized impression distribution $\sum_t c_{t,i}x_t / \sum_t x_t \approx \hat{x}_i$ is a non-convex problem; projecting onto the ray through $\hat{x}$ makes the distributional preference a concave, Lipschitz regularizer that online-learning machinery can handle, and it is scale-free (the bidder need not know in advance how many impressions they will win).
  • Concrete example: with the $\ell_2$ distance, $R(\bar{x}) = -\|\bar{x} - (\langle\bar{x},\hat{x}\rangle/\|\hat{x}\|_2^2)\hat{x}\|_2$ in closed form; for $m=3$ device-manufacturer categories with empirical mix $(.5,.3,.2)$, the advertiser can set target $\hat{x}=(.1,.3,.6)$ to skew toward the rarest category.
  • How it is used in this paper: inserted as $T \cdot R(\frac{1}{T}\sum_t c_t x_t)$ into the budget-constrained reward maximization (Eq. 2), then dualized and optimized by online mirror descent (Algorithm 1).

Double pacing (additive-then-multiplicative bid construction)

  • What it computes: input = valuation $v_t$, category vector $c_t$, dual variables $(\mu_t, \lambda_t)$; operation = bid $= (v_t - \langle\lambda_t, c_t\rangle)/(1+\mu_t)$; output = a paced second-price-auction bid.
  • Why it was created: shows the regularized allocation rule (Eq. 4) is implementable inside standard decentralized multiplicative-pacing systems — an additive category-dependent shading $\langle\lambda_t, c_t\rangle$ applied before the usual multiplicative pacing factor $1/(1+\mu_t)$.
  • Concrete example: the bidder wins item $t$ exactly when $(v_t - \langle\lambda_t, c_t\rangle)/(1+\mu_t) > p_t$, without knowing $p_t$ in advance (second-price truthfulness of the transformed value).
  • How it is used in this paper: Section 6 argument that the method drops into existing proxy-bidder pacing infrastructure with no centralized optimization.

Reuses: multiplicative pacing multiplier and dual online mirror descent for online allocation (Balseiro & Gur 2019; Balseiro, Lu & Mirrokni 2020) — standard in this literature, defined in the paper's Section 2.

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: stream of (value, price/score, category) tuples; a convex solver only for the per-step argmax (closed form for $\ell_2$ parity ray); paper used FICO Xpress 8.10 on a 24-core / 57 GB machine, but the per-step updates are lightweight.
  • Code: not available (no repository mentioned; data proprietary to Facebook).
  • Modifications: replace second-price win condition with ranking-score allocation; choose pseudo-distance $D$ ($\ell_2$ gives closed form); tune step size $\eta \sim T^{-1/2}$.
  • Unknowns: missing link — behavior when the input distribution is non-stationary or reacts to the algorithm's own allocations (the ranking feedback-loop case); missing link — how to set the target ray when the achievable exposure set is constrained by the ranker.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: single budget-constrained bidder in repeated single-item second-price auctions; inputs $(v_t, p_t, c_t)$ i.i.d. from unknown distribution; output = binary/fractional win decisions; goal = reward plus closeness of realized impression distribution over $m$ user categories to an advertiser-specified target $\hat{x}$; control lever = bid shading (dual variables), fully decentralized. Our setting (SP7 — exposure frequency via ranking proxy): controlling item/content exposure frequency distributions in a ranking or recommendation system, where exposure is determined by ranking-score proxies rather than explicit auction bids, and the target is a distribution over exposures rather than an advertiser utility. Differences:

  • Second-price auction allocation vs. ranking-based allocation: missing link — the truthfulness argument enabling the double-pacing bid transform ($\,(v_t-\langle\lambda,c_t\rangle)/(1+\mu)$) relies on second-price incentives; no direct analogue for a deterministic ranker.
  • Distributional target enforced through a scalar control (pacing multiplier + additive shading) vs. exposure steering through score adjustment: resolved conceptually — the dual variable $\lambda$ acting as a per-category additive penalty is exactly a "proxy signal" steering exposure frequency, transferable as an exposure-debt price on over-exposed categories.
  • i.i.d. stochastic input assumption vs. feedback loops in ranking (exposure changes future data): reference only — the regret guarantee does not survive adversarial/endogenous inputs. [SKIM - verify]
  • Budget constraint (hard resource) vs. exposure budget: resolved — exposure caps map naturally onto the per-iteration budget $\rho$.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the root cause it targets (distributional impression preferences are non-separable, hence outside standard pacing/online-learning machinery) is resolved structurally: the ray projection converts the non-convex realized-distribution constraint into a concave regularizer with a matching optimal-regret algorithm, rather than post-hoc patching allocations toward the target.
