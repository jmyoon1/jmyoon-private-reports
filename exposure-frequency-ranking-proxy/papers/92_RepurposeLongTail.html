Bypassing the Popularity Bias: Repurposing Models for Better Long-Tail Recommendation

Blahut et al. · arXiv · 2024

arXiv:2410.02776 · PDF · SP: SP8

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

재학습 없이 기존 모델을 재활용해 long-tail 추천을 개선하는 InvR. 일시적 relevance 표적 floor가 tail item을 유기적 추천으로 '킥스타트'하는(고유 item +18%) — exposure→학습 데이터 결합을 의도적으로 활용하는 — production 사례.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Industrial recommenders search for the best items per user, so high-quality long-tail publishers systematically receive too few impressions and clicks (popularity bias) to be economically viable on the platform. Prior fairness fixes via randomization (Plackett-Luce sampling, Rank Transposition) either ignore relevance or, per Bower et al., fail when applied at only one stage of a multi-stage industrial pipeline.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Invert the retrieval direction using components that already exist, with no retraining: for each under-exposed item of a selected long-tail publisher, query an ANN index of user embeddings with the item's embedding to get its top-N best users (item2users), transpose to user2items, and insert up to 3 such items per 20-item slate after the ranking stage and business logic, isolating them from the main pipeline's biases. Each treated item is guaranteed a constant minimum exposure (visible impressions), after which InvR support is switched off. Items per user are ordered by user rank w.r.t. the item, since absolute scores are popularity-biased. Cold-start items with useless ID-based embeddings are handled by standard random insertion.

Key Results주요 결과
  • InvR User rank: B50PS +33.3% (vs. InvR Score +9.9%, Random +9.2%) — long-tail half of items gains a third more exposure share
  • InvR User rank: CTR on InvR slots +300% and clicks +120% vs. the Random-assignment ablation (0%/0%), at PSEI +45%
  • Random assignment maximizes raw exposure (PSEI +181%) but with irrelevant recommendations (no CTR/click gain) — relevance-targeted exposure beats spraying
  • Cost of deployment: overall user average CTR −1.04%, average clicks per user −1.67%; T1PS (top-1% items' share) −1.6%
  • Long-term: InvR supplies only ~40% of an item's minimum exposure before the main recommender organically takes over ("kick-start" effect); main-pipeline unique recommended items +18% because InvR interactions feed back into training data

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Inverse Retrieval (InvR)

  • What it computes: item embedding (from an already-trained two-tower retrieval model) → ANN query against an index of user embeddings → top-N users most likely to find the item relevant. The standard user→items retrieval direction is inverted to item→users.
  • Why it was created: standard retrieval searches best items per user, so long-tail items rarely surface; inverting the search guarantees each selected long-tail item gets matched to its most promising users, bypassing the popularity bias of the main pipeline.
  • Concrete example: every 60 minutes, for each under-exposed item of a selected publisher, retrieve top-N users from a ScaNN index of 128-dim user embeddings; transpose item→users lists into user→items lists; insert up to 3 InvR items into each 20-item feed slate after the ranking stage.
  • How it is used in this paper: the core proposed mechanism, evaluated in production A/B tests at Seznam.cz.

User-rank ordering

  • What it computes: for multiple InvR items assigned to one user, order items by the rank of that user among all users for the item (instead of the absolute dot-product score).
  • Why it was created: absolute embedding scores are popularity-biased (popular items get higher scores regardless of relevance), which would defeat the long-tail purpose.
  • Concrete example: if user u is the 5th-best user for item A but 500th-best for item B, A is shown above B even if B's raw score is higher.
  • How it is used in this paper: the winning variant (InvR User rank) in the A/B ablation; deployed as production baseline.

B50PS (bottom 50 percent share)

  • What it computes: from the exposure Lorenz curve, the share of total exposure received by the least-popular half of all items.
  • Why it was created: adapts Lazovich et al.'s top-X-percent-share inequality metrics to capture changes specifically in the long-tail region.
  • Concrete example: InvR User rank lifted B50PS by +33.3% relative to baseline.
  • How it is used in this paper: primary system-level fairness metric, computed over all items.

PSEI (percentage of sufficiently exposed items)

  • What it computes: fraction of treated (selected-publisher) items whose visible impressions exceed a pre-defined minimum-exposure threshold.
  • Why it was created: B50PS covers all items; a complementary metric was needed for only the InvR-treated items against the exposure guarantee.
  • Concrete example: Random assignment gives +181% PSEI (uniform spraying maximizes exposure) but ~0% CTR gain; InvR variants give +41–45% PSEI with +261–300% CTR.
  • How it is used in this paper: second fairness metric; tracked long-term post-deployment (Figure 4).

Reverse k-MIPS (finding top-k users by inner product for a query item) is prior work (Amagata & Hara 2021/2023), reused conceptually here.

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: a trained user-item embedding model (two-tower or equivalent), an ANN library (ScaNN used), a periodic batch job (hourly here), and slate-insertion hooks after ranking; exposure logging for visible impressions
  • Code: not available (proprietary industrial system)
  • Modifications: for a research/benchmark setting, replace publisher business criteria with an item-popularity cutoff, define minimum exposure synthetically, and simulate slate insertion offline; cold-start needs content-based embeddings (named as future work)
  • Unknowns: missing link — exact minimum-exposure values, publisher/user selection thresholds, and N users-per-item are unspecified; missing link — why the popularity-biased Score ordering did not outperform on CTR is unexplained by the authors
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Amagata & Hara, "Reverse Maximum Inner Product Search" (2021; TWEB 2023) — the algorithmic foundation (reverse k-MIPS) for efficient item→users retrieval
  • Bower et al., "Random Isn't Always Fair" (2022) — motivates why single-stage randomization fails in industrial pipelines, the gap InvR's post-ranking insertion answers
  • Lazovich et al., "Measuring disparate outcomes ... distributional inequality metrics" (Patterns 2022) — source of the Lorenz-curve share metrics B50PS/T1PS derive from
  • Diaz et al., "Evaluating Stochastic Rankings with Expected Exposure" (CIKM 2020) — relevance-aware expected-exposure metrics, the main alternative measurement framework for SP8
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: proprietary industrial content-feed platform (Seznam.cz), millions of DAU; publisher-level provider fairness; exposure = visible impressions with a fixed per-item minimum; two-tower embeddings retrained every 5 min; online A/B with user-level randomization. Our setting (SP8 — exposure frequency as ranking proxy; long-tail exposure lift benchmark; O2 lightweight interventions): general/offline research setting studying how exposure frequency relates to ranking quality and how cheaply long-tail exposure can be lifted.   • Requires an already-trained two-tower model plus ANN infra: resolved — any user-item embedding model can substitute (paper states this explicitly), making it a genuinely lightweight O2-style intervention [SKIM - verify transfer cost]

Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the InvR overlay deliberately bypasses rather than fixes the popularity bias of the main model (an exposure-guarantee patch on the observable symptom), but two elements touch the root cause: user-rank ordering removes the popularity bias in score-based ordering, and InvR-generated interactions flowing back into training data measurably de-bias the main model (+18% unique items, PSEI continuing to grow post-deployment).
