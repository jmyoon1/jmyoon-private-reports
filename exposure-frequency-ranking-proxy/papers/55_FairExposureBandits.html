Fairness of Exposure in Stochastic Bandits

Wang et al. · ICML 2021 · 2021 · 61 citations

arXiv:2103.02735 · PDF · SP: SP6

P0Track A

한 줄 요약

확률적 bandit에서 merit 비례 exposure 공정성(FairX)을 정의한다. 단 merit가 bandit 자신의 추정 평균의 함수라 scorer를 대체하는 구조로, 신뢰된 고정 score 아래에서는 학습 문제가 폐형 목표로 퇴화한다 — 사용자의 '고정된 잡음 score' 영역은 아무도 다루지 않음을 드러내는 기준점.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Conventional stochastic bandit algorithms (UCB, TS, LinUCB, LinTS) used for recommendation converge to deterministically pulling the highest-mean arm. This produces winner-takes-all exposure allocation: items with nearly identical merit receive vastly different exposure, which is unfair to item-side stakeholders and degrades long-term platform health. Prior fairness-in-bandits work either preserved argmax behavior (Joseph et al.: better arm ≥ probability of worse arm, still winner-takes-all), imposed merit-blind minimum/maximum exposure quotas, or (Liu et al. 2017) handled only Bernoulli rewards with identity merit at a worse $T^{2/3}$ fairness-regret rate. Merit-based fairness of exposure (Singh & Joachims 2018) existed only in the batch/ranking setting, not online learning.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Formalize the FairX bandit setting: the learner deploys a stochastic policy $\pi_t$ each round and is judged against the unique optimal fair policy $\pi^\*(a) \propto f(\mu^\*_a)$. Four algorithms:

  1. FairX-UCB (MAB): maintain per-arm confidence intervals; pick the optimistic parameter $\mu_t = \arg\max_{\mu \in CR_t} \sum_a \frac{f(\mu_a)}{\sum_{a'} f(\mu_{a'})}\mu_a$ inside the confidence region, then play the fair policy induced by $\mu_t$ (stochastic selection, unlike deterministic UCB).
  2. FairX-TS (MAB): sample $\mu_t$ from the posterior, play $\pi_t(a) \propto f(\mu_{t,a})$ — avoids FairX-UCB's non-convex inner optimization.
  3. FairX-LinUCB / FairX-LinTS: same two templates with ridge-regression confidence ellipsoids / Gaussian posteriors over a shared parameter $\mu^\* \in \mathbb{R}^d$, contexts $x_{t,a}$.

Guarantees: fairness regret $\tilde{O}(L\sqrt{KT}/\gamma)$ (MAB) and $\tilde{O}(Ld\sqrt{T}/\gamma)$ (linear); reward regret $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{KT})$ and $\tilde{O}(d\sqrt{T})$; matching-necessity lower bounds (linear fairness regret without the minimum-merit or Lipschitz conditions).

Key Results주요 결과
  • FairX-UCB / FairX-TS: fairness regret $\tilde{O}(L\sqrt{KT}/\gamma)$, reward regret $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{KT})$ — the reward bound matches the conventional $\Omega(\sqrt{KT})$ lower bound up to logs; improves Liu et al. 2017's $(KT)^{2/3}$ fairness rate to $\sqrt{KT}$.
  • FairX-LinUCB / FairX-LinTS: fairness regret $\tilde{O}(Ld\sqrt{T}/\gamma)$, reward regret $\tilde{O}(d\sqrt{T})$.
  • Lower bounds: without minimum merit $\gamma$ (or with Lipschitz constant $L = \sqrt{T}$), any algorithm suffers $\mathbb{E}[FR_T] \ge 0.015T$ on some instance.
  • Empirically (yeast, mediamill, Yahoo! Today Module): conventional UCB/TS concentrate virtually all exposure on the single best arm after 2M rounds even when arm 12 has only slightly lower merit than arm 11; FairX variants track the optimal fair allocation and their fairness regret grows sub-linearly while conventional algorithms' grows linearly.
  • Simple FairX-EG ($\epsilon$-greedy with fair-policy construction) performs surprisingly competitively despite no comparable guarantee.
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: new method + theoretical analysis (new bandit objective, four algorithms, upper and lower bounds). Context: extends merit-based fairness of exposure (Singh & Joachims 2018, ranking/batch) to online bandit learning; improves over Liu et al. 2017 calibrated fairness ($T^{2/3} \to \sqrt{T}$, Bernoulli-only → general rewards/merits); builds on UCB (Auer 2002), linear bandit confidence sets (Dani et al. 2008; Abbasi-Yadkori et al. 2011), and Bayesian regret analysis (Russo & Van Roy 2014). Correctness: full proofs in appendix (Hoeffding/Azuma, self-normalized martingale bounds, Bretagnolle–Huber for lower bounds); experiments report mean ± std over 10 runs. Assumptions (bounded rewards, sub-Gaussian noise, known merit function) are standard and explicitly stated. Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

FairX bandit problem (merit-based fairness of exposure in bandits)

  • What it computes: a bandit formulation where the target policy is not the argmax-reward arm but the unique "optimal fair policy" $\pi^\*(a) = f(\mu^\*_a) / \sum_{a'} f(\mu^\*_{a'})$ — each arm's selection probability (exposure) is proportional to its merit $f(\mu^\*_a)$, an increasing function of its mean reward.
  • Why it was created: conventional bandits converge to deterministic winner-takes-all selection, so two arms with nearly identical merit (e.g. click-through rate) can receive drastically different exposure — unfair to items/sellers/artists and harmful to long-term platform dynamics (superstar effects, polarization).
  • Concrete example: with two arms of means $(\theta, 2\theta)$ and identity merit function, the optimal fair policy is $[1/3, 2/3]$ rather than $[0, 1]$.
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the objective against which both regrets are defined and to which all four proposed algorithms converge.

Fairness regret ($FR_T$)

  • What it computes: cumulative $\ell^1$ distance between deployed and optimal fair policy, $FR_T = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_a |\pi^\*(a) - \pi_t(a)|$.
  • Why it was created: reward regret alone cannot measure how quickly an algorithm learns to allocate exposure fairly; a metric for unfairness accumulation over rounds was needed.
  • Concrete example: a conventional UCB that converges to the single best arm accumulates $FR_T$ linear in $T$ (empirically ~12,000 at $T$=200k on Yahoo! data vs. sub-linear for FairX variants).
  • How it is used in this paper: primary theoretical quantity — sub-linear upper bounds ($\tilde{O}(L\sqrt{KT}/\gamma)$ for MAB) and linear lower bounds without the merit-function conditions are proven for it.

Reward regret w.r.t. the optimal fair policy ($RR_T$)

  • What it computes: $RR_T = \sum_t \sum_a (\pi^\*(a) - \pi_t(a))\mu^\*_a$ — reward gap to the fair policy, not the argmax policy.
  • Why it was created: under a fairness constraint the conventional regret baseline (best single arm) is unattainable and the wrong reference point.
  • Concrete example: an unfair policy can beat the optimal fair policy in reward, so $RR_T$ can be negative (observed for FairX-UCB in Fig. 2).
  • How it is used in this paper: second guarantee proven for all four algorithms, $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{KT})$ (MAB) and $\tilde{O}(d\sqrt{T})$ (linear).

Minimum-merit and Lipschitz conditions on the merit function

  • What it computes: $\min_\mu f(\mu) \ge \gamma > 0$ and $|f(\mu_1) - f(\mu_2)| \le L|\mu_1 - \mu_2|$.
  • Why it was created: the paper proves (Thms 3.1.4/3.1.5) that dropping either condition forces expected fairness regret $\ge 0.015T$ (linear) for some instance — i.e. the conditions are necessary for learnability of fair exposure.
  • Concrete example: with $f$ having minimum merit $1/\sqrt{T}$, no algorithm avoids linear fairness regret.
  • How it is used in this paper: assumptions of all fairness-regret upper bounds; $L/\gamma$ appears as a multiplicative factor.

Today Module, replay evaluation); projected gradient descent for the UCB variants' inner non-convex optimization (lr 0.01, 10 steps in the paper); TS variants need only posterior sampling.

  • Modifications: for a ranking/exposure-proxy setting, the single-selection exposure model must be replaced by a position-bias-weighted exposure over slates; the merit function must be estimated rather than assumed.

Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문

  • Liu et al., "Calibrated Fairness in Bandits" (2017) — the closest predecessor; this paper generalizes its reward/merit assumptions and improves its $T^{2/3}$ fairness rate.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: online stochastic bandits (MAB and linear); inputs = per-round arm set (optionally with context vectors), bandit feedback of one arm's reward; outputs = a stochastic policy per round; conditions = merit is a known increasing function $f$ of the true mean reward, with $f \ge \gamma > 0$ and $L$-Lipschitz; exposure = selection probability of the policy at each round (single-slot selection, not a ranked list). Our setting (SP6, exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy survey): exposure/impression frequency observed in logged or deployed ranking systems, used as a measurable proxy for item merit or ranking quality; typically ranked lists with position bias, offline logs rather than controllable online policies. Differences:

  • Single-arm selection vs. ranked-list exposure with position bias: missing link — the paper's exposure is exactly $\pi_t(a)$, with no position/attention model; extending to slates is not addressed here (Singh & Joachims 2018 handles the ranking side).
  • Merit defined as known function of true mean reward vs. merit inferred from noisy engagement proxies: missing link — the paper assumes $f$ is given; how exposure frequency serves as a proxy for merit is the converse question. [SKIM - verify]
  • Online control of the policy vs. offline/observational exposure logs: reference only — the paper requires deploying stochastic policies; our setting may only observe exposures.
  • Fairness regret as $\ell^1$ policy distance: resolved — directly reusable as a metric for how far an observed exposure distribution deviates from merit-proportional exposure.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper changes the bandit objective itself (target = merit-proportional exposure policy) rather than post-hoc patching exposure imbalance, and it proves necessity lower bounds showing exactly which structural conditions (minimum merit, Lipschitz merit) make fair-exposure learning possible at all.
