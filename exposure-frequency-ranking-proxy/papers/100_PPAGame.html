PPA-Game: Characterizing and Learning Competitive Dynamics Among Online Content Creators

Xu et al. · KDD 2025 · 2025 · 5 citations

arXiv:2403.15524 · PDF · SP: SP9

P0Track B

한 줄 요약

온라인 creator 간 경쟁 동학을 비례 배분 게임(PPA-Game)으로 특성화하고 학습한다(KDD 2025). long-tail 보상 하 순수 Nash 균형과 PoA 상한을 제공한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Game-theoretic models of content-creator competition on recommender platforms (YouTube/TikTok) use allocation rules that mismatch how platforms actually distribute exposure: winner-take-all models give all exposure to the single top-quality creator per topic (conflicting with platform diversity practice), and equal-split models ignore that creators have heterogeneous, topic-specific quality. Moreover, most prior equilibrium analyses are static — they characterize Nash equilibria without modeling how selfish creators, lacking prior knowledge of topic demand and their own quality, would learn their way to equilibrium.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여
  1. Define the PPA-Game (Definition 3.2): $N$ players choose among $K$ resources; resource $k$'s payoff $\mu_k$ is split proportionally to the weights $w_{j,k}$ of the players choosing it.
  2. Equilibrium analysis: PNE can fail to exist (explicit $N{=}7, K{=}4$ counterexample) but exists under long-tailed resource payoffs (Theorem 3.1), uniform per-player weights, homogeneous players, or homogeneous resources (Theorem 3.2). Price of anarchy $\leq 1 + (\min\{N,K\}-1)/N$ (Theorem 3.3).
  3. Online learning: an explore-then-alternate algorithm — exploration phase ($c_1 K$ rounds estimating $\hat\mu_{j,k}$ with a collision-minimizing round-robin), then alternating "Learning PNE" subphases (Pradelski–Young trial-and-error mood dynamics: discontent/content/watchful/hopeful, with a perturbed game $\mathcal{G}'$ to make the most-efficient PNE unique) and exponentially growing exploitation subphases. Per-player regret $\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Reg}_j(T)] = O(\log^{1+\eta} T)$ for any $\eta > 0$ (Theorem 4.2). [SKIM - proof details in appendix; mechanism verified from pp. 8-11.]
Key Results주요 결과
  • PNE fails to exist in only 1 of 20,000,000 randomly generated game configurations ($N, K \in [10,100]$, four distributions).
  • Price of anarchy bounded by $1 + (\min\{N,K\}-1)/N$ when a PNE exists — identical to the equal-split SMAA bound.
  • Per-player regret $O(\log^{1+\eta} T)$ for any $\eta > 0$; count of non-most-efficient-PNE rounds also $O(\log^{1+\eta} T)$ (Theorem 4.2).
  • Synthetic experiments ($T = 3{,}000{,}000$; $(N,K) \in \{(3,5),(5,3)\}$): baselines TotalReward, SelfishRobustMMAB, SMAA all show linear regret growth (they fail to identify the PPA-Game's PNE); the proposed method's regret curve flattens, indicating convergence to the most efficient PNE.
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: new method (game-theoretic model + online learning algorithm with regret guarantee) Context: builds on creator-competition games (Ben-Porat et al. 2018/2019/2020; Hron et al. 2022; Jagadeesan et al. 2022), directly generalizes SMAA (Xu et al. 2023, same lead author) from equal-split to proportional allocation; uses Pradelski–Young (2012) trial-and-error learning and the Bistritz–Leshem "Game of Thrones" MPMAB analysis. Correctness: theorems come with proofs (appendix); PNE-prevalence claim rests on a 20M-configuration Monte Carlo simulation rather than a general theorem — appropriately labeled as observations. Experiments are synthetic only, 50 simulations, no error bars visible in Fig. 2 [SKIM - verify]. Contributions: (1) proportional-allocation game formulation matching how recommenders split exposure across creator quality; (2) PNE existence characterization + PoA bound; (3) decentralized learning algorithm converging to the most efficient PNE with $O(\log^{1+\eta} T)$ regret. Clarity: game definition and algorithm pseudocode (Algorithm 1) are complete enough to reimplement; hyperparameters $(c_1, c_2, c_3, \eta, \epsilon, \Gamma)$ and their tuning ranges are stated.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: PPA-Game realistically characterizes creator competition; PNE existence is prevalent; algorithm converges to the most efficient PNE with polylog regret.
  • Demonstrated: theorems for existence conditions/PoA/regret; simulation-based prevalence; synthetic-only empirical validation.
  • Gap: realism of proportional allocation with static per-creator weights $w_{j,k}$ is argued by analogy to platforms, never validated against real platform exposure data. Regret guarantee requires $w_{j,k} > 0$ for all $j,k$ and a PNE to exist. [SKIM - verify constant/condition tightness in deep read.]
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Proportional Payoff Allocation Game (PPA-Game)

  • What it computes: input = $K$ resources (content topics) with total payoffs $\mu_k \in (0,1]$ and $N$ players (creators) with per-resource weights $w_{j,k} \in [0,1]$; operation = each player picks one resource, and each resource's payoff is split among the players who chose it in proportion to their weights: $\mathcal{U}_j(\pi) = \mu_k w_{j,k} / \sum_{j'} \mathbb{I}[\pi_{j'}=k] w_{j',k}$; output = per-player exposure payoffs.
  • Why it was created: prior creator-competition models used either winner-take-all allocation (only the top-quality creator gets exposure; Ben-Porat et al. 2019b/2020, Liu et al. 2020/2021) or equal-split averaging (Xu et al. 2023, SMAA) — both mismatched to real recommender platforms, which distribute exposure proportionally to heterogeneous creator quality.
  • Concrete example: payoffs $[0.6, 0.4, 0.2]$ with player weights $[1, 3/8, 1/4]$ yield two distinct pure Nash equilibria, $\pi^{(1)}=(1,2,3)$ with welfare 1.2 and $\pi^{(2)}=(2,1,1)$ with welfare 1.0 — the second is an inefficient PNE.
  • How it is used in this paper: the core object of study; PNE existence/non-existence, price of anarchy, and the online-learning framework are all built on it.

Pure Nash Equilibrium (PNE) existence conditions for proportional allocation

  • What it computes: sufficient conditions on $(\mu, W)$ under which better-response dynamics terminate. Main condition (Theorem 3.1, long-tailed resources): for all $k \neq k'$, $\mu_k/\mu_{k'} > N_0/\epsilon_0$ or its reciprocal, where $N_0$ = max players with positive weight on any resource, $\epsilon_0$ = smallest positive weight.
  • Why it was created: PPA-Game does not always admit a PNE (a 7-player, 4-resource counterexample exists), so identifying realistic regimes where stable creator configurations exist is necessary for the model to be predictive.
  • Concrete example: among 20,000,000 randomly sampled game configurations ($N,K \in [10,100]$, four weight/payoff distributions), only 1 lacked a PNE.
  • How it is used in this paper: grounds the claim that the equilibrium analysis applies to typical recommender-platform scenarios (long-tailed topic popularity, influencer-style uniform weights, homogeneous resources).

Multi-Player Multi-Armed Bandit (MPMAB) with proportional reward sharing

  • What it computes: $T$-round decentralized bandit where arm $k$ yields stochastic reward $X_k(t)$ with mean $\mu_k$; colliding players split $X_k(t)$ proportionally to weights instead of receiving zero (the standard MPMAB collision assumption). Each player observes their own reward $R_j(t)$ and the arm's total reward.
  • Why it was created: standard MPMAB assumes zero payoff on collision, which does not model exposure sharing on content platforms; SMAA (Xu et al. 2023) allows sharing but only equal-split.
  • Concrete example: [SKIM - verify in deep read] — the reward formula $R_j(t) = X_k(t) \cdot w_{j,k}/\sum_{j'} \mathbb{I}[\pi_{j'}(t)=k] w_{j',k}$ instantiates it.
  • How it is used in this paper: framework for the online-learning algorithm whose per-player regret is bounded by $O(\log^{1+\eta} T)$.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: simulation only — no data or special compute; Algorithm 1 is a per-player loop with $O(NK)$ state; finding the most efficient PNE inside the mood dynamics is decentralized (no centralized equilibrium solver needed).
  • Code: not mentioned in the paper [SKIM - check for repo in deep read / KDD page].
  • Modifications: for our survey's setting, would need (a) weights $w_{j,k}$ replaced by learned relevance scores from a ranking model, (b) non-stationary $\mu_k$, (c) a platform-side objective to compare allocation rules.
  • Unknowns: missing link: sensitivity of convergence time to the hyperparameters $(c_1, c_2, \epsilon)$ — theory says "for small enough $\epsilon$, large enough $T$" with unspecified constants; missing link: behavior when weights are misestimated by the platform rather than by players.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문

  • Boursier & Perchet (2020), "Selfish Robustness and Equilibria in Multi-Player Bandits" (COLT 2020) — selfish-player MPMAB with zero-on-collision; comparison baseline (SelfishRobustMMAB).

  • Yao et al. (2024b), "User Welfare Optimization in Recommender Systems with Competing Content Creators" (KDD 2024) — platform-side welfare view of the same creator-competition setting; relevant for SP9's mechanism-design angle.
  • Pradelski & Young (2012), "Learning Efficient Nash Equilibria in Distributed Systems" — the trial-and-error learning framework the algorithm's mood dynamics instantiate.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = $N$ strategic creators choosing among $K$ topics, exposure $\mu_k$ split proportionally to fixed quality weights $w_{j,k}$; outputs = equilibrium characterization + decentralized learning dynamics; conditions = selfish players, stochastic stationary arm rewards, statistical sensing (each player sees own reward and arm total), synthetic evaluation. Our setting (SP9 — exposure/frequency as the payoff mechanism creators compete over, and what proportional exposure allocation does to the creator ecosystem): we care about exposure allocation as a ranking-proxy mechanism — how the platform's choice of allocation rule (proportional vs. winner-take-all vs. equal) shapes creator incentives and content supply. Differences:

  • Paper takes the proportional allocation rule as given and analyzes creator behavior under it; our SP asks how exposure-as-payoff proxies distort or stabilize the ecosystem — the paper supplies the mechanism-level characterization for the proportional case: resolved (this is exactly the paper's contribution).
  • Creator quality weights $w_{j,k}$ are exogenous and fixed; in real ranking systems quality/relevance estimates are themselves learned and feedback-coupled to exposure: missing link.
  • Evaluation is fully synthetic; no real creator/platform data: missing link.
  • Exposure $\mu_k$ is stationary per topic; real topic demand is non-stationary: reference only (paper acknowledges fluctuation motivates the bandit framing but assumes i.i.d. rewards).
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper attacks the root modeling gap it names (allocation rules mismatched to platform exposure-sharing) by changing the game's payoff structure itself and re-deriving equilibrium existence and learning guarantees under the new rule, rather than patching the old models' predictions.
