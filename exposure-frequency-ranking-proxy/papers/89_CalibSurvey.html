Calibrated Recommendations: Survey and Future Directions

Silva et al. · arXiv · 2025

arXiv:2507.02643 · PDF · SP: SP8

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

calibrated recommendation 연구를 정리하고 네 가지 기준 분포 계열(역사적 빈도/merit 비례/engagement 학습/균등)의 결함을 드러내는 2025 survey. 기준 분포 taxonomy의 출처.

Problem해결하려는 문제

The calibration-in-recommendation literature since Steck (2018) has grown fast (53 papers, strongest growth 2024) but is fragmented: many target-distribution definitions (genre, popularity tiers, quality, user subprofiles), many divergence measures (KL, JS, EMD, weighted total variation, 57 benchmarked distances), many solvers (greedy, MILP, branch-and-bound, min-cost flow), and no unified benchmark comparing them. No prior survey structures this space.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

Systematic survey (ACM DL + Google Scholar, since 2018; 53 included papers plus two pre-Steck precursors, PPTM 2011 and Jugovac 2017). Contributions:

  1. A component decomposition of the standard calibration pipeline: baseline ranking → candidate selection (top-k, usually 100) → aggregate relevance → target/realized distributions → calibration measure → trade-off ($\lambda$) modeling → optimized item selection (NP-hard; greedy in practice).
  2. A taxonomy of the 53 papers: 58% technical proposals, 28% impact analyses, 14% comparison works.
  3. An evaluation-practice audit: 88% offline-only, 8% user studies, 2% (one paper) online field test; 27.1% do not even report their data split; movie domain (MovieLens) dominates (47 papers).
  4. Open-challenge list: threshold/λ selection, scalability (NP-hard re-ranking), per-user harm hidden by aggregate metrics, user-perception gap, cold start, and the absence of a unified benchmark.
Key Results주요 결과
  • 53 papers included (from 57 ACM DL + 356 Google Scholar hits, May 2025); publications grew every year, peaking at 18 in 2024.
  • Evaluation practice: 88% offline, 8% user studies, 2% online; 27.1% of papers report no data-split information.
  • Domain skew: movies used in 47 papers, music 16; most common baselines BPR (16), ItemKNN (13), UserKNN (10).
  • The single field test (Klimashevskaia et al. 2023, production system): popularity calibration promoted less-popular items with no CTR loss — suggesting the offline accuracy–calibration trade-off may not exist in real deployments.
  • User-study finding (Alves et al. 2024, Lesota et al. 2023): users notice calibration/fairness improvements only when explicitly labeled in the UI.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: comprehensive structured overview of calibrated recommendations, technical approaches, evaluation studies, and practical limitations.
  • Demonstrated: the taxonomy and statistics above are directly backed by the 53-paper corpus.
  • Gap: as a survey it proposes no method and validates no ranking of the surveyed approaches; its call that "benchmarking all proposals" is missing applies to itself — it does not empirically compare the methods it catalogues. Preprints were excluded, so the most recent (2024–25 arXiv-only) work is out of scope.
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

This is a survey; it consolidates rather than introduces concepts. The key terms of the line it maps:

Calibrated recommendations (Steck 2018)

  • What it computes: user interaction history → derive a target distribution over item attributes (e.g., genres) → re-rank a base recommender's top-k so the recommendation list's attribute distribution matches the target.
  • Why it was created: accuracy-only ranking can produce monothematic lists that crowd out a user's minority interests.
  • Concrete example: a user with 70 romance / 30 action movies watched should receive a list that is ~70% romance / 30% action.
  • How it is used in this paper: the founding definition around which the 53 surveyed papers are organized.

Target vs. realized distribution ($P(H_u)$, $Q(L_u)$)

  • What it computes: $p(g|u)$ = rating-weighted proportion of attribute $g$ in the user's history (Eq. 3); $q(g|u)$ = predicted-rating-weighted proportion of $g$ in the recommendation list (Eq. 4), with $\alpha$-smoothing $\tilde{Q} = (1-\alpha)Q + \alpha P$, $\alpha \approx 0.01$, to avoid zero-mass genres.
  • Why it was created: calibration needs comparable distributions on both the profile side and the list side.
  • Concrete example: an item with 4 genres contributes proportion $1/4 = 0.25$ per genre.
  • How it is used in this paper: the shared formal core of nearly all surveyed methods (Section 3.1.4).

Calibration trade-off objective

  • What it computes: $L_u^* = \arg\max_{L,|L|=N} (1-\lambda)\,\mathrm{REL}(L_u) - \lambda\,\mathrm{CL}(P_u, Q(L_u))$ (Eq. 7), typically solved greedily (Surrogate Submodular, $(1-1/e)$ guarantee) over a top-100 candidate set; CL is usually KL or JS divergence.
  • Why it was created: calibration competes with relevance; a single utility makes the trade-off explicit and optimizable.
  • Concrete example: $\lambda = 0$ is pure accuracy; $\lambda = 1$ is maximal calibration; studies sweep 0.0–1.0 in steps of 0.1.
  • How it is used in this paper: the reference architecture against which all algorithmic variants (MILP, branch-and-bound, min-cost flow, fuzzy D'Hondt) are catalogued.

Calibrated Popularity (CP) — Abdollahpouri et al.'s variant that replaces genres with popularity buckets (Head/Middle/Tail); the calibration target becomes the user's historical popularity-tier mix. Directly relevant to the SP's exposure-frequency framing. [SKIM - verify per-method details in deep read]

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: any base ranker's top-k scores + categorical item attributes; the standard greedy re-ranker is trivial to implement (Eqs. 1–7 fully specified).
  • Code: no artifact for the survey itself; individual surveyed methods vary. [SKIM]
  • Modifications: for the SP, swap the calibration attribute from genre to exposure/impression-frequency buckets (CP-style) and decide user-level vs. system-level target distribution.
  • Unknowns: missing link: no unified benchmark exists to say which calibration variant is best; missing link: long-term effects of calibration on user behavior (A/B tests too short).
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Steck (2018), "Calibrated Recommendations" (RecSys) — the founding paper; primary source for the framework this survey organizes.
  • Abdollahpouri et al. (2020), "The Connection Between Popularity Bias, Calibration, and Fairness" (RecSys) — the bridge from calibration to the SP's exposure/popularity framing.
  • Klimashevskaia et al. (2023), "Evaluating the Effects of Calibrated Popularity Bias Mitigation: A Field Study" (RecSys) — the only production A/B test; evidence the accuracy–calibration trade-off may vanish online.
  • Kleinberg, Ryu & Tardos (2024), "Calibrated Recommendations for Users with Decaying Attention" (SAGT) — theoretical treatment of optimal calibrated lists.
  • Klimashevskaia et al. (2024), "A Survey on Popularity Bias in Recommender Systems" (UMUAI) — companion survey for the popularity-bias side of the SP.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = explicit/implicit user–item interaction histories with categorical item attributes (genre, popularity tier); outputs = re-ranked top-N lists per user; conditions = mostly offline MovieLens/Last.fm evaluation, CF base rankers. Our setting (SP8, exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy): whether exposure/impression frequency is a valid proxy or target relative to relevance in ranking; interested in product-level exposure goals vs. user-profile-matching goals. Differences:

  • Calibration here is user-profile-matching (match the user's own historical distribution); the SP's exposure question also concerns system/product-level exposure targets — the survey touches this only via Abdollahpouri et al.'s system-wide target distribution and provider-fairness work: reference only.
  • Popularity-tier calibration (CP; Head/Middle/Tail buckets) is the closest bridge to exposure-frequency framing — threshold choice for popularity buckets is flagged as unresolved: missing link (how to set exposure/popularity thresholds is open even in this literature).
  • Evaluation regime: 88% offline with MovieLens vs. the SP's interest in real exposure dynamics; only one field test exists: missing link.
  • [SKIM - verify] whether any surveyed work uses exposure frequency (impressions) rather than interaction popularity as the calibration attribute.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Symptom — the calibration line this survey maps is dominated (and the survey says so) by post-hoc re-ranking that patches the observable distribution mismatch on top of a fixed base ranker, without addressing why base rankers over-concentrate on popular/majority-genre items in the first place; the survey itself is a landscape mapping and explicitly lists the unresolved root issues (threshold/λ selection, per-user harm, real-world validity) as open.
