Fast online ranking with fairness of exposure

Usunier et al. · FAccT 2022 · 2022

arXiv:2209.13019 · PDF · SP: SP5 · SP9

P2Track C

한 줄 요약

온라인 Frank-Wolfe의 iterate가 곧 실현 평균 exposure가 되도록 재정식화해 anytime O(1/√t) regret를 주는 고속 온라인 공정 ranking(OFFR).

Problem해결하려는 문제

Fairness-of-exposure rankings are obtained by maximizing a concave objective over randomized rankings, but total item exposure sums over users, which couples every user's optimal ranking to everyone else's. Existing solutions either (a) solve the global problem in batch for all users at once — O(n×m) memory and impractical compute at scale (Do et al. 2021) — or (b) use online heuristics like FairCo that apply to only one narrow fairness criterion, cannot target intermediate utility/fairness trade-offs, and guarantee only that the disparity term vanishes with no guarantee on user utility.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

OFFR, an online Frank-Wolfe scheme. At each time step t, user $i^{(t)} \sim \text{Categorical}(w)$ arrives (activities w unknown):

  1. Compute approximate gradient scores $\hat{g}^{(t)}_{ij}$ per item from online estimates (running averages of user utility $\hat{u}_i$, item exposures $\hat{v}_j$, qualities $\hat{q}_j$, or group exposures $\hat{v}_{j|s}$, depending on the objective) — O(m) flops, O(n+m) memory.
  2. Output the ranking $\sigma^{(t)} = \text{topk}(\hat{g}_i^{(t)})$ — the Frank-Wolfe linear-maximization step reduces to a top-k sort because exposure objectives are linear in the exposure vector (Prop. 1, from Do et al. 2021).
  3. Update the online estimates in O(m).

Main theorem (Thm. 2): if the gradient approximation error decays as $D_i/\sqrt{t}$, the regret satisfies $$R^{(t)} \le 2B_\mathcal{E}\sum_i (L_i+G_i)\frac{\ln(et)}{t} + \frac{6B_\mathcal{E}\sum_i \sqrt{w_i}(G_i+D_i)}{\sqrt{t}} = O(1/\sqrt{t}).$$ Prop. 3 verifies the $O(\sqrt{n/t})$ gradient-error condition for all three objectives using empirical activity estimates $\hat{w} = c^{(t)}/t$.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Regret $R^{(t)} = O(1/\sqrt{t})$ for all three objectives (Table 1), vs. $O(1/t)$ for batch-FW — but per-epoch compute is equal, and empirically OFFR beats batch-FW over the first ~1000 epochs on lastfm15k, especially for large fairness weight β.
  • Per-step complexity: O(m) score computation + O(m + k ln k) amortized top-k sort; storage O(n+m) — same order as a fairness-free recommender.
  • On lastfm15k (15k users/items, k=40), varying β from 0.001 to 100 traces a Pareto front of user utility vs. item fairness; for balanced exposure, near-perfect fairness (group disparity ≤ 1e-3) at very little utility cost.
  • vs. FairCo: OFFR converges to the trade-off dictated by β (FairCo does not optimize an objective and cannot target intermediate trade-offs); a "pacing" heuristic $\beta_t = \min(\beta, \gamma t/n)$ replicates FairCo's utility-first early dynamics.
  • MovieLens-1m replication is qualitatively identical.

Context: online variant of the batch Frank-Wolfe fair-ranking algorithm of Do et al. 2021 (Lorenz-dominance two-sided fairness); compares against FairCo (Morik et al. 2020); builds on Singh & Joachims 2018 fairness-of-exposure and Biega et al. 2018 amortized equity of attention. Correctness: full proofs in appendices A–C; experiments averaged over 3 seeds; assumptions (oracle relevance values $\mu_{ij}$, stationary user distribution, position-based model) are explicit and their limitations discussed. [SKIM — appendix proofs not verified line-by-line.] Contributions: (1) first general, scalable online algorithm for any smooth concave exposure objective with $O(1/\sqrt{t})$ regret; (2) per-step cost dominated by the top-k sort and O(n+m) memory — fairness "almost free" vs. plain relevance ranking; (3) new balanced-exposure-to-user-groups criterion. Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

OFFR (Online Frank-Wolfe for Fair Ranking)

  • What it computes: at each time step, given the arriving user's index → computes per-item scores as an approximate gradient of a concave exposure-fairness objective (using online estimates of user utilities and item exposures), then top-k sorts those scores → outputs a deterministic ranking whose long-run average exposure matrix converges to the optimum of the concave objective.
  • Why it was created: prior fairness-of-exposure optimizers either solve the coupled global problem in batch for all users at once (Do et al. 2021 — infeasible at production scale in compute and memory) or are heuristics with weak guarantees (FairCo).
  • Concrete example: on lastfm15k (15k users × 15k items, top-k=40, DCG weights $b_r = 1/\log_2(1+r)$), OFFR reaches lower regret than batch-FW for the first ~1000 epochs at equal per-epoch cost (one top-k sort per user).
  • How it is used in this paper: it is the paper's core contribution; instantiated on three fairness objectives (Alg. 1–3) with per-objective regret bounds.

Amortized/cumulative exposure targeting (average exposure matrix $\pi^{(t)}$)

  • What it computes: sequence of realized exposure vectors $e^{(\tau)}$ → per-user running average with stochastic step size $1/c_i^{(t)}$ (the user's visit count) → matrix $\pi^{(t)}$ whose entry $\pi_{ij}$ is item j's average exposure to user i so far.
  • Why it was created: exposure fairness is only meaningful cumulatively across rankings; one ranking cannot equalize exposure. Optimizing the average lets each instant stay a deterministic top-k while the sequence meets exposure targets.
  • Concrete example: if user i has been seen 4 times and item j appeared once at rank 1 (weight $b_1$), then $\pi_{ij} = b_1/4$.
  • How it is used: it is the optimization variable; the Frank-Wolfe update with step $1/(c_i^{(t)}+1)$ makes the iterate coincide with the empirical average exposure.

Balanced exposure to user groups (novel objective in this paper)

  • What it computes: user group partition S + exposure matrix → per-group item exposures $v_{j|s}$ → penalty $\sqrt{\eta + \sum_s (v_{j|s} - v_{j|avg})^2}$ pushing each item's exposure to be even across demographic user groups.
  • Why it was created: motivated by job-ad audits (Imana et al. 2021): an ad should reach men/women (or age groups) in similar proportion; prior objectives only handled item-side or two-sided fairness, not evenness of an item's audience.
  • Concrete example: a job ad shown at $v_{j|male}=0.3$ vs $v_{j|female}=0.1$ incurs penalty; near-perfect balance (disparity ≤ 1e-3) achievable on lastfm15k at very little user-utility cost.
  • How it is used: third instantiation (Alg. 3), with regret bound depending on the minimum group weight $\overline{w}_{\min}$.

Reused (defined elsewhere, standard in this literature): position-based exposure model, quality-weighted (merit-based) exposure, two-sided welfare objective (Do et al. 2021), FairCo dynamic-ranking baseline (Morik et al. 2020).

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: precomputed relevance matrix (or on-the-fly scorer), O(n+m) counters, a top-k sort per request; lastfm15k / MovieLens-1m data pipelines use standard implicit-feedback matrix factorization (Implicit toolkit, per App. D).
  • Code: not stated in the paper — no repository link found in the PDF. [SKIM — check for a later official release.]
  • Modifications: for our proxy-focused setting, µ would be replaced by estimated relevance updated online, which breaks the paper's oracle assumption and its regret analysis; the pacing heuristic and online exposure counters are directly reusable.
  • Unknowns: missing link: behavior when µ estimates are exposure-biased (the feedback loop the paper explicitly brackets out); missing link: lower bound for the $O(1/\sqrt{t})$ rate (authors conjecture unimprovable, left open).
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문

  • Morik, Singh, Hong & Joachims (Controlling fairness and bias in dynamic learning-to-rank, SIGIR 2020) — FairCo baseline; the contrast (disparity-driving controller vs. objective optimization) is instructive for SP9.
  • Jeunen & Goethals (Top-k contextual bandits with equity of exposure, RecSys 2021) — extends exposure fairness to the bandit/learning setting the paper leaves out — the bridge to estimated-relevance conditions.

Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = oracle (user,item) relevance values $\mu_{ij}$ given by matrix factorization treated as ground truth; users drawn i.i.d. from a fixed unknown activity distribution; position-based exposure model with fixed weights b; outputs = top-k ranking per request; conditions = stationary preferences, no learning of µ, no feedback loops, discrete sensitive attributes available for the group objective. Our setting (SP5/SP9 — exposure frequency as a ranking proxy): we study how exposure/impression frequency functions as a proxy signal in ranking pipelines, where relevance is estimated (not oracle) and exposure both drives and is driven by the ranking (feedback loop). Differences:

  • Oracle vs. estimated relevance: paper explicitly disentangles preference learning from fair ranking generation — reference only (the paper deliberately excludes the estimation problem; it notes fairness of the whole system hinges on unbiased µ estimation, which is our proxy question).
  • Stationarity / no feedback loops: paper assumes exposure does not alter future preferences or data — missing link for our setting, acknowledged as a limitation in §7 (exposure→interaction→relevance-estimate loop is exactly the exposure-frequency-proxy circularity).
  • Position-based model required for FW linearity: cascade or other click models break the linear structure — missing link (open problem stated by the authors).
  • Cumulative (amortized) exposure accounting across rankings: directly transferable to any setting where exposure targets are enforced over a stream — resolved: the online-averaging + stochastic-step-size construction carries over.
