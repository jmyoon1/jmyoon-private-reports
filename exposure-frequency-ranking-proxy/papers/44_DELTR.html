Survey조사›SP4›[44] Reducing Disparate Exposure in Ranking:

Reducing Disparate Exposure in Ranking: A Learning To Rank Approach

Zehlike & Castillo · arXiv · 2018 · 145 citations

arXiv:1805.08716 · PDF · SP: SP4

P2Track C

한 줄 요약

학습 단계에서 집단 간 exposure 격차를 줄이는 learning-to-rank(DELTR)를 제안한다. in-processing(재학습) 대조군에 속한다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Fair-ranking work before this paper was almost entirely post-processing (FA*IR, Celis et al., Biega et al., Singh & Joachims): a possibly-biased LTR model is trained as usual and its output re-ranked. This (a) still deploys an unfair trained model, (b) presumes a fixed fairness/relevance trade-off enforced by hard constraints that ignore relevance judgments, and (c) for pre-processing, requires constructing "fair training data," which the authors show is non-trivial and prone to reverse discrimination.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

DELTR: extend ListNet's listwise cross-entropy loss with a differentiable disparate-exposure penalty, $L_{DELTR} = L + \gamma U$, where $U$ is the squared hinge of the gap between non-protected and protected average group exposure computed from top-one probabilities (see Concepts). Train a linear scorer by gradient descent on the combined loss. Larger $\gamma$ weights exposure parity more; the hinge asymmetry stops optimization once parity is reached.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Across all five experimental settings (W3C experts/gender, engineering students/school-type and gender, law students/gender and race), DELTR dominates FA*IR pre- and post-processing on the relevance-vs-exposure frontier.
  • Law students (race): DELTR $\gamma_{large}$ reaches exposure ratio 1.014 (near parity) at Kendall's tau 0.130, vs. FA*IR post $p^+$ at 1.085 exposure but tau 0.080; FA*IR pre $p^+$ collapses to tau 0.041 with exposure 1.726 (strong reverse discrimination).
  • The fairness/relevance trade-off is not universal: on engineering students (school type), Standard LTR and DELTR both beat colorblind LTR on relevance (K. tau 0.390–0.391 vs. 0.382) and exposure (1.070–1.075 vs. 0.962) — including the protected attribute helped both objectives.
  • FA*IR pre-processing with $p^+$ on W3C: P@10 drops from ~0.18 to 0.052 and exposure ratio overshoots to 2.058 — "fair training data" via re-ordering can invert the bias.
  • DELTR handles both bias types (bias unrelated to utility; different score distributions at equal utility) without a-priori knowledge of which is present.
5Cs Analysis5Cs 분석

Category: new method (in-processing fair learning-to-rank) + empirical study. Context: builds on ListNet (Cao et al. 2007) and Singh & Joachims' exposure notion; compares against FA*IR (Zehlike et al. 2017) used as both pre- and post-processing; contrasts with Beutel et al. 2019's pairwise disparate-treatment approach (DELTR is listwise, disparate-impact). Correctness: results averaged over folds where folds exist (6 for W3C, 5 for engineering; law students only one 80/20 split). No error bars/CIs reported in Table 2. One dataset (W3C) has a synthetically injected gender bias. [SKIM — appendix-level rigor not checked] Contributions: (1) first listwise in-processing LTR method optimizing exposure parity at training time; (2) empirical taxonomy of bias types (bias unrelated to utility vs. different score distributions vs. pre-existing utility differences) showing "colorblind" can be best or worst depending on bias type; (3) demonstration that FA*IR-as-pre-processing can cause reverse discrimination and relevance collapse. Clarity: method is reproducible — loss and gradient are stated, code and datasets released.

Contribution vs Claim기여 대 주장
  • Stated claims: first listwise in-processing fair LTR; better than pre- and post-processing "across all tested scenarios"; asymmetric loss prevents reverse discrimination by design.
  • Demonstrated: superiority shown on 3 datasets / 5 settings against exactly one pre-/post-processing family (FA*IR) and ListNet baselines; no-over-compensation confirmed only in two synthetic settings mentioned in passing.
  • Gap: multi-group extension is "formalized" but explicitly not experimentally validated; systematic $\gamma$ selection is left to future work (chosen by hand from the $L/U$ ratio); the scorer is linear only — no evidence for neural rankers. [SKIM — verify no further baselines in appendix]
Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Disparate exposure (top-one-probability form)

  • What it computes: for each document, ListNet's top-one probability $P_{\hat{y}^{(q)}}(x_i^{(q)})$ multiplied by the position bias of position 1 ($v_1$) → per-document exposure; averaged within each group → per-group average exposure (eq. 3–4).
  • Why it was created: prior exposure definitions (Singh & Joachims 2018) operate on full probabilistic rankings, which are combinatorial; adapting exposure to top-one probabilities makes it match ListNet's loss and keeps it differentiable for gradient descent.
  • Concrete example: fairness in Table 2 is reported as the exposure ratio protected/non-protected; e.g. Standard LTR on Law Students (race) gives 0.853 (non-protected group over-exposed), DELTR $\gamma_{large}$ gives 1.014 (near parity).
  • How it is used in this paper: forms the unfairness term $U$ added to the training loss, and doubles as the evaluation metric.

DELTR loss (listwise fair LTR objective)

  • What it computes: $L_{DELTR}(y^{(q)}, \hat{y}^{(q)}) = L(y^{(q)}, \hat{y}^{(q)}) + \gamma\, U(\hat{y}^{(q)})$, where $L$ is ListNet's cross-entropy over top-one probabilities and $U(\hat{y}^{(q)}) = \max\big(0,\ \text{Exposure}(G_0|P_{\hat{y}^{(q)}}) - \text{Exposure}(G_1|P_{\hat{y}^{(q)}})\big)^2$ (squared hinge, eq. 5–6). Optimized by gradient descent over a linear scoring function $f_\omega(x) = \langle \omega \cdot x \rangle$.
  • Why it was created: post-processing fair-ranking methods still allow an unfair model to be trained and hard-code fairness constraints that may ignore relevance; pre-processing risks reverse discrimination. An in-processing term lets the model learn to discount protected features and their proxies during training.
  • Concrete example: $\gamma_{small}$ is set to the ratio $L/U$; $\gamma_{large}$ is 10x that (e.g. W3C experts: $\gamma_{small}=20K$, $\gamma_{large}=200K$).
  • How it is used in this paper: the core method; the squared hinge makes it asymmetric — it only pushes exposure up for the protected group, never penalizes rankings where the protected group already has equal-or-more exposure, so by design it cannot over-compensate into reverse discrimination.

Position bias $v_1$ and exposure follow Singh & Joachims (2018) "Fairness of Exposure in Rankings" — if that paper has a note in this survey, this is an adaptation of it, restricted to position 1.

Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: query-grouped training data with relevance judgments and a binary protected attribute per item; gradient-descent training of a linear model (trivial compute); position-bias value $v_1$; hand-tuned $\gamma$.
  • Code: available — experiments at https://github.com/MilkaLichtblau/DELTR-Experiments; standalone Java/Python library and Elasticsearch plugin at https://github.com/fair-search.
  • Modifications: replacing the linear scorer with a neural one requires re-deriving nothing (loss is differentiable) but is untested; extending beyond two groups is formalized only; adapting the exposure term from top-one probability to full-ranking exposure would change the objective.
  • Unknowns: missing link: how to set $\gamma$ systematically; missing link: behavior with listwise neural rankers and large candidate sets.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • Singh & Joachims, "Policy Learning for Fairness in Ranking" (NeurIPS 2019) — the other main in-processing exposure-fairness approach (policy-gradient over full rankings rather than top-one probability); natural comparison point.
  • Zehlike et al., "Fairness in Ranking" survey (ACM CSUR 2022) — same first author; situates DELTR in the pre/in/post-processing taxonomy this survey's SP4 feasibility map uses.
  • Beutel et al., "Fairness in Recommendation Ranking through Pairwise Comparisons" (KDD 2019) — pairwise disparate-treatment counterpart explicitly contrasted in this paper's related work.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: supervised listwise LTR with explicit per-query relevance judgments; two discrete social groups with a known binary protected attribute; linear scoring function; exposure computed only from top-one probability with position bias $v_1$; small tabular/text-feature datasets (200–22K items). Our setting (SP4, exposure-frequency-as-ranking-proxy survey): exposure/frequency disparities as a measurement or proxy signal in ranking systems; interventions ideally without retraining the scorer. Differences:

  • DELTR is an in-processing method that retrains the scorer — per the survey's O2 exclusion criteria this is recorded for the feasibility map only, not expanded along: reference only.
  • Exposure defined via top-one probability rather than full position-discount over the ranked list: missing link (whether conclusions carry to full DCG-style exposure) — [SKIM - verify].
  • Binary protected group with known membership vs. general item-frequency/exposure proxies without group labels: missing link.
  • Linear ranker on hand-engineered features vs. modern neural rankers: reference only.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Direct — the paper targets the root cause it names (an unfair model being trained on biased data) by moving the exposure-parity objective into the training loss itself rather than re-ranking symptoms after the fact; the caveat is that "fairness" is operationalized only as demographic-parity of top-one exposure, which leaves the deeper question of why the training data is biased untouched.
