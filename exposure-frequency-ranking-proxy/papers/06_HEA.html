Post-hoc Provider Fairness Adaptation via Hierarchical Exposure Alignment

Li et al. · arXiv · 2026

arXiv:2605.01524 · PDF · SP: SP1 · SP4

P4Track B

한 줄 요약

집계 KL 지표가 집단 내부의 노출 왜곡을 가리는 pseudo-fairness 현상을 보이고, 계층적 exposure 정렬로 이를 교정하는 재학습 불필요 adapter를 제안한다. 다만 내부 embedding 접근과 score 수정이 필요해 black-box 제약과는 맞지 않는다.

Problem해결하려는 문제

Existing provider-exposure-fairness methods split into two camps, both rigid: (1) in-processing methods (reweighting, fairness regularizers) couple fairness to model parameters, forcing expensive full retraining whenever the fairness objective changes; (2) post-processing rerankers (FairRec, CPFair, P-MMF) are model-agnostic but embed fixed fairness criteria in the reranking algorithm, so they cannot adapt to diverse or evolving fairness requirements. Additionally, the natural adapter-training objective — global KL to a target exposure distribution — treats all providers homogeneously and can report aggregate fairness while inter-group and intra-group exposure imbalance persists.

Solution / Contribution해결책 / 기여

PFA: freeze the pretrained recommender; attach a small MLP fairness adapter that maps frozen user–item embeddings to additive score corrections $\tilde{Y} = \hat{Y} + \Delta$. Train the adapter with:

$$\mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{HEFA} + \lambda_{acc} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{diffNDCG}$$

where $\mathcal{L}_{HEFA} = \lambda_{inter} D_{KL}(p^G \| t^G) + \lambda_{intra} \sum_{c=1}^{C} p^G_c \, D_{KL}(p^{(c)} \| t^{(c)})$, backed by an exact decomposition of global KL into inter-group + intra-group + calibration terms (Theorem 4.1), and $\mathcal{L}_{diffNDCG}$ is a soft-permutation-matrix relaxation of NDCG@K. Both losses are made differentiable via a differentiable sorting network. [SKIM - verify] proof details of Theorem 4.1 and diffNDCG gradient behavior not audited.

Key Results주요 결과
  • Amazon Beauty (BPRMF base): PFA reaches NDCG 0.0943 with Gini 0.6282 — best NDCG and best Gini among all 8 fairness baselines (base model: NDCG 0.1000, Gini 0.7260; best in-processing baseline SDRO: NDCG 0.0935, Gini 0.6305).
  • Consistent lowest Gini on all three datasets (Beauty 0.6282, Movies & TV 0.6403, RateBeer 0.8467) while retaining the highest NDCG among fairness-aware methods.
  • Subgroup analysis (Fig. 5): existing methods (CPFair, FairRec, FairDual, Multi-FR) improve inter-group exposure shares while raising within-group Gini — the "pseudo-fairness" phenomenon; PFA lowers both mid- and tail-group intra-group Gini.
  • Ablation (Fig. 4b): HEFA-based objectives beat direct KL counterparts — comparable fairness at higher accuracy.
  • Inference time comparable to in-processing methods; optimization-based rerankers (FairRec) time out on RateBeer (2.7M interactions).

Concepts Introduced도입된 개념

Provider exposure (position-weighted)

  • What it computes: for each provider $s$, sums over all users and all of that provider's items the position-bias weight $\pi(v \mid L_u) = 1/\log_2(1+\mathrm{rank}(v, L_u))$ of items appearing in top-$K$ lists → a scalar exposure score $e_s$ per provider (Eq. 1–2).
  • Why it was created: standard practice from prior fair-ranking work (Biega et al. 2018, Morik et al. 2020); reused here, not new — it converts discrete list membership into a rank-discounted attention proxy.
  • Concrete example: an item at rank 1 contributes $1/\log_2 2 = 1.0$; at rank 3 it contributes $1/\log_2 4 = 0.5$.
  • How it is used in this paper: the quantity whose distribution across providers is aligned to a target fair distribution.

Fairness adapter (PFA)

  • What it computes: concatenated frozen user+item embeddings $[\tilde{E}_u \| E_v] \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times N \times 2d}$ → 3-layer MLP → additive score correction $\Delta \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times N}$, added to the frozen recommender's base scores: $\tilde{Y} = \hat{Y} + \Delta$.
  • Why it was created: in-processing fairness methods require full retraining when the fairness objective changes; post-hoc rerankers hard-code fixed fairness criteria. The adapter decouples fairness optimization from backbone training (PEFT-style).
  • Concrete example: default adapter is a 2-layer MLP with hidden dim 32 — 2,113 parameters total (Table 4).
  • How it is used in this paper: the only trainable component; trained with HEFA + differentiable NDCG loss, used at inference as a plain additive score patch.

Hierarchical Exposure Fairness Alignment (HEFA)

  • What it computes: partitions providers into groups by historical exposure (head 20% / mid 60% / tail 20%), then decomposes the global KL divergence $D_{KL}(p \| t)$ between achieved and target exposure distributions into inter-group KL + exposure-weighted intra-group KL + a calibration term (Theorem 4.1), and optimizes $\mathcal{L}_{HEFA} = \lambda_{inter} D_{KL}(p^G \| t^G) + \lambda_{intra} \sum_c p^G_c D_{KL}(p^{(c)} \| t^{(c)})$.
  • Why it was created: global KL alignment can be satisfied at the aggregate level while severe inter-group and intra-group imbalances persist ("pseudo-fairness"), because it implicitly treats all providers equally despite imbalanced group sizes.
  • Concrete example: with $t^G_c = 1/C$ and $\lambda_{inter} = \lambda_{intra} = 1$, HEFA reduces to global KL only when group targets match aggregated provider targets; raising $\lambda_{inter}$ to 5:1 shifts exposure share from the head group to mid/tail groups (Fig. 7).
  • How it is used in this paper: the fairness training objective for the adapter.

Differentiable sorting network (for exposure + NDCG)

  • What it computes: predicted score vector → layered soft compare-and-swap (Cauchy-smoothed interpolation, $\beta = 10$) → doubly stochastic soft permutation matrix $P_{soft}$, whose entry $[P_{soft}]_{vk}$ is the probability item $v$ lands at rank $k$. Enables differentiable expected exposure (Eq. 9) and differentiable NDCG (Eq. 19).
  • Why it was created: defined in Petersen et al. (ICML 2021 / ICLR 2022); reused here because both exposure and NDCG depend on discrete ranks, which block gradients.
  • Concrete example: each soft swap is a $2 \times 2$ doubly stochastic block $\begin{bmatrix}1-\alpha & \alpha\\ \alpha & 1-\alpha\end{bmatrix}$ embedded in an $N \times N$ identity.
  • How it is used in this paper: the differentiability bridge that lets the adapter be trained end-to-end against rank-dependent fairness and accuracy losses.
Implementation Feasibility구현 가능성
  • Needs: pretrained CF embeddings, provider labels per item, a differentiable sorting network implementation (Petersen et al.), single consumer GPU (RTX 4080 used); adapter itself is ~2K parameters.
  • Code: available at https://github.com/Tam-JQK/Post-train.
  • Modifications: for our exposure-frequency-as-proxy question, the reusable parts are the differentiable exposure estimator (Eq. 9) and the hierarchical KL decomposition — the adapter/fairness objective would need repurposing. Differentiable sorting is $O(N^2)$-ish in the permutation matrix per user; candidate-set truncation needed at scale.
  • Unknowns: missing link: sensitivity of results to the choice of position-bias curve; missing link: behavior under non-uniform fairness targets (claimed, untested); missing link: how provider group boundaries (fixed 20/60/20 split) affect the decomposition's benefit.
Follow-up Papers관련 후속 논문
  • FairSort: Learning to Fair Rank for Personalized Recommendations in Two-Sided Platforms (Wu et al., TKDE 2025) — same exposure formalization and target-distribution framing; cited [39] as the source of the fairness-target convention.
  • LTP-MMF: Toward Long-Term Provider Max-Min Fairness under Recommendation Feedback Loops (Xu et al., TOIS 2025) — extends exposure fairness to the feedback-loop setting this paper excludes; relevant if the survey covers exposure→popularity dynamics.
  • Understanding Accuracy-Fairness Trade-offs in Re-ranking through Elasticity in Economics (ElasticRank; Xu et al., SIGIR 2025) — strongest recent reranking baseline; characterizes the accuracy–fairness trade-off this paper navigates empirically.
  • Differentiable Sorting Networks for Scalable Sorting and Ranking Supervision (Petersen et al., ICML 2021) — the differentiability machinery; relevant wherever rank-dependent exposure statistics must be optimized end-to-end.
Condition Delta — transfer to our setting조건 차이 — 우리 설정으로의 전이

Paper's setting: inputs = frozen collaborative-filtering embeddings (BPRMF/LightGCN/SGL, dim 32) + provider labels (brand/brewer) per item; outputs = top-$K$ ($K=20$) recommendation lists; conditions = static offline evaluation, 3 public datasets, exposure measured with log-decay position bias, uniform fairness target, provider groups fixed from training-set exposure counts. Our setting: SP context — exposure frequency used as a ranking proxy; we care about how exposure allocation is measured, whether frequency-of-exposure statistics can stand in for ranking quality/fairness signals, and how exposure-based objectives distort or preserve ranking. [SKIM - verify SP1/SP4 exact framing] Differences:

  • Paper treats exposure as the target to redistribute, whereas our question treats exposure frequency as a proxy signal for ranking: reference only — the exposure formalization (Eq. 1–2) and the pseudo-fairness finding (aggregate exposure metrics masking within-group skew) transfer as evidence, the fairness-adapter machinery does not directly.
  • Position-bias model is a fixed logarithmic decay, not learned or validated against real attention data: missing link for any claim that exposure ≈ user attention.
  • Static offline top-$K$ lists; no feedback loop between exposure and future training data (LTP-MMF cited as covering that): missing link if our SP concerns longitudinal exposure→popularity dynamics.
  • Provider-level aggregation (brands/brewers), not item-level exposure: resolved — paper explicitly cites TaxRank/ElasticRank as the item-level analogues.
Root-cause vs. Symptom근본 원인 대 증상

Mixed — the HEFA decomposition directly attacks the root cause of pseudo-fairness in KL-based exposure alignment (aggregate objectives structurally mask inter/intra-group imbalance, proven via the exact KL decomposition), but the overall framework is an additive score patch on a frozen backbone: the popularity-concentration mechanism inside the base recommender (BPR training on skewed interactions) is left intact and merely corrected downstream.
